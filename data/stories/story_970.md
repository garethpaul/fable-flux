# Story story_970

**Spike and the Stubborn Jar**

Spike was a bright red tricycle who lived in the Sunny Days Nursery. He had shiny silver handlebars and a cheerful bell that went "ring-ring!" Spike loved helping the children every day.

One morning, Spike rolled into the art room. The walls were covered with colorful paintings. Crayons and markers sat in neat rows. Everything smelled like fresh paper and paint.

"Oh no!" cried Miss Emma, the teacher. She held a big jar of blue paint. The lid was stuck tight. "The children want to paint the sky today. But I can't open this jar!"

Spike's bell jingled softly. He wanted to help Miss Emma. The children would be sad without blue paint. How could a tricycle open a jar?

Spike thought hard. His front wheel spun slowly. "I know!" he said to himself. "I'll use my strong handlebars!"

Spike rolled close to the table. He turned his handlebars toward the jar. Very carefully, he tried to grip the lid. The metal felt cold against his handlebars.

Spike pushed and twisted. His red frame wiggled. His bell rang "ting-ting-ting!" But the jar lid wouldn't budge. It was stuck like glue.

"This is harder than I thought," Spike puffed. His little engine felt tired. The jar sat there, still closed tight.

Miss Emma patted Spike's seat gently. "Thank you for trying, Spike. You're very kind."

But Spike didn't give up. He rolled around the room, thinking. His wheels squeaked softly on the smooth floor. There had to be another way!

Then Spike saw the rubber mat by the sink. It had bumpy dots all over it. The mat helped people grip slippery things. Spike's bell rang with excitement!

"That's it!" Spike said. "I need better grip!"

Spike carefully pushed the rubber mat with his front wheel. The mat slid across the floor. It made a soft swooshing sound. Spike positioned it right next to the paint jar.

"Miss Emma," Spike called. "Put the jar on this mat!"

Miss Emma smiled. She understood Spike's plan. She placed the jar on the bumpy rubber mat. The jar sat steady and secure.

Now Spike tried again. He rolled close and gripped the lid with his handlebars. This time, the rubber mat helped. It kept the jar from slipping and sliding.

Spike twisted slowly to the left. His handlebars felt stronger now. The jar stayed still on the grippy mat. He could feel the lid starting to move!

"Turn, turn, turn," Spike whispered to himself. His whole body worked together. His wheels, his frame, his handlebars - everything helped.

Pop! The lid came off with a happy sound. Beautiful blue paint sparkled inside the jar. It looked like a summer sky in a bottle.

"Hooray!" cheered Miss Emma. "You did it, Spike! You opened the jar!"

Spike's bell rang proudly. "Ring-ring-ring!" His red paint seemed to shine brighter. He felt warm and happy inside.

Just then, the children came running in. "Time to paint!" they called. Their voices filled the room with joy.

Miss Emma showed them the open jar. "Spike helped us today. He didn't give up when it was hard."

The children gathered around Spike. They patted his seat and rang his bell. "Thank you, Spike!" they said. "You're the best tricycle ever!"

As the children painted beautiful blue skies, Spike watched happily. Their brushes made soft swishing sounds. The paint smelled fresh and clean. Everyone was smiling.

Spike learned something important that day. When one way doesn't work, try another way. Think of new ideas. Ask for help when you need it. Never give up on helping friends.

The blue paint jar sat open on the table. Inside, the paint sparkled like magic. And Spike knew that tomorrow would bring new adventures and new ways to help.

His bell gave one last happy ring. "Ring!"

The end.
