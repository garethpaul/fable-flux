# Story story_1093

Sam sat on the soft grass in her backyard. The morning sun felt warm on her face. She looked down at her bright red shoes. The laces were all twisted and messy.

"Oh no," Sam said quietly. "My shoes are untied again."

Sam wiggled her toes inside her shoes. The laces flopped around like sleepy snakes. She had tried to tie them this morning. But the loops got all tangled up.

Mom was inside making breakfast. Dad was getting ready for work. Sam wanted to play on her swing set. But loose laces might make her trip and fall.

"I can do this myself," Sam said with a big breath.

She bent down and grabbed both laces. She pulled them tight across her foot. Then she tried to make a bow like Mom showed her. She made one loop. Then she tried to wrap the other lace around it.

The loop fell apart. The laces got twisted again. Sam's fingers felt clumsy and slow.

"This is too hard," Sam said. Her bottom lip started to wobble.

She tried again. This time she made the loops smaller. She twisted and pulled and wrapped the laces. But they came undone right away.

Sam sat back on the grass. A tear rolled down her cheek. The grass felt cool under her hands. A gentle breeze tickled her hair.

"Maybe I'm too little," she whispered.

Just then, her big brother Jake came outside. He was bouncing his orange basketball. The ball made a happy thumping sound on the concrete.

"Hey Sam," Jake said with a smile. "Want to play catch?"

"I can't," Sam said sadly. "My shoes won't stay tied. I might trip."

Jake sat down next to Sam on the grass. He put his basketball aside. "Shoe tying is tricky," he said kindly. "I remember when I couldn't do it either."

"Really?" Sam asked. Her eyes got wide.

"Really," Jake nodded. "Want me to show you a different way?"

Sam nodded eagerly. Jake pointed to her red shoes.

"First, let's make the laces the same length," Jake said. He helped Sam pull the laces until they matched.

"Now make an X," Jake said. Sam crossed the laces over each other.

"Pull one lace under and through," Jake showed her slowly. Sam copied what he did. Her small fingers moved carefully.

"Good job!" Jake said. "Now comes the fun part. Make bunny ears."

Sam giggled. "Bunny ears?"

"Two loops that look like rabbit ears," Jake explained. He helped Sam make two small loops with the laces.

"Now the bunny ears give each other a hug," Jake said. Sam wrapped one loop around the other.

"Pull them through and tighten," Jake said gently.

Sam pulled carefully. The loops stayed put! She had made a real bow on her shoe.

"I did it!" Sam jumped up happily. The bow stayed tied and neat.

"You sure did," Jake said proudly. "Now try the other shoe by yourself."

Sam sat back down. This time her fingers felt more sure. She made the X with the laces. She pulled one through. She made two bunny ears. She wrapped them in a hug. She pulled them tight.

Both shoes were tied! The bows looked perfect and even.

"Look at me!" Sam called to Jake. She stood up and marched around the yard. Her shoes stayed tied tight. She felt so proud and grown up.

"Great job, Sam," Jake said. "You kept trying even when it was hard."

Sam beamed with happiness. The sun felt extra warm now. The grass smelled fresh and sweet. Her red shoes looked so neat and tidy.

"Can we play catch now?" Sam asked.

"Absolutely," Jake said, picking up his basketball.

Sam ran to get her glove from the porch. Her tied shoes felt secure and comfortable. She didn't worry about tripping anymore.

As they played catch in the sunny backyard, Sam felt proud of herself. She had learned something new and important. When things seemed too hard, she could ask for help. And if she kept trying, she could learn anything.

Her shoes stayed perfectly tied all morning long.

The end.
