# Jack and the Stubborn Jar

Jack loved playing in Grandma's garden. The flowers smelled sweet like candy. The grass felt soft under his bare feet. Today, Jack had a special job to do.

"Here you go, Jack," said Grandma with a smile. She handed him a big glass jar. "The butterfly seeds are inside. Can you open it for me?"

Jack looked at the jar. It was round and smooth. The metal lid sparkled in the sunshine. Inside, he could see tiny brown seeds waiting to become beautiful butterflies flowers.

"I can do it!" Jack said proudly. He twisted the lid with both hands. He turned it left. He turned it right. But the lid wouldn't budge.

"Hmm," Jack said. He tried again. Twist, twist, twist. His little hands gripped the cold metal lid. Still nothing happened.

Jack sat down on the garden path. The smooth stones felt warm from the sun. He looked at the stubborn jar. The seeds seemed to look back at him.

"Maybe I need to be stronger," Jack thought. He stood up and held the jar tight against his tummy. He used all his muscles. His face turned red. But the lid stayed stuck.

Jack felt frustrated. A little tear rolled down his cheek. The butterflies in the garden fluttered around him. They seemed to be saying, "Don't give up, Jack!"

"What else can I try?" Jack wondered aloud. He looked around the garden for ideas. He saw Grandma's watering can. He saw the wooden garden bench. He saw Dad's toolbox by the fence.

"Maybe I need help from something else," Jack said. He carried the jar to the wooden bench. The bench was rough and bumpy. Jack placed the jar on the bench and tried to twist the lid again.

This time, something different happened! The bench helped hold the jar steady. Jack could use both hands on just the lid. Twist, twist, twist!

"It's moving!" Jack shouted with excitement. The lid made a tiny clicking sound. It was working!

Jack kept turning. Click, click, click. The lid slowly came loose. His heart felt happy and bouncy.

"Pop!" The lid came off completely. Jack held it up high like a prize. "I did it! I did it!"

The seeds inside smelled earthy and fresh. They were ready to be planted. Jack carefully poured them into his palm. They felt smooth and cool.

"Jack!" called Grandma from across the garden. "How are you doing with that jar?"

"I opened it!" Jack called back. He ran to show her, being careful not to spill the precious seeds.

Grandma's eyes twinkled. "You're so clever! How did you figure it out?"

"I tried lots of ways," Jack explained. "First I used my hands. Then I tried being stronger. Then I used the bench to help me. The bench held the jar while I twisted the lid!"

"That's wonderful problem-solving," Grandma said. She gave Jack a warm hug. "You didn't give up, even when it was hard."

Together, they planted the seeds in neat little rows. Jack made small holes with his finger. Grandma showed him how deep to plant each seed. They covered them gently with soft dirt.

"In a few weeks, these will grow into beautiful flowers," Grandma said. "The butterflies will love them."

Jack watered the seeds with the small watering can. The water made tiny pattering sounds on the dirt. Everything smelled fresh and green.

As they walked back to the house, Jack felt proud and happy. He had solved a tricky problem all by himself. Well, almost by himself. The bench had helped too.

"Sometimes we need to try different ways," Jack told Grandma. "And sometimes we need helpers."

"That's exactly right," Grandma agreed. "You're getting very wise, Jack."

Jack smiled big. He couldn't wait to see the flowers grow. And he couldn't wait for the next problem to solve. Because now he knew something important: if one way doesn't work, he could always try another way.

The garden hummed with happy sounds. Jack hummed too.

The end.
