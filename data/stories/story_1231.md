# Story story_1231

**The Sheep Who Solved the Toy Problem**

Woolly the sheep loved to help. She had soft, fluffy white wool and kind brown eyes. Every morning, she worked in the big farm kitchen.

Today was special. The farmer's children had played all morning. Toys covered the kitchen floor everywhere. Red blocks sat by the stove. Blue cars hid under the table. Yellow dolls leaned against chairs. Purple balls rolled near the sink.

"Oh my!" said Woolly. "So many toys to put away!"

The toy box lived upstairs in the playroom. Woolly looked at all the scattered toys. She counted them slowly. "One, two, three... twenty toys!"

Her small hooves could only hold one toy at a time. "This will take forever," she worried.

Woolly picked up a red block. She carried it upstairs. Step by step, she climbed. Her hooves clicked on each wooden step. She dropped the block in the toy box with a soft thud.

Back downstairs she went. Click, click, click went her hooves. She picked up a blue car. Up the stairs again. Click, click, click. Drop, thud.

After six trips, Woolly felt very tired. Her legs ached. Her breathing came fast. Sweat dripped from her woolly forehead.

"There must be a better way," she panted.

Woolly sat down to think. She wiggled her ears. She tapped her hoof. Then she had an idea!

"I'll use my wool!" she said excitedly.

Woolly rolled on the kitchen floor. Her fluffy wool picked up toys like a magnet. The red blocks stuck to her left side. Blue cars clung to her back. Yellow dolls nestled in her wool. Purple balls bounced and settled in.

Soon, Woolly looked like a walking toy store. Toys poked out everywhere. She felt lumpy and bumpy. But she smiled proudly.

Woolly started toward the stairs. But oh no! The toys were too heavy. Her legs wobbled. She took one step and tumbled over. Toys scattered across the floor again.

"Baa!" she cried sadly. "That didn't work either."

Woolly felt like giving up. She sat among the toys and sighed. A tear rolled down her fuzzy cheek.

Just then, Farmer Brown walked into the kitchen. He saw Woolly surrounded by toys. He saw her sad face.

"What's wrong, Woolly?" he asked gently.

"I want to clean up," she explained. "But I can't carry all these toys. My hooves are too small. My wool makes me too heavy."

Farmer Brown smiled kindly. "Let me help you think," he said.

He looked around the kitchen. His eyes stopped at something in the corner. "Look, Woolly! The laundry basket!"

Woolly's eyes grew wide. The big wicker basket sat empty and waiting.

"We can work together," said Farmer Brown.

Woolly felt excited again. She picked up toys with her mouth. Farmer Brown picked up toys with his hands. Together, they filled the basket quickly.

The red blocks clinked together. The blue cars rolled smoothly. The yellow dolls sat quietly. The purple balls bounced softly.

"Now we can carry them all at once!" said Woolly.

Farmer Brown lifted one handle. Woolly grabbed the other handle with her teeth. Together, they carried the heavy basket upstairs.

In the playroom, they emptied the basket. All twenty toys tumbled into the toy box. The room looked clean and tidy again.

"Thank you for helping," said Farmer Brown. "You never gave up."

Woolly's wool sparkled in the sunlight. She felt proud and happy. "I learned something important today," she said.

"What's that?" asked Farmer Brown.

"Sometimes the best solution needs a friend," Woolly replied. "And it's okay to ask for help."

That evening, Woolly told all the farm animals about her adventure. She learned that big problems often have simple solutions. She also learned that working together makes everything easier.

From that day on, Woolly always remembered to ask for help when she needed it. And she always offered to help others too.

The kitchen stayed clean. The toys stayed organized. And Woolly stayed happy, knowing she had solved her very first big problem.

The end.
