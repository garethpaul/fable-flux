# Story story_915

**The Little Blue Boat and Grandma Rose**

On top of a green hill lived a little blue boat named Splash. The hill was far from any water. Splash sat alone under the big oak tree every day.

"I wish I had someone to play with," Splash said sadly. His bright blue paint was starting to fade. His little sail drooped down like a tired puppy.

The wind made soft whooshing sounds through the grass. Birds chirped in the trees above. But Splash felt very lonely on the quiet hill.

One sunny morning, Grandma Rose walked up the hill. She had silver hair and kind brown eyes. Her walking stick made tiny tapping sounds on the ground.

"Hello there, little boat," Grandma Rose said gently. "You look sad today."

Splash's sail perked up a little. "I am sad," he said. "I have no friends to play with. Boats need friends too."

Grandma Rose sat down on the soft grass. Her flowery dress spread out like a colorful blanket. "Would you like to be my friend?" she asked with a warm smile.

"Really?" Splash asked. His blue paint seemed to sparkle again. "But I'm just a boat. I can't even float here."

"That's okay," Grandma Rose laughed. Her laugh sounded like tiny silver bells. "We can play lots of games together."

Grandma Rose pushed Splash gently down the hill. "Wheee!" they both called out. The green grass tickled Splash's bottom. The wind filled his little sail.

"This feels like sailing!" Splash said happily. "But even better!"

They rolled back up the hill together. Grandma Rose's cheeks turned pink from the exercise. But she was smiling big.

"Let's play pretend," Grandma Rose suggested. She climbed carefully into Splash's boat. "We can sail to magical places."

"Where should we go?" Splash asked excitedly.

"To the Candy Island!" Grandma Rose said. "I can smell the sweet cotton candy already."

Splash closed his eyes and imagined. "I can feel the warm ocean waves," he said. "And hear the seagulls calling."

They pretended to sail all morning. They visited Rainbow Falls and Butterfly Beach. They met friendly dolphins and singing whales.

"This is the best day ever," Splash said. His sail danced in the breeze. "I never knew playing could be so fun."

Grandma Rose patted his side gently. "Friends make everything more fun," she said.

After lunch, they played hide and seek. Splash hid behind the big oak tree. Grandma Rose found him right away.

"Your sail was sticking out," she giggled.

Then Grandma Rose hid behind a bush. Splash found her too. Her silver hair was shining in the sunlight.

As the sun got lower, they sat together quietly. The hill was peaceful and warm. Butterflies danced around the wildflowers.

"Grandma Rose," Splash said softly. "Will you come back tomorrow?"

"Of course, dear friend," she said. "I'll bring us a picnic lunch too."

Splash felt so happy his paint seemed brighter. "I'm not lonely anymore," he said.

"Neither am I," Grandma Rose smiled. "I was lonely too, you know."

"Really?" Splash asked.

"Really," she nodded. "But now we have each other."

The next day, Grandma Rose came back with a basket. They shared sandwiches and cookies under the oak tree. Other children saw them playing and came to join.

Soon, Splash had many friends on the hill. But Grandma Rose was always his very best friend.

Every afternoon, they sailed to new pretend places. Sometimes they just talked and watched the clouds. The hill became the happiest place in the whole world.

"Thank you for being my friend," Splash said one evening.

"Thank you for being mine," Grandma Rose replied.

The little blue boat never felt lonely again. And neither did Grandma Rose. Their friendship filled the hill with joy and laughter every single day.

From that day on, the hill was known as Friendship Hill. And everyone who visited could feel the love between a little blue boat and his dear friend, Grandma Rose.

The end.
