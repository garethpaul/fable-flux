# Story story_1324

**Grandma Rose and the Worried Woodland School**

Grandma Rose loved her cozy cottage in the woods. Every morning, she fed the squirrels and rabbits. She picked berries and sang happy songs.

One sunny day, Grandma Rose heard crying. She followed the sad sounds deeper into the forest. Behind a big oak tree, she found a little fox named Finn.

"What's wrong, dear?" asked Grandma Rose gently.

Finn sniffled. "Tomorrow is my first day of forest school. I'm so scared! What if the other animals don't like me? What if I can't find my classroom?"

Grandma Rose's heart felt warm and caring. She remembered feeling nervous about new things too. "Oh sweetie, I understand. Let me help you feel better."

Grandma Rose thought hard. Then she smiled. "I know! Let's practice being brave. Stand up tall like a mighty bear!"

Finn tried to stand tall. But his little legs shook. His tail drooped low. "I still feel scared, Grandma Rose," he whispered.

"Hmm," said Grandma Rose. "That didn't work very well."

She looked around the peaceful forest. Birds chirped in the trees. Flowers swayed in the gentle breeze. The air smelled like pine and fresh earth.

"I have another idea!" said Grandma Rose. Her eyes twinkled with excitement. "Let's walk to the school together. We can see what it looks like."

Finn's ears perked up a little. "Really? You would do that?"

"Of course, dear one," said Grandma Rose. She took Finn's small paw in her wrinkled hand.

They walked through the forest together. Grandma Rose pointed out pretty butterflies. She showed Finn colorful mushrooms. She picked sweet wild strawberries for them to share.

Soon they reached a clearing. There stood the forest school! It had big windows and bright red doors. A wooden sign said "Welcome!" in cheerful letters.

"Look how friendly it seems," said Grandma Rose softly.

They peeked through the windows. Inside, they saw cozy reading corners. There were art supplies and building blocks. Picture books filled the shelves.

"The classroom looks fun," whispered Finn. His voice sounded less scared.

In the playground, they saw a tire swing. There was a sandbox and climbing logs. Everything looked safe and exciting.

"The other animals will love playing with you here," said Grandma Rose.

Just then, a kind-looking owl flew over. "Hello there! I'm Mrs. Hoot, the teacher. Are you starting school tomorrow?"

Finn nodded shyly. His paws felt less sweaty now.

"Wonderful!" hooted Mrs. Hoot. "I have special name tags ready. Would you like to see yours?"

She showed Finn a beautiful name tag. It had his name written in gold letters. Little stars decorated the edges.

"This is just for me?" asked Finn. His tail started to wag.

"Just for you," smiled Mrs. Hoot. "Every student gets their very own special tag."

Grandma Rose squeezed Finn's paw gently. "See? You already belong here."

As they walked home, Finn felt much better. The forest smelled like adventure now. The trees seemed to whisper encouraging words.

"Thank you, Grandma Rose," said Finn. "I'm still a little nervous. But I'm excited too!"

"That's perfectly normal, sweetie," said Grandma Rose. "Being brave doesn't mean not feeling scared. It means doing something good even when you feel worried."

The next morning, Finn walked to school with confidence. He wore his special name tag proudly. Other young animals smiled and waved at him.

Grandma Rose watched from her garden. Her heart felt full of joy. She had learned something important too. Sometimes the best solutions come from understanding and kindness.

That evening, Finn visited Grandma Rose. His eyes sparkled with happiness.

"School was wonderful!" he said. "I made three new friends. We built a fort together!"

Grandma Rose hugged him tight. "I'm so proud of you for being brave."

From that day on, whenever forest animals felt worried about new things, they visited Grandma Rose. She always helped them find courage through understanding and gentle preparation.

The end.
