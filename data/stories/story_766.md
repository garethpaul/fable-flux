# Story story_766

**Penny's Shoe Problem**

Penny the horse lived in a cozy attic. The wooden floors creaked under her hooves. Sunlight streamed through the round window. Dust danced in the golden beams.

Every morning, Penny looked at her special red shoes. They had long, white laces that curled and twisted. All the other horses could tie their shoes. But Penny could not.

"I want to wear my pretty shoes," Penny said sadly. She touched the soft leather with her nose. The shoes smelled like flowers and sunshine.

Penny tried to pick up the laces. Her hooves were too big and round. The laces slipped away like wiggly worms. They fell to the floor with tiny thumps.

"This is too hard," Penny whimpered. Her brown eyes filled with tears. She stamped her hoof on the creaky floor.

Mrs. Robin flew through the open window. Her red breast was bright and cheerful. She perched on Penny's mane.

"Why are you sad, dear Penny?" chirped Mrs. Robin.

"I cannot tie my shoes," Penny sniffled. "My hooves are too clumsy. I will never wear my beautiful red shoes."

Mrs. Robin tilted her tiny head. "Let me help you think," she said kindly.

Penny had an idea. "Maybe I can use my teeth!" she said excitedly.

She bent her long neck down low. Her soft lips grabbed one white lace. She pulled it through the metal holes. The lace felt smooth in her mouth.

But the laces were slippery. They tasted salty and strange. When Penny tried to make a bow, the laces fell out. They landed in a tangled mess.

"Oh no!" Penny cried. The laces looked like a bowl of spaghetti. "This is even worse than before!"

Mrs. Robin fluttered her wings. "Do not give up, sweet Penny. There must be another way."

Penny sat down on the soft attic rug. She thought very hard. Her ears twitched back and forth. The old attic was quiet except for the wind outside.

Suddenly, Penny's eyes grew wide. "I have another idea!" she neighed happily.

She trotted to her toy box. Inside were colorful ribbons and sparkly buttons. Penny found a long, purple ribbon that shimmered like water.

"Mrs. Robin, will you help me?" asked Penny politely.

"Of course!" chirped the little bird.

Mrs. Robin was very clever with her tiny claws. She grabbed one end of the ribbon. Penny held the other end gently in her teeth.

Together, they wrapped the ribbon around Penny's hoof. Mrs. Robin flew in circles, weaving the ribbon through the shoe holes. The purple ribbon looked like a beautiful dance in the air.

"Pull it tight!" sang Mrs. Robin.

Penny pulled carefully. The ribbon made a perfect, pretty bow. It looked like a purple butterfly sitting on her shoe.

"We did it!" Penny pranced with joy. Her hooves made happy clicking sounds on the wooden floor.

They put ribbons on all four shoes. Purple, pink, yellow, and blue. Penny looked like a rainbow horse.

"Thank you, Mrs. Robin!" Penny nuzzled her friend gently. "You helped me solve my problem."

"You solved it yourself," chirped Mrs. Robin proudly. "You did not give up. You tried a new way."

Penny clip-clopped around the attic. Her colorful ribbons fluttered like tiny flags. The shoes fit perfectly on her hooves.

From that day on, Penny wore her special shoes everywhere. She learned that some problems need creative solutions. When one way does not work, there is always another way to try.

Other horses came to visit Penny's attic. They loved her rainbow ribbon shoes. Penny taught them her special way of tying shoes too.

"Every problem has a solution," Penny would say with a smile. "Sometimes you just need a good friend and a creative idea."

The attic filled with laughter and the sound of happy hooves. Penny's ribbon shoes reminded everyone that being different can be wonderful.

And Mrs. Robin? She became Penny's very best friend forever.

The end.
