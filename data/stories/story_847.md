# Story story_847

**Drumma's Shoelace Adventure**

Drumma the drum lived in the long, sunny hallway of Melody Elementary School. She had a shiny red surface and golden rims that sparkled when the light hit them. Every morning, children walked past her on their way to music class.

But Drumma had a big problem. She couldn't tie her shoes.

"Look at my beautiful new shoelaces!" Drumma said one Tuesday morning. Two bright blue ribbons hung from small holes near her base. The music teacher had given them to her as a special gift.

The ribbons dragged on the floor. They got tangled around her drum legs. When children tried to play her, the loose laces made soft whooshing sounds.

"I wish I could tie these myself," Drumma sighed. She watched the children walk by with their neat, tied shoes. Their laces made perfect little bows.

Mrs. Chen, the music teacher, noticed Drumma's sad expression. "What's wrong, dear Drumma?" she asked gently.

"I can't tie my shoelaces," Drumma replied. "They keep getting messy. I want to look neat like the children."

Mrs. Chen smiled warmly. "Let's try to solve this together."

First, Mrs. Chen tried to help. She bent down and picked up Drumma's blue laces. "Watch carefully," she said. "First, you make a loop."

But Drumma had no hands. She couldn't grab the laces. She tried to use her drumstick holders, but they were too far away. The laces slipped and fell.

"This is too hard," Drumma said sadly. "I'll never have pretty tied shoes."

The children in music class heard Drumma's worry. Little Emma stepped forward. She had curly brown hair and kind eyes.

"Maybe we can help differently," Emma suggested. "What if we work together?"

Emma sat cross-legged on the floor next to Drumma. "I'll be your hands," she said. "You can tell me what to do."

Drumma felt hopeful again. "Really? You'd help me?"

"Of course!" Emma said. "Friends help each other."

Emma picked up the blue laces. "Okay, Drumma. What should I do first?"

"Make two loops," Drumma said excitedly. "Like bunny ears!"

Emma made two loops with the soft blue ribbons. They looked like tiny blue butterflies.

"Now cross them over," Drumma instructed. She had watched Mrs. Chen do this many times.

Emma crossed the loops carefully. Her small fingers moved slowly and gently.

"Pull one loop through the hole," Drumma said. "Then pull tight!"

Emma followed Drumma's directions perfectly. She pulled the loops snug but not too tight. The blue laces formed a beautiful bow.

"We did it!" Drumma cheered. Her voice echoed happily in the hallway. "Look at my perfect bow!"

The other children clapped and cheered. The bow sat prettily on Drumma's front side. The blue ribbons matched her happy mood.

"Thank you, Emma," Drumma said gratefully. "You were wonderful hands for me."

Emma giggled. "Thank you for teaching me. I didn't know drums could know about tying shoes!"

From that day on, Emma visited Drumma every morning before music class. They practiced tying bows together. Sometimes other children helped too.

Drumma learned that asking for help wasn't giving up. It was being smart. She also learned that working together made hard things easier.

The blue bows became Drumma's favorite part of herself. When children played her during music class, she felt proud and pretty. Her sound was clear and strong.

Mrs. Chen watched with a warm smile. "Drumma, you solved your problem beautifully," she said. "You didn't give up, and you found a creative solution."

Drumma's golden rims sparkled with joy. "I learned that friends make everything better," she said. "And that there's always more than one way to solve a problem."

Every morning, the hallway filled with the sound of children's laughter and Drumma's happy drumbeats. Her blue bows danced gently as she played, reminding everyone that persistence and friendship can solve any problem.

The end.
