# Woolly's Big Bath Adventure

In a cozy basement below a red farmhouse, lived a fluffy white sheep named Woolly. The basement was Woolly's special playroom. Colorful toys filled wooden shelves. Soft rugs covered the cool floor. A small window let in golden sunshine.

One sunny morning, Farmer Mae called down the stairs. "Woolly! Bath time!" Her voice echoed in the basement.

Woolly's ears drooped. His tail stopped wagging. "No bath!" he bleated loudly. "I don't want a bath!"

Woolly loved playing in mud puddles. He loved rolling in grass. He loved being dirty and smelly. But baths? Baths were scary and wet!

"Come on up, little sheep," called Farmer Mae.

Woolly shook his woolly head. He had an idea! He would hide behind his toy box. Woolly squeezed his fluffy body behind the wooden box. "She can't see me now," he whispered.

Farmer Mae's footsteps came down the stairs. "Woolly? Where are you, little one?"

Woolly held his breath. But his white wool stuck out everywhere! Farmer Mae giggled. "I can see your fluffy tail, Woolly."

Woolly sighed. Hiding didn't work. He needed another plan!

Next, Woolly tried running. He zoomed around the basement. His hooves clicked on the floor. "Can't catch me!" he bleated happily.

But Farmer Mae was patient. She sat on the bottom step. "I'll wait right here, Woolly," she said kindly.

After five minutes of running, Woolly felt tired. His legs were wobbly. Running didn't work either!

Then Woolly had his biggest idea yet. He would make himself too dirty for a bath! He rolled on his dusty rug. He rubbed against the basement walls. He even stuck his nose in an old flower pot.

"Look at me!" Woolly called proudly. "I'm too dirty now!"

Farmer Mae smiled gently. "Oh my! Now you really need that bath, little sheep."

Woolly's shoulders sagged. None of his plans worked. He felt worried and scared.

Farmer Mae sat beside him on the soft rug. "What's wrong, Woolly? Why don't you want a bath?"

Woolly's voice was very small. "Baths are cold and scary. The water makes loud splashing sounds. I don't like getting wet."

"Oh, sweet Woolly," said Farmer Mae. "I understand now. Would you like to see the bath first?"

Woolly nodded slowly. Maybe looking wouldn't be so bad.

They walked upstairs together. In the bathroom, Farmer Mae showed Woolly a small, shallow tub. The water was warm and bubbly. It smelled like sweet lavender flowers.

"Feel the water," said Farmer Mae gently.

Woolly touched the water with one hoof. It was warm! It felt nice and tingly. The bubbles tickled his hoof and made him giggle.

"The water isn't scary," Woolly said with surprise.

"Would you like to try stepping in?" asked Farmer Mae.

Woolly took a deep breath. He stepped one hoof into the tub. Then another hoof. The warm water felt wonderful on his tired legs!

"This feels good!" Woolly bleated happily.

Farmer Mae used a soft sponge to wash Woolly's wool. The soap made more bubbles. Woolly played with the fluffy bubbles. He made bubble beards and bubble hats.

"I love bubbles!" laughed Woolly.

When bath time ended, Farmer Mae wrapped Woolly in a fluffy towel. She dried his wool gently. Woolly felt clean and fresh and cozy.

"Thank you for helping me be brave," said Woolly.

"You solved this problem all by yourself," said Farmer Mae proudly. "You tried different ideas. You kept going when things felt hard. And you told me your feelings."

Woolly smiled big. His wool was soft and white again. He smelled like sweet flowers. Best of all, he felt proud and brave.

From that day on, Woolly looked forward to bath time. He learned that trying new things could be fun. Sometimes scary things weren't scary at all!

And in his cozy basement playroom, Woolly played happily, knowing he could solve any problem with patience and courage.
