# Story story_1228

Jack sat at the big dining room table. Her sparkly purple shoes lay on the floor. The long laces looked like wiggly worms.

"Time for breakfast!" called Mom from the kitchen.

Jack looked down at her feet. Her socks had tiny rainbows on them. But her shoes just sat there, waiting.

She picked up one shoe. The laces felt smooth and slippery. Jack tried to remember what Dad showed her yesterday.

"Loop, swoop, and pull," she whispered.

Jack made one loop with her right hand. She tried to wrap the other lace around it. But the loop slipped away like a sneaky snake.

The laces fell down in a messy pile.

"Oh no," Jack said softly.

She tried again. This time she held the loop very tight. Her little fingers squeezed hard. But when she pulled, everything came undone.

Jack's bottom lip started to wobble. The dining room felt very quiet. Sunlight streamed through the big window, making patterns on the wooden table.

"I can't do it," she said to her stuffed elephant, Peanut.

Peanut sat on the chair next to her. His gray ears looked very wise.

Jack heard Mom's footsteps coming closer. Quick as a bunny, she shoved her feet into the shoes. The laces dragged on the floor like loose spaghetti.

"Ready for pancakes?" Mom asked, peeking around the corner.

Jack nodded and stood up carefully. She took one step forward. Then another step.

TRIP!

Down she went! Jack tumbled onto the soft dining room rug. Her shoe laces had gotten all tangled up.

"Ow," Jack said, though it didn't really hurt.

Mom rushed over. "Oh sweetie, what happened?"

"My shoes won't stay tied," Jack said. Her voice sounded very small. "The laces are too tricky."

Mom sat down on the rug next to Jack. "Shoe tying is hard work. Want to try together?"

Jack nodded. Mom's hands were warm and gentle.

"Let's use Peanut to help," Mom said. She picked up the stuffed elephant. "Pretend Peanut has laces too."

Mom took two ribbons from her pocket. She tied them around Peanut's middle. The ribbons were bright yellow like sunshine.

"First, we cross the laces," Mom said slowly. She moved Peanut's ribbons very carefully. "Like giving Peanut a hug."

Jack watched with big eyes. The ribbons crossed over each other.

"Now we make rabbit ears," Mom continued. She made two loops that stuck up. "See? Bunny ears!"

Jack giggled. They did look like ears!

"The ears give each other a hug too," Mom said. She wrapped one loop around the other. "Then they jump through the hole."

Mom pulled gently. A perfect bow appeared on Peanut's tummy.

"Now you try on your real shoe," Mom said.

Jack took a deep breath. The air smelled like pancakes and syrup from the kitchen. She crossed her laces just like Mom showed her.

"Hug," she whispered.

Then she made two loops. They looked a little wobbly, but they stayed up.

"Bunny ears," Jack said with a smile.

She wrapped one ear around the other. Her tongue poked out as she concentrated very hard.

"Jump through!" Jack said.

She pulled the loops tight. A bow appeared! It wasn't perfect, but it was hers.

"I did it!" Jack shouted. She jumped up and down. The bow stayed tied!

"You sure did," Mom said. "You kept trying even when it felt hard."

Jack tied her other shoe too. This one came out even better. She felt proud and happy inside, like warm sunshine in her chest.

At breakfast, Jack showed Dad her bows. "I learned with Peanut's help," she said.

"Those are the best shoe bows I've ever seen," Dad said.

Jack wiggled her toes inside her shoes. The bows stayed perfect all through breakfast. She had learned something new, and it felt wonderful.

From that day on, Jack tied her own shoes every morning. Sometimes the bows were crooked. Sometimes they were loose. But they were always hers.

The end.
