# Story story_700

**The Little Train Who Didn't Want a Bath**

In the dusty attic of an old house lived Thomas, a little red toy train. He had bright yellow wheels and a shiny black smokestack. Thomas loved to chug around the wooden tracks that zigzagged between old boxes and forgotten treasures.

One sunny morning, Thomas woke up feeling itchy. His red paint was covered in thick, gray dust. Cobwebs stuck to his wheels like sticky spaghetti. "Achoo!" he sneezed, sending dust clouds puffing into the air.

Mrs. Mouse peeked out from behind a hatbox. "Thomas, you need a bath!" she squeaked. "You're so dusty I can barely see your pretty red color!"

Thomas shook his little train body. "No, no, no!" he cried. "I don't like water! Trains don't take baths!" He remembered stories about trains getting rusty in the rain. The thought made his wheels wobble with worry.

But the dust made his eyes water. It tickled his nose and made him sneeze again and again. "Achoo! Achoo! ACHOO!" The dust was everywhere - on his windows, in his whistle, even between his train car connections.

Mrs. Mouse had an idea. "What if we clean you without water?" she suggested. She found an old feather duster in a nearby box. The feather was soft and fluffy, like a cloud.

"This might work!" Thomas said hopefully. He sat very still while Mrs. Mouse tickled the feather duster all over his train body. The feathers felt nice and gentle on his paint.

But when Mrs. Mouse finished, Thomas looked in an old mirror. He was still dusty! The feather duster had only moved the dust around. Some cobwebs still hung from his smokestack like party streamers.

"Oh no," Thomas sighed. His shoulders drooped sadly. "I'm still dirty."

Mrs. Mouse tapped her tiny paw on her chin. "Don't give up, Thomas! Let me think of another way." She scurried around the attic, looking behind boxes and under old quilts.

"I found something!" she called out. Mrs. Mouse returned with a small spray bottle filled with soapy water and a soft, yellow sponge. "This water has bubbles in it. See how they sparkle?"

Thomas watched the bubbles dance in the sunlight streaming through the attic window. They looked like tiny rainbows floating in the air. "Those bubbles are pretty," he admitted.

"The water will be gentle," Mrs. Mouse promised. "Just a little bit at a time."

Thomas took a deep breath. "Okay. I'll try." He was tired of being dusty and sneezy.

Mrs. Mouse sprayed just a tiny bit of soapy water on the soft sponge. She gently wiped Thomas's red sides. The water felt cool and refreshing, not scary at all. The soap smelled like fresh lemons.

"This feels nice!" Thomas said, surprised. The dust came off easily, revealing his bright red paint underneath. Mrs. Mouse carefully cleaned his yellow wheels, his black smokestack, and even his tiny windows.

When she finished, Thomas looked in the mirror again. "Wow! I'm shiny and bright!" His red paint gleamed like a fire truck. His yellow wheels sparkled like sunshine. His black smokestack stood tall and proud.

"Choo-choo!" Thomas whistled happily. He rolled around his tracks, feeling light and clean. No more sneezing! No more itchy dust!

"Thank you, Mrs. Mouse!" Thomas called out. "I learned something important today. Sometimes trying something new isn't scary. It can actually feel wonderful!"

Mrs. Mouse smiled. "And I learned that trying different solutions helps us solve problems. The feather duster didn't work, but the soapy water did!"

From that day on, Thomas wasn't afraid of getting clean. Whenever he felt dusty, he asked Mrs. Mouse for a gentle bubble bath. He discovered that being clean made him feel happy, healthy, and ready for new adventures around the attic tracks.

And Thomas never forgot the lesson: when one solution doesn't work, keep trying until you find one that does!

The end.
