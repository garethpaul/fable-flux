# Story story_1030

**Sam and the Toy Problem**

Sam loved bath time. The warm water felt good on his skin. Bubbles popped and tickled his nose. Best of all, he had many toys to play with.

Tonight, Sam looked at all his bath toys. There was a red boat that bobbed up and down. A yellow duck that squeaked when squeezed. Three small fish that could swim. A purple cup for pouring water. And his favorite green frog that could hop.

"Time to put the toys away," called Mom from the hallway. "Bath time is over."

Sam looked at all his toys floating around him. His eyes grew wide. How could he carry them all at once? He had only two small hands.

Sam tried to scoop up everything. He grabbed the boat in his right hand. He picked up the duck in his left hand. Then he tried to hold the fish too. But the fish slipped through his fingers. Splash! They fell back into the water.

"Oh no," Sam said. He felt frustrated. His bottom lip stuck out a little.

Sam tried again. This time he hugged all the toys against his chest. The wet toys felt slippery and cold. The duck squeaked as he squeezed it tight. But when he stood up, everything started sliding down. The cup fell first. Then the fish. Splash, splash, splash!

Sam sat back down in the warm water. He felt like crying. Why was this so hard? He was a big boy. Big boys should be able to carry their toys.

"What's wrong, sweetie?" Mom asked, peeking into the bathroom.

"I can't carry all my toys," Sam said sadly. "They keep falling."

Mom smiled warmly. "That is tricky. What if you tried a different way?"

Sam thought hard. He looked at the toys floating around him. Then he had an idea. His face lit up with excitement.

"I know!" Sam said. "I can make trips!"

First, Sam picked up just the boat and the duck. He could hold these easily in his two hands. He climbed out of the tub carefully. His feet made wet prints on the bath mat. He walked to the toy basket and gently placed the boat and duck inside.

"Good thinking!" Mom said proudly.

Sam hurried back to the tub. This time he picked up the three fish and the purple cup. The fish felt smooth and the cup was light. He carried them safely to the basket too.

Last, Sam went back for his favorite green frog. He held it gently in both hands. The frog's bumpy skin felt funny against his palms. Sam gave it a little kiss before putting it in the basket with the other toys.

"All done!" Sam announced happily. He felt proud and tall.

"You solved that problem all by yourself," Mom said, wrapping Sam in a soft, fluffy towel. "You didn't give up when it was hard."

Sam snuggled into the warm towel. It smelled like sunshine and flowers. "Making trips was easier than carrying everything," he said.

"Sometimes the best way is not the fastest way," Mom said, drying his hair gently. "You were very smart to try a different solution."

Sam looked at his toy basket. All his bath toys were safely inside, ready for tomorrow's bath. The red boat sat next to the yellow duck. The three fish were lined up in a row. The purple cup held the green frog, like they were friends.

"Can I have bubbles again tomorrow?" Sam asked.

"Of course," Mom laughed. "And now you know how to clean up like a big boy."

Sam felt happy and proud. He had solved his problem all by himself. Sometimes things seemed too hard at first. But if he thought about it and tried different ways, he could figure it out.

As Mom tucked him into bed, Sam smiled. Tomorrow he would play with his bath toys again. And he already knew the secret to cleaning up. Take your time. Make trips. Don't give up.

Bath time was the best time.

The end.
