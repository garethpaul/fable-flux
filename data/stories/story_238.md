# Jack and the Missing Blocks

Jack sat on the soft carpet in the living room. He rubbed his eyes and yawned. The morning sun streamed through the big window. It made golden squares on the floor.

"Time to build!" Jack said happily.

He looked around for his favorite blocks. The red ones. The blue ones. The yellow ones too. But where were they?

Jack scratched his head. "That's funny," he whispered.

Yesterday, his blocks were right here. Right by the couch. Now the spot was empty. Just carpet and dust bunnies.

"Mommy!" Jack called. "Where are my blocks?"

Mommy peeked in from the kitchen. "I don't know, sweetie. Where did you leave them?"

Jack's bottom lip started to wobble. "I can't remember," he said sadly.

The living room felt too quiet. No clicking sounds. No tower-building sounds. No happy block sounds at all.

Jack decided to look everywhere. He would find those blocks!

First, he checked under the couch. He got on his hands and knees. He peered into the dark space. Dust tickled his nose.

"Achoo!" he sneezed.

No blocks under there. Just a lost sock.

Next, Jack looked behind the TV stand. He squeezed into the narrow space. It was dark and dusty too.

Still no blocks.

Jack's shoulders drooped a little. But he didn't give up. Not yet.

He opened the toy chest. Clink, clank, rattle! Toys tumbled out. Cars and puzzles. Books and balls. But no blocks anywhere.

"Hmm," Jack said, tapping his chin.

Maybe the blocks were upstairs? Jack climbed the steps slowly. Each step creaked under his feet. He looked in his bedroom. Under the bed. In the closet. Behind the rocking chair.

No blocks there either.

Jack sat on his bed. His eyes felt watery. Building blocks was his favorite thing. How could he build without them?

Then Jack remembered something. Yesterday, Daddy was cleaning. Moving things around. Putting toys away.

"Maybe Daddy moved them!" Jack said.

He ran downstairs quickly. His feet pounded on each step. Thump, thump, thump!

"Daddy!" Jack called. "Did you move my blocks?"

Daddy looked up from his newspaper. "Blocks? Let me think." He rubbed his beard. "Oh yes! I put toys in the basement. To make room for cleaning."

Jack's face lit up like sunshine. "The basement!"

Down, down, down they went. The basement stairs were wooden. They creaked and groaned. It smelled like laundry soap down there.

Daddy pointed to a corner. Three big boxes sat there. Cardboard boxes with tape on top.

"Your toys are in those boxes," Daddy said.

Jack's heart jumped with joy. He ran to the first box. Daddy helped him open it. Inside were stuffed animals and toy cars.

The second box had puzzles and books. Still no blocks.

Jack's hands shook a little. What if the blocks weren't here? What if they were lost forever?

The third box was heavier. Daddy cut the tape carefully. Jack held his breath.

Inside the box, colors sparkled. Red blocks! Blue blocks! Yellow blocks! Green ones too!

"My blocks!" Jack shouted happily.

He reached in and grabbed a handful. They felt smooth and cool. Perfect for building. Perfect for playing.

"Thank you, Daddy!" Jack hugged his father tight.

"You worked hard to find them," Daddy said. "You didn't give up."

Jack carried his blocks upstairs. One box at a time. His arms were strong. His heart was happy.

Back in the living room, Jack dumped the blocks out. They scattered across the carpet. Click, clack, rattle! Such wonderful sounds.

Jack started building right away. A tall tower. A long bridge. A castle with walls.

"I found them!" he told Mommy proudly.

"You sure did," Mommy smiled. "You kept looking until you found them."

Jack built all morning long. The sun moved across the floor. His towers grew taller and taller.

Sometimes things get lost. Sometimes you can't find them right away. But if you keep trying, if you don't give up, you can solve the problem.

Jack learned something important that day. When something seems impossible, just keep looking. Keep trying. Keep believing.

And soon, you'll find exactly what you need.

The blocks clicked happily as Jack built his best castle ever.

The end.
