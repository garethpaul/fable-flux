# Story story_1300

Emma loved the big red swing at the playground. It was her favorite thing in the whole world. The swing was shiny and smooth. It made her feel like she was flying.

Every day after lunch, Emma ran to the swing. She would pump her legs hard. Up and down, up and down. The wind tickled her face. Her hair danced in the breeze. Emma smiled so big her cheeks hurt.

One sunny morning, Emma raced to her swing. But someone else was already there. A little boy with curly hair was swinging back and forth. Emma's smile turned into a frown.

"That's my swing!" Emma said loudly. She crossed her arms tight.

The boy looked at her with big brown eyes. "I was here first," he said softly.

Emma felt angry bubbles in her tummy. She wanted her swing right now. "You have to get off!" she shouted. "It's mine!"

The boy kept swinging. Other children stopped playing. They looked at Emma. Emma's face turned red like a tomato.

Mrs. Garcia, the playground helper, walked over. "Emma, what's wrong?" she asked gently.

"He took my swing!" Emma pointed at the boy. "Make him get off!"

Mrs. Garcia knelt down. "Emma, this swing belongs to everyone. We need to share it."

"I don't want to share!" Emma stomped her foot. The ground felt hard under her shoe.

Mrs. Garcia nodded. "Sharing is hard sometimes. But it makes playing more fun."

Emma didn't believe that. She sat on a bench. She watched the boy swing. He looked happy. Emma felt sad and mad at the same time.

After a while, Emma had an idea. She marched over to the boy. "You can swing for five more minutes," she announced. "Then it's my turn forever!"

The boy shook his head. "That's not fair," he said.

Emma's first plan didn't work. She felt frustrated. Her hands made tight fists.

Then Emma noticed something. The boy was swinging all alone. He looked a little lonely. Emma remembered how much more fun games were with friends.

Emma took a deep breath. The air smelled like flowers and grass. She walked back to the swing.

"Hi," she said quietly. "I'm Emma. What's your name?"

"I'm Sam," the boy said. He slowed down his swinging.

"Do you want to play a game?" Emma asked. Her voice was nicer now.

Sam's eyes lit up. "What kind of game?"

"We can take turns," Emma said. "You swing for ten pushes. Then I swing for ten pushes. We can count together!"

Sam smiled. "That sounds fun!"

Emma helped Sam count. "One, two, three!" Their voices mixed together like a song.

When Sam reached ten, he jumped off. "Your turn, Emma!"

Emma climbed onto the warm swing seat. Sam pushed her gently. "One, two, three!" they counted again.

Emma felt the wind in her hair. But something was different. The swing felt even more special now. Sharing made it better, not worse.

When Emma's turn ended, she didn't want to stop. But she remembered how good it felt when Sam was kind to her.

"Your turn again, Sam!" Emma said with a big smile.

They played together all afternoon. They took turns on the slide too. They shared the sandbox and the monkey bars.

When it was time to go home, Sam waved. "Thanks for sharing, Emma. You're a good friend!"

Emma's heart felt warm and fuzzy. "Thanks for playing with me!"

As Emma walked home, she thought about her day. Sharing wasn't scary after all. It was actually more fun than playing alone.

The next day, Emma couldn't wait to go back to the playground. She hoped Sam would be there. She had learned something important. The best things in life were even better when you shared them with friends.

Emma skipped all the way home, already planning tomorrow's games. Her favorite swing would always be special. But now she knew the real magic happened when she shared it with others.

The end.
