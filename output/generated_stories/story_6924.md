# Finny Finds Feelings in the Attic

Finny was a playful dolphin with bright eyes and a big, friendly smile. One very calm afternoon, Finny swam up a spiral sea-stair and popped into a quiet attic. How could a dolphin be in an attic? In stories, anything can happen!

The attic was cool and still. Dust danced in thin sunbeams. Old boxes made tiny cardboard mountains. A wooden floor whispered creak, creak under Finny’s smooth tail. Finny took a deep breath. The room smelled like old paper and a little like lemon polish.

“Oh! Look at that,” Finny said, pointing with a flipper. “A red notebook! And shiny blue crayons!”

Finny loved to write little stories and draw wiggly waves. Writing felt like playing with words. Drawing felt like swimming through color. Finny flipped open the notebook. The pages were blank and bright.

“I will write a story today,” Finny planned. “I will make a list first.” Finny wiggled a flipper and whispered, “Good planning helps a story grow.”

Finny wrote the plan slowly:
1. Pick a kind idea.
2. Draw a picture.
3. Write little sentences.
4. Check for honesty.
5. Read it out loud.

As Finny wrote, a small cardboard box near the window shook. Tap, tap. Finny looked up. “Hello? Is someone there?”

Out peeked a tiny gray mouse with soft whiskers and big, worried eyes. “Squeak. My name is Pip,” said the mouse. “This attic is quiet, but today my heart is noisy.” Pip’s whiskers drooped.

Finny turned gently and made room with a fin. “Hi, Pip. I am Finny. Your heart feels noisy? Tell me more.”

Pip blinked. “I lost my favorite pencil. It is yellow with a little star. I need it to practice my letters. Without it, I feel sad and small.”

Finny listened with both ears and both eyes. Finny did not interrupt. Finny nodded softly. “I hear you, Pip. You love your pencil. You feel sad without it.”

Pip sighed with relief. “Yes. Thank you for understanding.”

Finny looked at the red notebook and then back to Pip. Finny’s own flippers tingled with a wish to write. But Finny’s heart said, Help first. That is empathy. 

“Let’s make a plan together,” Finny said. “Step one: take calm breaths.” They breathed in and out slowly. The attic felt even more quiet.

“Step two,” Finny said, tapping the notebook, “we will look in careful places. We will look with our eyes and also with our feelings.”

They searched under a low wooden chair—creak. They checked a basket with soft yarn—rustle. They peeked into a tall hat—plop! No pencil.

Pip’s eyes grew wet. “Maybe it is gone.”

Finny placed a gentle flipper near Pip while keeping a respectful space. “It is okay to be sad,” Finny said softly. “We will keep trying. We are a team.”

They listened. The attic hummed. The clock went tick, tock. Then Finny heard a tiny roll, roll sound. “What is that?”

They followed the sound to a little trunk by the window. Finny lifted the lid with care. Inside were papers, a silver bell, and—roll, roll—a yellow pencil with a star!

“Pip! Is this yours?” Finny asked.

Pip’s whiskers sprang up. “Yes! That is my pencil!”

Finny felt warm and glad. “We did it.” Finny smiled but then paused and looked at the red notebook. “Pip, I want to tell the truth. I wanted to write my own story first. I felt excited. But when I saw you were sad, my heart said to help. I am glad I listened.”

Pip nodded. “That is kind. Thank you.” Pip hugged the pencil carefully. “Would you like to use it for your story? We can share.”

Finny thought for a moment. “Sharing sounds fair. We can both write.” That was integrity—doing the right thing and being honest.

Finny turned a page. “I want to write a story about feelings and plans.” Pip sat close, tail curled around the pencil. The sun made a warm stripe on the floor. Dust sparkles floated like tiny stars.

Finny said, “Let’s use simple words.”

They began.

Title: We Help Each Other.

Finny wrote: “Pip felt sad. I listened. We made a plan. We looked. We found the pencil. We felt better. The End.”

Pip added a picture of a mouse and a dolphin smiling. Pip drew the yellow pencil with a bright star. Finny shaded a soft window and tiny dust sparkles.

“Let’s check for honesty,” Finny said, reading the plan again. They read the story aloud, slow and clear. The words felt true.

Pip clapped. “I like it. It feels kind.”

Finny smiled wider. “Me too. When we understand feelings, we help. When we plan, we solve problems. When we tell the truth, we shine.”

The attic seemed even cozier now. The quiet felt friendly. The wooden floor gave one last creak, as if it agreed.

Pip tucked the pencil behind one ear. “I will practice my letters every day. I will also take care of my things.”

Finny closed the red notebook and patted it. “I will keep listening with my heart. I will plan my steps. I will use my words to be kind.”

They waved goodbye, not too loud for the quiet attic. Finny slid back down the spiral sea-stair, feeling calm and proud.

That day, Finny learned that empathy is listening and caring. Planning is making small steps. Integrity is telling the truth and doing what is right. And writing can carry kind feelings from one heart to another.

The End.
