# Joey Roo Counts to Calm

Joey Roo was a joyful kangaroo joey with soft, springy feet and a bright, bouncy tail. One sunny morning, he hopped into the garage workshop with a happy wiggle. The workshop smelled like pine wood and lemon soap. Sunlight made tiny dust sparkles dance in the air. Tools rested on hooks. Boxes sat in neat rows. The floor felt cool under Joey’s toes.

“Wow,” Joey whispered. “So many fun things!” His ears twitched with curiosity.

Mama Roo followed, carrying a small toolbox. “This is our garage workshop, Joey,” she said in a gentle voice. “We build and fix things here. We use tools with care and respect.”

Joey nodded. “I can look?”

“You can look with your eyes and hands kept safe,” said Mama. “Remember our calm plan: stop, breathe, count, and ask.”

Joey smiled. “Stop, breathe, count to four, and ask!”

He hopped to a wooden box of colorful screws. They glittered like tiny candies. He wanted to touch them all. His paws tingled. His tail wiggled fast. He reached out—

“Stop,” Joey whispered to himself. He put his paws on his belly. He took a slow breath in. “Shhh.” He counted softly, “One, two, three, four.” Then he asked, “Mama, may I see the screws?”

Mama Roo smiled. “Yes. Let’s put them in a little tray.” Joey poured them gently. Tink, tink, tink! The sounds were like little bells. He felt proud. “I used my calm plan,” he said.

Next, Joey found a shiny tape measure. It made a snappy sound when it pulled back. Zip! His eyes grew wide. He wanted to pull it fast, fast, fast.

His tail swished. His paws buzzed. Joey felt zippy inside, like a jumping bean. He remembered the plan. “Stop.” He placed the tape measure down. In, out. “One, two, three, four.” He asked, “Mama, can we try it together?”

“Yes,” Mama said. She put her paw over Joey’s. “Slow and safe.” They pulled it out. Zip… stop. They measured a plank. “It is four tail-lengths!” Joey giggled.

Mama nodded. “You are using self-regulation. That means you are the boss of your body and your feelings.”

“I am the boss of my body,” Joey echoed, standing tall.

On the workbench lay a small wooden box with wheels, a toy car waiting for paint. Joey clapped. “Can I paint?” Paint pots sat nearby—blue like sky, green like grass, yellow like sunshine. Brushes lay soft and fuzzy.

Mama Roo said, “Yes, after we make a calm plan. We paint slow. We keep our space clean. We take calm breaths when we feel wiggly.”

Joey hopped in place. His wiggles felt big now, like bubbles about to pop. He wanted to splash all the colors at once. His paws reached for three brushes at the same time. The brushes wobbled. A paint pot tipped. Plop! A yellow dot spilled on the table. Joey’s eyes grew round. “Oh no.”

He felt hot in his cheeks. His tail thumped the floor. Thump, thump. He wanted to rush and grab everything, but Mama’s words floated in his mind. Stop, breathe, count, and ask.

Joey put both paws on his belly. He closed his eyes for a little blink. He took a slow breath. In through the nose, out through the mouth. “One, two, three, four,” he counted. His tail slowed. His cheeks cooled. He opened his eyes and said, “Mama, I spilled. I need help.”

Mama handed him a soft cloth. “Thank you for telling me and using your calm plan. Let’s wipe together.”

They wiped the dot. Swish, swish. The lemon soap smell came back. The table felt smooth and clean.

Joey smiled. “I can paint slow now.”

He dipped one brush in blue. Drip, drip. He tapped it on the edge. Tap, tap. He painted a stripe. Then he washed the brush in a cup of water. Swirl, swirl. The water turned swishy blue. He took a breath—“One, two, three, four”—and chose green next. He painted little dots like leaves. Then yellow swirls like sunshine.

“You are calm and creative,” Mama said. “You are making outdoor colors.”

Joey beamed. “It looks like the sky and grass outside!” He bounced, but just a tiny bounce.

When the car was painted, Mama said, “Let’s take it to the backyard to dry.” They carried it out. Warm air kissed Joey’s nose. Birds chirped, “Cheep, cheep!” The grass tickled his feet.

Joey placed the car on a sunny spot. He waited patiently, taking slow breaths when he felt eager. “One, two, three, four,” he hummed.

When the paint dried, Joey pushed the car down the porch ramp. Vroom! The wheels spun. The car zipped across the patio and into the yard. Joey laughed. “Outdoor racing!”

Mama clapped. “You respected the tools. You used self-regulation. You made something beautiful and fun.”

Joey hugged Mama. “When I feel wiggly, I can stop, breathe, count, and ask. I can be the boss of my body.”

Mama nodded. “That is our calm plan.”

Joey drove the car around a little stick track he made in the grass. He added pinecones for tunnels and leaves for flags. He used his big ideas and gentle paws. He was curious, careful, and kind.

As the sun began to dip, Joey whispered to his car, “Slow and steady,” and to himself, “I can do hard things with calm.”

He felt proud. He felt peaceful. He felt joyful, like a small hop in his heart.

The End.
