# Little Fawn on the Rooftop

On a sunny morning, a gentle deer fawn named Dapple blinked awake. The air felt cool on her nose. She could smell warm bread drifting up, and hear soft city sounds—birds chirping, a bus humming far below, and a kitty meowing. Dapple wiggled her ears. She was on a rooftop garden!

Green plants tickled her legs. Bright flowers nodded in the breeze. A tiny fountain splashed and sparkled. Dapple had followed the smell of leaves and water up the stairs, step by careful step. Now she stood near the edge and peered over. The street looked very far away.

“Oh,” whispered Dapple, “this roof is high.”

A pigeon landed nearby and bobbed his head. “Coo-coo! Hello, little fawn. I’m Pip. You look worried.”

“I climbed up,” Dapple said in a small voice, “but I don’t know how to go down.”

Pip puffed his feathers kindly. “You came up. You can go down.”

Dapple’s heart thumped. “I’m not sure.”

Pip tilted his head. “Let’s think. You have your legs. You have your eyes. You have your breath. You can use them.”

Dapple took a slow breath. In. Out. The air tasted like mint from the roof garden. Her nose felt cool. Her hooves felt warm on the sunlit tiles. “I can use me,” she said.

Pip smiled. “That’s self-reliance. It means you try with your own body and your own brain.”

Dapple looked at the stair door. It seemed big and dark. Her tummy fluttered. A soft meow came from a flower pot. A gray cat popped out, stretching. “Hello,” purred the cat. “I’m Miso. You can do it, dear deer.”

Dapple giggled. “Hi, Miso.” She peeked at the door again. “I feel wobbly.”

Miso swished her tail. “When I feel wobbly, I take tiny steps. I tell myself: ‘I can try. I can learn.’”

“I can try. I can learn,” Dapple repeated. The words felt warm, like sunshine on her back.

She went to the door. The metal felt cool when her nose touched it. The door swung open with a soft squeak. Inside, the stairwell smelled like dust and soap. It was darker than the bright rooftop, but a small window let in a stripe of light.

Dapple put one hoof on the top step. Tap. She listened. The stair was strong. She put another hoof. Tap-tap. Her ears flicked. Her heart beat slower now. “I am careful,” she said. “I am brave.”

Pip hopped to the railing. “I will fly down beside you.”

Miso trotted to the top step and sat. “I will wait here. When you come back up, we can play.”

Dapple took another step. Tap-tap-tap. The sound echoed. She looked down at the next step. She looked at the stripe of light. She took a breath when she felt unsure. In. Out. “I can do this,” she whispered.

Halfway down, she heard a new sound. “Whirr, whirr,” went a fan in the wall. The breeze from the fan ruffled her fur. The sound surprised Dapple, and she froze.

Pip cooed softly. “What do you know, Dapple?”

“I know this is a fan,” Dapple said. “It makes wind. It cannot hurt me. I can keep going.”

She placed her hoof again. Tap. She felt proud. The stairs were long, but she was moving, one tiny step at a time.

Down another turn, Dapple saw a big window with a view. The sky was bright blue. She could see a fluffy cloud shaped like a cookie. She smiled. “Hello, cookie cloud.”

At the bottom, the last step felt solid and kind. Thump. Dapple stood on the cool floor, and her legs relaxed. She did it! She did it herself.

A man in a green hat opened a side door. He held a watering can and smelled like fresh soil. “Oh! Hello, little fawn,” he said, surprised but gentle. “Did you come from the roof?”

Dapple nodded. “Yes. I walked down by myself.”

The man smiled. “That is brave and smart. Here is a sip of water.” He set a clean bowl down and stepped back. Dapple bent and drank. The water felt cold and sweet. She raised her head and licked a drop from her nose.

“Thank you,” Dapple said. “I will go back up to my friends now. I can do that, too.”

She turned and climbed the stairs, one careful step at a time. Tap-tap-tap. Her body remembered. Her breath was steady. She looked, she listened, she moved. When the door came, she pushed it with her nose. Squeak. Bright sunshine poured over her like a warm blanket.

“Dapple!” cheered Pip, flapping.

“You did it!” purred Miso, rubbing against Dapple’s leg.

Dapple’s eyes shone. “I used my legs, my eyes, and my breath. I asked myself good thoughts. I was kind to me. I can try. I can learn.”

Pip nodded. “That is self-reliance.”

Miso added, “And you showed kindness—to yourself and to us.”

Dapple nuzzled them both. The fountain splashed. The flowers waved. Far below, the bus hummed on. Dapple felt tall inside, even though she was small.

She looked at the stairs again and smiled. “When I face a big thing, I can take tiny steps. I can breathe. I can think. I can do it.”

Pip cooed, “And when you need help, you can ask kindly. Helpers can cheer, but your strong steps are yours.”

Dapple laughed, a light, happy sound. “Yes. My steps are mine.”

The sun warmed her back. The minty air filled her nose. She closed her eyes for a moment, feeling calm and proud. Then she opened them and trotted to play among the flowers with her friends, ready for new little tries, one step at a time.

The End.
