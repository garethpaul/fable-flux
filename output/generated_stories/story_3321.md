# Soft Steps in the Dining Room

In a cozy dining room, a gentle deer fawn named Soft Steps stood on the rug. The room smelled like warm oats and sweet apples. Sunlight slid across the wooden floor and made the spoons sparkle.

Soft Steps’ family had set the table. There were tiny plates, shiny forks, and a blue cup just for the fawn. Mama Deer placed a bowl of oatmeal in front of Soft Steps and said, “Breakfast is ready, my little one.”

Soft Steps smiled. “I want to pour the milk all by myself,” the fawn said. The milk jug was cool and heavy. Soft Steps wrapped small hooves around the handle and tried to lift.

But the jug slipped. Milk splashed. It went plop, sploosh, across the table and onto the rug. The oatmeal looked like a tiny boat in a white sea.

Soft Steps felt the hot spark of anger in a small chest. Ears drooped. Little hooves stomped—thump, thump. “No! I can’t do it! It’s all wrong!” the fawn cried. A tight feeling squeezed the tummy. Tears filled the big brown eyes.

Mama Deer took a slow breath. She kept her voice soft. “I see you feel upset,” she said. “Spills happen. Let’s pause.”

Soft Steps sniffled. “But I wanted to do it right.”

“We can try again,” Mama said. “First, we help our body calm. Put a hoof on your belly. Feel it move. Smell the cocoa,” she whispered, pretending to hold a warm cup. “Blow the cocoa to cool it.”

Soft Steps copied Mama. Inhale, slow and deep—smell the cocoa. Exhale, soft and steady—blow the cocoa. The air felt warm on the nose. The chest rose like a small balloon and fell like a leaf. One breath. Two breaths. Three. The tight feeling loosened.

The fawn’s body felt softer. “I feel a little better,” Soft Steps said.

Mama Deer smiled. “That’s self-regulation. You noticed big feelings and helped them get small.”

Papa Deer brought a towel. “Let’s clean together,” he said. The towel felt squishy and warm. Swish, swish, swish. They wiped the table. Pat, pat, pat. They dabbed the rug. Soft Steps listened to the gentle sounds and to the clock saying tick-tock, tick-tock. The dining room felt calm again.

Soft Steps looked at the blue cup and the milk jug. “I want to try again,” the fawn said, a little whisper, a little brave.

Mama nodded. “That’s resilience,” she said. “We keep trying, even after a spill.”

Papa set a small pitcher on the table. “This one is lighter,” he said. “We can practice.”

Soft Steps wrapped hooves around the little handle. “Smell the cocoa,” the fawn whispered to self. Inhale. “Blow the cocoa.” Exhale. The fawn’s shoulders relaxed. “Pour slow like a tiny waterfall,” Mama coached.

Soft Steps tilted the pitcher. Trick-le, trick-le. The milk made soft music as it slid into the bowl. Not a drop fell on the table. The oatmeal boat found its sweet milk sea—just right.

“I did it!” Soft Steps giggled. The fawn’s tail flicked with joy.

“You tried again,” Mama said. “That’s brave.”

Just then, big sister Fawn, Snip, pranced in. She didn’t see the damp spot on the rug and stepped on the edge of Soft Steps’ napkin. Whoosh! The blue cup wobbled, wiggled, and tipped. Milk drops hopped like little frogs. A cold splash touched Soft Steps’ leg.

Snip’s eyes went wide. “Oh no! I made a mess! I’m sorry!”

Soft Steps felt a little spark, like before. The body got tight again. The fawn remembered: hoof on belly. Smell the cocoa. Blow the cocoa. One breath. Two breaths. The spark cooled into a tiny glow.

“It’s okay,” Soft Steps said softly. “Spills happen. I forgive you.” The fawn handed Snip a towel. “We can clean together.”

Snip smiled with relief. “Thank you.” Swish, swish. Pat, pat. The milk frogs disappeared.

Papa Deer clapped gently. “I saw you use your calm breaths,” he said.

Mama Deer nodded. “You showed anger-management and kindness.”

Soft Steps stood taller. The dining room smelled sweet again. The spoons still sparkled. The blue cup stood steady, filled just enough.

They all sat down. The oatmeal was warm and creamy. Soft Steps took a bite. “Mmm,” the fawn hummed, tasting soft oats and cool milk. Little cinnamon specks tickled the nose.

Snip said, “You taught me the cocoa breath. Can we do it together?”

“Yes,” Soft Steps said. “Smell the cocoa.” They inhaled slowly. “Blow the cocoa.” They exhaled gently. The whole room felt slow and happy.

Mama Deer said, “When tricky things happen, we can pause, breathe, and try again.”

Soft Steps licked a sweet drop from a whisker. “That’s resilience,” the fawn said. “I can learn. I can try again. I can be calm.”

After breakfast, Soft Steps carefully poured a tiny splash of milk into the blue cup, just to practice. Trick-le, trick-le. No spills.

The fawn’s heart felt warm. Mistakes were not the end. They were steps. Soft Steps smiled at the shiny spoons, the cozy rug, and the family around the table.

“I can do hard things,” the gentle deer fawn said. “One breath, one try, and then another.”

The End.
