# Joey Roo and the Gentle Fix

Joey Roo was a joyful kangaroo joey with soft brown fur and big springy feet. One sunny morning, he hopped into Grandpa Gus’s garage workshop. The garage smelled like wood shavings and lemon soap. Sunlight made little dust sparkles dance in the air. Tools hung on the wall like shiny toys—hammers, wrenches, and a bright red toolbox that clicked when it closed.

Joey Roo’s best friend, Tilly Turtle, was there too. Tilly wore a tiny yellow scarf and moved slowly but smiled fast. “Hi, Joey!” Tilly said in her small, sweet voice. “I brought my tricycle. The bell goes clunk instead of ding.”

Grandpa Gus winked. “You two can fix it right here in the workshop. I’ll be in the garden if you need me.”

Joey Roo felt excited. He loved to help. He loved to hop. He loved to try. “We can do it!” he said. His tail made a happy thump on the floor.

Tilly rolled the little red tricycle onto a mat. Joey Roo touched the bell. It felt cool and smooth. He turned it. The bell went clunk again. “Hmm,” Joey said. “It’s a little stuck.”

Joey Roo grabbed a small screwdriver. It felt light in his paw. He tried to open the bell, but the screw didn’t move. Joey frowned. He tried again, harder. The screwdriver slipped. “Oh!” he squeaked. The tool made a tiny screech on the metal. His heart beat faster. His ears felt hot.

“I want it fixed now,” Joey said. He hopped in a quick circle. His breaths came fast. His tummy felt twisty.

Tilly watched him with kind turtle eyes. “Joey,” she said softly, “let’s be calm. We can take a slow breath.”

Joey Roo blinked. He remembered something Grandpa Gus always said: “Calm hands fix better.” Joey placed the screwdriver down. He put his paws on his belly. The workshop smelled like pine. The floor felt cool under his feet.

“Let’s try the calm way,” Tilly whispered.

Joey nodded. “Breathe in…” he said. He took a slow breath. His chest lifted. “Breathe out…” He let the air go. He felt a little lighter. The dust sparkles still danced. The clock ticked tick-tock, tick-tock.

“Again?” Tilly asked.

“Again,” Joey said. They breathed together. In… and out… Their shoulders relaxed. Joey’s ears cooled down. His tummy felt soft now.

“Okay,” Joey said in a gentle voice. “Now we try again. Calm hands.”

He looked around the workshop. He saw a tiny drop of oil on a shelf. He saw a small cloth. He had an idea. “Let’s use a little oil,” he said. “Just a tiny drop.”

“That’s smart,” Tilly said. “Good idea.”

Joey put one small drop on the bell screw. The oil smelled a little like nuts. He waited a moment. He counted with Tilly, “One… two… three…” Then he picked up the screwdriver again. His paws were steady. He turned it slowly. Turn… turn… The screw came loose with a soft click.

“We did it!” Tilly cheered.

Inside the bell, a little spring was bent. Joey touched it gently. “Oh, you poor spring,” he said. “You’re tired and twisty. We can help.” He used the small pliers to bend it back a tiny bit. The pliers felt cool and firm.

“Let’s test it,” Tilly whispered.

Joey Roo put the bell back together. He turned it carefully. Ding! The sound was bright and happy. It rang like a little silver star.

“Ding!” Joey laughed. “We fixed it!”

Tilly clapped her turtle hands. “Thank you, Joey. You stayed calm. You had a good idea. You helped me.”

Grandpa Gus peeked in with muddy gloves. “I heard a ding!” he chuckled. “What happened here?”

Joey stood tall. “We took slow breaths. We used calm hands. We put one tiny drop of oil. Then we fixed the spring.”

Grandpa Gus smiled. “That is fine work. Calm minds and kind friends make strong helpers.”

Tilly rang the bell again. Ding! Ding! It echoed around the garage. The sound made Joey think of sunshine. He felt proud, but not jumpy. He felt peaceful.

“Joey,” Tilly said, “when things feel hard, we can breathe together.”

Joey nodded. “Yes. When I feel twisty inside, I stop. I breathe in… and out… Then I look. Then I try a small idea.”

Grandpa Gus handed them each a slice of apple. The apples smelled sweet and tasted crisp. They crunched happily. The garage felt warm and safe.

Joey Roo put the tools back where they belonged. He closed the red toolbox. Click. He patted Tilly’s tricycle. “All set,” he said.

Tilly gave him a gentle hug. “You’re a good friend.”

Joey hugged back. “You too.”

As they rolled the tricycle outside, the bell gave one more bright ding. Joey looked at the sky. The clouds were soft and slow, like Tilly’s steps. He took one last calm breath and smiled.

That day, Joey learned a big lesson in a small garage: when you feel wiggly or worried, stop, breathe, and try a little idea. Calmness helps you see. Friendship helps you try. Initiative means starting with a small, smart step.

They rode and rang and laughed all afternoon, peaceful and proud.

The End.
