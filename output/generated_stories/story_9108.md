# Ollie Otter’s Road-Trip Story Game

Ollie the playful otter buckled into his car seat with a wiggle and a giggle. The family car smelled like warm vanilla from a tiny air freshener, and the seats felt soft and squishy. Outside the windows, trees whooshed by like green paint strokes.

Mama slid into the driver’s seat. Papa clicked his seat belt. “Ready for the big day, Ollie?” Papa asked.

Ollie nodded, whiskers twitching. “Swim class day! I want to splash and flip! I want to win the noodle race!” His paws clapped, thump-thump on his lap.

The car hummed and purred down the road. But soon, red lights flashed ahead. The car slowed. It stopped. Cars waited in a long, shiny line.

Ollie pressed his nose to the window. “Why are we not moving? I want to go fast like a rocket-fish.”

“It’s traffic,” Mama said gently. “We’ll get there. Let’s take some deep breaths.”

Ollie scrunched his face. His tummy felt tight like a knotted rope. “I don’t like waiting.” His tail flicked against the seat. “I want to race now!”

Papa looked back and smiled. “When I feel grumpy, I tell a story to my grumps. Want to try our Story Game?”

Ollie sniffed. “A story? But stories are for quiet time.”

“Stories can also help us move feelings,” Mama said. “We take turns. We use our senses and gentle words. We make our own happy plan.”

Ollie’s ears perked up. “Okay. I will try.”

Papa began. “Once upon a car ride, there was a brave otter named—”

“Ollie!” Ollie squeaked.

“—named Ollie,” Papa continued, “who loved a sport very much.”

“Swimming!” Ollie said, paws paddling in the air. He felt a little lighter already.

Mama added, “But the road made him wait. His heart went boom-boom too fast.”

Ollie took a deep breath in through his nose. He smelled warm pretzels in a paper bag. His tummy rumbled. “My story-otter smells pretzels,” he said. “He is hungry and mad and squirmy.”

Papa nodded. “What does brave Ollie do with big mad?”

Ollie looked at his paws. They were small and strong. “He squeezes his paws. One, two, three.” He squeezed. “He blows out slow, like bubbles.” He puffed. “Pop, pop.”

“Good plan,” Mama said. “What does he tell himself?”

Ollie thought. The car’s air felt cool on his whiskers. He whispered, “Not yet is okay. I can try later.”

Papa smiled. “That’s a growth mindset.” He said it slow: “Grow-th mind-set.”

Ollie tried the big words. They felt bouncy and fun. “Grow-th mind-set,” he sang.

Mama grinned. “Now let’s add sounds. What does the road sound like?”

Ollie listened. The blinker went tick-tock, tick-tock. A bird chirped on a light post. A faraway dog barked, arf-arf. He giggled. “In our story, the road goes tick-tock, and the bird says, ‘You can wait, little rock!’”

Papa laughed. “Nice rhyme!”

Ollie wiggled. “And story-Ollie makes a plan for sports. He will practice kicks in his seat.” He kicked gently, toes pointing. “Point and splash,” he said softly. “Point and splash.”

Mama joined the story. “He also uses his eyes to notice. He sees a cloud shaped like a big sea shell.”

Ollie pressed his face to the glass again. The sky was bright and cottony. “I see one like a noodle!” he said. “And a whale with a hat.”

“Add it to our story,” Papa said. “What does the whale say?”

Ollie made his voice deep. “The whale says, ‘Keep going, small swimmer. Slow water still moves.’”

Ollie’s tight tummy felt looser. His tail stopped flicking. He reached for the pretzel bag. “In the story, brave Ollie eats one pretzel,” he said. “Crunch.” He took a bite. “Crunch.”

“The road is moving a little,” Mama said. The car rolled forward. “You’re doing great.”

Ollie blinked. “Can I make the ending?”

“Please do,” Papa said.

Ollie cleared his throat. “Once upon a car ride, brave Ollie learned to tell a story when he felt mad. He used his senses—sniff, see, hear—and he used his words—‘Not yet is okay.’ He practiced tiny kicks. He made a plan. When the road opened, he kept his calm. Then he went to swim class. He did the noodle race. He didn’t win first, but he kept trying. He felt proud. The end.”

Mama clapped softly on the steering wheel. “What a strong story.”

Papa nodded. “You turned frustration into a friend.”

The cars sped up. The windows showed the big blue pool building. Ollie’s heart thumped, but this time it felt like a happy drum.

After class, wet and shiny, Ollie buckled in again. He smelled pool water and warm towels. “I didn’t win,” he said, water drops tickling his fur. “But I kicked better. Coach said, ‘Great try!’”

Mama smiled in the mirror. “How do you feel?”

Ollie grinned. “Proud. And I have a new story.”

Papa raised an eyebrow. “What is it called?”

Ollie puffed his chest. “The Otter Who Kept Going.” He tapped his head. “I can tell it anytime. In the car. At the pool. When I wait.”

Mama turned on the blinker. Tick-tock. “Stories help us grow.”

Ollie hugged his towel. “And I can try again tomorrow.”

The End.
