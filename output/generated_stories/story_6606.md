# Ollie Owl’s Little Steps

In a bright, playful living room, sunbeams danced on a soft, bouncy rug. Pillows were piled like tiny mountains. Crayons rested in a cheerful cup. The air smelled like warm toast and oranges. It was a cozy, happy place.

A wise old owl named Ollie perched on the back of the couch. His feathers were soft and speckled like tiny snowflakes. His big gold eyes twinkled kindly. “Hoo-hoo,” said Ollie. “Good morning!”

Mia, a small child with curly hair, looked up. She held a toy drum. “Hi, Ollie! I want to do many things today,” she said. “I want to drum, and dance, and clean up, and drink water, and read a book. But it feels like too much.” Mia’s lip wobbled. She felt a little swirl in her tummy.

Ollie blinked slowly. “When we want to do many things, we can make a goal,” he said in a gentle voice. “A goal is a plan for one good thing we will do.”

Mia hugged a pillow. “Just one?”

“One at a time,” said Ollie. “Little steps make big dreams.”

“How do I make a goal?” Mia asked.

Ollie hopped closer and spread his wings like a blanket. “We choose one thing. We say it out loud. We make a small, clear plan. We try. Then we cheer!”

Mia smiled a tiny smile. “Okay. My goal is to drink more water.”

“Wonderful,” Ollie hooted. “Water helps our bodies feel good. Let us make a small plan.”

They looked around the room. The sun made the water bottle on the table sparkle. The bottle was blue, with stars. “I will drink one full bottle by snack time,” Mia said.

Ollie nodded. “That is clear and kind. Now we write it.” He tapped a notepad with his feather. Mia drew a big star and a bottle. She giggled. “This is my water star.”

“Next,” said Ollie, “we make it easy.” He moved the bottle to the middle of the rug. “Now it is ready for you.”

Mia took a sip. The water felt cool and fresh on her tongue. “Ahh,” she said, smiling.

Time went by with happy sounds. Tap-tap went the toy drum. Swish-swish went a feather duster as Mia did little cleaning dances. The living room smelled like lemon spray and oranges. Ollie hummed a soft hoot-song.

Soon, Mia looked at the bottle. It was still half full. “Oh,” she said. “I forgot to sip. I was busy.” Her shoulders drooped.

Ollie fluffed his feathers. “It is okay to forget,” he said kindly. “Goals are not for being perfect. Goals help us try again. Let us add a helper step.”

“What helper step?” Mia asked.

“Pair a sip with a sound,” said Ollie. “Every time you hear a drum beat, take a drink.”

Mia’s eyes lit up. “Sip with the beat!”

She tapped the drum. Dum! She took a sip. “Sip!” Dum-dum! “Sip-sip!” The bottle gurgled and glugged. “This is fun,” she giggled. Water dripped a tiny drop on her chin. It felt cool. She wiped it away with her sleeve.

After a little while, the bottle was empty. Mia held it up high. “I did it!”

Ollie clapped his wings. “Hoo-ray! You set a goal. You made a plan. You tried. You changed the plan when it was hard. And you did it.”

Mia bounced on the rug. “Now can I pick a new goal?”

“Yes,” said Ollie. “One at a time. What feels good for your body next?”

Mia put a hand on her tummy. “Move and stretch!” she said. “My goal is to stretch and dance for five minutes.”

“Wonderful,” said Ollie. “Let us make it clear and kind. Five minutes is a short, happy time. We can use the sunny window as our timer. When the sun spot moves from the pillow to the plant, we finish.”

Music floated from a small speaker—soft, happy tunes. Mia stretched her arms up high. “I reach the clouds,” she said. She bent low. “I touch my toes.” The rug felt warm. Her socks were snug and soft. She twirled, and the room swirled in colors—blue pillow, red drum, yellow sun.

After a minute, Mia puffed. “This is hard,” she said.

Ollie nodded. “Hard can be okay. We can make a tiny rest step.” He showed her how to take a deep breath. “In through your nose. Out through your mouth.”

Mia breathed in. The air smelled like oranges. She breathed out. “Ahh.” She giggled. “Ready!”

They danced and stretched until the sun spot slid to the plant. Mia stopped and bowed. “I did it again!”

Ollie smiled. “Your body feels strong. Your heart feels proud.”

Mia hugged Ollie. His feathers were soft against her cheek. “Now I know how to make goals,” she said. “Pick one, make a plan, take little steps, and try again if it’s hard.”

Ollie hooted happily. “Yes. Little steps make big dreams. Water, rest, stretch, move, clean, play—one small goal at a time.”

Mia drew two new stars on the notepad: one for water, one for stretch. The page shone with her goals. The room felt bright and calm.

“Tomorrow,” Mia said, “I will make another small goal.”

“And I will cheer,” said Ollie.

They shared a crunchy apple slice and a cozy quiet moment. The sun warmed their faces. Mia felt proud, healthy, and happy.

The End.
