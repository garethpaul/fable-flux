# Leo Learns to Listen

In a cozy home office, sunlight made warm stripes on the floor. The room smelled like paper and lemon polish. Keys clicked on a keyboard. A printer hummed a gentle song. Shelves held colorful books, and a soft rug felt fuzzy under paws.

A brave little lion cub named Leo padded in. His golden fur shone, and his whiskers twitched with curiosity. “Roar!” Leo whispered, practicing his big roar. He wanted to help. He wanted to play. He wanted to do everything at once.

Mama sat at the desk, typing. “Click, click, click,” went the keys.

Leo’s eyes grew wide. He saw shiny markers, stacks of paper, and a big, bouncy chair that could spin. He wanted to spin now. He wanted to draw now. He wanted to talk now.

“Mama, look at me!” Leo said, hopping onto the rug. He puffed his chest. “I am a brave helper!”

Mama smiled and reached out to scratch under Leo’s chin. “You are brave, little lion,” she said softly. “I’m working right now. The home office is a quiet place. We use soft voices, we wait our turn, and we share the space.”

Leo wriggled. A soft voice? Waiting? Sharing the space? He wanted to roar and spin!

He scooted to the chair and gave it a little push. It squeaked. “Eee!” The chair wobbled. The printer hummed louder. Mama paused.

“Leo,” Mama said in a calm voice, “please stop spinning the chair. The chair is for sitting when we work. That is being respectful.”

Leo’s ears drooped. “Respectful?”

Mama nodded. “Respect means we care about other people and their work. We use kind bodies and kind voices. We ask before we touch.”

Leo rubbed the fuzzy rug with his paw. It felt nice. He wanted to do the right thing. He wanted to be respectful. But the markers looked so bright. He reached for a blue one and popped the cap. “Sniff.” It smelled like blueberries.

“Leo,” Mama said gently, “did you ask to use the markers?”

Leo shook his head. “No.”

“How can you be respectful?” Mama asked.

Leo took a small breath. He put the cap back on. “Mama, may I use the blue marker, please?”

Mama smiled. “Yes, you may. Thank you for asking. You can draw at the little table. I will work for five minutes. Then we can take a break together.”

Leo’s tail swished. He felt warm and proud. He carried the marker to a small table by the window. Sunlight kissed his back. He drew a big round sun and a tiny mouse. Scratch, scratch, went the marker on the paper.

He wanted to sing. He wanted to roar his drawing song. He remembered: soft voices. He hummed instead. “Hmm, hmm.” It sounded like a soft bee. He peeked at Mama. She smiled and kept typing.

Soon, Leo heard a chime. “Ding!” Mama’s timer went off. “Break time,” she said. “Thank you for waiting.”

Leo bounced to Mama. “Can we share a snack?” he asked.

“We can,” Mama said. She opened a small box. Inside were apple slices and crackers. The apples smelled sweet and juicy. “Crunch!” went the slices as they took bites together.

“I like sharing,” Leo said with a crumb on his whisker.

“I like sharing, too,” Mama said. “Thank you for being respectful and patient.”

After snack time, Mama pointed to a basket of soft things: headphones, sticky notes, and a small sand timer. “These help us,” she said. “Headphones mean quiet time. Sticky notes are for messages. The sand timer shows how long we wait.”

Leo touched the headphones. They felt squishy. “Can I try?” he asked.

“Yes,” said Mama. “When the sand is all at the bottom, we trade turns.”

Leo put on the headphones. The room sounded cozy and calm. He watched the tiny sand fall. “Shh,” went the soft sound of the grains. While he waited, he built a tower of sticky note pads, one on top of another: pink, yellow, blue. He stuck a note on his chest. It said, “Leo is learning.”

“Leo,” Mama said with a chuckle, “that is a good message.”

When the sand timer finished, Leo gave the headphones back without a fuss. “Your turn,” he said. He felt big inside, like a mountain of kindness.

Mama put on the headphones and whispered, “Thank you.”

Suddenly, the printer began to buzz and spit out paper. “Bzzzz.” A page fell toward the floor. Leo’s paws twitched. He wanted to grab it first. He stopped and asked, “May I catch the paper, please?”

“Yes,” Mama said. “Please hold it gently.”

Leo reached out with careful paws. He caught the warm paper. It felt smooth and soft. He handed it to Mama. “Here you go,” he said.

Mama’s eyes shone. “That was very respectful, Leo. You asked. You waited. You used gentle paws. You shared the space.”

Leo’s chest felt warm and proud again. “I can be a respectful lion,” he said. “I can listen. I can share. I can wait.”

Mama hugged him. He could smell her soap: clean and lemony. “You are a respectful lion,” she said. “When we respect others, everyone feels safe and happy.”

Leo looked around the home office. It still had bright markers and a bouncy chair. It still had warm sun and soft rugs. It also had something new: a brave little lion who knew how to be kind.

“I like this quiet place,” Leo said. “I can help. I can learn.”

“And we can do it together,” Mama said.

Leo smiled and whispered, “Roar,” in the softest, sweetest voice.

The End.
