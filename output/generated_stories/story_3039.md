# Benny's Sorry Letter

Benny the beaver sat on the peaceful front porch, his big flat tail tapping nervously on the wooden boards. The morning sun felt warm on his brown fur, and he could smell the sweet flowers blooming in the garden. But Benny didn't feel happy at all.

"Oh no, oh no," Benny whispered to himself. "What have I done?"

Yesterday, Benny had been so excited to build a new dam that he had taken some sticks from his friend Rosie Rabbit's fence without asking. Now there was a big hole in her garden fence, and her carrots might get eaten by hungry deer!

Benny's mama came out onto the porch, carrying two cups of warm apple cider. "Good morning, my little builder," she said gently. "You look worried. What's troubling you?"

"Mama," Benny said, his voice small and sad, "I made a big mistake. I took sticks from Rosie's fence without asking, and now her garden isn't safe anymore."

Mama Beaver sat down beside Benny and gave him a warm hug. "That does sound like a problem," she said. "But you know what? When we make mistakes, we can always find ways to make things right again."

"But how?" Benny asked, his brown eyes wide with worry.

"Well," Mama said thoughtfully, "what do you think would help Rosie feel better?"

Benny thought hard, his little paws fidgeting. "Maybe... maybe I could say sorry?"

"That's a wonderful start!" Mama smiled. "And how could you tell her you're sorry?"

Suddenly, Benny's eyes lit up. "I could write her a letter! But Mama, I'm not very good at writing yet."

"That's okay, sweetheart," Mama said. "Writing gets easier with practice. Would you like me to help you?"

Benny nodded eagerly. Mama brought out some colorful paper and crayons. Together, they sat on the porch as Benny carefully wrote his very first sorry letter.

"Dear Rosie," Benny said as he wrote in big, wobbly letters. "I am sorry I took your fence sticks. I will fix it. Your friend, Benny."

Then Benny drew a picture of himself and Rosie playing together, with a beautiful, strong fence around her garden.

"This is wonderful, Benny!" Mama said. "But remember, saying sorry is just the first step. What else can you do?"

Benny thought again. "I could help fix her fence! I know all about building with sticks!"

"Perfect!" Mama clapped her paws together.

After breakfast, Benny walked to Rosie's house, his letter clutched carefully in his paws. His heart was beating fast, but he felt brave.

"Rosie!" he called. "I have something for you!"

Rosie Rabbit hopped over, looking surprised. Benny handed her the letter and watched as she read it slowly.

"Oh, Benny," Rosie said softly. "Thank you for this beautiful letter. I was sad about my fence, but I'm so happy you want to make it right."

Together, Benny and Rosie spent the whole morning fixing the fence. Benny used his strong teeth to cut new sticks to just the right size, and Rosie helped him place them perfectly.

When they finished, the fence looked even better than before!

"You know what, Benny?" Rosie said, giving him a big hug. "Mistakes happen, but what matters most is that you cared enough to fix things. That makes you a really good friend."

Benny felt warm and happy inside. He had learned that saying sorry and making things right felt much better than hiding from his mistakes.

The End.
