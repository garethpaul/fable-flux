# Fawn’s Tasty Timer

In a warm kitchen, a gentle deer fawn named Fern stood on a soft rug. The oven hummed. The window let in golden morning light. Cinnamon and orange peels made the air smell sweet.

Fern’s antler buds tickled. Her small hooves tapped. “I want pancakes now,” she said, tail swishing. “I am so hungry!”

Mama Deer smiled and stirred a big bowl. “We will make world pancakes,” Mama said. “We’ll taste pancakes from many places. It will take time. We can do it, step by step.”

Fern wrinkled her nose. “But I want them fast.” Her tummy grumbled, grrrr.

Mama put a tiny red timer on the counter. Tick-tick-tick. “This is our helper,” Mama said. “When we feel frustrated, we can breathe and wait for the timer. We can plan. We can help.”

Fern looked at the timer. Tick-tick. “I feel squirmy,” she said.

Mama nodded. “Let’s say: I can pause, I can plan, I can try.” She touched her hoof to her chest. “Breathe in, like smelling soup. Breathe out, like blowing on it to cool.”

Fern breathed in. Mmmm, cinnamon. She breathed out. “I can pause, I can plan, I can try,” she whispered.

“First,” Mama said, “we’ll make a small crêpe, like in France. Thin and swirly.” She poured a little batter. Ssssss. It smelled buttery. The timer ticked for one minute.

Fern’s ears drooped. “It is hard to wait,” she said. Her tail twitched faster.

Mama pointed to a bowl of fruit. “Time-management tip,” she said kindly. “While we wait, we do a small job. Can you wash berries?”

Fern ran cool water over blueberries. Splash. Plop. “I am helping!” she said. Her tummy felt calmer. Tick—ding! Mama flipped the crêpe. Swish!

Fern tasted a tiny piece. “It is soft and sweet,” she said, smiling.

“Next,” Mama said, “we’ll make tiny pancakes called blini, like in Russia. They are little and round.” She set the timer. Tick-tick-tick.

Fern felt the squirm try to come back. “I feel the tight feeling,” she said.

“You noticed it,” Mama said. “That is strong.” Mama tapped the timer. “Let’s count blueberries while we wait.”

Fern lined them up. “One, two, three, four…” Her hooves clicked the counter in a gentle beat. The timer dinged. The blini puffed up like little moons.

Fern tasted one with a dollop of yogurt. “Tangy,” she giggled.

“Now,” Mama said, “a tortilla pancake, like in Mexico. We’ll warm it and add bananas and peanut butter.” The pan hissed. The kitchen filled with nutty, warm smells.

Fern watched the peanut butter melt. Drip, drip. The timer ticked. Tick-tick.

“I want it now,” Fern said, voice loud, eyes shiny.

Mama knelt to Fern’s level. “Big feelings are okay,” she said. “What can we do when we feel frustration?”

Fern held her hoof on her chest. “Pause. Plan. Try.” She breathed in, then out. “I can set the napkins,” she said. “One for Mama. One for me.”

“That is a smart plan,” Mama said.

Ding! They folded the warm tortilla and took a bite. “Sweet and creamy,” Fern said, cheeks sticky.

“Last,” Mama said, “we’ll make a fluffy pancake like in Japan. Very soft. It needs more time.” She turned the timer to a longer tick. Tick-tick-tick-tick.

Fern’s eyes got wide. “More time?” Her shoulders rose.

Mama nodded. “Yes. Big softness takes patience. Let’s make a waiting game. We tap a rhythm.” Mama tapped: tap, tap-tap, tap.

Fern tapped along. Tap, tap-tap, tap. “We can also make a list,” she said. “Step one: stir. Step two: pour. Step three: wait. Step four: taste!”

“You are managing your time,” Mama said. “And your feelings.”

The batter sizzled. The kitchen glowed warm. Fern smelled vanilla and heard the soft timer song inside her mind: tick, tick, you can wait, little friend.

Ding! Mama lifted the fluffy pancake. It jiggled. “Like a cloud!” Fern laughed.

They placed all the pancakes on a big plate: a swirly crêpe, little blini, a warm tortilla, and a fluffy cloud. Fern added colorful fruit—blue, red, yellow—and tiny bowls of yogurt and honey.

Fern took a bite of each. “Every pancake is different,” she said. “Like friends from different places.”

Mama smiled. “Yes. We can learn from many cultures. We can try new foods. That is good for our bodies. That is nutrition.”

Fern patted her tummy. “My body feels strong. My heart feels calm.” She looked at the red timer. “You helped me,” she told it. “And I helped me too.”

“What did you do when you felt frustrated?” Mama asked.

“I paused,” Fern said proudly. “I planned a small job. I tried again. Waiting is hard, but I can do it.”

Mama hugged her. The kitchen smelled sweet and safe. The sunlight hugged them back.

Fern licked a dab of yogurt from her nose. “Next time,” she said, “can we try a pancake from India? A dosa?”

Mama laughed softly. “Yes. We can plan it. We’ll set our timer, and we will wait together.”

Fern nodded. “I can pause, I can plan, I can try,” she said one more time. Her tail swished in a happy, slow rhythm.

They clinked their forks. Tink! Fern took another bite, calm and proud.

The End.
