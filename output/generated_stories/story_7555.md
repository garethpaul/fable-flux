# Bibi Bird and the Sparkly Soap Mural

Bibi was a tiny singing bird with bright blue feathers and a beak that hummed like a flute. She lived near a sunny window. One rainy morning, Bibi fluttered into the bathroom to practice her songs. Drip, drip, drip went the faucet. Swish, swish went the shower curtain. The air smelled like orange soap and warm towels.

“Ooo, the echo in here is lovely,” chirped Bibi. “La-la-liii!” Her voice bounced off the tiles and made her chest feel fizzy with joy.

Bibi looked at the smooth, shiny tiles on the wall. They were white like clouds. Next to the sink, she noticed a cup of colorful toothbrushes and a tray of soaps. One soap had sparkles. Another smelled like lemon. A paintbrush lay beside them, drying from a craft night.

“I will make a song picture!” Bibi trilled. “A mural! Art for everyone! Art helps us share feelings.”

She dipped the brush into a bit of foamy soap and swirled it on the tile. Swish, swirl, swoop! The brush made a big rainbow shape. She added dots like raindrops. She sang as she painted, “For the world, for friends, for me and you!”

The bathroom window fogged with steam from the warm pipes, and the mirror got soft and misty. Bibi felt proud. The picture was bright and bubbly. But the soap dripped. Plip. Plop. It slid down the wall and oozed onto the floor.

Bibi stopped singing. “Oh,” she peeped. “Mess.”

Outside, Bibi heard a voice. It was Grandma Ana, the neighbor who always waved and said, “We share our home with everyone. That’s civic care!”

Bibi fluttered to the doorway and peeked out. “Grandma Ana?” she called.

Grandma Ana smiled and stepped into the bathroom. She looked at the swirly soap mural, the drips, and the slippery floor. Her shoes squeaked. “Hello, Bibi. That is a pretty picture,” she said gently. “I can see the rainbow and the raindrops. But the floor is wet. Someone may slip. And soap is for washing hands, not for painting walls.”

Bibi’s feathers drooped. “I wanted to make art to share with the world,” she whispered. “I forgot to ask. I forgot to think.”

Grandma Ana nodded kindly. “Sharing art is wonderful. Art helps us tell stories. But we also have to care for our shared spaces. This bathroom is for everyone who lives here or visits. Civic responsibility means we follow directions, keep things clean, and keep people safe.”

Bibi tilted her head. “Civic… res-pon-si-bi-li-ty,” she practiced. “That means I help my community.”

“Yes,” said Grandma Ana. “We help our home, our street, and even our big world. We follow directions like ‘soap for hands,’ ‘paint on paper,’ and ‘wipe up spills.’ That is how we are good neighbors and global helpers.”

Bibi took a deep breath. The bathroom smelled like orange soap and a little like trouble. She puffed her chest. “I want to fix this. I can fix it.”

Grandma Ana smiled. “Let’s make a plan. Problem, solution.”

“Problem,” chirped Bibi. “I made a soap mural in the wrong place. It is slippery.”

“Solution,” said Grandma Ana. “We will clean the tiles and the floor. Then we will make art in the right place. We will use paper and water-safe paint. We will follow directions.”

Bibi nodded. “I will also sing a song about caring for our shared spaces!”

Together they gathered a bucket of warm water, a soft sponge, and a dry towel. Bibi dipped her tiny claws in the warm water. It felt like a hug. She dabbed the sponge. Squish, squish. She hummed a cleaning tune. “Wipe, wipe, wipe, we care for our world. Wipe, wipe, wipe, every boy and girl.”

Grandma Ana wiped the high tiles. Bibi wiped the low ones. They patted the floor dry. Pat, pat, pat. The bathroom shone again. The mirror was clear, and the floor was safe.

“Look,” Bibi chirped. “Clean!”

Grandma Ana clapped softly. “Well done, helper bird.”

Bibi fluttered with pride. “Now, may I make art on paper? I will put the paper on the counter, and I will keep the water in a little cup. I will follow directions.”

“Yes,” said Grandma Ana. “Let’s open the window a bit for fresh air. We will use a mat to protect the counter. That is being responsible.”

Bibi set up a small paper, a tiny cup of washable paint, and the drying paintbrush. She dipped the brush and sang, “Swish in the cup, tap on the side, paint on the paper, safe and wide.” She painted a rainbow and raindrops again, and this time she added a big earth, round and blue and green.

“This is our world,” Bibi chirped. “People everywhere share bathrooms, parks, and water. We all need to take care.”

Grandma Ana’s eyes shone. “That is global citizenship. We think beyond our room. We think about everyone.”

Bibi wrote a tiny note on the paper with a soft pencil. It said: “Please use soap for hands. Please paint on paper. Love, Bibi.” She taped the note near the sink with gentle, removable tape, just like the label on the toothbrush cup.

She hopped back and sang her new song:
“Care for the sink, care for the floor,
Follow the signs, and help even more.
Clean hands, kind hearts, art in the right place,
We share our world, every home, every space.”

The bathroom felt warm and friendly. The tiles sparkled. The picture on paper glowed with color. Bibi’s heart felt light.

Grandma Ana gave a thumbs-up. “Problem solved, solution found.”

Bibi chirped happily. “I learned: I can make art and also care for our shared spaces. I can follow directions and be a good neighbor to my home and to the world.”

She fluttered to the window and let a soft breeze tickle her feathers. “La-la-liii,” she sang, bright and clear.

The End.
