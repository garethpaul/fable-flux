# Tully Turtle’s Helping Heart

Tully was a shy turtle with a soft, speckled shell. He liked quiet corners and slow, careful steps. One sunny morning, Tully peeked into the creative art room at the community center. It smelled like fruity markers and fresh paper. Paints sparkled in jars. Brushes stood up like tall, colorful grass.

“Hello,” said Ms. Poppy, the art teacher. She wore a smock with paint dots all over it—red, blue, yellow, green. “Welcome, friends! Today, our community needs help. The playground sign is old and faded. We will make a new Welcome sign for everyone to enjoy!”

Children cheered. “Yay!” cried Leo, waving a big paintbrush. “I can paint bright letters!”

“I can glue,” said Mei, clapping sticky hands. “I love glue.”

Tully tucked his head a little into his shell. He whispered, “I can help, maybe… a little.” His voice was quiet like a tiny bell.

Ms. Poppy smiled kindly. “Every helper is important,” she said. “Helping our community is a way to show love. We all have something special to share.”

On the table, there were smooth wooden boards for the sign. There were jars of buttons, soft yarn, shiny sequins, and paper that felt bumpy or silky. Tully touched a piece of paper. It felt like the soft inside of a seashell. He liked the feeling.

“Let’s plan,” said Ms. Poppy. “What should the sign say?”

“Welcome, Friends!” shouted Leo.

“Welcome, Everyone!” added Mei.

Tully lifted his head a little more. “Maybe…” he said, “we can add a turtle and a bunny and a bird. Everyone is different. Everyone is welcome.”

“That’s a beautiful idea,” Ms. Poppy said. “You are thinking about celebrating differences. Thank you, Tully.”

Tully’s cheeks felt warm with a shy, happy glow.

The group began to work. Leo painted big, bold letters. Swoosh, swoosh went his brush. Mei dotted glue—dot, dot, dot—and pressed shiny sequins—click, click, click.

Tully looked at the yarn. It was soft and quiet. He liked that. He picked a gentle green and a warm brown. He began to make a small turtle shape with yarn lines. He placed each piece very carefully. Press. Press. Press.

Suddenly, there was a problem. “Oh no,” Leo said. “I made the L too big. There’s no room for the E!”

Mei frowned. “The sequins spilled!” The little circles skittered across the table like tiny rainbow beetles.

Tully’s heart beat faster. He wanted to help, but he felt shy. “What if I say the wrong thing?” he thought. Tully took a slow, deep breath. In… and out… like the ocean waves he heard in his dreams.

Ms. Poppy noticed. “Remember,” she said softly, “a growth mindset means we can try new ideas and keep going. Mistakes help us learn.”

Tully looked carefully at the board. He had an idea. “Maybe… we can make the L into a tree,” he whispered. “Then the E can sit next to it.”

Leo blinked. “A tree L?”

“Yes,” Tully said, a little braver. “We can paint leaves on the tall part.”

Leo grinned. “That’s cool! Let’s try.”

Tully turned to Mei. “We can sweep the sequins with a soft brush,” he said. “Then we can put them in a jar and share.”

Mei nodded. “Good plan!”

They worked together. Swish went the brush as they gathered the sequins. Leo painted the L into a tree with green leaf dots. Tully added a tiny yarn bird on the branch. Chirp, chirp, his mind sang.

Soon, more helpers came. A bunny sticker here. A rainbow ribbon there. Tully kept placing yarn—curvy lines for a bunny ear, a soft spiral for a snail shell. His slow, careful work made gentle shapes that felt friendly and calm.

“Your yarn animals are wonderful,” Ms. Poppy said. “They make the sign warm and welcoming.”

Tully smiled. “I like going slow,” he said. “Slow helps me see.”

“Going slow is a superpower,” Ms. Poppy replied. “We all help in our own ways.”

Just then, a little kid named Sam peeked in. “Can I help?” Sam asked. “I’m little.”

Tully looked at Sam’s small hands. He remembered feeling small and unsure. “Yes,” Tully said. “You can place buttons for butterfly spots.” He showed Sam how to press, press, press, with gentle taps.

Sam beamed. “I’m helping!”

“You are,” Tully said. His voice was stronger now.

At last, the sign read, “Welcome, Everyone!” The L looked like a leafy tree. The O was a bright sun. A yarn turtle, bunny, bird, and snail smiled from the edges. Sequins twinkled like tiny stars.

They carried the sign to the playground. Children playing on swings stopped to look. Parents smiled. A breeze smelled like fresh grass and soap bubbles. The sun warmed Tully’s shell.

“It’s beautiful,” a dad said. “Thank you for helping our community.”

Tully felt shy and proud at the same time. His heart felt big and steady, like a drum. Ms. Poppy put a small ribbon on the sign that read, “Made with love by helpers.”

Leo bumped shoulders with Tully. “Your idea saved the letters,” he said.

Mei held up the jar. “Teamwork saved the sequins!”

Tully nodded. “We all helped,” he said. “We used our different ways.”

Ms. Poppy gathered everyone. “What did we learn today?”

Tully took a breath. “Helping our community makes everyone feel welcome,” he said. “Trying new ideas helps us grow. And being different is good. We can help in our own special ways.”

“Exactly,” said Ms. Poppy.

Tully looked at the sign shining in the sun. He didn’t hide in his shell. He stood tall, soft and steady. In his slow way, he had made a big, bright difference.

The End.
