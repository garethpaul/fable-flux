# Milo the Mouse Finds His Calm

Milo was a kind little mouse who lived in a cozy corner of a garage workshop. The garage smelled like fresh wood and warm rubber. Sunlight made shiny stripes on the floor. Tools hung on the wall, and a soft broom leaned in the corner like a tall, friendly tree.

Every morning, Milo waved to the tools. “Good morning, hammer. Good morning, screws,” he squeaked. He loved to help. He loved to share. He loved to be kind.

One day, the garage buzzed with busy sounds. The big door rolled up. Vrrr-rrroll! A radio played a bouncy song. Tap-tap-tap went the hammer. Whirr went the drill. Milo’s whiskers twitched. His ears wiggled. There was so much noise and so much to do.

Today, Milo wanted to make a tiny wooden cart for his toy acorn. He had a little scrap of wood, two bottle-cap wheels, and a piece of string. “I can do it,” Milo told himself. “I will be patient. I will be calm.”

But then, a tall stack of boxes teetered. Clunk! A metal pan fell. CLANG! The radio sang louder. And the drill went WHIRRRR. Milo’s heart thumped fast. His little paws felt jumpy.

“I feel wiggly inside,” Milo whispered. He tried to push the bottle caps on the little stick, but his paws shook. The caps rolled away. Clink, clink, clink.

“I want to finish now!” Milo squeaked. His tail curled tight.

Milo remembered something he practiced. “When I feel too big inside, I can make myself small and calm,” he said. He sat on a soft rag and closed his eyes. “Smell the fresh wood in… one, two, three. Blow out the candle… one, two, three.” He put a tiny paw on his chest and felt it go thump, thump, thump… slower, slower.

He opened one eye. The radio was still loud. The drill still whirred. “I can ask for what I need,” Milo told himself. “I can use my brave voice.”

Milo scampered to the radio. “Excuse me, radio,” he said kindly. “This song is fun, but it is too loud for me.” The radio did not answer, but the person in the workshop heard Milo’s squeaky voice. The person smiled and turned the knob. Click. The music got softer. La-la-la, gentle and sweet.

“Thank you,” Milo said. His shoulders dropped. He felt a little better.

He walked to the drill and stood a safe distance away. “Excuse me,” Milo spoke up to the person again. “The drill is very noisy. May I work when it is off? Or may I work in a quiet corner?”

The person nodded and pointed to a little shelf near the window. It had a soft cloth and a tiny lamp. The sun made warm patches there. “That spot is just right,” Milo said. He felt proud for asking.

Milo carried his wood, wheels, and string to the quiet corner. He sat crisscross with his tail wrapped gently around his feet. He took three slow breaths again. In through his nose, smelling wood and a tiny hint of lemon cleaner. Out through his mouth, like blowing a bubble.

“Okay, paws,” he said softly. “Slow and steady.”

One bottle cap rolled away again. Clink! Milo felt a little bubble of mad. He noticed it. “Hello, mad bubble,” he said. “You can float, and I can breathe.” He squeezed his tiny paws, then opened them. Squeeze, open. Squeeze, open. His paws felt ready.

Milo looked around the workshop. He had an idea. “I can be resourceful,” he said. He found a small piece of tape. Stick! He put the tape on the end of the little stick so the bottle cap would not slip. He used a cotton swab as a tiny spacer. He tied the string in a bow he had practiced. Loop, swoop, pull!

The tiny cart began to look real. The wheels went roll-roll. The wood felt smooth under his paws. The string felt soft. Milo’s whiskers danced. “I did it with calm, and I did it with care,” he sang quietly.

Just then, the broom wiggled when a breeze came in. Swish! It tipped and brushed against his cart. The cart wobbled. “Oh!” Milo gasped. He felt a hot spark of worry.

“Pause,” he told himself. “Breathe.” He took one breath. Two. Three. The spark cooled. He set the broom back up and said, “Excuse me, broom. Please stand still.” He smiled at his own joke.

Milo rolled his acorn into the cart. He gave the string a gentle tug. The cart moved smoothly across the sunny stripe on the floor. Roll… roll… roll. “Hooray!” Milo cheered. “I can do hard things when I calm my body and use my words.”

The person in the workshop clapped gently. Clap, clap. “Good job, little friend,” they said. Milo’s heart felt warm like toast.

Milo looked at the tools and called, “Thank you for the help, quiet corner. Thank you, brave voice. Thank you, slow breaths.” He smiled at his cart and gave it one more roll.

That night, when the garage was still and soft, Milo curled up in his cozy corner. He whispered, “Today I learned to stop, to breathe, to ask for what I need, and to think of smart ways to solve problems. When I take care of my feelings, I can take care of my work too.”

He closed his eyes. The garage hummed a tiny, gentle hum. Milo felt safe, calm, and proud.

The End.
