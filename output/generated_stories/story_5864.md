# Benny Beaver Builds the Balcony

Benny was a busy beaver with bright eyes and a big, cheerful tail. He lived with his family in a cozy apartment. Outside their living room was a sunny balcony garden. Pots of mint and basil smelled fresh. Cherry tomatoes glowed red like tiny lanterns. Wind chimes tinkled softly, singing hello.

One morning, Mama said, “We will host a little balcony picnic today. Our neighbors will visit and share snacks from their homes! Let’s build a veggie stand for the food.”

Benny’s tail thumped with joy. “Build? I can build!” His whiskers twitched. He loved building more than anything.

“But first,” Mama said, smiling, “we make a plan.”

Benny took a deep breath. He loved to go, go, go. But he knew plans help builders. He grabbed a crayon and a little notepad.

“Step one,” Benny said, drawing big shapes. “We measure.” He stretched a soft tape measure across the balcony rails. “One, two, three big steps long,” he counted, padding his paws on the warm floor. The wood felt smooth under his feet.

“Step two,” Mama said, “we gather safe pieces.” They had light wooden crates, soft rope, and fabric from an old picnic blanket. The blanket was sunny yellow, with tiny blue squares. It smelled like soap and strawberries.

“Step three,” Benny said, tapping his crayon. “We build!”

Before building, Mama added, “And we warm up our bodies. Builders need strong, ready muscles.”

Benny giggled. Exercise sounded fun. They did big beaver stretches. They reached high like tall sunflowers. They twisted side to side like swaying chimes. They touched their toes. Benny’s tail swished. His heart went thump-thump in a happy rhythm.

“Now I feel strong,” Benny said. “I feel ready!”

They placed two crates side by side. Benny checked the balance with his paws. “Wobbly,” he said.

“What can we do?” Mama asked.

Benny thought. Planning time! “We can flip one crate,” he said. “The flat side down.”

They flipped it. Benny tested again. “Steady!” he cheered.

He looped soft rope around the crates to hold them as a table. He pulled it snug but not too tight. “Like a hug,” he said, smiling.

Next, Benny spread the yellow blanket over the top. The fabric fluttered in the gentle breeze and whispered shhhh as it settled. He smoothed it with careful paws. “Smooth like calm water,” he said.

They added small shelves using two more crates on their sides. “This is for the mini carrots,” Benny said. “And this shelf is for mint tea cups.”

The doorbell rang. Neighbors arrived with bright smiles and tasty treats. Mrs. Chen brought dumplings that smelled warm and cozy. Mateo and his abuela brought sweet plantain chips and a tiny shaker. “This is a maraca,” Abuela said, shaking it. It made a cheerful ch-ch-ch.

Nia brought little flatbread with hummus. She wore a scarf with happy colors. “We say karibu,” she told Benny. “It means welcome.”

Benny’s eyes sparkled. “Karibu!” he tried. His voice felt friendly and warm.

Mama whispered, “We can make a welcome sign.”

Benny wrote “Welcome” and “Karibu” and “Hola.” He drew a tiny dumpling, a plantain chip, and a mint leaf. He taped the sign to the side of the stand. The tape made a soft rip-rip sound.

“Time to test our stand,” Mama said.

Benny bent his knees and lifted a bowl of cherry tomatoes. He remembered to keep the bowl close to his body—just like Mama taught him for safe lifting. “Strong and careful,” he said. He set the bowl on the top shelf. It didn’t wobble. He grinned.

Everyone took a turn placing food. The stand held steady. The balcony smelled like mint, dumplings, and sunshine. Wind chimes tinkled. A pigeon cooed from a nearby roof, low and sleepy.

Mateo shook the maraca. “Ch-ch-ch! Let’s do a building dance!”

They all did tiny steps around the stand. Benny clapped, stomped, and wiggled his tail. “Exercise helps builders!” he laughed. His cheeks felt warm. His legs felt strong.

After the dance, Benny noticed a small problem. A corner of the blanket kept flapping up, tickle-tickle, on the tomatoes.

“Hm,” he said, tapping his chin. “We need a fix.”

“Builder brains think, then try,” Mama said.

Benny found clothespins in a drawer. He clipped the fluttery corners to the crates. Click! Click! Now the blanket stayed in place. The tomatoes rested like red buttons on a sweater.

Abuela smiled. “Buen trabajo, Benny. Good job.”

Benny’s heart felt big. “Thank you,” he said. He liked learning new words.

Everyone tasted and talked. Mrs. Chen showed Benny how to pinch a tiny dumpling shape with careful fingers. Nia taught him to say “asante,” which means thank you. Benny taught everyone the beaver builder clap—clap, clap, tail thump!

When the sun began to soften and glow orange, Mama said, “Let’s clean up and check our stand.”

Benny made a clean-up plan. “First, lids on. Next, dishes to the bin. Then, wipe-wipe.” He moved his body with steady steps. He stacked the crates and rolled the rope neatly. The maraca gave one last ch-ch-ch as Mateo waved goodbye.

Benny looked at the balcony. It was tidy and calm. The stand was strong and ready for another day.

“I built with a plan,” Benny said. “I used my body. I listened. I learned words from friends.”

Mama hugged him. “You did. Building takes planning, exercise, and care. Sharing builds friendships, too.”

Benny’s tail made a happy thump. He felt proud, cozy, and sleepy.

“Tomorrow,” he whispered, “I’ll build a bird perch.”

“Tomorrow,” Mama agreed.

The End.
