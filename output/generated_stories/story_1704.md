# Leo's Colorful Discovery

The morning sun streamed through the big windows of the art room. Paintbrushes stood tall in jars like colorful flowers. Paper and crayons covered every table. Little Leo the lion cub walked in with his fluffy mane bouncing.

"Good morning, Leo!" said Mrs. Chen, the art teacher. She wore a paint-splattered apron covered in rainbow colors.

Leo looked around the room. Children sat at different tables, all working on art projects. But something made Leo's whiskers twitch with worry. Everyone was making the same thing - red hearts for the Valentine's Day display.

"Here's your paper, Leo," said Mrs. Chen, placing a white sheet in front of him. "Make a beautiful red heart like everyone else."

Leo picked up a red crayon. It felt heavy in his small paw. He looked at his friend Maya, who was carefully coloring inside the lines of her heart. Then he looked at Sam, whose heart was perfectly neat and tidy.

But Leo's tummy felt funny. At home, his family made decorations with bright orange and gold colors for Chinese New Year. His grandmother taught him that orange meant good luck and happiness.

"Mrs. Chen," Leo said softly, his voice barely louder than a whisper. "Can I use orange instead?"

Mrs. Chen stopped walking around the room. "Orange? But Leo, we're making red hearts for Valentine's Day. Everyone needs to make the same thing."

Leo's ears drooped down. He wanted to be brave like the big lions in his picture books, but this felt scary. His paws felt sweaty as he held the red crayon.

"I... I just really like orange," Leo said, looking down at his paws.

Maya looked up from her red heart. "Why do you like orange, Leo?"

Leo's golden eyes lit up a little. "My grandma says orange is special in my family. We use it for decorations when we celebrate Chinese New Year. It makes me feel happy and brave."

Sam put down his crayon too. "That sounds really cool! My family uses blue and white for Hanukkah. Those colors make me feel special too."

Mrs. Chen walked over to Leo's table. She knelt down so she was the same height as the little lion cub. "Leo, can you tell me more about Chinese New Year?"

Leo's whiskers perked up. "We hang orange lanterns and eat special cookies! And we give red envelopes with money inside for good luck!"

"That sounds wonderful," said Mrs. Chen, smiling warmly. "You know what? I think our Valentine's display could use some variety. Would you like to make an orange heart and teach us about your traditions?"

Leo's whole face brightened. His tail swished with excitement. "Really? Can I make it orange?"

"Of course! And maybe Maya and Sam can share their special colors and traditions too."

Soon, the art room buzzed with excited chatter. Leo carefully colored his heart bright orange, adding small gold dots like the decorations at home. Maya decided to add some purple stripes to her heart, explaining how her family celebrated Diwali with colorful rangoli patterns. Sam made his heart blue and white, sharing stories about spinning dreidels.

Mrs. Chen clapped her hands together. "Look at our beautiful, diverse Valentine's display! Each heart tells a special story about your families and traditions."

Leo stood back and admired all the different hearts hanging on the wall. Red, orange, purple, blue, and white hearts created a rainbow of love and friendship.

"Thank you for being brave and sharing your traditions, Leo," said Mrs. Chen. "You taught us all something new today."

Leo puffed out his chest proudly. Being different wasn't scary after all - it made everything more beautiful and interesting.

The End.
