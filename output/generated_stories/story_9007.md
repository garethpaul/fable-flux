# Leo’s Tidy-Time Promise

In a quiet attic, dust danced in the sunlight like tiny stars. Boxes were stacked like little mountains. The wooden floor felt smooth and cool. It smelled like old books and pine. A brave little lion cub named Leo padded in on soft paws. His whiskers twitched with excitement.

“Today is map day!” Leo said, his tail swishing. “I will find Grandma’s world map and learn about faraway places.”

Mama Lion peeked up the stairs. “Remember your routine, Leo,” she said gently. “First, tidy. Then, explore. After, put things back. That’s our attic plan.”

Leo nodded, but his eyes sparkled at a colorful kite poking out of a box. “Just one peek,” he whispered.

He tugged the kite. Thump! A stack of buttons in a tin slid and spilled. Click, clack, clink! Buttons rolled like little beetles across the floor.

“Oh no,” Leo gasped. He jumped to catch them, but his paw bumped a tower of picture books. Flop! They spread out like a paper river.

Mama’s steps paused on the stairs. Leo’s ears drooped. He felt a hot flutter in his tummy. He had not followed the tidy-first routine.

“I wanted the map,” Leo said in a small voice. “I was too fast. I made a mess.”

Mama’s eyes were kind. “Everyone makes mistakes,” she said. “What can we do now?”

Leo took a deep breath. He remembered his brave heart. “I can make amends,” he said. “I can fix what I did.”

“That’s a strong choice,” Mama said, smiling. “Let’s make a plan. Plans help with time.”

Leo sat and tapped his paw softly. Tap, tap. “Plan steps,” he said. “One: pick up buttons. Two: stack books. Three: find Grandma’s map. Four: put things back.”

“Good coordination,” Mama said. “We’ll work together. We can sing the Tidy-Time song.”

They sang, “Pick, place, pat, and pause,” in a gentle rhythm. The buttons made tiny clicks as Leo dropped them into the tin. Click, clack, clink. He listened and felt calm. He lined up books by size—small, medium, big. The covers smelled like crayons and old paper. He patted each book. Pat, pat.

When a button rolled under a trunk, Leo stretched. “I can’t reach,” he said.

“Ask for help,” Mama said. “That’s part of good time management. It saves time.”

“Can you lift the trunk, please?” Leo asked.

Together they lifted. Leo scooped the button. “Teamwork!” he cheered. “We are good at coordination.”

Soon the attic looked neat again. Leo’s chest felt light. “I made amends,” he said. “I said sorry with actions.”

Mama nodded. “Saying sorry is kind. Fixing the mistake is kinder. You followed your routine, even after a mix-up.”

“Now the map?” Leo asked.

“Now the map,” Mama said.

They opened a wide drawer. A big, crinkly paper map lay inside. It smelled like adventure. Colors bloomed—blue oceans, green forests, golden deserts, and white icy tops.

Leo’s eyes grew wide. “The world is so big,” he whispered. He pointed to a bright blue curve. “What is this?”

“That is the ocean,” Mama said. “Many friends live near it.”

Leo pointed to a tiny dot. “And this?”

“A faraway city,” Mama said. “People wake up at different times there. While you sleep, they may eat breakfast. That’s called time zones.”

Leo thought about time. “So my tidy-time helps me too,” he said. “If I use time well, I can learn more about the world.”

He traced the path of a red line on the map. “Grandma traveled here,” he said. “She sent a postcard.” He found the postcard in the drawer and read the simple words with Mama: “Hello from far away! Be brave. Be kind.”

Leo smiled. “Being brave is saying sorry and making amends. Being kind is putting things back.”

“Exactly,” Mama said.

They set a little sand timer. Swish. “Five minutes to look,” Mama said. “Then we put the map away.”

Leo agreed. He watched the sand fall like golden rain. He whispered, “Blue ocean. Green forest. Golden sand.” He imagined friends waving from different places around the world. “Hello!” he said softly. “I will visit with my eyes today.”

When the last grain fell, Leo rolled the map carefully. His paws moved slow and steady. “I am keeping my promise,” he said. He slid the map back and closed the drawer with a quiet click.

The attic was calm. The sun made warm squares on the floor. Leo snuggled against Mama. “I feel proud,” he said. “I fixed my mistake. I followed our routine. I used my time well.”

Mama kissed his forehead. “That is the brave kind of roar,” she said.

Leo gave a tiny, happy roar—r-r-r—softer than the attic dust. “Tomorrow,” he said, “I will tidy first. Then I will explore again. The world is big, and my heart is kind.”

The End.
