# Rocko Raccoon’s Sunny Backyard Band

Rocko was a musical raccoon with soft gray fur and bright, curious eyes. He loved to make music in the sunny backyard. The grass felt tickly under his paws. The air smelled like sweet oranges. Bees buzzed, birds chirped, and Rocko hummed along. “La-la-la!” he sang, tapping a little tin can like a drum.

One morning, Rocko looked around the yard. There were bottles by the fence, crinkly paper near the sandbox, and a wobbly pile of cans by the gate. “Oh no,” said Rocko. His tummy felt twisty. “This looks messy. It makes the birds sad.” A robin chirped, “Cheep?” and tilted her head. Rocko whispered, “It’s okay, little bird. I will help.”

Rocko wanted to play music, but the junk made sharp clinks under his paws. He stepped on a bottle and winced. “Ouch!” His ears drooped. “I feel upset. I want to play, but I also want to help. Hmm.”

Rocko took a deep breath. He placed one paw on his heart and one paw on his belly. “Name it to tame it,” he said softly. “I feel worried.” He breathed in, then out. The sun warmed his back. He felt a little better.

Just then, Mia the mouse peeked from under a leaf. “Hi, Rocko. Why do you look so scrunchy?” she squeaked.

“The backyard is messy,” said Rocko. “I think all this belongs in the recycling bin. But it is a big job.”

Mia looked around. “We can do big things together,” she said. “Little paws, big help!”

Rocko smiled. “Thank you, Mia. That is generous.”

They found three big bins by the porch: one for paper, one for plastic, and one for metal. Each bin had a bright sticker: blue for paper, green for plastic, yellow for metal.

Rocko tapped each sticker like a drum. “Boom, boom, boom! Paper, plastic, metal!” He clapped a beat, and Mia giggled.

They started to sort. “This crinkly bottle is plastic,” said Rocko, dropping it into the green bin. Plunk! “This shiny can is metal,” said Mia, dropping it into the yellow bin. Ting! “This soft newspaper is paper,” said Rocko, and the blue bin went thump.

Plunk. Ting. Thump. Their sorting made a song. Rocko swayed his tail to the rhythm. “We can recycle and make music!” he sang. The breeze smelled like fresh grass. The sun felt warm on their faces.

Then Rocko found a long cardboard tube. “Oh! A trumpet!” He put it to his mouth and made a gentle whooo sound. Mia clapped. “Again! Again!”

Rocko laughed, but then he saw a glass bottle stuck under a bush. It wobbled when he tugged. His paws slipped. “I can’t get it,” he said, feeling his shoulders droop. “I feel frustrated.”

Mia touched his paw. “Let’s pause and breathe,” she said. They both breathed in and out together. In… and out… Rocko felt calm return. “I can ask for help,” he said. “Asking is strong.”

He called to the robin. “Little bird, can you point the way?” The robin hopped and chirped at the other side of the bush. Rocko tried that angle. With one careful wiggle, the bottle slid out. “I did it!” said Rocko. Mia cheered. Ting-ting went the metal bin as she clapped with two small coins she found.

Soon, the backyard looked bright and clean. The green leaves shone. The sandbox looked ready for castles. Rocko wiped his paws. “We sorted paper, plastic, and metal,” he said proudly. “Recycling keeps our backyard happy. It helps the Earth breathe.”

Mia nodded. “And it helps our feelings, too. I feel proud!”

Rocko felt proud, too. His tummy felt light and bubbly, like a giggle. “Now we can make music,” he said. “But… I want to share.”

He picked up the long tube trumpet, two little can-drums, and a shaker made from a bottle with beans inside. He gave the can-drums to Mia. “For you,” he said. “You helped so much.”

Mia’s eyes sparkled. “Thank you. That is very generous.”

Rocko put the shaker in the robin’s nest, safe on a low branch. “For you,” he said softly. The robin chirped a happy tune.

They began to practice. Rocko blew a soft whooo into the tube. Mia tapped the cans: tap-tap-tap. The robin shook the shaker: sha-sha-sha. At first, the beats were bumpy. Mia missed a tap. Rocko squeaked the tube. They giggled.

“Practice makes us stronger,” said Rocko. “We can try again.”

They counted, “One, two, three!” Tap-tap-tap. Whooo. Sha-sha-sha. The sounds blended like sunshine and breeze. Neighbors peeked over the fence and smiled. The backyard felt safe, tidy, and full of happy music.

When the song ended, Rocko bowed. “We recycled, we practiced, and we shared,” he said. “That makes my heart feel big.”

Mia nodded. “Mine too.”

Rocko looked at the bins. “Tomorrow, we will check the yard again,” he said. “We will sort what we can, reuse what we can, and make more music.”

The sun slipped a little lower, painting the sky peach. Rocko hugged Mia. “Thank you for helping me with my big feelings,” he said. “Naming them helped. Breathing helped. Sharing helped.”

“And recycling helped the Earth,” said Mia.

Rocko smiled and played a gentle goodnight tune. The backyard hummed with kind, clean joy.

The End.
