# Joey Jumps to Help

In a sunny backyard, a joyful kangaroo joey named Kiki bounced on soft green grass. The air smelled like warm flowers and fresh soil. Birds chirped, “Cheep-cheep!” and the breeze tickled Kiki’s fur.

“I love our backyard!” Kiki giggled. “It’s sunny and kind.”

Mama Roo smiled from the porch. “Remember, Kiki,” she said gently, “being kind is something we do, not just something we feel.”

Kiki nodded and bounced higher. “I can do kind!”

As Kiki hopped, she saw a little ant hill beside the garden path. Tiny ants marched in a line, carrying crumbs. Kiki leaned close and listened. The ants made tiny tap-tap sounds on the dirt.

Just then, a big leaf blew down and covered part of their line. An ant bumped the leaf and squeaked, “Oh no!”

Kiki’s heart felt soft. “I can help,” she whispered. “I can care.”

Kiki bent down and gently lifted the leaf with two careful paws. She moved it to the side so the ants could walk again.

The ants wiggled their antennae. In Kiki’s mind, she imagined them saying, “Thank you!” Kiki smiled. “You’re welcome, tiny friends.”

She felt a warm glow in her chest. That’s empathy, Mama would say. It means, “I feel with you.”

Kiki hopped along and heard a rustle near the fence. Her friend, Pip the puppy, was tangled in a garden ribbon. “Yip-yip!” cried Pip. His ears drooped, and his tail was stuck.

Kiki knelt down. “Hi, Pip. I see you’re upset. I’m here.”

Pip whimpered softly. Kiki looked at his face and saw watery eyes. “You feel scared,” Kiki said in a calm voice. “Let’s breathe together.”

“Sniff… whoo,” breathed Kiki slowly. Pip sniffed and blew out, too. “Sniff… whoo.”

“Better?” Kiki asked.

Pip’s tail thumped once. Kiki carefully wiggled the ribbon loose. “Almost free,” she said. “One more loop… done!”

Pip jumped and licked Kiki’s cheek. “Yip-yip!” He wagged hard enough to shake.

Kiki giggled. “I’m happy you’re happy.”

Mama called from the porch, “Great helping, Kiki. You looked, you listened, and you cared.”

Kiki puffed up with pride and hopped toward the veggie patch. The carrots stood in neat rows, and the tomatoes were shiny and red. Buzz! A bee hovered over a flower. The sun made everything glow.

Kiki noticed paper cups rolling near the gate. A napkin flapped on the grass like a little ghost. “Oh!” she said. “Our backyard looks messy.”

She thought about the ants and Pip. If trash blew into the ant hill, the ants could get stuck. If Pip stepped on a cup, he might slip. The birds might peck the napkin and get sick. Kiki felt a tug in her tummy.

“This is our place,” she said. “I can help it stay nice.”

That’s civic responsibility, Mama would say. It means, “We take care of our home and our shared spaces.”

Kiki grabbed a small bucket and some gloves from the porch. She sang, “Pick it up, tidy up, gentle paws today!” She picked up the cups and the napkin. She found a bottle cap near the swing and a straw by the hose. Clink, crinkle, rustle.

Neighbor Nana peeked over the fence. “Hello, Kiki! Thank you for cleaning. The yard looks bright.”

Kiki waved. “We all share the backyard air,” she said. “Clean is kind.”

Her legs started to feel wobbly from hopping and helping. Her tummy rumbled like a small drum. Kiki sat on the soft grass and took a sip of cool water from her cup. “Ahhh,” she sighed. She nibbled a crunchy apple slice and closed her eyes for a moment. The breeze brushed her cheeks.

Mama Roo came over with a shady hat. “Here, love. Helpers need care, too.”

Kiki put on the hat. “Self-care!” she said. “I drink water. I rest. Then I can help more.”

She stretched her toes and listened to the backyard sounds: buzz of bees, chirp of birds, swish of leaves, and Pip’s happy panting. Kiki’s body felt calm and strong again.

Suddenly, she heard a soft cry. “Tweet-tweet?” A little bird sat on the ground near the garden, a small string wrapped around its foot.

Kiki moved slowly and knelt low. “Hi, little bird,” she whispered. “I see you. You look scared.”

She looked at the bird’s face and saw its tiny chest puff and flutter. “Let’s breathe slow,” Kiki whispered, breathing in and out softly. “I will help.”

Kiki called, “Mama, may I have the safety scissors?”

Mama came with small, safe scissors and stood close. “We will be gentle.”

Kiki spoke to the bird, “I will cut the string. Snip-snip. You might feel a tickle.”

Snip. The string fell away. The bird shook its feathers. “Tweet!” It hopped, then lifted into the warm, sunny air. The bird circled once as if to say, “Thank you!” and flew to a high branch.

Kiki clapped. Pip barked, “Yip!”

Mama hugged Kiki. “You helped with your heart, your paws, and your mind today.”

Kiki felt that warm glow again. “I looked, I listened, I cared. I helped our friends. I helped our yard. I helped myself.”

Mama nodded. “That is empathy, civic care, and self-care.”

Kiki stood tall in the sunny backyard. “I can be kind every day,” she said. “I can care for others, for our place, and for me.”

The breeze whispered through the leaves, the ants marched on, Pip chased a safe, soft ball, and the little bird sang from the tree.

Kiki bounced once, twice, and three times high, smiling wide. “Kind feels good,” she said. “Kind helps everyone.”

The End.
