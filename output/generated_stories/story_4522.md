# Twirl in the Guest Room

Timo was a shy turtle with a smooth green shell and gentle eyes. He walked softly, tap-tap, across the carpet of the guest room. The room smelled like clean linen and lemon soap. Sunshine made little squares on the floor. A big mirror stood against the wall, and a soft blue rug felt warm under Timo’s tiny feet.

Timo whispered, “I want to dance.” His voice was small, like a tiny bell. But his tummy felt wiggly, and his cheeks felt hot. He pulled his head into his shell. “What if I look silly?” he wondered.

On the bed sat a small basket with ribbons, buttons, and a notepad. A paper sign on the wall had big letters: D-A-N-C-E. Timo sounded them out slowly, “D… A… N… C… E. Dance.” He smiled a little. “I can learn the word, and I can learn the moves.”

He peeked at the mirror. “Hello, Timo,” he said softly, practicing words. “I feel shy, and that is okay.” He put his hand on his shell. “I can breathe.” He took a long breath in. One, two, three. He let it out slowly. One, two, three. His tummy felt calmer.

“Let’s get ready,” Timo said. He looked around the guest room and started to organize. He pushed the chair to the side, shhh, so he had a safe space. He folded the blanket and put it on the bed. He lined up his tiny shoes by the door—left, right, neat and tidy. He picked three ribbons from the basket and laid them in a row: red, blue, yellow. “This is my dance space,” he said. “Clean and cozy.”

Timo tapped the music button on a small radio. Soft music floated like feathers—dum, da-dum, dum. The beat was slow, like a heart. Timo’s toes wiggled. He whispered, “Step, step, slide.” He tried it. Step. Step. Slide. His shell swayed, and he giggled. “That felt nice.”

He looked in the mirror again. “I can use my words and my body,” he told himself. “I can tell a story with dance.” He tried to show “happy” with big arm circles. He tried to show “shy” with small steps and a tiny peek from his shell. He tried to show “brave” with a tall stand and a long reach.

Then—thump! The radio button slipped, and the music stopped. The room was quiet. Timo’s eyes went wide. “Oh no,” he said. “Problem.” His heart beat faster. He sat down on the blue rug and took another slow breath. “I feel worried,” he said out loud. “Worried is okay. I can think of a plan.”

He looked at the ribbons. He looked at the notepad. “I can make my own music,” he said. “I can use sounds and words.” He tapped his shell, tok-tok. He clapped softly, clap-clap. He hummed, mmm-mmm. He wrote on the notepad: tap, clap, hum. He smiled. “I can be resourceful.”

Timo stood up and tried his dance again.

“Tap, tap,” he said, stepping forward.
“Clap, clap,” he said, spreading his arms.
“Hum, hum,” he sang, swaying side to side.

He made a little rhyme to help remember. “Tap and clap, then hum along. Little steps make big and strong.” He liked the words. They felt smooth in his mouth, like warm soup. He danced to his own sounds and his own language. The guest room felt bright again.

Just then, the door made a tiny creak. Aunt Bea peeped in. “Hi, Timo,” she whispered. “I heard a lovely hum.”

Timo’s cheeks felt warm, but he took a slow breath. “I am dancing,” he said. “I felt shy. The music stopped. I made my own beat. Tap, clap, hum.” He pointed to the neat list on his notepad.

Aunt Bea smiled. “That is smart and brave,” she said. “May I watch?”

Timo nodded. “Yes,” he said softly. “Please.”

He showed Aunt Bea his steps. “This is happy.” Big arm circles. “This is shy.” Small steps and a peek. “This is brave.” Tall stand and long reach. He tapped, he clapped, he hummed. The ribbons fluttered when he twirled, whoosh, whoosh. The soft rug tickled his toes. The mirror showed a turtle who was trying, learning, and smiling.

Aunt Bea clapped. “Wonderful! You used your feelings and your words. You organized your space. You made your own music. You danced your story.”

Timo felt a warm glow in his chest. “I did it,” he said. He looked at the D-A-N-C-E sign and read it again. “Dance means moving with feelings,” he said proudly. “Dance can use music, or sounds, or words. Dance can be me.”

He wrote a new sign for the wall: TAP, CLAP, HUM. He put it next to DANCE. He lined up the ribbons again, red, blue, yellow. He folded the notepad shut and set it on the bed.

Aunt Bea asked, “What did you learn today?”

Timo smiled. “When I feel shy, I can breathe and say my feeling. I can organize my space. I can use what I have. I can dance my feelings. Dancing helps my heart feel brave.”

They turned the radio back on, and the soft music came back—dum, da-dum, dum. Timo and Aunt Bea danced together, slow and sweet, step, step, slide. The guest room felt like a happy hug.

The End.
