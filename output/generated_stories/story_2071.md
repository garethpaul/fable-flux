# Fawn’s First Nap Time Plan

In a comfortable nursery, soft and warm like a sunlit blanket, a gentle deer fawn named Fawn blinked her big brown eyes. The room smelled like clean cotton and a little bit like sweet apples. A clock went tick-tock, tick-tock. A mobile with many colors spun slowly, tiny paper stars rustling, swish-swish.

Today was nap time, but nap time felt hard.

Fawn’s teacher, Miss Raya, smiled. “Nap time, little friends,” she said softly. “Find a cozy spot.”

Friends from all around the world lay on their mats. Mina hummed a lullaby from her family’s home, a soft tune that went “la-la-lee.” Jun patted his pillow twice—tap tap—just like his grandpa taught him. Ama tucked a bright kente cloth square over her toes. Sami set a small blue scarf beside his cheek, smelling like lavender.

Fawn looked at her mat. It was plain and new. She sniffed. It smelled like soap, not like home. Her ears drooped.

“I don’t know how to rest here,” Fawn whispered to herself. Her little heart went thump-thump.

She tried to copy Mina’s hum. “La-la—” But her voice felt wobbly. She tried Jun’s tap tap. It sounded too loud. She tried to tuck like Ama, but she had no cloth. She tried Sami’s sniff, but the mat did not smell like lavender. Fawn’s eyes filled with tiny tears.

Miss Raya knelt beside her. “Fawn, what do you need?” she asked in a calm voice.

Fawn took a breath in, then out. She remembered what Mama Deer said: “When you feel small, use your small voice to ask a small brave question.” Fawn pressed her hoof to her chest. “I need help,” she whispered. Then she tried again, a little louder. “I need help, please.”

“That is strong self-advocacy,” Miss Raya said. “Let’s make a nap plan that is just right for you.”

Fawn blinked. “A plan for me?”

“Yes,” Miss Raya smiled. “You can help make it. You know yourself best.”

Fawn looked around. She listened to Mina’s song, soft like a feather. She watched Jun’s careful taps, steady and sure. She felt the warm room, the quiet breaths of friends, and the gentle swish-swish of the mobile.

Fawn thought, I can make a plan. I can help myself.

“What helps you at home?” Miss Raya asked.

Fawn pictured her nest of leaves. She remembered the soft light of afternoon, the smell of pine, and Mama’s warm hum that went “mmm-mmm.” She remembered a tiny smooth stick she liked to hold.

“I like a little light,” Fawn said. “And something soft to hold. And a hum.”

Miss Raya nodded. “We have a night-light lamp. It makes a tiny glow like a firefly.” She clicked it on. The lamp made a gentle, golden dot. Fawn’s ears perked up.

“We also have a basket of comfort things,” said Miss Raya. She set a basket down. It held small blankets and soft toys from many places—bright patterns, silky squares, a knitted rainbow, and a little pillow with a moon stitched on.

Fawn touched a tiny green blanket with leaf shapes. It was smooth and cool. “This feels like leaves,” she said. She smiled a small smile.

“What about a sound?” Miss Raya asked. “Would you like a hum, or a soft song?”

Fawn looked at Mina, who smiled back. “In my home, we sing this,” Mina offered. She hummed her tune, la-la-lee, like a soft river.

Fawn listened. “I like that,” Fawn said. “But I also like ‘mmm-mmm.’ Can I try my own hum?”

“Yes,” Miss Raya said. “You can hum for yourself. That is self-reliance.”

Fawn lay down on her mat. She tucked the green leaf blanket under her chin. She held a small, smooth wooden ring from the basket, warm in her hoof. She breathed in. The room smelled like clean cotton, apples, and a little pine from the wooden ring. She breathed out. The tiny lamp glowed, like a friendly star.

“Do you want to set your own steps?” Miss Raya asked.

Fawn nodded. “Step one: I take three slow breaths. Step two: I hum ‘mmm-mmm’ very quiet. Step three: I close my eyes, and I think of trees.”

Miss Raya whispered, “That is a strong plan.”

Fawn took three slow breaths. In… and out. In… and out. In… and out. She hummed, “mmm-mmm,” a little buzz in her chest. She pictured tall trees, green and kind. Leaves rustled, hush-hush. A bird sang one tiny note, peep.

Around the room, friends followed their own ways. Jun tapped, tap tap. Ama tucked her bright cloth. Sami sniffed his lavender scarf. Mina hummed la-la-lee. Everyone was different, and that was good. Their ways were like many colors on the same soft blanket.

Fawn felt her body grow warm and heavy. “My plan works,” she whispered. She smiled. Her eyes fluttered closed like butterfly wings. The world felt gentle and safe. Tick-tock, tick-tock, went the clock.

When nap time ended, Miss Raya rang a tiny bell, ding. Fawn stretched her legs and yawned. “I did it!” she said, standing tall.

“You helped yourself,” Miss Raya said. “You asked for what you needed, and you made a plan.”

Mina grinned. “I liked your ‘mmm-mmm.’”

“I liked your la-la-lee,” Fawn said. “We all have our own ways.”

Fawn’s heart felt big and bright. She learned that she could speak up, make a plan, and rest, even in a new place. She learned that friends from many homes do things in many ways, and each way can be right.

That day, Fawn carried her plan in her mind like a little light. If a problem came, she knew she could try steps, ask for help, and trust herself.

The lesson was clear: I can help myself, and I can say what I need.

The End.
