# Owl Hoots and Calm Breaths

On a warm evening, a wise old owl named Olwyn perched on a flat rooftop. The shingles felt warm and smooth under her claws. The sky was soft orange, and the air smelled like fresh bread from the bakery below. Olwyn blinked her big gold eyes and listened. She loved the rooftop because she could see the whole town and hear tiny sounds—the flap of a moth, the clink of dishes, and the hush of the wind.

“Tonight,” Olwyn hooted softly, “I have a goal. I will teach one friend how to stay calm.”

Just then, a little fox named Finley scampered up the stairs to the rooftop. His fur was bright and fluffy, but his face was tight and red. His paws stomped. Thump, thump, thump.

“I’m so mad!” Finley yelled. “I was building a block tower with my friend Tia. It fell down. She laughed. I shouted. Now I feel hot and prickly!”

Olwyn tilted her head. “I hear your big feelings,” she said kindly. “Your body feels hot and prickly. Your paws stomp. Your voice is loud.”

Finley nodded. “Yes! My tummy is twisty, too.”

Olwyn puffed her feathers in thought. The cool night breeze touched their cheeks. “I can help,” she said. “Tonight my goal is to teach calm. Would you like to learn?”

Finley sniffed. “Okay,” he said in a small voice. “I don’t like feeling angry.”

“Anger is a feeling,” Olwyn said. “Feelings come and go, like wind in the trees. We can learn what to do with them.”

Olwyn spread one wing. “Step one,” she said, “is to pause.”

Finley copied her and spread a tiny paw. “Pause,” he repeated.

Olwyn closed her eyes and took a slow breath. “In through the nose, out through the beak.” She breathed in—sniff, sniff—the warm scent of bread and the sweet smell of rooftop flowers. She breathed out—hoo, hoo—soft as a cloud.

“Let’s try five calm breaths together,” Olwyn said. “That is our first goal. Ready?”

Finley nodded. Together they breathed:
In—sniff—out—hoo.
In—sniff—out—hoo.
In—sniff—out—hoo.
In—sniff—out—hoo.
In—sniff—out—hoo.

Finley’s shoulders dropped. His face softened. “I feel cooler,” he said. “My tummy is less twisty.”

“Good,” Olwyn smiled. “Step two is to name the feeling. Can you tell me what you feel?”

“I feel angry,” said Finley. “I felt hurt when Tia laughed.”

Olwyn nodded. “You named it. That helps your brain feel safe.”

“Step three,” Olwyn said, tapping the roof gently with her talon, “is to choose a tool. We can squeeze a soft thing, count five, take calm breaths, or say, ‘I need a break.’ Which tool will you pick?”

Finley looked around and saw a little rooftop cushion. He squeezed it. Squish. “I like squeezing,” he said. “And breathing, and counting.”

Olwyn clacked her beak once. “Perfect. Now for step four: set a small goal to help others. When we help, our hearts grow calm.”

Finley’s ears perked up. “Help? But I was mad.”

“Helping can heal,” Olwyn said. “Your goal could be: ‘I will say how I feel and invite Tia to build again.’”

Finley gulped. “That sounds big.”

“We make big goals small,” said Olwyn. She blinked slowly. “Try this: One, take three calm breaths. Two, tell Tia, ‘I felt angry when you laughed.’ Three, ask, ‘Can we try again together?’ That’s three small steps.”

Finley took a breath. The breeze brushed his whiskers. “I can do three steps,” he said. “Three is not too big.”

Olwyn smiled. “Let us practice. I will be Tia.”

Finley stood tall. “Breaths first,” he whispered. He breathed in—sniff—out—hoo, three times. Then he said, “Tia, I felt angry when you laughed. Can we try again together?”

Olwyn, in a gentle voice, answered, “I am sorry, Finley. I did not mean to hurt you. Yes, let’s try again.”

Finley’s eyes shone. “That felt good.”

“Your body learned a calm plan,” Olwyn said. “Anger did not drive. You did.”

They both listened. In the street, a bell dinged on a bicycle. Far away, a dog barked once and then went quiet. The stars began to peek out like tiny silver seeds.

“I want to help others too,” said Finley. “Maybe other friends feel angry sometimes.”

Olwyn ruffled her feathers. “That is a kind goal. How would you help?”

Finley thought. “I can teach them the four steps: pause, name the feeling, choose a tool, and set a small helping goal.”

Olwyn hooted with joy. “You have become a helper!”

The next day, Finley went to see Tia. He felt a bubble of worry, but he remembered his plan. He paused. He breathed—sniff, hoo—three times. He said, “Tia, I felt angry when you laughed. Can we try again together?”

Tia’s ears lowered. “I’m sorry,” she said. “I didn’t know. Let’s build a new tower.”

They stacked blocks—red, blue, and yellow. Clack, clack. Finley showed Tia the calm breaths. Tia giggled, but this time it was a happy giggle. “Sniff, hoo!” they said together.

The tower wobbled and fell. Crash! Finley felt heat rise, but he paused. He took a breath. He squeezed a soft block. “It’s okay,” he said. “Let’s try again.”

They tried again. And again. Soon they had a tall, strong tower. The sun felt warm on their fur. They high-fived. Smack!

That evening, Finley climbed back to the rooftop. “Olwyn!” he called. “My plan worked. I helped Tia, and we built a tower!”

Olwyn’s eyes twinkled. “You set a goal, you used tools, and you helped a friend. That is anger management. That is kindness.”

Finley curled beside Olwyn. The night smelled like flowers and bread. The stars looked like tiny lamps. Together they breathed slow and calm. Hoo—hoo.

The clear lesson: When anger comes, pause, name it, choose a calm tool, and take small steps to help. You can feel big feelings and still make kind choices.

The End.
