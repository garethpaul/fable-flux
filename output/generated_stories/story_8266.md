# Pebble Letters on the Rooftop

Pip was a cheerful penguin with bright eyes and a happy wiggle. One sunny morning, Pip climbed the stairs to the rooftop. The roof felt warm under Pip’s little feet. The breeze was gentle and smelled like fresh toast from the café below. Pigeons cooed, “Coo-coo,” and a fluffy cloud looked like a giant marshmallow.

Pip spread out a tiny blanket and a notebook. “Today I will write,” Pip said with a smile. “I want to write my family story.”

Pip opened the notebook. The first page was big and white. Pip held a crayon. It was blue and smelled like blueberries.

But Pip’s wing made a wobbly line. “Oh!” Pip frowned. “My letters look like noodles.” Pip tried again. The letters were bumpy and crooked. Pip’s tummy felt tight.

A pigeon named Pippa landed beside Pip. “Coo-coo! What are you doing?” she asked.

“I am writing my story,” said Pip. “It is about our family tradition. Every year we share stories on the first snow. But my letters are not ready.”

Pippa tilted her head. “Coo! Letters take practice. We can learn together. We can make it fun!”

Pip blinked. “How?”

Pippa pecked at a shiny pebble. “We can write with pebbles!” The rooftop had many smooth pebbles near the plants. They clicked softly when they rolled.

Pip clapped. “That sounds fun! Pebble letters!”

They began with the letter P for Pip. Pip picked up pebbles. Click, click. He set them in a round belly with a little stick, like this: P. The pebbles felt cool and smooth. “P for Pip! P for Pippa! P for penguin!” Pip giggled.

“Coo-coo! Good job,” said Pippa. “Now try I.” Pip made a straight line. Tap, tap. “I is like a little icicle,” Pip said, proud.

But when Pip tried to make a B, the pebbles rolled away. “Oh no! My B is a blob.” Pip’s beak drooped.

Pippa fluffed her wings. “Let’s make a plan. First, we make a path with tape to hold the pebbles. Then we place them slowly. One. Two. Three.”

Pip nodded. “That is a good plan.” This was negotiation. They talked. They agreed. They made steps.

They taped a gentle curve. Pip placed pebbles along the curve. Pat, pat, pat. The B began to bloom. “It works!” Pip cheered. The wind whispered, and the sun warmed Pip’s back.

A neighbor cat named Miso peeked over the ledge. “Mrrrow? What is this?” Her whiskers twitched.

“We are writing,” said Pip. “We are learning our letters with pebbles. We are sharing our family tradition.”

Miso purred. “Writing sounds cozy. Can I help?”

Pip thought. “Yes! You can help by holding the tape.”

Miso held the tape with her paw. Pippa brought more pebbles. Pip shaped the letters and said them out loud. “P-I-P. That spells Pip!” He smiled so big his cheeks felt warm.

Pip took a deep breath. “I want to write my story now.” He opened the notebook again. The paper smelled clean. He held the blue crayon. “I will go slow. I will talk as I write.”

He spoke each word: “My. Name. Is. Pip.” He wrote slowly. Some letters were still wiggly. That was okay. Pip was learning.

Pippa read the words. “Coo! I can read it! Keep going.”

Pip wrote more, with help. “We have a tradition. On first snow, we share stories. We sip warm fish soup. We hum a soft song.” Pip could almost taste the soup. He could hear the gentle humming. He could see Grandma Penguin’s scarf, red like a berry.

Miso sniffed. “Mmm. Fish soup. Your story makes my tummy sing.”

A little cloud covered the sun for a moment. The rooftop felt cooler. Pip hugged himself and kept writing. “Today I write on the rooftop with friends. We use pebbles to learn. We help each other.”

Pip stopped and looked at the page. The lines were not perfect. Some letters were big. Some letters were small. But the story was true. It felt warm, like a hug.

Pip looked at Pippa and Miso. “Thank you for helping me solve my problem,” he said. “I could not make my letters before. Now I can try and try.”

Pippa bobbed her head. “Coo! You used initiative. You asked for help. You made a plan.”

Miso flicked her tail. “And you shared a tradition. You taught us your song.”

Pip smiled, and sang the soft family song. The breeze carried the tune. The pebbles sparkled. The notebook pages fluttered like little wings.

At last, Pip wrote the last line: “I will share my story on the first snow.” Pip put a tiny heart at the end. Heart dots felt happy.

Pip closed the notebook and traced the pebble letters with a gentle wing. “Writing is practice,” Pip said. “Writing is sharing. Writing lets my heart talk.”

Pippa cooed. “And learning is a journey.”

Miso purred. “Together is the best way.”

They sat on the warm rooftop, listening to the city hum. Pip took a deep breath of toasty air. He felt proud, calm, and brave.

The lesson was clear in Pip’s heart: When a problem feels big, make a plan, ask for help, and keep trying. Writing helps us tell our stories and share our traditions with friends.

The End.
