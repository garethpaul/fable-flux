# Owl Oliver and the Painty Problem

The creative art room was bright and busy. Sunlight danced on jars of colorful paint. Paper smelled like fresh trees. Little hands made soft swish-swish sounds with brushes. In the corner, a big friendly owl named Oliver watched with wise, warm eyes.

Oliver was a wise old owl. His feathers were soft and speckled, like tiny snowflakes. He blinked slowly and smiled with his eyes. He loved helping children make art and make good choices.

Today, two friends, Mia and Theo, both wanted the same sparkly silver paint. The jar sat in the middle of the table, shining like a tiny moon.

“I want the silver for my robot,” Mia said. Her voice wobbled a little. She hugged her paper close. “My robot needs a shiny belly.”

“I want the silver for my rocket,” Theo said. His eyebrows scrunched. “My rocket needs a shiny nose.”

Mia and Theo both reached out. Their fingers touched the same jar. Clink!

They froze. Their cheeks turned pink. The room felt tight and quiet.

Oliver fluttered down to the table. His wings made a soft whoosh. He looked at Mia. He looked at Theo. Then he spoke in a gentle, low voice.

“Whoo-hoo,” said Oliver. “We have a paint problem. When we both want one thing, what can we do?”

Mia sighed. “I don’t know.”

Theo looked at the floor. “I don’t know either.”

Oliver nodded. “This is a good time for calm words. Let’s use our wise owl steps. Step one: Take a breath.” He breathed in slowly. Mia and Theo copied—inhale, exhale. The air smelled like glue and oranges from snack time.

“Step two,” said Oliver, “use kind words. Say what you need.”

Mia said softly, “I need the silver paint for my robot’s belly.”

Theo said, “I need the silver paint for my rocket’s nose.”

Oliver smiled with his eyes. “Good. Step three: Think of ideas. We can share. We can take turns. We can trade. We can find another way. Let’s volunteer to help each other.”

Mia blinked. “Volunteer?”

“It means we choose to help,” said Oliver. “Helping makes hearts feel light.”

Theo’s shoulders relaxed. “I can help Mia paint her robot belly.”

Mia nodded. “And I can help Theo with his rocket nose.”

Oliver clapped his wings softly. “Wonderful! Now step four: Decide together. That is negotiation. We talk and pick a plan.”

Theo looked at the silver jar. “We can set a timer. Mia can go first for two minutes. I will help. Then I go for two minutes. Mia will help me.”

Mia smiled. “Yes! Two minutes each. Help each other.”

Oliver set a tiny sand timer on the table. The sand whispered down like sugar. “Plan made,” he hooted. “You both decided. That’s good decision-making.”

Mia dipped a small brush into the silver paint. It felt cool and smooth. Swish, swish. Theo held the paper still for her. “I like your robot belly,” he said. “It looks like it can jiggle.”

Mia giggled. “Thank you for helping, Theo.”

The timer’s last grain of sand dropped. “My turn,” Theo said. He dipped his brush. The silver glittered in the light. Mia held his paper. “Careful on the tip,” she whispered. “Make it pointy, like a carrot.”

Theo laughed. “A rocket carrot!” He painted slowly. Swish. Swish.

Soon, the timer emptied again. Both paintings sparkled. Mia’s robot had a shiny belly. Theo’s rocket had a shiny nose. The air felt easy and happy again.

Oliver tilted his head. “How do your hearts feel now?”

“Warm,” Mia said, putting her hand on her chest.

“Light,” Theo said, smiling wide.

“Tell me,” Oliver asked, “what helped the most?”

Mia counted on her fingers. “Breathing. Kind words. Sharing. Helping.”

Theo added, “Making a plan together. Taking turns.”

Oliver nodded. “That is conflict-resolution. You solved a problem with calm and care. You volunteered to help. You used negotiation to make a fair plan. You made a good decision together.”

Mia and Theo looked around. Other children were painting too. A new friend, Lina, held a brush and frowned. “I can’t reach the big paper,” she said.

Mia raised her hand. “I’ll volunteer to help!” She moved the paper closer for Lina.

Theo said, “I’ll hold the tape while you stick the corners.”

Lina smiled. “Thank you. This feels better.”

Oliver watched, his eyes shining. “When we help,” he said softly, “the room shines brighter than silver paint.”

The children kept painting. The room tick-tocked with happy sounds: brush swish, gentle chatter, tiny clinks of jars. The sparkly jar sat in the middle, shared and safe.

Before clean-up, Mia turned to Theo. “Next time, if we both want the same thing, what will we do?”

Theo answered, “We will breathe, use kind words, think of ideas, volunteer to help, and make a fair plan.”

Oliver gave a proud hoot. “Wise owls, all of you.”

The End.
