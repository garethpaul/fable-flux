# Benny Beaver’s Honest Day

Benny was a busy beaver with bright, bucky teeth and a warm, wiggly tail. He lived near a sunny backyard where the grass felt soft, the flowers smelled sweet, and the birds sang, “Cheep, cheep!” in the tall trees. The sunshine made everything glow. Benny loved to work and play outside. He loved to build, dig, and write little notes with his short pencil tucked behind his ear.

One bright morning, Benny tip-tapped his paws on the warm deck and looked around the backyard. “Today,” he said, “I will help. I will do my best and tell the truth.” He sniffed the air. It smelled like fresh cut grass and strawberries. He heard a breeze go whoosh and felt it ruffle his whiskers.

Benny’s friend Mia, a small robin, flapped down. “Good morning, Benny! What are you doing today?”

Benny smiled. “Hi, Mia! I’m making garden signs. I will write the names of the plants so everyone knows what grows. I want to be helpful.”

Mia hopped and chirped. “Cheep! I like that.” She pecked gently at a smooth wooden sign. “This one can say ‘Tomatoes.’”

Benny nodded. He took out his pencil and carefully wrote big, round letters: TOMATOES. He said each sound slowly. “To-ma-toes.” He drew a tiny red tomato with a green top. He blew on the sign. “All done!”

Mia trilled, “That looks great!” The sun warmed Benny’s fur. He felt proud. He felt strong. He felt honest.

Next, Benny found a sign for carrots. The dirt smelled rich and earthy. Worms wiggled below. Benny smiled and wrote, “CARROTS,” then drew an orange carrot with a leafy hat. He planted the sign by the carrot patch.

Just then, Tilly, a curious turtle, peeked out from under a leafy bush. “Hello, Benny,” Tilly said in a slow, kind voice. “I am looking for my shiny red button. Have you seen it?”

Benny looked left and right. He did not see a button. “No, Tilly. I will look with you,” he said. They searched under the watering can. Clink! They searched by the sand box. Crunch! They searched near the strawberry plants. Sniff, sniff. No button.

While they looked, Benny saw another wooden sign. He picked it up and read the letters. It said “PEAS.” But the plants there were long and twisty beans. Benny frowned. His tummy felt tight. “This sign is wrong,” he said softly. “It says peas, but these are beans.”

Mia fluttered in a small circle. “Oh dear. Should we leave it? Should we pretend we did not see it?”

Benny pressed his paws together. The sun was bright and hot; sweat tickled his brow. He thought about being quick. He thought about being busy. He thought about doing what was right.

“No,” he said. “I will fix it. I must tell the truth. These are beans.”

Benny took a deep breath. He erased the word “PEAS.” The eraser smelled like pink rubber and made a squeaky sound. He wrote, “BEANS,” careful and clear. Then he drew a long, green bean. He stood up tall. “Now it’s right.”

At that moment, Tilly called, “I found my button!” It glowed red in the sunshine, tucked between two stones. “Thank you for helping me look.”

Benny clapped his paws. “Hooray, Tilly!”

Mia tilted her head. “Benny, you could have left the sign. No one would know. Why did you fix it?”

Benny touched the new sign. “Because it is true. If the sign is wrong, someone might get mixed up. They might think beans are peas. That would not be kind. Integrity means telling the truth and doing the right thing, even when it is hard, even when no one is looking.”

Mia chirped softly. “Integrity,” she repeated. “I like that word.”

Benny smiled. “Me too. It makes my heart feel warm.”

They kept working. Benny wrote more signs: “LETTUCE,” “SUNFLOWERS,” and “MINT.” He smelled the mint and smiled at the cool, sweet scent. He felt the rough wood under his paws. He listened to bees buzz by the big yellow flowers: bzz-bzz-bzz.

Soon, a breeze puffed, and one of Benny’s papers flew away. “Oh!” he said. He chased it across the bright lawn. The grass brushed his ankles. “Come back, paper!” He laughed and grabbed it near the sandbox.

When he stood up, he saw a little scribble on the deck. A small crayon lay nearby. It looked like someone drew a zigzag on the clean wood. Benny’s cheeks went warm. He had used that crayon earlier to practice drawing a flower. He had made the scribble by mistake.

Mia peered at the mark. “Who did that?”

Benny took a breath. The wind tickled his ears. “I did,” he said. “I made the mark. I will clean it.” He picked up a cloth and some water. Swish, swish. He scrubbed. The water felt cool on his paws. The crayon mark faded, then disappeared.

Tilly smiled. “That was honest, Benny.”

Benny nodded. “Integrity means telling the truth, fixing mistakes, and caring for others.”

By the time the sun dipped a little, the backyard looked neat and clear. Signs stood tall. Plants looked happy. Birds sang sweet songs. Tilly wore her shiny red button. Mia danced in the air.

Benny felt calm and proud. He put his pencil back behind his ear. “Today was a good day,” he said.

Mia chirped, “You worked hard. You told the truth. You helped.”

Benny’s eyes shone. “Yes. When we tell the truth and do what is right, we help the whole backyard.”

He patted the bean sign one more time. “Beans,” he read, with a smile.

Then they shared cool mint leaves and watched the golden light wiggle through the leaves. The air smelled fresh. The grass felt soft. Their hearts felt light.

The clear lesson of the day was simple: Integrity means telling the truth, fixing mistakes, and doing what is right, even when no one is watching.

The End.
