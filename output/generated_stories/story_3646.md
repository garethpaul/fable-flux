# Tippy Toes in the Dining Room

In a sunny house with shiny floors, a gentle elephant calf named Emi stood in the dining room. The table smelled like warm banana bread. The chairs were smooth and cool. The curtains swished softly when the window was open. Emi’s big ears wiggled as she listened.

Today was a special day. It was Family Dance Time.

“A little drum beat makes my feet move!” said Papa Elephant, tapping a wooden spoon on a bowl. Tap-tap, tap-tap.

Mama Elephant clapped softly. “Let’s dance together.”

Emi loved music. She loved the tap-tap and the clap-clap. But her heart felt wiggly today. Emi’s toes wanted to dance, but her tummy felt tight. Her tail swished nervously.

“I want to try,” Emi whispered, “but I feel… funny.” Her voice was small, like a tiny bell.

“What kind of funny?” asked Mama, kneeling so her eyes were level with Emi’s. Her trunk was warm and kind.

Emi looked at the big table, the tall chairs, the shiny floor. She felt the cool air on her skin, and the smell of bananas made her mouth water. She put her trunk on her chest. “My tummy feels twisty. My feet feel heavy,” she said.

Papa nodded. “Those are important feelings,” he said. “Thank you for telling us.”

Emi took a breath. In. Out. The drum beats kept going. Tap-tap, tap-tap.

Cousin Zuzu, a cheerful zebra, bounced into the dining room. “Let’s spin! Let’s dip! Whee!” Zuzu’s hooves clicked on the floor like tiny raindrops.

Emi smiled a little, but when Zuzu reached out to pull her into a big spin, Emi stepped back. Her ears warmed. She didn’t want to be pulled.

Emi held up her trunk like a stop sign. “Please wait,” she said. Her voice shook, but she used her brave words. “My body needs space.”

Zuzu froze, then nodded fast. “Okay, Emi. Thank you for telling me,” Zuzu said, and took a gentle step back.

Emi’s shoulders dropped. She felt a small bubble of calm. Mama smiled. “That was strong and kind,” Mama said. “You know your body.”

Emi took another breath. In. Out. She listened to her feelings like they were soft music. Tap-tap. Clap-clap. Her heart said, I want to dance my way.

“I can try my own steps,” Emi said. “Slow steps. Small steps.”

Papa lowered the spoon and tapped more softly. Tap… tap… tap. The sound was like a heartbeat.

Emi swayed from side to side. Her feet slid on the smooth floor. Shhh… shhh… Her ears fluttered. Her trunk traced tiny circles in the air. She made a quiet dance. It felt safe. It felt like her.

Zuzu watched and whispered, “That looks calm. Can I copy your small steps?”

Emi nodded. “Yes, if you stay right there,” she said, pointing to a spot a little bit away. “I need space.”

“I can do that,” Zuzu said, smiling. She moved in her own small circle. Click… click… click.

Mama and Papa followed with little claps and careful toes. Clap… clap… tip… toe. The dining room filled with soft sounds and happy eyes. The banana bread smell was warm and sweet. The curtains moved like a friendly wave.

Then Auntie Bee came in with a round bowl of salad. “I love big jumps!” she sang, and hopped once. The bowl wobbled. The lettuce rustled, rustle-rustle, like leaves in wind.

Emi felt the twist come back. Her ears drooped. “I don’t like big jumps near me,” she said quickly. “It scares me.”

Auntie Bee stopped and put the bowl on the table. “Thank you for telling me, sweet Emi,” she said. “I will make my dance small.”

Emi’s body loosened again. She smiled. “Thank you,” she said.

Papa handed Emi the wooden spoon. “Would you like to tap the beat?” he asked.

Emi held the spoon. It felt smooth and warm in her trunk. She tapped the edge of a plate very gently. Ting… ting… ting. The sound was soft like a whispering star.

“I’m the beat helper,” Emi said. “I can start and stop.”

Everyone looked at Emi and waited. Emi stood tall. “Start,” she said. Ting… ting… ting. Feet moved softly. Ears wiggled. Smiles grew.

“Stop,” Emi said, and she held up her trunk. The room went quiet. Even the curtains seemed to listen.

Emi giggled. She felt bright and brave. She could feel her feelings, and she could say her needs. She could dance her own way. She could help the family dance in a way that felt safe for everyone.

After a few starts and stops, Mama cut the banana bread. The warm smell floated around the room. Emi took a bite. It was soft and sweet. Her tummy felt warm now, not twisty.

Mama hugged Emi gently. “I’m proud of you,” she said. “You listened to your feelings.”

Papa nodded. “You used your words to set your space,” he said.

Zuzu bumped hooves with Emi from a safe little distance. “And you made a great dance,” Zuzu added.

Emi blinked happily. “When I feel twisty, I can stop and breathe,” she said. “I can say what I need. I can dance in a way that is just right for me.”

Everyone cheered quietly. Clap… clap… clap.

Emi tapped the spoon one more time. Ting. “I’m ready for a tiny spin,” she said. She twirled once, slow and easy, her trunk swishing like a ribbon. Safe. Happy. Proud.

The End.
