# Benny Beaver Breathes and Builds

Benny was a busy beaver with bright, curious eyes and a flat, paddle tail. He lived with his family in a cozy apartment. On the sunny balcony, Benny had a little garden with tiny trees, mint leaves, soft moss, and a shallow tub of water that went plip-plop when drops fell. He loved his balcony garden. It smelled like fresh earth and minty green. Bees hummed nearby, and the city sounds whispered below.

Every morning, Benny said, “Good morning, garden! I’m ready to build.” He liked to stack little sticks along the edge of the water tub, just like a small dam. He wanted it to be neat and strong.

One day, a gust of wind whooshed by. Whoosh! The wind knocked Benny’s careful sticks. Clatter-clack! The tiny dam fell down.

“Oh no,” Benny said. His tummy felt twisty. His whiskers drooped. “I worked so hard.”

Benny’s mama peeked through the sliding door. “Hi, Benny. I see you’re busy. How do you feel?”

“I feel wobbly inside,” Benny said. “My dam fell.”

Mama nodded. “That’s a big feeling.” She came out and sat on a little stool. “Let’s try a quiet moment.”

Benny wiggled. “But I want to fix it now!”

Mama smiled. “We will. First, we breathe. Breathing helps your body feel calm and ready. Watch me.”

Mama put her paw on her belly. “In through the nose, like smelling mint.” She sniffed slowly. “Out through the mouth, like blowing on a bubble.” She blew softly. “Ahh.”

Benny sniffed the mint leaves. They smelled cool and sweet. He put his paw on his belly. In. Out. In. Out. He listened to the bees hum and the leaves rustle. His shoulders felt softer. The twisty feeling got smaller.

“How do you feel now?” Mama asked.

“Quieter,” Benny said. He smiled a little. “Like the water when it stops splashing.”

“Wonderful,” Mama said. “When we are calm, we can think and try again. That’s called persistence—keep trying, little by little.”

Benny nodded. “I can try again.”

He picked up a stick. It felt smooth and a little damp. He set it down gently. Tap. He listened. Tap. He watched. Still. He added a pebble. Plunk. He took one slow breath between each piece.

“In,” Benny whispered. “Out.”

A small bird with bright eyes landed on the railing. “Cheep! What are you doing?” the bird chirped.

“I’m building a tiny dam,” Benny said. “And I’m breathing to help me focus.”

“Cheep-cheep! That sounds smart,” said the bird. “Can I watch?”

“Yes,” Benny said. “But quietly, please. I’m listening to my breath and to the garden.”

Benny continued. Stick. Breath. Pebble. Breath. He felt the sun warm on his fur. He smelled soil and mint. He heard city cars far away and a bee buzzing close. Buzzzz. He noticed everything, one thing at a time, like little dots on a page.

Suddenly, a big truck rumbled on the street below. Rrrr-rrrr! The balcony shook a tiny bit. One stick wobbled and rolled away. Clack!

Benny’s paws froze. His heart beat faster. “I want to shout,” he said, “but I will breathe first.”

He closed his eyes. In. Smell the mint. Out. Blow a bubble. In. Out.

When he opened his eyes, he felt steady again. “I can fix it,” he said. “I can try again.”

The bird cheeped quietly, “That is persistence.”

Benny carefully picked up the wobbly stick. He found a better spot, lower and snug. Tap. This time, it stayed.

After a while, the tiny dam looked neat and cozy. Water made a soft shhh sound as it touched the sticks. Benny felt proud, like his chest had sunshine inside.

Mama stepped out again. “I see slow breaths. I see careful paws. I see trying again and again.” She smiled. “That’s mindfulness and persistence.”

“What is mindfulness?” the little bird asked.

Mama said, “Mindfulness is paying attention to right now, with kind eyes and kind breathing. We notice our body, our breath, and our world. Then we choose what to do next.”

Benny nodded. “Mindfulness helped me build. And it helped my big feelings get small.”

“Would you like to tell the story of your building?” Mama asked.

Benny’s eyes sparkled. He loved stories. He stood tall and spoke in a clear voice, like a tiny storyteller.

“Once there was a busy beaver,” he began, “on a balcony garden that smelled like mint. A windy whoosh knocked his dam. He felt wobbly. He breathed in and out—smell the mint, blow a bubble—and his body got calm. He listened to bees and trucks and his own breath. He tried again, one little piece at a time. When a stick rolled away, he breathed again and kept going. In the end, the dam was strong, and his heart felt sunny.”

The bird clapped its wings. “Cheep! Cheep! What a good story.”

Mama hugged Benny. “You used mindfulness. You told a story. You kept trying. That is how we learn.”

Benny patted his tiny dam. “Thank you, breath. Thank you, garden. I can use mindfulness every day.”

He took one more calm breath. In. Out. The garden hummed, the sun warmed his fur, and the water whispered shhh.

Benny smiled. “I am ready for my next little step.”

The End.
