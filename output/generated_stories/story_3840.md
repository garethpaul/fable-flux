# Tiko the Tortoise Saves the Drips

In a bright, clean bathroom with shiny tiles, a wise tortoise named Tiko lived in a cozy basket by the tub. The bathroom smelled like lemon soap. Sunlight made tiny rainbows on the mirror. Drip, drip, drip went the faucet, making soft music.

Tiko blinked his gentle eyes. He loved quiet mornings. He listened to the water sounds. Drip, drip. Then he noticed a big puddle growing. The sink was dripping fast!

“Oh my,” Tiko said in his slow, calm voice. “That is a lot of drips. Water is precious. We should not waste it.”

From the toothbrush cup came a tiny squeak. A green toothbrush with soft bristles wiggled. “Hi! I’m Buzzy Brush,” it chirped. “I like the drips! They tickle my handle.”

Tiko smiled kindly. “Hello, Buzzy. The drips may feel fun, but they use up water. Water helps plants, animals, and people. We must take care of it.”

Buzzy Brush tilted. “I didn’t know that. I just like to buzz and swish and make foam!”

From the bath ledge, a fluffy blue towel called out, “I’m Tulla Towel! I don’t like puddles. They make my corners soggy.” Tulla shook and little drops flew up and went splat on the tiles.

“Friends,” Tiko said, “let’s work together. We can stop the drips and keep the bathroom happy.”

Buzzy buzzed with worry. “But I am small. I can’t turn the big, round faucet.”

Tulla sighed. “I am soft, not strong. I can’t twist it either.”

Tiko nodded. “We are all different. That is okay. Different is good. We can help in our own ways.”

He took a slow step. Tap. Tap. Tap. His shell made a gentle clack on the tiles. The floor felt cool on his feet. He could smell minty toothpaste in the air.

Tiko tried to push the faucet with his front foot. “Hmm,” he hummed. “It is very tight.”

A little rubber duck on the rim of the tub squeaked, “Quack! I can cheer you on! Go, Tiko! Go!”

“Thank you,” Tiko said, smiling. “Cheering is helping too.”

Tiko looked around. He had an idea. “Buzzy, can you show me where the handle is smooth? My foot slips.”

Buzzy leaned close. “Here! The bumpy part! It feels grippy. Zzz!”

“Good eyes, Buzzy,” said Tiko. “Tulla, may I use your corner like a soft glove? That will help me hold the handle without slipping.”

Tulla brightened. “Yes! Wrap me around your foot!”

Tiko gently wrapped one of Tulla’s corners around his front foot. The towel felt warm and fuzzy. He placed his foot on the bumpy part of the faucet. He pushed. The handle squeaked. Eeeeee. Drip. Drip. Dr—

“Almost!” Buzzy sang. “You can do it!”

“Quack-quack! You are strong!” squeaked the duck.

Tiko took a deep breath. He pushed again, slow and steady. Squeeeeak… click!

The dripping stopped. The bathroom became quiet. A tiny last drop fell into the sink. Plink!

“We did it,” Tiko said softly. He felt proud, and his heart felt warm. “We saved water, together.”

Buzzy brushed happy circles in the cup. “Friend team! Friend team!”

Tulla fluffed. “My corners are dry now. Thank you, Tiko.”

The rubber duck bobbed. “Quack! Hooray!”

Just then, a little child peeked into the bathroom. The child had bright eyes and small feet in fuzzy socks. “Hi, Tiko,” the child whispered, patting Tiko’s shell gently. “Thanks for stopping the drip.”

Tiko smiled up. “Hello, friend. Remember to turn off the tap tight after you wash. Use only what you need. That is being kind to the Earth.”

The child nodded. “I can do that! And I can sing the scrubbing song while I wash my hands.”

“Good idea,” Tiko said. “Let’s sing together. Scrub fronts, scrub backs, scrub between, and thumbs. Then turn the tap off—no more drips—so water wisely hums.”

They sang in soft voices. The bathroom felt warm and happy. The mirror sparkled. The lemon soap smelled sweet.

Later that day, Tiko saw a tiny ant near the drain. The ant looked worried. “The puddle is gone,” the ant said. “I came for a drink.”

Tiko thought for a moment. “We can still share water without waste,” he said kindly. He nodded to the child, who filled a small bottle cap with just a little water and set it safely on the counter, away from the sink. The ant took tiny sips. “Thank you,” said the ant, and marched away smiling.

Buzzy buzzed softly. “I learned something new. Friends help each other. We listen. We try. We save water.”

Tulla added, “We are all different: hard shell, soft towel, tiny brush, squeaky duck, and a little child. But together, we make a great team.”

Tiko nodded. “That is true. Friendship means caring, sharing, and solving problems side by side.”

He looked at the shiny faucet. No more drips. The bathroom was peaceful. Everyone felt safe and proud.

“Good job, friends,” Tiko said. “Today, we were gentle, smart, and kind to the Earth.”

The End.
