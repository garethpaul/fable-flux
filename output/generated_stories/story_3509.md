# Clover Writes in the Laundry Room

Clover was a gentle deer fawn with soft, speckled fur and bright, curious eyes. One rainy afternoon, Clover visited Nana Doe’s house. Pitter-patter, the rain tapped on the windows. The warm laundry room hummed and whooshed. “Whoosh, whirr,” sang the washing machine. “Hummmm,” purred the dryer. The air smelled like clean soap and cotton.

Clover peeked inside the laundry room. “Nana, may I write a story here?” Clover asked, holding a small notebook and a thick blue crayon.

“Yes, little one,” Nana Doe said with a smile. “You can sit on the soft mat. Remember: look with your eyes, not your hooves. The machines are for grown-ups.”

Clover nodded. “I will be safe,” Clover said.

Clover sat on a fluffy rug by a basket of tiny socks. The socks were warm and smelled like sunshine. Clover opened the notebook. “I will write a story about puddles!” Clover said. The crayon felt big and a little slippery. Scratch, scratch, it went on the paper.

Clover wrote a big wiggly C. Then the crayon wobbled and made a squiggle. “Oh,” said Clover. “That is not how I wanted it.” Clover’s ears drooped a little.

Nana Doe folded a soft towel. “It’s okay to feel wiggly,” she said. “Take a slow breath. Try again.”

Clover put a hoof on the notebook and breathed in slowly. In… and out… The washing machine went whoosh, like a giant breath too. Clover smiled. “I can try again.”

Clover wrote an L and an O. The O looked like a potato. “My O looks funny,” Clover said, feeling a tiny pinch of frustration.

Nana Doe pointed to a picture on the wall. It showed a big red circle with a line: no touching hot things. “When we feel frustrated, we stop, we look, we listen,” Nana Doe said. “That is a safety rule for feelings too.”

“Stop, look, listen,” Clover repeated softly. “Stop my hoof. Look at my crayon. Listen to my breath.” Clover listened: whoosh, hum, pitter-patter. The sounds were like a calm song.

Clover tried the O again. Round and slow. “Better!” Clover cheered.

Suddenly, the dryer beeped. Beep-beep! Clover jumped. The dryer door had a shiny handle. It looked like a silver moon. Clover reached out a little hoof.

Nana Doe gently touched Clover’s shoulder. “Pause, Clover,” she said kindly. “Is the dryer safe for little hooves?”

Clover pulled the hoof back. “No,” Clover said. “The dryer can be hot. I look with my eyes, not my hooves.”

“Good choice,” Nana Doe said. “You kept your body safe.”

Clover felt proud and wrote the next letters: V and E. Clover giggled. “I wrote CLOVE! I need an R.” The R was tricky. The crayon slipped and made a squiggle again. Clover’s cheeks felt warm. “I can’t do it,” Clover whispered.

Nana Doe knelt on the rug. She gave Clover a cool sip of water. “Let’s use our calm plan,” she said. “One: Put the crayon down. Two: Shake out your hooves. Three: Take three breaths. Four: Try a smaller R.”

Clover put the crayon down. The blue tip tapped the paper. Tap. Clover shook tiny hooves. Wiggle-wiggle. Clover took three slow breaths. In… out… In… out… In… out… The hum of the dryer sounded like a sleepy bumblebee.

Clover picked up the crayon and drew a small R, one little line and a tiny bump. “I did it!” Clover shouted. The sound bounced around the room and made the socks wiggle.

Nana Doe clapped softly. “You stayed safe, and you kept trying.”

Clover wanted to decorate the page. The shiny laundry bottles on the shelf had bright colors: green, yellow, pink. They looked like paints. Clover’s eyes grew wide. “Maybe I can make dots,” Clover said, reaching toward the lowest bottle.

Nana Doe shook her head gently. “Those are not for little ones,” she said. “Laundry soap can hurt eyes and tummies. We leave bottles on the shelf.”

Clover pulled back. “I will use stickers,” Clover said. Clover opened a small sticker sheet from the notebook pocket—stars and puddles and little umbrellas. Peel, press. Peel, press. “Safe and fun,” Clover said.

Clover read the page aloud: “Clover jumps in puddles.” The letters were not perfect, but they were brave. Clover drew a big blue puddle with soft ripples. The crayon made a soft shh-shh sound.

Beep! The washer stopped. Nana Doe opened it with a potholder. Steam puffed up—warm and white like a cloud. “I use tools and care,” Nana Doe said. “That keeps me safe.”

Clover nodded. “I watch with my eyes,” Clover said. “I sit on the mat. I keep my hooves to myself.”

“Exactly,” Nana Doe said. “And when you feel frustrated, you breathe and try again.”

Clover put the finished page on the basket so Nana could see. “I wrote my story!” Clover beamed.

Nana Doe placed a gold star sticker on the corner. “You wrote safely. You coped with big feelings. You made good choices.”

Clover’s tail flicked with joy. The rain outside slowed to a gentle drip, drip. The laundry room smelled sweet and warm. Clover tucked the notebook under a foreleg. “Safety first, calm heart, and keep writing,” Clover said.

Nana Doe smiled. “That is a wonderful lesson.”

Clover nuzzled Nana’s shoulder. “Tomorrow, I will write about rainbows,” Clover said, “but I will still look with my eyes, not my hooves.”

“Tomorrow will be bright,” Nana Doe said.

Clover pranced toward the doorway, careful and light, feeling proud, calm, and safe.

The End.
