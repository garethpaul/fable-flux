# Poppy the Parrot’s Cozy Closet Club

Poppy the parrot loved colors, songs, and making new things. Her feathers were bright green and blue, and her beak clicked when she thought. Click, click! She lived with Nana Rosa, who had a big wooden closet that smelled like clean soap and warm lavender.

Inside that closet was Poppy’s hideaway. Soft scarves hung like rainbows. Shoe boxes made little seats. A flashlight glowed like a tiny sun. It was quiet and cozy. “Welcome to the Cozy Closet Club!” Poppy chirped, flapping happily.

Every afternoon, Poppy had a routine. Wash, brush, tidy, and then create. She sang her routine like a song:
“Wash my wings,
Brush my beak,
Tidy things,
Create each week!”

One rainy day, Poppy wanted to make a Friendship Flag to hang in the closet. She thought of Nana Rosa’s story about a flag her family used for special days. “We waved it and sang together,” Nana had said, smiling. “It was our tradition.”

Poppy puffed up with excitement. “I can make a new flag and share the tradition!” she squeaked. She laid out shiny ribbons, soft socks, and safe stickers from her craft box. But when she tried to tie the ribbons, they slipped. When she stuck the stickers, they went crooked. Her wings felt sticky. Her beak got glue on it. “Oh no,” Poppy squawked. “This is hard!”

She dropped her head. A little tear prickled. The cozy closet didn’t feel so cozy now. “Maybe I can’t do it,” she whispered.

Just then, Nana Rosa peeked in. She smelled like warm tea and oranges. “Hola, Poppy,” she said gently. “I hear a quiet closet. What’s happening?”

Poppy sniffed. “I wanted to make a Friendship Flag. But I messed up. The ribbons won’t listen. The stickers are crooked. My beak is sticky. I can’t do it.”

Nana Rosa sat on a box seat and patted a scarf. “Come sit,” she said. “You can learn. We say, ‘I can’t do it… yet.’”

“Yet?” Poppy tilted her head. Click, click.

“Sí,” Nana smiled. “Yet means you are still learning. We keep trying. We grow. That is our family tradition too.”

Poppy’s eyes brightened. “I can’t do it… yet,” she repeated softly. The words felt warm, like the flashlight sun.

Nana Rosa pointed to the little sink. “First, our routine. Wash and brush. Clean helps calm the brain.”

Poppy nodded. She ran warm water. Splish, splash! She washed her wings with gentle soap, bubbles popping like tiny kisses. She brushed her beak, smooth and shiny. “Brush, brush!” she sang. She wiped the closet shelf with a soft cloth. Swipe, swipe! The air smelled fresh and lemony.

“I feel better,” Poppy said. Her chest felt light.

“Now we try small steps,” Nana Rosa said. “One ribbon at a time.”

Poppy took a deep breath. In, out. “Step one,” she chirped. She chose a red ribbon. It was silky and cool. She looped it slowly around a stick. It slipped—but only a little. “Oops!” She tried again. Loop, tuck, pull. “I did it!” she cheered.

“Step two,” said Nana. “Sticker time.”

Poppy pressed a star sticker. It leaned sideways. She frowned, then smiled. “It’s a dancing star,” she giggled. She pressed another, this time using two careful claws. Press, press. Straight!

“Step three,” Nana Rosa said. “Ask for help if you need it. Helpfulness is a strong wing.”

Poppy nodded. “Nana, can you hold the stick while I tie?”

“Of course,” Nana said. Together, they tied blue, yellow, and green ribbons. The colors swished softly. Swish, swish. Poppy’s heart swelled.

They added a soft sock triangle with safe fabric glue. It smelled sweet and sticky. Poppy hummed her routine song as they worked. “Wash my wings, brush my beak, tidy things, create each week!”

Soon, the flag was done. It was wiggly, colorful, and full of dancing stars. Poppy stroked the fabric. It felt bumpy and proud.

“Now we share the tradition,” Nana Rosa said. She taught Poppy a simple family song. “La la la, we try and grow. La la la, together we go.”

Poppy sang with her, bright and clear. “La la la!” The closet felt cozy again—warmer than before, filled with love and the soft rustle of ribbons.

They hung the Friendship Flag in the closet hideaway. The flashlight made the stickers sparkle like tiny moons. Poppy flapped her wings. “I did it! I learned! I grew!”

Nana Rosa hugged her gently. “You kept trying. You used your routine. You asked for help. You shared a tradition. That is a strong mind.”

Poppy stood tall. “Today, I learned the power of yet,” she said. “When things are hard, I can wash, brush, tidy, and try again. I can ask for help. I can share and grow.”

Nana Rosa smiled. “Sí, little parrot. We grow every day.”

Poppy clicked her beak, happy. Click, click. She looked at the flag and whispered, “I can do hard things… yet.” Then louder, with a grin, “And soon!”

The closet hideaway glowed with color, song, and the sweet scent of clean. Poppy felt brave, helpful, and ready for tomorrow’s routine—and tomorrow’s try.

The End.
