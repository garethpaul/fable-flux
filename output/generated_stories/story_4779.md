# Koala Kori and the Backyard Words

In a sunny backyard, under a warm blue sky, lived a calm koala named Kori. Kori loved to sit in a comfy hammock between two trees and listen. She listened to the wind say whoosh, the bees say buzz, and the leaves say shh-shh. Kori also loved words. She whispered them and tasted them like sweet leaves. “Hello,” she said softly, rolling the sound like a friend’s hand. “Thank you,” she said, smiling with her eyes. “Please,” she said, making the word gentle, like a soft pillow.

Kori’s friends came to play every morning. There was Pip the playful puppy, Mia the chatty mouse, and Tito the tiny turtle. The backyard felt warm on their fur and shells. The grass tickled their toes. The flowers smelled like lemon and honey.

One bright day, Kori brought a new idea. “Let’s make a word garden,” she said. “We can plant words in the air and help them grow.”

“How do words grow?” asked Pip, ears up.

“With care,” said Kori. “We water them with kindness. We give them sun with smiles. We let them rest with quiet.”

Mia clapped her paws. “I know many words! Fast words, slow words, long words, short words.”

Tito peeked out. “I like small words,” he said. “Hi. Bye. Yes.”

They started. Kori tied colorful ribbons on a small clothesline between the trees. She wrote simple words on little leaf-shaped cards. “Hello. Please. Thank you. Share. Wait. Sorry.” She set out tiny clothespins. The sun warmed the cards, and the breeze made them flutter. The leaves danced with soft shh-shh sounds.

Pip ran in circles, tail wagging. “I’ll hang them!” he barked, zooming. He grabbed a bunch of cards in his mouth.

“Careful, Pip,” said Kori, calm and kind. “Slow helps words grow.”

But Pip zipped too fast. The cards fell in the grass. One card got a muddy paw print. Another card hid under a flower.

“Oh no,” Pip whined. “I didn’t mean to.”

“It’s okay,” said Kori. She put her paw on his paw. “We can fix it together.”

Mia squeaked, “I’ll find the hiding word!” She sniffed and pulled a card from under a marigold. It said “Sorry.”

Tito moved slowly to the muddy card. He looked closely. “This one says ‘Please,’” he said. “But it is messy.”

Kori took a deep breath. She closed her eyes and listened. The wind whooshed. The birds chirped. Her heart felt calm, like still water. “We can wash the ‘Please,’” she said. “And, Pip, we can try a new way.”

Pip’s ears drooped. “I was too fast,” he said, honest and small. “I’m sorry.”

Mia nodded. “Let’s plant the ‘Sorry’ word first!”

Kori smiled. “Great idea.” She hung the “Sorry” card in the middle. The sun lit it up. Pip looked at it and took another deep breath. “Sorry,” he said again, slower. The word felt warm.

Kori patted his head. “Thank you for telling the truth,” she said. “That is honesty. Honest words help trust grow.”

“Can we make a helper?” Tito asked. “So the cards won’t fall?”

Kori’s eyes sparkled. “Yes! That is innovation. We can make a gentle word hanger.” She gathered smooth sticks and soft yarn from the craft box. Mia tied tiny knots. Tito held the sticks steady with his shell. Pip brought clothespins one by one, walking slowly.

They made a little ladder of sticks and yarn. It swayed softly but stayed strong. Kori hung the words up again. “Hello. Please. Thank you. Share. Wait. Sorry.” The words waved like friendly flags. The air smelled like fresh wood and flowers.

“Let’s try the words,” Kori said. “We can listen to how they feel.”

Pip wagged. “Hello, Mia,” he barked, gentle. His voice sounded like sunshine. Mia’s whiskers twitched.

“Please pass the blue ribbon,” Mia said to Tito. Her voice was light, like a butterfly.

Tito smiled. “Thank you,” he said to Mia. His words were slow and sweet, like syrup. He looked at Kori. “I like this. Words can be soft.”

Kori nodded. “Every word has a feeling. We can choose peaceful words. Our words are like songs in the air.” She closed her eyes and hummed. The word cards fluttered and made tiny tapping sounds as they touched the clothespins.

The friends played a game. When the wind blew, they picked a word that matched the breeze. When the sun warmed their backs, they chose a bright word. When the clouds drifted by, they picked a quiet word.

Then Mia spoke very fast. “I know a brand-new word! Super-zip-zip-hurry!” She giggled and hung an empty card, scribbling her word.

Pip bounced. “I like that!”

But when Pip tried “Super-zip-zip-hurry,” he bumped the ladder again. A card slipped. Pip froze. His eyes went wide.

Kori steadied the ladder. She took a breath. “Let’s ask a question,” she said softly. “Does this new word help us be gentle?”

Mia looked down. “Maybe it makes us too fast,” she said, honest. “I’m sorry, Kori.”

Kori hugged Mia. “Thank you for being honest. We can forgive mistakes. We learn together.”

Pip raised his paw. “Can we keep the new word, but use it for running races only?”

Kori smiled. “That is a smart plan. Words can have homes. ‘Super-zip-zip-hurry’ can live in the race game. ‘Please’ and ‘Wait’ can live here in the word garden.”

They tried again. Pip said, “Please.” He moved slow and sweet. Mia said, “Wait,” and counted, “One, two,” in a soft voice. Tito said, “Thank you,” and the word sounded like a hug.

At last, the little ladder held all the cards. The wind sighed. The flowers glowed. The backyard felt calm and bright. Kori’s heart felt full, like a warm blanket.

Kori gathered her friends. “Today we learned to love words,” she said. “We used honesty to tell the truth. We used forgiveness to fix mistakes. And with innovation, we made a new way. The best words help us be kind.”

Pip pressed his paw to his chest. “I will use gentle words.”

Mia nodded. “I will listen to how words feel.”

Tito smiled. “I will take my time.”

Kori whispered, “Our words can grow like a garden.” They all listened to the soft shh-shh of the leaves and felt the sunshine on their faces.

The End.
