# Ellie Elephant Says Sorry

In a comfortable nursery with sun-yellow curtains, soft rugs, and shelves of bright books, a gentle elephant calf named Ellie liked to play. The room smelled like crayons and clean soap. The window was open, and a warm breeze tickled the hanging paper stars. Ellie’s big ears flapped happily as she hummed a little tune.

On the round play table, children from many places sat together. Mai stacked wooden blocks. Noor brushed a doll’s hair with tiny pink bows. Theo rolled a toy bus that went click-click. A globe sat in the corner, blue and green, and Ellie liked to tap it gently and whisper, “Hello, world.”

“Let’s build a tall tower,” Ellie said, her voice soft like a lullaby.

“Yes!” cheered Mai. “We can share the blocks.”

Ellie carefully picked up a block with her trunk. The wood felt smooth and cool. Clink, clink, clink. The tower grew taller and taller. Ellie felt proud. Her heart did a happy thump-thump.

Then Ellie saw her favorite shiny block, the one with a little bell inside. She reached out quick—too quick. Her trunk bumped the tower. Oh no!

Crash!

The tower tumbled down. Blocks skittered across the table. The bell block went ding-a-ling as it rolled to the floor.

Mai’s eyes filled with tears. “My tower,” she whispered.

Noor hugged her doll close. Theo stopped his bus. The room felt too quiet. Ellie’s tummy felt squirmy, like butterflies. She wished she could pick the blocks up fast and put the tower back together. She wished she could make the sadness go away.

Ellie stared at her feet. “I didn’t mean to,” she whispered. “I didn’t mean to.”

Teacher Asha walked over. She wore a scarf with tiny stars, and she smelled like orange peels. She knelt down so her eyes were kind and level. “I see a big crash,” she said softly. “What happened?”

Ellie’s ears drooped. Her cheeks felt hot. She wanted to hide under the rug. She wanted to pretend nothing happened. But Teacher Asha’s voice was gentle, and Mai’s tears made Ellie’s heart feel heavy.

Ellie took a deep breath. In and out. She remembered the globe. She remembered how they all shared the nursery, like a little world. In their little world, being fair and kind mattered.

“I bumped the tower,” Ellie said. Her voice shook, but she kept talking. “I reached too fast. I’m sorry, Mai. I am truly, truly sorry.”

The room was quiet again, but it felt softer now. Mai sniffled. “You are sorry?”

Ellie nodded. “Yes. I want to make it fair. I can help fix it. I can wait my turn for the shiny block. We can share.”

Teacher Asha smiled. “That was a brave apology, Ellie. You said what happened. You said you are sorry. You said how you will help. That is fair.”

Ellie’s tummy stopped squirming. Her ears perked up a little.

Mai wiped her eyes with the back of her hand. “Thank you for saying sorry,” she said. “It was an accident. Let’s build again. We can make it even taller.”

Ellie’s smile grew. “Together,” she said.

The children scooped up the blocks. Clack, clack, clack. The sound was friendly. Noor lined up small squares. Theo steadied the base. Ellie watched their hands, waited her turn, and lifted each block slowly, carefully. Tap. Tap. Tap. She listened to the gentle sounds of play and to her own breath, slow and steady.

As the tower rose, Teacher Asha brought the globe to the table. She spun it gently. The globe whooshed, a soft windy sound. “Look,” she said. “Children all around the world build, share, and sometimes make mistakes. And all around the world, we can say sorry and make it right.”

Ellie touched the globe with her trunk, very light. “Hello, world,” she whispered again. “I am learning.”

When the tower was as tall as Ellie’s nose, they all stepped back. It wobbled a little, then stood strong.

“We did it!” Theo cheered.

“It is beautiful,” Noor breathed.

“It is fair,” Mai said. “We all had a turn.”

Ellie’s heart felt warm, like sunshine through the window. She looked at Mai. “Thank you for forgiving me,” she said.

Mai smiled. “Thank you for saying sorry.”

Teacher Asha clapped softly. “What did we learn today?”

Ellie lifted her head. “When I make a mistake, I can say sorry. I can help fix it. I can be fair and share.”

“And that,” Teacher Asha said, “makes our little world a kinder place.”

Ellie rang the shiny bell block—just once—ding! Everyone giggled. The sound was bright and happy. The paper stars swayed. The warm breeze smelled sweet, like cookies from a faraway kitchen.

Ellie tucked the shiny block into the middle of the tower, where it would not fall. She felt calm and strong. She felt like a good friend.

In their comfortable nursery, under sun-yellow curtains, Ellie and her friends kept playing, sharing, and caring—together.

The End.
