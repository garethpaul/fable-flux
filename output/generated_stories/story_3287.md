# Owl Hoots for Himself

In a cozy, comfortable nursery, soft lamps glowed like tiny moons. A fluffy rug felt warm under little feet. Shelves held picture books and gentle toys. The air smelled like clean cotton and cocoa from snack time.

High on the windowsill sat Otis, a wise old owl. His feathers were soft gray and brown, like warm toast with honey. His golden eyes blinked kindly. He loved to watch over the nursery and help the little ones learn.

Morning music chimed. Children giggled and played. Blocks clicked. Crayons rolled. A small breeze ruffled the curtains with a whisper, shhh.

Otis fluffed his feathers and smiled. “Good morning, room,” he hooted softly. “Today we learn to use our brave voices.”

Lulu the lamb tugged a blanket. Max the monkey banged two drums. Nora the night-light glowed brighter and brighter. Otis listened. He heard a tiny sigh.

On the rug, Pip the penguin toy rocked side to side. He wanted to draw a sun. Next to him, Tilly the turtle toy held the fat yellow crayon. Pip reached for it. Tilly pulled it close.

“Can I please use the yellow?” Pip whispered. His voice was small, like a feather.

Tilly didn’t hear. The room was noisy. Pip’s flippers drooped. He looked at the floor.

Otis spread his wings. “Hoo-hoo,” he called gently. “Pip, I hear your quiet wish. Let’s try again with a brave, kind voice.”

Pip looked up. “But I don’t want to be bossy,” he said.

“Being bossy is taking without asking,” said Otis. “Being brave is asking with kind words. We can be kind and strong at the same time.”

Pip took a deep breath. He felt cool air in his beak. His tummy rose and fell. “Okay,” he said.

Otis nodded. “We can use our ‘I’ words. Try this: ‘I want a turn with the yellow. May I have it, please?’”

Pip waddled closer to Tilly. He stood tall for a small toy. “I want a turn with the yellow. May I have it, please?”

Tilly looked up. Her shell made a soft tap on the floor. “Oh!” she said. “I didn’t hear you before. Yes, Pip. I can share. After I finish this circle, it’s your turn.”

Pip smiled. “Thank you.” His heart felt warm, like cocoa in a cup. He waited while Tilly made a careful circle. Then she passed the crayon to Pip. It felt smooth and a little warm from her hand.

Otis hooted happily. “That is assertiveness,” he said. “That means using your voice to say what you need, with respect.”

Across the room, Max the monkey drummed faster and faster. Boom-bap, boom-bap! Lulu the lamb covered her ears. “Too loud,” she bleated softly. “My ears hurt.”

Otis floated down to Lulu. “Your ears matter,” he said. “Your comfort matters. You can take care of you. Let’s use your voice.”

Lulu took a small breath. She walked to Max. “Max,” she said, looking at his eyes, “the drums are too loud for me. Please play softer.”

Max paused. He blinked. “Oh,” he said. “I didn’t know.” He tapped the drum gently. Tap-tap. “Is this better?”

Lulu’s ears relaxed. “Yes,” she said. She gave a woolly smile. “Thank you.”

The room felt calm again. The lamps glowed cozy and warm. Crayons made soft scritches on paper. Someone peeled an orange. The smell was bright and sunny.

Soon it was snack time. A big bowl of apple slices sat on the table. Pip reached for three slices at once. His tummy rumbled.

Otis tilted his head. “Let’s listen to our bodies,” he said. “Self-care is being kind to yourself. It also means noticing when you are hungry and when you are full.”

Pip put one slice back. He held two. “My tummy says two is enough,” he said. He chewed slowly. Crunch, crunch. The sweet juice dripped down his beak. He dabbed it with a napkin.

From the reading corner came a wobbly sound. Nora the night-light flickered. “I feel tired,” she whispered. “I want a quiet space.”

Otis nodded. “You can ask for what you need.”

Nora glowed softly and spoke up. “Friends,” she said, “I feel sleepy. May we have a quiet story?”

The room hushed like a soft blanket. “Yes,” said Tilly. “Yes,” said Max. Pip nodded with a full, happy tummy.

Otis fluttered to the rocking chair. It creaked like a friendly door. He opened a picture book that smelled like paper and ink. “Before we read,” he said, “let’s practice our brave words together.”

The children and toys sat close. The rug felt springy under their knees.

Otis led them in a little rhyme:
- I look with kind eyes.
- I use my strong voice.
- I say what I need.
- I care for my choice.

They repeated the words. Their voices were soft and sure. The nursery felt safe and bright.

Otis smiled. “Today we learned to be assertive,” he said. “We asked with kind words. We listened. We took care of ourselves.”

Pip hugged his drawing with the big yellow sun. Lulu’s ears were happy. Max kept a gentle beat on his drum. Nora glowed steady and calm.

Otis closed the book. “Brave and kind,” he hooted. “That’s you.”

The lamps hummed. The curtains swished. Everyone felt warm, cozy, and proud.

And the wise old owl, with golden eyes shining, whispered, “When you need something, say it clearly and kindly. Your voice matters.”

The End.
