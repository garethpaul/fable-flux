# Koa the Koala Takes the Lead

Koa the koala was calm and cuddly. His soft gray fur felt like a warm blanket. One sunny morning, he padded into the dining room. The room smelled like fresh toast and sweet strawberries. The table was messy. Crumbs sprinkled the wood like tiny stars. Cups were tipped to the side. Napkins were wrinkled on the floor.

Koa blinked his gentle eyes. “Oh,” he whispered. “This looks busy.”

Grandma Wren shuffled in with a teapot that puffed little clouds of steam. “Good morning, Koa,” she sang. “I was going to tidy up, but my wings are tired today.”

Koa’s ears twitched. He heard the clock tick-tock, tick-tock. He felt the smooth chair under his paw. He took a deep breath. Calm in, calm out.

“I can help,” Koa said. His voice was soft, but brave. “I will start. I can do it.”

Grandma Wren smiled. “Thank you, dear. That is kind.”

Koa picked up a napkin. He folded it slowly. Fold. Pat. Fold. Pat. He picked up a cup. He set it upright. Clink. He brushed crumbs into his paw. Sprinkle, sprinkle.

Just then, his friends peeked in. Benny the bunny hopped. Lila the lamb trotted. Tiko the tiny turtle scooted, shell shiny like a button.

“Hi, Koa!” Benny said. “Play time?”

Koa looked at the messy table. He looked at his friends. His calm heart felt a gentle squeeze. He wanted to play. He also wanted to help.

“I started cleaning,” Koa said. “Grandma is tired. I want to finish. I can do this.”

Benny wiggled his nose. “Cleaning is slow,” he said.

Lila sniffed the air. “The strawberries smell yummy,” she said. “Maybe we can eat and play!”

Tiko peered up. “The cups look tippy,” he said. “We could make them safe.”

Koa thought and nodded. “I had an idea,” he said. “I took the first step. That is called taking initiative. It means I start helping without being asked.”

Benny’s ears perked. “You started all by yourself?”

“Yes,” Koa said. “And I would like to finish. But I also want to be with you.”

Lila tapped her hoof. “What if we help together?” she asked. “Then we can play sooner.”

Koa smiled. “That is a good plan,” he said. “We can make a list. Team work time.”

He held up one fuzzy finger. “One: Set the cups straight.”

He held up another. “Two: Fold the napkins.”

Another. “Three: Sweep the crumbs.”

Benny hopped to the cups. “I can do number one!” Clink. Clink. Each cup sat still.

Lila folded napkins. “I can do number two!” Fold. Pat. Fold. Pat. She hummed a song.

Tiko moved slow and steady. “I can sweep crumbs,” he said. His tiny brush went swish, swish, swish.

Koa wiped the table with a warm, damp cloth. The cloth felt squishy and cool. Circles, circles, circles. Soon the wood shone.

Grandma Wren came back with little plates. “Oh my,” she said. Her eyes twinkled. “You started this, Koa, and you all worked together. Thank you.”

Koa’s cheeks felt warm and happy. “We took initiative,” he said. “We helped because we noticed a need.”

Benny nodded. “But I still want to play,” he said.

Koa listened. He understood his friend’s feeling. “We can make a compromise,” Koa said. “A compromise means we find a way that works for everyone.”

“How?” asked Lila.

Koa pointed to the table. “We finish one more thing,” he said. “Then we play a short game right here. After the game, we carry the plates to the sink. Deal?”

Benny thought. Lila thought. Tiko thought. They all smiled. “Deal!” they said.

They finished the last crumbs. Swish. Swish. Then Koa set the plates in a neat line. Grandma placed sweet strawberries on each plate. “A thank-you snack,” she said.

“Let’s play ‘Pass the Plate,’” Koa said. “We pass gently in a circle.”

They passed the plates slowly. Around and around. Giggle. Smile. Careful paws, hooves, and tiny turtle toes. Koa watched to keep the circle calm and safe.

After two circles, Koa clapped softly. “Game done,” he said. “Now we carry the plates to the sink.”

They each carried a plate. Tap, tap, tap on the counter. The kitchen smelled like soap and sunshine.

Grandma Wren hugged them with warm wings. “You saw what needed to be done, Koa,” she said. “You started. You led with love. You listened. You made a fair plan.”

Koa felt the calm glow in his chest. “Taking initiative feels good,” he said. “Helping, understanding, and compromise make our team strong.”

Benny patted Koa’s shoulder. “Next time, I want to start, too,” he said.

Lila nodded. “Me too,” she said. “We can all take turns.”

Tiko smiled slowly. “I will start small,” he said. “Small can still be strong.”

Koa beamed. “Yes,” he said. “Small starts can make big help.”

They sat at the tidy table and shared the sweet strawberries. The dining room felt bright and cozy. The clock ticked softly. Their hearts felt full.

The End.
