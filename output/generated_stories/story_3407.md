# Koala Kori’s Porch Practice

Koala Kori lived in a cozy house with a wide, peaceful front porch. The porch smelled like fresh eucalyptus leaves and warm wood. The morning air felt cool and soft on Kori’s fuzzy cheeks. Birds chirped, leaves whispered, and the porch swing creaked a tiny, friendly song.

Today, Kori wanted to do something new. “I will try to pour my own tea,” Kori said, placing a tiny teapot on the little porch table. “I can do it. I will go slow.”

Kori liked to breathe in and out, calm and steady. “In,” said Kori, taking a deep breath. “Out,” Kori whispered, letting the breath drift like a cloud. The kettle made a gentle hiss. The cup waited with a shiny smile.

Kori’s friend, Cricket Pip, hopped onto the porch step. “Chirp! What are you doing, Kori?” Pip asked.

“I am practicing,” Kori said. “I want to learn. I will be patient.”

Pip tilted his head. “Chirp! I believe in you.”

Kori’s paws were soft and careful. The teapot was warm. “I can do hard things,” Kori told the cup. “I can learn.” Kori lifted the teapot just a little. The lid rattled. A tiny drop slipped onto the saucer. Plip!

“Oh,” Kori said. “A little spill.” Kori felt a wobble inside, like a jelly bean bouncing. “It’s okay,” Kori whispered to the wobble. “I can try again.”

Kori put the teapot down and took another breath. “In. Out.” The porch smelled like mint from the tea. A breeze touched Kori’s ears. The swing hummed along. Pip chirped a gentle beat, like a tiny drum: “Chirp… chirp… you can do it.”

Kori smiled. “Thank you, Pip. I am grateful for your song.”

From the fence, Neighbor Hen, Henny, called, “Bawk-bawk! Good morning! What a brave koala!” Her feathers flicked like soft paintbrushes. “Take your time.”

“I will,” said Kori. “I will be patient with my paws.”

Kori wiped the little drop with a cloth. “Clean and ready,” Kori said. Then Kori tried again. Lift. Tip. The tea fell in a smooth, golden ribbon. Plink-plink-plink. It filled the cup with a nice, cozy sound.

“I did it!” Kori laughed, eyes bright and wide.

Pip clapped his tiny wings. “Chirp-chirp-hooray! Your calm helped you,” he said.

Kori nodded. “I waited. I breathed. I believed in me.”

Henny bobbed her head. “Bawk! That is self-confidence,” she said. “You trusted yourself.”

Kori felt warm and light, like sunshine on fur. “Yes,” Kori said. “I can learn new things.”

Kori took a small sip. The tea tasted soft and sweet. “Mmm,” Kori hummed. “Would you like some, friends?” Kori poured a teeny cup for Pip and a warm one for Henny. The porch filled with happy slurps and little smiles.

After tea, Kori looked at a basket of fruit. There was a round orange, a fuzzy peach, and a shiny apple. “I will try to slice the peach,” Kori said. “Slow and safe.”

Pip hopped closer. “Chirp! Remember your breath.”

Kori held a small, safe butter knife and a wooden board. “In. Out.” The knife made a soft shh-shh sound. The peach opened, juicy and bright. A sweet smell floated into the air.

“I did it again,” Kori said, cheeks full of pride.

Henny fluffed her feathers. “Bawk-bawk! You were patient. Patient paws make neat work!”

Kori giggled and gave each friend a peach slice. The juice ran down Kori’s thumb. It felt cool and sticky. Kori licked it and laughed. “Peachy!”

A cloud slid over the sun, and the porch became soft and shady. Kori looked at the porch swing. “I want to try to swing higher,” Kori said. “But I feel a tiny bit scared.”

Pip patted Kori’s paw. “Chirp. It’s okay to feel that. You can go little by little.”

Kori sat on the swing. The wood was smooth and friendly. “In. Out.” Push. The swing moved a tiny bit. Sway-sway. “I can do it,” Kori said, voice calm like a lullaby. Push. Sway. A little higher. The breeze tickled Kori’s nose.

Kori laughed. “I feel brave!” The swing sang creak-creak, like a kind old song. “Thank you, body,” Kori whispered. “Thank you, breath. Thank you, porch.”

Soon, Kori rested and placed both feet on the floor. The day felt bright again. “Friends,” Kori said, “today I learned something big.”

“What did you learn?” Pip asked, eyes shiny.

Kori counted on gentle fingers. “One: Be patient. Go slow. Two: Be grateful. Say thank you for help and for my breath. Three: Believe in me. I am strong and calm. I can learn.”

Henny nodded. “That is a good lesson for the heart.”

Kori poured a last tiny cup of tea and held it up. “To self-confidence,” Kori said. “To trusting ourselves, even when we are learning.”

Pip chirped, “To patience!”

Henny clucked, “To gratitude!”

They sipped and smiled. The birds sang. The leaves rustled like soft applause. The porch felt peaceful and proud, like a good friend giving a gentle hug.

Kori leaned back and sighed, happy and calm. “I can do hard things, a little bit at a time,” Kori said. “And that makes my heart feel big and brave.”

The End.
