# Roo Finds His Feelings

Roo was a small, determined rabbit with soft gray fur and big listening ears. He lived with his family in a cozy home. Outside their sliding door was a sunny balcony garden. Pots of mint, tomatoes, and bright yellow flowers lined the railing. Bees hummed. Leaves wiggled in the gentle wind. The air smelled like fresh earth and sweet basil.

Today was Planting Day. Roo wanted to plant tiny carrot seeds in a sunny pot. He hopped to the balcony and patted the soil. “I can do it!” he said, puffing his chest. He picked up the watering can. It was almost as big as his belly.

Mama Rabbit came outside and smiled. “Remember, Roo,” she said softly, “the watering can is heavy. Pour slowly.”

Roo nodded fast. “I’m strong! I can do it all by myself.”

He tipped the can. Water rushed out in a big splash. Oh no! The soil turned muddy. The carrot seeds floated up like little boats. Roo’s nose trembled.

“I ruined it,” he whispered. His tummy felt tight, like a knotted string. His ears drooped. His paws squeezed the empty seed packet. His face felt hot. He wanted to cry and also stomp, all at once.

Mama knelt beside him. “I see a furrowed brow and droopy ears,” she said kindly. “What is your feeling telling you?”

Roo blinked. He took a small breath. He tried to look inside, like peeking into a pocket. “I feel mad and sad,” he said. “Mad at the splash. Sad about my seeds.”

“That’s emotional-awareness,” Mama said. “You noticed your feelings and named them. When we name a feeling, it gets easier to tame.”

Roo touched his chest. “Mad and sad,” he repeated. Saying the words made his shoulders relax a little.

A gentle breeze brushed his whiskers. It smelled like lemon thyme. Roo closed his eyes and listened. The wind whispered shhhh. Bees buzzed. A pigeon cooed on the railing.

“I’m listening,” Roo said softly.

Mama nodded. “Would you like help, or would you like to try again with a plan?”

Roo thought. His nose twitched five times. “A plan,” he decided. “First, I will scoop out the extra water. Then I will pat the soil. Then I will sprinkle new seeds. Then I will water slowly.”

“That sounds like critical thinking,” Mama said. “What could help you pour slowly?”

Roo looked at the tools. “A little cup!” he said. “The watering can is too big.”

He found a tiny blue cup with painted stars. It fit nicely in his paw. He also found a small wooden stick. “I can draw lines in the soil,” he said. “Like a map.”

Roo took three calm breaths. In… out. In… out. In… out. His mad-and-sad feelings felt smaller, like clouds moving away.

He scooped the extra water into a bucket. He patted the soil. tap tap tap. He drew three gentle lines with the stick. “Little seed beds,” he whispered.

Mama watched quietly. “I’m right here if you need me,” she said, and she kept her voice soft like warm tea.

Roo opened the seed packet. The carrot seeds were tiny and brown. They tickled his paw. “Hello, little seeds,” he said. “I will take good care of you.”

He sprinkled them like snowflakes. Then he used the tiny cup to pour a small sip of water. drip… drip… drip. The soil turned dark and cozy, but it did not splash.

Roo smiled. “I did it,” he said. His ears stood up tall again.

A ladybug crawled onto the edge of the pot. Roo giggled. “Do you like the new garden bed?” The ladybug opened its wings and then closed them, like a tiny red umbrella.

“I noticed you listened to your body,” Mama said. “You felt tight and hot, and you took breaths. You listened to me and to the wind. You made a plan. That is persistence.”

Roo’s chest felt warm in a good way. “When I feel mad or sad,” he said, “I can name it, breathe, and make a plan.”

“Yes,” Mama said. “And you can also use creative expression to help your feelings.”

Roo’s eyes lit up. He picked up a piece of chalk from the balcony shelf. On a small slate, he drew a bright orange carrot with a big green top. He added a smiling sun and a happy cloud. He drew himself with tall ears and a tiny blue cup.

“I drew my feelings,” Roo said. “Now I feel proud.”

They sat together and listened. The tomato vines rustled. The mint leaves smelled cool and sweet. Roo placed the slate next to the pot.

“Grow well, little seeds,” he whispered. “I will keep listening to you.”

Mama hugged him. “What did you learn today, Roo?”

Roo wiggled his whiskers. “When big feelings splash like water, I can stop, name them, breathe, listen, and try again. I can ask for help. I can make a plan. I can keep going.”

The sun slid behind a soft cloud. Roo felt calm and brave. He hopped once, twice, three times, just for joy.

Inside, he stuck a note on the fridge with a carrot sticker. It said, “Name it. Breathe. Plan. Try.”

Roo looked back at the balcony garden. He could almost hear the seeds resting under the soil, cozy and safe.

“I’m proud of me,” he whispered.

The End.
