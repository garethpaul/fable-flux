# Bear Cub Benny and the Safe Kitchen Helpers

The warm kitchen smelled like cinnamon toast and honey. Sunlight made golden squares on the floor. A kettle hummed softly. Bear Cub Benny wiggled his little paws. “I want to help,” he said. His round ears perked up, and his fluffy tail swished. “Can I do a big chore today?”

Mama Bear smiled and tied a tiny leaf-green apron around him. “Thank you for wanting to help, Benny. Helping is kind,” she said. “But the kitchen has rules to keep us safe.”

Benny nodded. “Safe first,” he repeated. He liked how the word felt. It was like a warm hug.

“Let’s say our safety rules,” said Mama. She held up one fuzzy finger. “Rule one: Ask a grown-up before touching hot or sharp things.” She held up another finger. “Rule two: Use tools the right way.” A third finger. “Rule three: Clean as we go, so we don’t slip.” A fourth. “Rule four: If we don’t know, we stop and ask.”

Benny tapped his chin. “I can do that!”

They looked around the cozy kitchen. The wooden table was dusted with flour. Berries sat in a blue bowl, shiny and sweet. A broom leaned in the corner. The kettle puffed a tiny cloud.

“Today we will make honey-berry toast,” Mama said. “You can wash berries, pat them dry, and help wipe the table.”

Benny puffed out his chest. “Berry washer, table wiper, at your service!”

He climbed onto his step stool. It had little paw prints painted on it. The sink water was cool and splashy. “Swish, swish,” Benny sang as he washed the berries in a colander. The water glittered. He sniffed. “Mmm, they smell like summer.”

“Great job,” said Mama. “Use the towel to pat them dry.”

Benny pressed the soft towel. “Pat, pat, pat,” he said. The berries looked happy and shiny.

Then Benny saw the toaster on the counter. The metal sides were warm. “Can I put the bread in?” he asked, reaching a paw toward the slots.

Mama Bear put her paw over his. “Pause and think,” she said gently. “Is it hot or sharp?”

Benny pulled his paw back. He looked, he listened, he sniffed. “It looks hot,” he said. “I hear a hum. I smell toast.”

“That’s right,” Mama said. “Hot things can burn. What should you do?”

“Ask a grown-up,” Benny said. “Mama, can you do the toaster?”

“Yes,” Mama said. “Thank you for choosing safe.” She pressed the lever. The toaster clicked.

Benny felt proud. His tummy felt calm, not jumpy.

Next, Benny saw a shiny butter knife. It was dull, but it was still a tool. He frowned. “Is this sharp?” he asked.

“Good question,” said Mama. “This knife is not sharp like a big knife, but you still ask before using. You may spread with it while I watch.”

Benny nodded. He took a deep breath and spread honey slowly. “Slide, slide,” he whispered. Sticky sweetness made little golden lines. He licked his lips, but he remembered another rule. “Hands first, snack second,” he said.

Mama laughed softly. “You’re thinking like a safety star.”

Toast popped up—boing! Benny jumped and giggled. “It sounds like a frog!” The toast smelled nutty and warm. Mama used a mitt to lift it. “Hot mitt for hot toast,” she said.

Benny sprinkled berries on top. “Plink, plink.” He made a smiley face with blueberries. “Happy toast!”

They set the plates on the table. Benny folded napkins into little triangles. He felt useful and big.

Then, drip. Plop. A small puddle formed near the sink. Benny’s paw stepped in it. “Oops!” His paw slid a little. He wobbled.

He stopped his body, just like he practiced. “Freeze and check,” he said. He looked down. “There’s water! Someone could slip.”

“What can we do?” Mama asked.

Benny put a paw to his ear. “Clean as we go,” he said. He grabbed a towel and pressed it to the floor. “Squeeze, wipe, wipe.” The towel drank the water. The floor felt dry again. “All safe!”

“You solved the problem,” Mama said. “You kept us safe. I’m grateful for your careful eyes.”

Benny’s cheeks felt warm with gladness. “I like helping,” he said. “Helping and safety go together.”

They ate their toast. Crunch. Sweet honey and juicy berries made Benny hum. “Yum,” he said. “Thank you, Mama, for cooking with me.”

“Thank you, Benny, for your help,” Mama said. “You washed, wiped, asked, and fixed. Those are big skills.”

Benny licked a crumb from his paw and looked around. “What chore next?”

“We can sweep,” Mama said. She handed him the small broom. “Short strokes, slow and steady.”

“Sweep, sweep,” Benny sang, guiding crumbs into a little pile while Mama held the dustpan. He kept his paws away from the pan’s edge. He watched his steps so he didn’t bump the stove.

When they were done, the kitchen felt peaceful and clean. The kettle was quiet. The counters shone. Benny took off his apron and hung it on a hook.

“What did you learn today?” Mama asked.

Benny counted on his paws. “Ask a grown-up for hot or sharp. Use tools right. Clean as we go. If I don’t know, I stop and ask.” He smiled. “Safety first helps me help better.”

Mama gave him a gentle bear hug. “That’s our big lesson. You are a safe, smart helper. I am proud and thankful.”

Benny’s heart felt as cozy as the warm kitchen. He wiggled his ears. “I’m a safety star!”

“Yes,” Mama said. “And tomorrow, we can make soup.”

Benny grinned. “I’ll remember the rules.”

The End.
