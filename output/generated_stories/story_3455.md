# Rooftop Parade of Different Drums

On a sunny afternoon, a gentle elephant calf named Ellie climbed the wide stairs to the rooftop with a soft thump-thump of her feet. The rooftop garden smelled like mint and rain. Wind tickled Ellie’s ears. Colorful flags fluttered—red, blue, yellow, and green. The rooftop was where the neighbors met to drum and dance.

Ellie’s trunk wiggled with excitement. “I want to drum too,” she said in her soft, rumbly voice.

On the rooftop, many friends were there. Mia wore sparkly shoes and tapped-tapped a tiny drum. Kofi had bright beads and a deep, boom-boom drum. Jin wore a sun hat and shook a jingle bell. Sam had a soft scarf to wave. Nana Rosa, the kind neighbor, smiled and clapped. “Welcome, everyone! We are all different, and that makes our music strong.”

Ellie tried to pick up a small drum. It slipped. Thud! “Oh no,” Ellie whispered. Her ears drooped. The drum looked so tiny in her big, careful paws.

Mia giggled and then covered her mouth. “Oops. Sorry. I forgot to use kind giggles.”

Ellie felt a warm sting in her cheeks. The wind felt too strong now. “Maybe I should go home,” she said. Her tummy fluttered like a little bird. She wanted to be honest, but she was afraid.

Nana Rosa stepped close. She smelled like cinnamon tea. “Ellie, tell us how you feel,” Nana Rosa said gently.

Ellie took a slow breath in. She counted one, two. She breathed out. “I feel sad,” she said. “I am big. The drum is small. I am different. Maybe I don’t fit.”

Kofi nodded. “I am different too. My drum is big and loud. Sometimes people say it is ‘too much.’ But my drum has a deep story.” He tapped it—boom, boom—like a heart.

Jin shook their jingle bell—chika-chika. “My bell is small and bright. I like small sounds.”

Mia looked at Ellie. “I’m sorry I giggled,” she said. “I was surprised, not mean. I want to be kind. Can you forgive me?”

Ellie’s ears lifted a little. “Yes,” she said. “Thank you for being honest.”

Nana Rosa clapped once. “We will practice together. We will try, try, try.”

Ellie wiped her eyes with the end of her trunk. Then she sniffed. “Oh!” Nana Rosa said, “Let’s wash hands and trunks before we play. Drums like clean hands.” She brought out a bowl of warm, soapy water. It smelled like oranges.

Everyone lined up. Splash, swish, rub, rinse! Ellie dipped her trunk and paws. The bubbles felt tickly. Jin sang, “Wash, wash, wash, rinse, rinse, rinse,” in a happy tune.

Now the rooftop felt fresh. The flags snapped in the wind. Sun warmed the pots of mint and thyme. Birds peeped from the ledge.

Nana Rosa brought a special drum on a stand. It was wide and steady. “Ellie, this drum is strong like you,” she said.

Ellie touched it gently—dum. She smiled. “It feels good.”

Mia scooted close. “Can you show me your gentle dum?”

Ellie nodded and practiced. Dum… dum-dum. Her trunk moved slow and careful. Kofi listened. “Nice! Try boom with my drum, and dum with yours.” He played boom, and Ellie added dum. Boom-dum. Boom-boom-dum. Soon the sounds stacked like building blocks.

Jin shook the bell—chika-chika—between the beats. Sam waved the scarf in big loops. The scarf swished like a rainbow fish. Everyone moved together—different sounds, different steps. The music felt like a hug.

But a little problem came back. The wind pushed a flag loose. It flapped wild and covered Ellie’s eyes. “Eek!” she squeaked, and her beat wobbled. Mia stopped. “Oh no!”

Ellie took another slow breath. “I feel scared,” she said. “I need help.”

“Thank you for telling us,” Nana Rosa said. She tied the flag again, tight and safe. Ellie shook out her ears. “I want to try again,” she said.

They practiced together. Slow first. Then faster. Boom, dum, chika, clap! Ellie kept the steady dum. She smiled with her whole face. Her tummy felt calm and warm.

Neighbors came to watch. A tiny toddler clapped. A grandpa tapped his cane. The sun slid lower and painted the sky peach and gold.

Nana Rosa raised her hand. “Rooftop Parade!” she called. Everyone marched in a small circle. Each friend took a turn to lead. When it was Ellie’s turn, she said, “Follow my gentle dum.” They did. Big friends, small friends, loud sounds, soft sounds—each one different, all together.

At the end, they bowed. The rooftop filled with happy cheers. Ellie’s heart thumped like a drum, but now it felt brave.

Mia hugged Ellie’s leg. “You led us so well,” she said.

Ellie nodded. “I was honest. I practiced. And I kept clean hands.” She giggled. “We are all different. That makes our music better.”

Nana Rosa smiled. “Yes. Diversity is our drumbeat.”

Ellie looked at her friends, each unique. She smelled mint and soap and warm air. She felt proud and peaceful. “I fit,” she said softly. “We all fit.”

The End.
