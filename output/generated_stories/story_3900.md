# Splash at the Dinner Table

Splash was a playful dolphin with bright eyes and a big, happy smile. Splash loved to flip, twirl, and make tiny bubbles. Today, Splash’s adventure did not happen in the ocean. It happened in a cozy dining room.

The dining room smelled like warm bread and sweet fruit. The table was set with shiny spoons, soft napkins, and plates with little fish patterns. The window let in a warm, golden light. A clock on the wall tick-tick-ticked like a gentle drum.

“Dinner time!” called Mama Dolphin, her voice soft and kind.

Splash zipped in, tail swishing and heart bouncing. “Dinner time! Dinner time! Can I play while I eat?” Splash asked, making a small spin.

Mama smiled and shook her head. “First we do our dinner routine,” she said. “We wash our fins, we sit, we taste our food, and we talk kindly. Then we can play.”

Splash liked to play more than anything. Routine sounded slow. But Splash also liked Mama’s warm smile. “Okay,” Splash said, trying to be brave and calm.

Splash swam—swoosh—to the little sink by the table. The water felt cool and tickly on Splash’s fins. “Swish, swish, swish,” Splash sang as the bubbles popped on the shiny skin.

At the table, Splash sat on a comfy cushion. The chair made a tiny creak. On Splash’s plate were soft carrots, rice shaped like tiny shells, and a small piece of grilled fish that smelled like the ocean after rain. There was also a bright green pea that looked like a little bead, and a cup of water that sparkled.

“First bite together,” Mama said.

Splash took a bite. “Mmm,” Splash hummed. The rice was warm. The carrots were sweet and a little soft.

Knock-knock! There was a gentle tap on the door. It was Tiko, the tiny turtle from next door, with a shy smile. “Hello,” Tiko said. “Can I join?”

Mama opened the door wide. “Yes, come in. We have room for friends.”

Splash waved a fin. “Hi, Tiko! Sit next to me.”

Tiko climbed up slowly, little feet going tap-tap on the chair. “I don’t like peas,” Tiko whispered.

Splash looked at the bright pea on the plate. Splash did not like peas either. A little frown tugged at Splash’s mouth. “I wish I could just play,” Splash grumbled.

Mama heard the grumble. “Splash, remember our routine. Wash, sit, taste, talk kindly,” she said in a soft voice. “Routine helps our bodies and our hearts.”

“But it’s hard,” Splash said. “I just want to flip!”

Mama nodded. “When something is hard, we can use persistence. That means we try, and we try again, and we don’t give up.”

Splash took a deep breath. The breath felt like a warm bubble in the chest. “Okay. I can try.”

Splash touched the pea with the tip of a fin. “Hello, pea,” Splash whispered, making Tiko giggle.

Tiko picked up a tiny pea too. “Hello, pea,” Tiko echoed.

Splash put the pea on the tongue. It felt round and a little cool. “Pop!” it went when Splash bit down. The taste was green and new. Not Splash’s favorite, but not so bad either.

“I did it!” Splash said, eyes wide.

“You did,” Mama cheered. “That’s persistence.”

Tiko smiled and tried a pea too. “Pop!” Tiko said. “I did it too.”

They kept going with the routine. Bite, sip, chat. Bite, sip, chat. The clock went tick-tick-tick. The water was fresh. The rice was cozy and soft in the mouth. Splash told a small story about a bubble that wanted to be a balloon. “The bubble goes up, up, up,” Splash said, moving a fin slow and high.

Tiko laughed. “Then it goes pop!”

Splash felt proud. But then, Splash saw the napkin and forgot. Splash flipped a little in the chair and knocked the water cup. The water tipped. Splash! Water ran across the table like a tiny river.

“Oh no,” Splash gasped. Tiko’s eyes grew big. The table felt cold and wet.

Mama took a calm breath. “We have a routine for spills too,” she said kindly. “We pause, we breathe, we fix.”

Splash pressed fins together. “Pause.” Deep breath in. Deep breath out. The warm bubble feeling came back.

“Now we fix,” Mama said, handing out napkins.

Splash dab-dab-dabbed the water. Tiko dabbed too. They moved slow and steady. The table became dry again, and everyone smiled.

“You solved the problem,” Mama said. “That is conflict-resolution. When something goes wrong, we work together and make it right.”

Splash’s chest felt big and light. “We did it,” Splash said to Tiko.

“We did it,” Tiko echoed, grinning.

Dinner went on with the routine. Wash, sit, taste, talk kindly. Splash tried one more pea. Pop. Tiko tried a tiny carrot. Crunch. They listened to the clock—tick-tick-tick—and to their own happy munch-munch-munch.

At the end, Mama said, “Last step: we clear our plates and say thank you.”

“Thank you, dinner,” Splash said, carrying the plate to the sink.

“Thank you,” Tiko whispered, shy but proud.

Mama hugged them both. “Our routine helps us feel safe, strong, and kind,” she said. “With persistence and teamwork, we can do hard things. And there is always room at our table.”

Splash smiled a big, bright smile. “I still love to flip,” Splash said, “but I love our routine too.”

Tiko nodded. “Me too.”

They all took one more deep breath together. The dining room felt warm and peaceful, like a gentle tide. The clock ticked. The plates shone. Splash’s heart felt steady and happy.

And then, when dinner was done, there was time to play.

The End.
