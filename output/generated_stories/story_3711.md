# Whiskers and the Wobbly Wagon

In a sunny house on Maple Lane, there was a garage workshop. The air smelled like pine wood and warm rubber. Sunlight made little dust sparkles dance. Tools hung on a pegboard: a red hammer, a blue tape measure, and a yellow screwdriver. On the floor sat a kit box with a picture of a little wagon.

A clever cat named Whiskers peeked in. Whiskers had bright green eyes, soft gray fur, and a curious nose that twitched at new things.

“Me-ow! I will build a wagon,” said Whiskers. “I can carry apples. I can give rides to my teddy.”

Whiskers opened the box. Inside were shiny wheels, nuts and bolts, and the flat wooden bed of the wagon. There was also a paper with steps. It said: Step 1, Step 2, Step 3.

Whiskers picked up the hammer. Tap tap! Clink clink! The sounds bounced around the garage. “I don’t need the steps,” said Whiskers. “I am a clever cat.”

Whiskers pushed parts together. The wagon looked almost right. Whiskers purred with pride.

Then the wagon wobbled. Wiggle, wobble, wobble, wobble. The front wheel went crooked and squeaked. Eeee-eeep!

Whiskers’ whiskers drooped. “Oh no,” said Whiskers. “This makes me feel grumpy.” Whiskers felt a tight feeling in the tummy and a hot feeling in the face.

Whiskers tried again. Tap tap! Twist twist! Wiggle, wobble. The wheel fell off and rolled away, round and round, until it bumped a paint can. Bonk!

“I am frustrated,” said Whiskers. “I want to stomp. I want to hiss.” Whiskers took a big breath in. One, two, three. Then out. One, two, three.

Whiskers remembered something kind. “When I feel mad, I can pause. I can use calm paws.”

Whiskers sat on a soft mat. The mat felt warm and a little squishy. “I can ask for help,” Whiskers said softly. “I can follow the steps.”

Just then, Handy Hedgehog waddled in. Handy Hedgehog wore tiny work gloves. “Hello, Whiskers,” he said. “I heard a squeak. Do you need a hand?”

Whiskers nodded. “My wagon is wobbly. I did not follow the steps. I feel frustrated.”

Handy Hedgehog smiled. “It is okay to feel that. Let’s use our calm. Let’s read the directions together.”

Whiskers and Handy Hedgehog spread the paper on the workbench. The paper felt smooth and a little crinkly at the edges. Step 1 said, Put the axle through the holes. Step 2 said, Slide on the wheels. Step 3 said, Tighten nuts, not too tight.

Whiskers tapped the words with a paw. “We can do it,” said Whiskers. “But, um, can we make the handle red?”

Handy Hedgehog thought. “We can make the handle red if we still follow the steps,” he said. “That is a fair deal.”

Whiskers smiled. “Deal!” said Whiskers. This was a good negotiation. Both friends got something they wanted.

They worked together. Clink! The axle slid in. It felt cool and smooth. Roll! The wheels went on. Handy Hedgehog held the wagon steady. Whiskers turned the yellow screwdriver slow and careful. Twist, twist, twist.

“Not too tight,” said Handy Hedgehog. “Listen to the squeak.”

Whiskers listened. The garage was quiet except for the soft twist sound and the tick-tick of the clock. No squeak. No wobble.

Whiskers’ tail swished. “I am doing it,” Whiskers said. “I am following directions. I am feeling calm.”

Then came the handle. Handy Hedgehog opened a small can of red paint. It smelled a little sharp. Whiskers dipped a tiny brush. Swish, swish. The handle turned bright cherry red, shiny like a fresh apple.

They waited. Waiting felt long. “I feel wiggly,” said Whiskers.

Handy Hedgehog nodded. “Waiting can be hard. Let’s count the tick-ticks.” Together they counted to ten. The paint was dry!

Whiskers pulled the wagon. Roll, roll, roll. Smooth and straight. No wiggle. No wobble. The wheels whispered on the floor.

Whiskers placed three apples inside. Thump, thump, thump. The wagon held them all.

“I did it!” Whiskers cheered. “Thank you for helping me calm down and follow the steps.”

Handy Hedgehog patted Whiskers’ shoulder. “You did the hard work,” he said. “You paused when you were mad. You asked for help. You followed directions. And you made a fair deal.”

Whiskers thought about the hot face and tight tummy from before. Now Whiskers felt light and proud, like a kite in the sky.

Whiskers pulled the wagon to the door and called, “Teddy, do you want a ride?”

Teddy did not answer, because Teddy was a stuffed bear, but Whiskers giggled anyway and tucked Teddy in next to the apples. “Let’s go!” said Whiskers.

As the wagon rolled into the sunny yard, Whiskers sang, “Pause and breathe, count to three. Ask for help, then try with me!”

The lesson was clear in Whiskers’ heart:
- When I feel frustrated, I can pause and breathe.
- I can follow directions, step by step.
- I can make deals that are fair, so everyone feels good.

Whiskers winked. “That is how a clever cat builds a wagon,” said Whiskers.

The End.
