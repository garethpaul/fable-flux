# Benny Bunny Bucks Up in the Backseat

Benny Bunny was a small, soft rabbit with bright eyes and long, twitchy ears. He was a determined rabbit. When he said, “I can try!” he really meant it. Today, Benny sat in the family car with Mama, Papa, and his baby sister, Dot.

The sun made the windows warm. The seat felt bouncy. The car smelled like orange slices and crayons. The engine hummed, “Vvvvrrr, vvvvrrr,” like a sleepy cat.

Benny had a big plan. He wanted to build a car-seat garden with his toys. He had carrot blocks, a tiny watering can, three bunny books, a soft blanket, and a red ball. He stacked and stacked. He smiled. “I can do it!”

Dot reached for the red ball. “Ba! Ba!” she giggled.

Benny hugged the ball. “This is for my garden,” he said.

The car turned. The stack wobbled. The red ball rolled away—plop! The books slid—swish! The watering can tipped—clink! The blanket flopped over Benny’s lap. Everything was everywhere.

“Oh no,” Benny whispered. His ears drooped. “My garden is a mess.”

Papa glanced in the mirror. “Looks like a bumpy road and a bumpy moment,” he said gently.

Mama said, “Take a breath, Benny. In… and out…”

Benny breathed in. He smelled orange and a little baby soap. He breathed out and felt his shoulders soften. But his face still felt hot.

“I can’t do it,” Benny said in a small voice.

Mama turned and smiled. “You can’t do it yet,” she said. “That little word ‘yet’ is a helper.”

“Yet?” Benny repeated. He tasted the word. It felt bouncy, like a hop.

Papa said, “Problems are puzzles. Let’s try a plan.”

Benny rubbed his paws together. He liked plans. “I’m a determined rabbit,” he told himself. “I try, I learn, I try again.”

Dot tapped the red ball with her tiny paw. “Ba! Share?” she squeaked.

Benny looked at his sister’s round eyes. He took a deep breath. “Okay, Dot. We can share.” He handed her the red ball. Dot clapped. “Ba-ba!”

Benny felt a warm glow in his chest. Sharing felt good. He noticed the mess again. Toys poked his legs. The seatbelt felt tight. He didn’t like it.

“Mama,” he asked, “how do I stop the wobble?”

“Let’s organize,” Mama said. She handed Benny two small baskets from the door pocket. One smelled like clean plastic; the other like the lemon wipes inside. “One basket for soft things. One for hard things.”

Benny nodded. “Soft in soft, hard in hard,” he said. He picked up the blanket. It was fluffy and warm. “Soft,” he whispered, and tucked it in the soft basket. He picked up the books. Their covers felt smooth and cool. “Hard,” he said, and slid them in the hard basket. The watering can went with the hard things. He found a little sticker sheet and put it in the soft basket so it wouldn’t crinkle.

The car hummed on. “Vvvvrrr.”

Benny looked at his carrot blocks. “These are hard,” he said. He put them in the hard basket and clicked the lid. Click! It felt safe. The basket didn’t wobble when the car turned.

He smiled. “I did it a little.”

Papa said, “You solved part of the puzzle. What’s next?”

Benny’s ears perked up. “I want a tiny garden that can’t fall.”

He peeked at the seat pocket. It was snug and stretchy. He slid one book in the pocket. It fit! He tucked one carrot block there too, just one, and left the rest in the basket. He put the soft blanket over his lap like a gentle cloud.

Dot bounced the red ball on her knees. “Bip! Bip!” She laughed and held it out. “Share?”

Benny took a small bounce and gave it back. “We can take turns,” he said. “When you’re done, it’s my turn.”

“Done!” Dot said, and after a few bounces, she passed it to Benny. They smiled.

The car hit a little bump. “Thump!” Benny watched. Nothing spilled. The baskets stayed put. The pocket held tight. His tiny garden stood: one carrot block, one book, and his blanket like soft grass.

“I did it!” Benny cheered. His cheeks felt cool now, and his heart felt big.

Mama clapped softly. “You tried, you learned, you tried again.”

Papa said, “That’s a growth mindset, Benny.”

“Grow…th?” Benny said slowly.

“It means your brain grows when you practice,” Mama said. “You get better by trying, not by being perfect right away.”

Benny nodded. “I can’t do it… yet,” he said with a giggle. He looked at Dot. “And we can share.”

Dot patted the hard basket. “Neat!” she said.

Benny sat tall. He felt organized. He felt kind. He felt determined. The car hummed along. The orange smell was sweet. The sunlight made little dots on the seat like tiny stars.

Benny whispered to the humming car, “Problems are puzzles. I can try, learn, and try again.” He tucked his blanket, passed the ball back to Dot, and looked at his tiny, tidy garden. It made his heart feel calm.

When they reached home, Papa opened the door. Benny lifted the baskets with careful paws. “Soft in soft, hard in hard,” he sang.

Mama smiled. “What did you learn today?”

Benny grinned. “I learned to share, to organize, and to use my yet.”

Mama hugged him. “That’s our determined rabbit.”

Benny’s ears made a happy wiggle. “I can do hard things… with practice.”

The End.
