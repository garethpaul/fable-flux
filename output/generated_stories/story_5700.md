# Ellie’s Clean Trunk and Colorful Letters

Ellie was a gentle elephant calf with soft, flappy ears and a tiny, wiggly trunk. Today was art day! She stepped into the creative art room. The room smelled like fresh paper and lemony soap. Sunlight made the paint jars sparkle—red, blue, yellow, and green.

“Good morning, Ellie,” said Ms. Dot, the art teacher. “Today we will paint and write our names.”

Ellie’s eyes grew wide. “Paint and write? That sounds fun!” Her trunk swished with excitement.

On the big table, there were paintbrushes, chunky crayons, and a bowl of soapy water with a clean towel. On the wall, there was a chart with big, friendly letters. E-L-L-I-E.

“My name has lots of lines,” Ellie whispered. “I can try!”

Ms. Dot smiled. “First, we wash our hands and trunks. Clean hands make bright art. Clean trunks help us write.”

Ellie looked at her trunk. It had a tiny smudge from a grape snack. She sniffed. “I want bright art,” she said.

She dipped her trunk in the warm, bubbly water. Swish, swish. The bubbles tickled. “Hee-hee!” Ellie giggled. She rubbed gently with the towel. Pat, pat. Her trunk felt fresh and cool.

“Now I’m ready!” Ellie said.

She picked a big sheet of paper that felt smooth under her toes. She chose bright blue paint. “I will paint a big E!” Ellie dipped her brush. Drip, drop. The paint was thick and shiny. She tried to make a straight line. Wiggle, wiggle—the line curved like a river.

“Oh,” Ellie said softly. Her tummy felt wobbly. “My E looks like a funny snake.”

Ms. Dot came closer. “It’s okay to try again. Art is about practice. Let’s breathe.” They took a slow breath together. In and out.

“Can I try with a crayon?” Ellie asked.

“Yes,” said Ms. Dot. “Crayons are great for writing. Your clean trunk will help you hold it.”

Ellie picked a red crayon. It smelled like sweet wax. She wrapped her trunk around it. “Hello, crayon,” she whispered. She pressed gently on the paper. Scratch, scratch. She made one long line down. “That is the back of E!”

“Good start,” Ms. Dot said. “Now make three short lines.”

Ellie tried. One… two… three. The lines were a little crooked. “Oops.”

Ms. Dot nodded. “Crooked lines can still be letters. You’re learning.”

Ellie smiled a bit. “I am learning.”

She set the red crayon down and rinsed her trunk again. Swish, swish. She dried with the towel. Pat, pat. “Clean again,” she said. “Now paint time!”

She chose sunny yellow. “I will paint the L!” Brush in trunk, she made one long line and one short line. “L for Ellie!” The paint felt cool and smooth as it slid across the paper.

Ellie’s friend, Pip the penguin, waddled over. “Hi, Ellie! Your letters look happy.”

“Thanks,” Ellie said. “Do you wash your flippers for art?”

“Yes,” Pip said, dipping his flippers into the soapy bowl. “Clean flippers make neat shapes!”

Ellie looked at her paper. E-L were there, but she wanted more. “I want to write my whole name and make it colorful.”

She tried the I with a shiny green crayon. Straight down. “I!” Then another L with purple paint—swish! Finally, she needed the last E.

Ellie picked blue again. But her trunk felt a little sticky from the purple paint. The brush slipped, and the E turned into a blotchy blob. “Oh no!” Ellie’s ears drooped. “My art is messy.”

Ms. Dot pointed to the towel. “What can help when things feel sticky?”

Ellie looked at the bowl. “Wash!” she said. She washed her trunk. Swish, swish. She sang a tiny song, “Wash, wash, wash the paint, make it clean and bright!” She dried carefully. Pat, pat.

“Try again,” Ms. Dot encouraged. “Use slow, small moves.”

Ellie dipped her fresh trunk into the blue paint and took a deep breath. She made one long line. Then three short lines. One, two, three. The E appeared, neat and blue.

“I did it!” Ellie beamed. Her heart felt warm and proud.

She looked at her paper. E-L-L-I-E. Her name was written in colors—red, yellow, purple, green, and blue. Around the letters, she painted tiny stars and drew little soap bubbles with a white crayon. Pop, pop, pop!

Pip clapped his flippers. “Your letters sparkle!”

Ellie nodded. “Clean trunk helps my art. Slow moves help my writing. Try again when it’s hard.”

Ms. Dot hung Ellie’s art on the wall. The colors shone in the sunlight. “Ellie,” she said, “you made beautiful art and careful letters. You also showed good hygiene and patience.”

Ellie touched her clean trunk and smiled. “Art is fun. Writing is practice. Washing helps me do both.”

The bell chimed. It was time to tidy up. Ellie washed her brush until the water ran clear. She wiped the table and put the crayons back in their box. Everything smelled fresh again.

“Goodbye, art room,” Ellie said. “See you next time for more colors and letters!”

As she left, Ellie hummed her washing song and felt brave and happy. She learned that clean hands and trunks make bright art, careful writing takes time, and trying again makes strong hearts.

The End.
