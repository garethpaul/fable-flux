# Monkey Milo’s Wiggle Study Break

Monkey Milo loved his cozy study corner. It had a small desk, a soft blue chair, a cup of bright crayons, and a basket of books that smelled like paper and sunshine. A round clock went tick-tock on the wall. The window let in warm light that made tiny dust sparkles dance.

Today, Milo wanted to draw a big banana boat. He sat in his chair and picked a yellow crayon. “I can do this,” he whispered. “I am brave. I am ready.” He made one big curve. Then another. But his legs felt jumpy. His tail twitched. His toes wiggled.

“My body wants to move,” Milo said. “But I need to draw too.” He looked at the clock. Tick-tock, tick-tock. His tummy felt fizzy, like tiny soda bubbles. His head felt slow, like a sleepy turtle.

Mama Monkey peeked in. “Hello, Milo,” she said in a soft voice. “How is your drawing?”

Milo made a squiggly face. “My body is wiggly,” he said. “I want to move, but I also want to finish my banana boat.”

Mama smiled. “Being brave can mean knowing what your body needs,” she said. “Movement can help your mind. Let’s make a plan. We can take little move breaks.”

Milo sat up tall. “Yes! A plan.” He loved plans. Plans felt like maps.

Mama pointed to the clock. “Try drawing for two tick-tocks. Then do a gentle move for one tick-tock. That is called preparation. You get ready to move, and you get ready to focus.”

Milo nodded. “I will try.” He pressed his crayon to the paper. He drew a sail and a happy banana face. Tick-tock, tick-tock.

“Move break!” Milo said. “I will do quiet hops.”

He stood on the soft rug in his study corner. “One hop. Two hop. Three hop.” His feet thumped softly. Thump, thump, thump. His tail swished. Swish! He took a deep breath in. He let a big breath out. “Ahhh.”

Milo sat again. His head felt clear, like bright sky. He drew tiny waves in blue. He added sun sparkles. He hummed a little tune.

Just then, his crayon rolled off the desk and bonked the floor. Bonk! His little sister, Mimi, poked her nose into the study corner. “I heard a bonk,” she giggled. “Can I draw too?”

Milo froze. The study corner was small. Two chairs would not fit. He felt a swirl in his tummy. He wanted to say, “No,” but he remembered respect for others. He looked at Mimi’s hopeful eyes.

“Mimi,” Milo said kindly, “this is my study corner. It is a quiet place. I want to finish my banana boat. But I also want you to draw.”

Mimi’s smile drooped like a sleepy flower. “Oh.”

Milo took a brave breath. He had an idea. “We can share time,” he said. “I will draw for two tick-tocks. Then you can draw for one tick-tock. We can also make a move spot for breaks.”

Mimi’s eyes lit up. “A move spot?”

Milo pointed to a little space by the window. “We can wiggle there,” he said. “Quiet wiggles. Like jelly.”

Mimi clapped softly. “Jelly wiggles!”

They prepared their plan. Milo put a yellow sticker on the desk for his turn and a pink sticker for Mimi’s turn. He put a small pillow by the window for the move spot. “We will be gentle,” he said. “We will share. We will use peaceful words.”

Milo drew for two tick-tocks. Mimi watched and whispered, “Nice waves.” Then Milo said, “Move break!” He and Mimi went to the move spot. They wiggled like jelly. Wiggle-wobble. Sway-swoosh. Their feet brushed the rug. Their arms floated like leaves. They counted, “One, two, three,” in soft voices.

Then Mimi had her turn at the desk. She drew a tiny fish with big eyes. “Blub, blub,” she said. Milo smiled and stood tall like a tree. He lifted his arms up and down. “Tree breaths,” he said. Inhale, exhale. His chest felt warm and calm.

They took turns again. Hop. Wiggle. Stretch. Draw. The clock went tick-tock. The light in the window felt golden and cozy. The study corner smelled like crayons and banana snacks.

Soon, Milo looked at his paper and grinned. “My banana boat is done!” It had a brave face, a tall sail, and blue waves that made little shhh sounds in his mind.

Mimi held up her fish. “My fish has bubbles!” she cheered.

Mama peeked in once more. “I see teamwork,” she said. “I see respect. I see peaceful sharing.”

Milo puffed with pride. “Move breaks helped my brain,” he said. “When my body moves, my mind can focus.”

Mimi nodded. “And sharing feels nice.”

Milo gently tapped the stickers. “Our plan worked. We prepared. We moved. We were kind.”

They put their drawings on the wall. The banana boat “sailed” above the fish. Milo did one last quiet hop and a happy tail swish. “Thank you, body,” he whispered. “Thank you for moving.”

The End.
