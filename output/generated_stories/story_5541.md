# Miko’s Morning Fix-It Routine

In a sunny garage workshop, a courageous monkey named Miko lived with Auntie Jo. The garage smelled like pine wood and orange soap. Sunlight made warm stripes on the floor. Tools hung on hooks like shiny silver raindrops—hammers, wrenches, and a bright red tape measure.

Miko wore tiny blue overalls with a yellow banana patch. He loved to help. He loved to climb. And he loved to learn. Most of all, he was trying to learn a new routine called Morning Fix-It Time.

Every day, Auntie Jo would say, “Good morning, Miko! First we stretch, then we check our tools, then we fix little things. That’s our routine.”

Miko would wiggle his tail and nod. “I can do it! Stretch, check, fix!”

One Monday, the workshop felt extra busy. A squeaky red wagon waited by the door. A wobbly stool sat by the sink. A box of loose screws made a tiny jingling song. Ting-ting-ting!

Auntie Jo tapped the big wall clock. Tick-tock, tick-tock. “Morning Fix-It Time,” she said. “Ready, brave helper?”

Miko’s eyes shone. “Ready!”

He spread his arms wide. “Big monkey stretch!” His shoulders felt warm. He bent and touched his toes. “Hello, toes!” He took a deep breath. The air smelled like clean soap and oranges.

“Good stretching,” Auntie Jo smiled. “Now tool check.”

Miko climbed the step stool. He pointed to the hooks and sang, “Hammer, wrench, tape, brush, screws in a jar, rags in a box.” He patted each spot to make sure the tools were in their home. Tap, tap, tap.

He reached for the small wrench. Clink! “Here!”

He reached for the oil can. Drip. “Here!”

He reached for the safety glasses. “Uh-oh.” The hook was empty.

Miko’s stomach made a tiny flip. “They’re gone,” he whispered.

Auntie Jo knelt to his height. “What does our routine say?”

Miko touched the yellow banana patch on his overalls. It felt smooth. He said, “Stretch, check, fix. And… if something is missing, we look. We tell the truth. We keep trying.”

“That’s integrity and resilience,” Auntie Jo said gently. “We do the right thing and we keep going.”

Miko took a brave breath. “I will find them.”

He looked under the workbench. Dust tickled his nose. “Ah-choo!” He looked in the paint cabinet. It smelled like lemons and wood. He listened near the bucket. Drip… drop… drip. No glasses.

Miko’s ears drooped. “Maybe I can’t do Morning Fix-It Time.”

Auntie Jo touched his shoulder. “You can. Try one more place.”

Miko looked up at the pegboard. His monkey eyes noticed a shiny edge peeking from a rag pocket on the wall. He reached carefully. “Got them!” He held up the safety glasses. They felt smooth and cool in his hands.

“You found them,” Auntie Jo said. “You were honest, you kept looking, and you did it yourself.”

Miko grinned. “Now we can fix!”

He slid on the glasses. Click. He put a drop of oil on the red wagon wheel. Squeak… squeak… then quiet. He rolled the wagon back and forth. “Smooth!”

Next, he checked the wobbly stool. “Wobble-wobble.” He turned the small wrench. Clink, clink. Tight. He pressed the stool. Steady now.

Tick-tock, tick-tock, sang the clock. The garage felt calm and happy. The sunlight on the floor felt like a warm blanket.

Auntie Jo said, “Last part of the routine?”

Miko nodded. “Clean up and put things away.” He wiped the little oil drop with a rag. He put the wrench back on its hook. He tapped the jar of screws to make sure the lid was tight. Ting… stop. He washed his hands with orange soap. It made tiny bubbles that popped, pop, pop!

Just then, a neighbor knocked. Knock-knock. “Hello!” Mr. Lee peeked in. He held a broken toy car. “The wheel fell off. Can you help?”

Miko’s heart did a brave thump. He wanted to grab the car and fix it fast. But he remembered the routine. He said, “First, we stretch, check, and put on glasses.” He stretched his arms. “Big monkey stretch!” He checked the tools. He put on his glasses. Click.

He looked at the toy car wheel. “I can try.”

He turned the tiny screw. Clink. He pressed the wheel. Snap. He rolled the car on the table. Rrrrrr… it worked! He handed it back.

Mr. Lee smiled. “Thank you, Miko. You took your time. You did it right.”

Miko felt tall inside. “I followed my routine.”

When the work was done, Miko put everything back. Hammer, wrench, tape, brush. He hung the safety glasses in the right spot. He looked at Auntie Jo. “I didn’t hide when the glasses were missing. I told the truth. I kept trying. I did it myself.”

Auntie Jo hugged him tight. Her apron smelled like oranges and sunshine. “That’s routine, resilience, independence, and integrity all in one brave monkey.”

Miko patted the red wagon. He tapped the steady stool. He waved to the happy toy car. The garage hummed a soft song—tick-tock, drip-drop, rrrrrr. Miko’s heart hummed with it.

He said, “Every day, I can do my routine. It helps me know what to do. It helps me be brave.”

Auntie Jo nodded. “And when something is hard, you try again.”

Miko smiled. “Stretch, check, fix, and put away.”

“Exactly,” said Auntie Jo.

The End.
