# Gertie the Giraffe Learns to Try

Gertie the giraffe was staying in Grandma's cozy guest room for the weekend. The room smelled like lavender and had soft yellow walls that made everything feel warm and sunny. But Gertie felt a little worried.

"I want to help Grandma make cookies," Gertie said to herself, looking at her long legs and tall neck in the mirror. "But what if I'm too big? What if I mess up?"

Gertie's spots seemed to droop with sadness. She had tried to help in the kitchen before, but sometimes her long neck knocked things over, and her big hooves felt clumsy.

Just then, Grandma peeked into the guest room. Her eyes twinkled like stars. "Good morning, sweet Gertie! Would you like to help me bake chocolate chip cookies?"

Gertie's ears drooped. "I don't think I can, Grandma. I'm not good at cooking. Last time I tried, I spilled flour everywhere."

Grandma sat on the soft blue chair by the window. The morning sunlight made her silver hair shine. "Oh, my dear," she said gently. "Do you know what I think when I make mistakes?"

"What?" Gertie asked, her brown eyes curious.

"I think, 'I can't do this... YET!'" Grandma smiled. "That little word 'yet' is very special. It means I'm still learning, and that's perfectly okay."

Gertie tilted her long neck thoughtfully. "Yet?"

"Yes! You couldn't tie your shoes when you were little, but you learned. You couldn't reach the tall leaves on trees, but you grew. And now, you're learning to cook!"

Gertie felt a tiny spark of hope in her chest. "Maybe... maybe I could try again?"

In the kitchen, Grandma showed Gertie how to crack eggs slowly and carefully. "Take your time," Grandma said. "Learning takes patience."

At first, Gertie's big hooves felt clumsy holding the egg. CRACK! A piece of shell fell into the bowl.

"Oh no!" Gertie sighed. "I messed up again!"

"That's okay," Grandma said warmly. "Let's fish out that shell piece together. Even I drop shells sometimes! The important thing is that you're trying and learning."

They scooped out the shell, and Gertie tried again. This time, she cracked the egg perfectly! The golden yolk plopped into the bowl with a satisfying plop.

"I did it!" Gertie exclaimed, her spots seeming brighter.

Next came mixing the dough. Gertie used her long neck to her advantage, looking down into the bowl from above to see everything perfectly. Her careful stirring made the dough smooth and chocolatey.

"You know what, Gertie?" Grandma said as they shaped the cookies. "Your height and long neck are actually helping you be a wonderful baker! You can see everything so well."

When the cookies came out of the oven, they were golden brown and smelled like happiness. Gertie had made them herself!

"I thought I couldn't bake," Gertie said, taking a warm, gooey bite. "But I just couldn't bake... yet!"

Grandma hugged Gertie's long neck. "That's right, my dear. When we practice with patience and believe in ourselves, we can learn anything. Your brain grows stronger every time you try something new."

That night in the guest room, Gertie looked in the mirror again. This time, her spots seemed to sparkle with confidence. She had learned that making mistakes was part of learning, and that "yet" was the most powerful word of all.

The End.
