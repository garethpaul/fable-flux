# Roo's Big Backyard Day

Roo was a joyful kangaroo joey. Roo lived in a house with a sunny backyard. The grass was warm and tickly. The sky was bright and blue. The air smelled like sweet oranges and fresh dirt. Birds sang, “Tweet, tweet!” and a breeze whispered, “Whoosh.”

Roo bounced to the back door. “Today I try by myself,” Roo said. “Today I do big-kid things!”

Mama Kangaroo smiled. “You can try, Roo. I am near if you need help. Breathe slow. Use kind hands. Use kind words with yourself too.”

Roo put a paw on the doorknob. It felt cool and smooth. “I can do it,” Roo whispered. Twist, click! The door opened. Roo’s heart felt proud and fizzy, like tiny bubbles. “I did it!”

Outside, Roo saw a little garden patch. Red tomatoes glowed. Green beans curled like tiny ropes. There was a small watering can. It was shiny and blue.

“I want to give the plants a drink,” said Roo. “I can try by myself.”

Roo lifted the can. “Oh! It is heavy.” Roo’s arms wobbled. A little splash splashed on Roo’s toes. It felt cold and made Roo giggle. “I will take small steps,” Roo said. Step, step, step. Pour, pour, pour. The dirt drank the water and turned dark and muddy. Worms peeked up to say hello.

Roo felt strong inside. “I am doing it!”

A bright butterfly flew by. Its wings were orange and black with tiny white dots. “Hello, butterfly,” Roo said softly. “I am taking care of the garden.”

Next, Roo saw a tiny flag bucket. Inside were little flags from many places. One had red and white stripes with blue stars. One had green, white, and orange. One had red with a yellow sun. There were so many colors!

Roo picked up a small globe ball. It felt rubbery. “The world is big,” Roo said. “Many people. Many places.” Roo placed the little flags in the flower bed. “Friends can be different and all belong,” Roo said. “We can be kind to everyone.”

Roo looked at the patio. Tiny leaves had fallen from the lemon tree. They made a crunchy carpet. A small child-sized broom leaned on the wall. “I can help keep the backyard clean,” Roo said. “I can try.”

Roo held the broom. Swish, swish. Leaves moved into a pile. A ladybug crawled by, shiny and red. Roo paused. “Hello, little friend,” Roo whispered. “I will sweep around you.” Roo made a neat circle so the ladybug was safe. “Helping means caring for others,” Roo said. Roo felt warm and glad.

But then Roo saw the snack table. A water bottle sat there. It was closed tight. A little bowl of carrot sticks waited too. Roo’s tummy rumbled. “Grumble, grumble,” it said.

Roo tried to open the bottle. Twist. Twist. It did not open. Roo’s ears drooped. Roo’s face felt hot and scrunchy. “I feel frustrated,” Roo said, with a small frown. Roo put a paw on the bottle and stopped.

“Mama says, ‘Name your feeling. Breathe slow,’” Roo remembered. Roo put a paw on Roo’s chest. “In,” Roo said. “Out,” Roo said. “In. Out.” The sun felt warm on Roo’s fur. The breeze kissed Roo’s cheeks.

“I can try again,” Roo said. Roo wrapped both paws around the bottle. Twist—pop! It opened! “I did it by myself!” Roo took a sip. The water was cool and made Roo’s mouth feel happy. Crunch, crunch went the carrots. Roo chewed slowly. “This is self-care,” Roo said. “I listen to my body.”

A soft ding came from the little timer on the garden wall. It meant “water time is done.” Roo turned the spout off with a gentle click. “I save water,” Roo said. “That helps everyone.” Roo picked up a small bag for trash and looked around. “I will keep the yard clean,” Roo said. Roo found a tiny wrapper and put it in the bag. “This is being a good helper in my community.”

Suddenly, Roo heard a shout from over the fence. “Hola!” called Luna, the neighbor, in a happy voice. “Hello!” Roo called back. “I am taking care of the backyard!” Luna waved. “Good job! In my home we say, ‘Buen trabajo!’ That means ‘good job!’” Roo smiled wide. “Buen trabajo,” Roo tried. The word felt bouncy and new. Roo giggled. “We can learn from friends.”

Roo looked around. The garden sipped. The flags fluttered. The patio was tidy. The ladybug was safe. The snack was eaten. Roo’s heart felt big and bright.

Mama stepped outside and sniffed the lemony air. “Roo, what a wonderful job,” she said. “You tried many things. You cared for your body. You cared for the garden. You cared for the backyard and our neighbors.”

Roo bounced once, twice, three times. “I felt frustrated, and I breathed. I tried again. I did many things by myself!” Roo hugged Mama, then patted Roo’s own chest. “I am proud of me.”

Mama nodded. “You were independent and kind. That is a big-kid day.”

Roo looked up at the blue, blue sky. “Tomorrow I can try more,” Roo said. “I can help. I can learn. I can be brave and gentle.”

The sun warmed Roo’s paws. The birds sang a sweet song. Roo whispered, “I can do it,” and smiled.

The End.
