# Tiko the Turtle’s Colorful Corner

In a quiet study corner, with soft pillows and a small desk, lived a shy turtle named Tiko. Tiko had a smooth green shell and big gentle eyes. The study corner smelled like crayons and paper. Sunlight made tiny warm squares on the floor. It was a cozy place to think and draw.

On the desk sat a little box of crayons, a cup of colored pencils, and a stack of clean white paper. A tiny globe sat on the shelf, bright and round. Tiko loved to look at it. He would whisper, “So many places,” and tuck his head back into his shell.

One morning, Tiko peeked out. He could hear the soft scratch-scratch of pencils from the hallway. Friends were drawing in the big room. Tiko wanted to draw too, but his voice was small. He stayed in the study corner, where it was quiet.

“I wish I could make art,” Tiko said softly. “But what if it’s not good? What if I make a mistake?”

A gentle breeze slipped through the window. It made the pages flutter. Flap, flap. Tiko took a deep breath. He felt the paper with his tiny turtle toes. It felt smooth and cool. He opened the crayon box. The colors smiled up at him—red, blue, yellow, green, purple, and more.

Then Tiko saw something new on the shelf. It was a book with shiny pages. The cover showed a mask, a pattern, and a bright quilt. The title said, “Art Around the World.”

Tiko’s eyes grew wide. “Art from everywhere?” he whispered.

He opened the book. Each page showed a different kind of art. One page showed handprints from far-away caves. Another page showed bright patterns from a festival. There were soft quilts, dancing kites, painted bowls, and tiny beads that sparkled. Every picture told a story.

Tiko touched the page with a quilt. “This quilt has little squares,” he said. “Each square is a piece. Together they make something warm.” He turned to a page with a mask. “This mask is for a dance. The colors help people feel brave.” He looked at a page with a kite. “This kite flies high at a celebration. It makes the sky look happy.”

Tiko smiled a little smile. “Maybe my art can tell a story too.”

He picked up a purple crayon. Scratch, scratch. He drew a small square. Then he drew a second square, and a third. “These are pieces,” he said. “Like the quilt.” He picked blue and drew wavy lines. “This is water,” he whispered. “Like my pond.” He chose yellow to make a sun. He added tiny dots, like beads, around the sun. “This is for sparkle,” he said.

Tiko looked at the globe. He wanted to add more. He chose red and orange and made a little kite in the sky. “For celebrations,” he said. He drew a smiling mask at the corner of the page with green and gold. “For courage,” he whispered, feeling a little braver.

He sat back. His picture was busy with colors and shapes. It told a story about his pond, the warm sun, and happy celebrations from many places. But Tiko still felt shy. “What if it’s not good?” he asked the quiet room.

Just then, Tiko’s friend, Mina the mouse, peeked in. She carried a tiny pencil and wore a blue bow. “Hi, Tiko,” Mina squeaked. “It smells like crayons in here. Are you making art?”

Tiko tucked his head halfway into his shell. “Maybe,” he said. “But I’m shy.”

Mina stepped closer. Tap, tap, tap went her tiny feet. She peeked at the paper. Her eyes grew round. “Wow,” Mina said. “I see water lines and a kite and a brave mask. I see dots like beads! It looks like a story.”

Tiko blinked. “A story?”

“Yes,” said Mina. “Your art is telling me about your world and other worlds too. That is special.”

Tiko took a small breath. The air smelled like paper and sunshine. He felt warm and proud. “I learned from this book,” he said, patting the shiny pages. “Art can be different in different places. I can learn. I can try.”

Mina smiled. “Let’s share it.”

Tiko’s head popped out all the way. “Share?”

“We can hang it on the study corner wall,” Mina said. “We can make a little gallery. Everyone can look and learn.”

Tiko’s heart thumped. Thump-thump. He was still shy, but he nodded. Together, they taped the picture to the wall. The tape made a soft ripp sound.

Soon, other friends peeked in. A rabbit, a bird, and a lamb stood in the doorway. “Ooo,” said the bird. “So many colors!” The rabbit twitched its nose. “What is the kite for?” The lamb bleated softly, “Why the mask?”

Tiko took a deep breath. “The kite is for a celebration from far away,” he said. “The mask helps people feel brave in a dance. The dots are like beads that shine. The squares are like a quilt that keeps folks warm. My water and sun are from my home.”

The friends nodded. “We learned something new,” said the rabbit. “We like your art,” said the lamb.

Tiko felt lighter. “Thank you,” he said. “Art can teach us. Art can help us feel brave. And we can keep getting better if we practice.”

Mina clapped. “Let’s make more! We can make a gallery of many places. We can add smells and sounds too.”

They drew kites that whooshed, quilts that looked cozy, bowls with swirls, and masks with bright smiles. The study corner filled with color. It felt warm and friendly. Tiko still felt shy sometimes, but now he knew: trying is brave, learning is kind, and art is for everyone.

The End.
