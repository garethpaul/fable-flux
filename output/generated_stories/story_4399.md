# Milo Mouse Finds His Calm

Milo was a kind mouse with soft gray fur and bright whiskers. He lived in a cozy corner of a home office. The office smelled like warm paper and fresh pencils. The desk lamp made a gentle glow. The keyboard clicked. The printer hummed. Milo liked the sounds. They felt like songs.

One sunny morning, Milo wanted to make a thank-you card for his friend, Bella Bird. She had shared a shiny red berry with him. Milo set out tiny crayons, a little glue stick, and a stack of small paper squares. He whispered, “I can do it. I can make a kind card.”

Milo climbed onto the desk. His little paws felt the cool, smooth wood. He listened to the house. Click. Click. Hummm. Tap. Tap. The sounds were busy.

Just then, the printer started. “Brrrrr-rrrr,” it buzzed. A page came out with a whoosh. Milo’s ears twitched. His tummy felt fluttery.

“Too loud,” Milo said softly.

He tried to draw a big red heart. But the pencil kept rolling away. His crayon broke in half. A drop of glue fell on his paw. Sticky! He wrinkled his nose. “Oh no,” he squeaked. “This is hard.”

The lamp felt bright. The chair creaked. The keyboard clicked faster. “Click-click-click-click!” Milo’s shoulders climbed up to his ears. His heart went thump-thump. He wanted to shout. He wanted to push the crayons. He wanted to run and hide.

Milo stopped.

He remembered something his friend, Grandma Mouse, had taught him. “When big feelings come,” Grandma had said, “we can stop, we can breathe, we can listen to our bodies. We can use our calm tools.”

Milo put a paw on his chest and one on his belly. He whispered, “Pause.”

He closed his eyes and took a slow breath in. In through his nose. He felt cool air. He let the breath out like a quiet wind. “Hoooo,” he sighed. His shoulders softened.

“Again,” he said. In, two, three. Out, two, three. He listened to his breath. He felt his paws tingle less. He smelled the pencil wood. He heard the lamp hum like a sleepy bee.

Milo opened his eyes. “I can choose what I need,” he told himself. That was self-advocacy. He could ask for help. He could ask for quiet. He could make a plan.

He padded to the edge of the desk and waved to the person in the office. “Excuse me,” Milo said in his tiny voice. “I am making a card. The sounds are big for me. Can we have a little quiet time?”

The person smiled and nodded. “Sure, Milo,” they whispered. The keyboard slowed. The printer stopped. The lamp dimmed just a bit. The room felt softer.

Milo also helped himself. He moved the crayons into a tiny dish so they would not roll. He placed the glue on a scrap of paper. He made a little “work square” with tape so he could keep his paws in one spot. He put on his mini soft hat that Grandma had knit. It felt snug and safe.

He spoke kindly to himself. “I can do hard things. I can go slow. I can listen.”

Milo wiggled his toes. Tap-tap. He counted four things he could see: red crayon, blue paper, shiny paper clip, fuzzy hat. He counted three things he could feel: smooth desk, soft hat, warm paws. He counted two things he could hear: lamp hum, gentle breath. He counted one smell: pencil wood.

“Ready,” he said.

Milo drew the heart again. This time, he pressed gently. The red color glided like a sled. He used the broken crayon pieces like little stamps. Tap-tap-tap! Dot-dot-dot! He squeezed a tiny circle of glue. Squeeeze. He stuck a small gold star in the middle. Stick! He added a smile under the heart. Happy!

Bella Bird fluttered to the windowsill. “Cheep-cheep! What are you making?” she asked.

“A thank-you card for you,” said Milo. He held it up and beamed. “I felt big feelings. I wanted to shout. But I stopped. I breathed. I asked for quiet. I made a plan. I used my calm tools.”

Bella hopped closer. “You listened to your body,” she chirped. “That is brave.”

Milo nodded. “It is called self-regulation,” he said slowly. “It means I can help my feelings and my actions. I can choose kind steps.”

Bella smiled. “Cheep! That helps me too. When I hear loud trucks, I can pause and breathe.”

The person in the office gave Milo a tiny thumbs-up. “Nice self-advocacy, Milo,” they whispered. “Thank you for telling me what you needed.”

Milo’s heart felt warm, like toast. He wrote “THANK YOU” in big, careful letters. He added a tiny mouse and a tiny bird holding hands. He blew on the glue. Puff-puff. The card smelled sweet and papery.

Milo gave the card to Bella. She sang a soft song. The office felt calm and friendly. The lamp hummed. The air was gentle. Milo’s tummy was quiet now.

Milo looked around his cozy home office. He knew the sounds. He knew his tools. He knew his voice. He patted his chest. “Pause, breathe, choose,” he said. “I can do it again next time.”

And that was the clear lesson he learned: When feelings get big, we can stop, breathe, listen, and ask for what we need. We can make a plan and move gently. That is self-regulation.

The End.
