# Ellie’s Big Little Try

Ellie was a gentle elephant calf with soft gray ears and a tiny, curly tail. She lived in a comfortable nursery with warm yellow walls, a cozy rug with bright ABC letters, and a window that let in soft morning light. The nursery smelled like clean blankets and sweet bananas. Toys rested in baskets, and a rocking chair creaked kindly when the wind hummed.

Today was Big Little Talent Day. All the young animals would show something they could do. Ellie’s tummy fluttered like a butterfly. She wanted to stack the colorful blocks into a tall, tall tower. “I want to try,” she whispered, touching a cool blue block. “But what if I can’t?”

Her friend, Pip the panda, waved a furry paw. “Hi, Ellie! You can do it,” he said, munching on a crunchy carrot. The crunch sounded loud in the quiet room.

Ellie nodded, but her heart thumped. Her ears felt warm. She picked up a red block. It felt smooth and heavy in her little trunk. She placed it on top of a yellow block. Tap. Tap. Steady.

“Good job,” hummed Nani, the gentle nursery helper, rocking in the chair that made soft squeaks. “Remember, small steps. Breathe in, breathe out.”

Ellie breathed in. She smelled the sweet banana snack on the table. She breathed out. She tried again. Green block. Blue block. Tap. Tap.

Then—oh! Pip bumped the rug with his rolling ball. The tower wobbled. Wobble, wobble… topple! The blocks clattered like tiny drums. Ellie's eyes filled with tears. Her cheeks felt hot. “My tower,” she sniffled. “It fell.”

Pip’s round ears drooped. “I’m sorry, Ellie,” he said, hugging the ball to his belly. “I didn’t mean to.”

Ellie’s trunk drooped. “I can’t do it,” she whispered. “I’m too little.”

Nani stood and placed a soft hand on Ellie’s back. “Being little is okay,” Nani said, voice calm like a lullaby. “Learning takes time. Your try was brave. That is big.”

Ellie blinked. Brave? Her heart felt a tiny spark. She looked at Pip. He looked sorry, with wide panda eyes. “I forgive you,” Ellie said, sniffing. “Maybe we can fix it together?”

Pip’s smile came back. “Yes! Let’s make it taller!”

Ellie giggled, just a little. “We can try,” she said. “I can try again.”

They picked up the blocks. The blocks felt cool in Ellie’s trunk. She loved the click sound when they touched. Pip held the rug straight with his paws. “No more wobble,” he said.

“Thank you,” Ellie said softly. “I’m glad you’re helping.”

“Friends help,” Pip said, nodding.

Block by block, Ellie stacked. Yellow, red, green, blue. She took her time. She breathed in and out. “I can do this,” she whispered to herself. “I am strong. I am gentle. I can try.”

The tower grew as tall as a picture book. Then as tall as Pip’s shoulder. Ellie’s legs felt tingly. She added one last purple block. The tower stood steady and proud.

The other animals clapped their paws, hooves, and wings. Clap-clap, stomp-stomp, flap-flap! The room buzzed with happy sounds.

Nani beamed. “Ellie, tell us how you did it.”

Ellie looked at the tower, then at her friends. She felt warm in a happy way, like sunlight on her back. “I was scared when it fell,” she said. “I felt small. But I tried again. I breathed. Pip helped. I forgave him. I used small steps. I did not give up.”

Pip raised his paw. “I said sorry and made amends,” he added. “I watched the rug. I held it flat.”

Nani nodded. “That is resilience,” Nani said. “You both kept going. That is gratitude, too—saying thank you for help. And Ellie, you believed in yourself. That is self-confidence.”

Ellie smiled big. Her trunk made a happy little trumpet. “I believe in me,” she said. The words tickled her tongue. They felt good, like a hug.

The sun moved across the window, and sparkles of light danced on the tower. Ellie looked around the comfortable nursery—the soft pillows, the neat baskets, the colorful letters on the rug. She felt safe. She felt proud.

“Snack time!” Nani called. Banana slices and tiny crackers went crunch, crunch. Ellie shared with Pip. “Thank you for helping,” she said.

“Thank you for forgiving me,” Pip said. “You’re brave.”

Ellie patted the tower with her trunk. “I’m brave, and I can try,” she said. “Even if it falls, I can try again.”

Nani smiled. “That is the lesson for us all,” she said. “When we feel small, we take small steps. We breathe. We ask for help. We make amends. We keep going. And we believe in ourselves.”

Ellie’s ears wiggled happily. She felt her heart shine like a little lantern. She looked at the purple block on top and whispered, “I did it.”

The End.
