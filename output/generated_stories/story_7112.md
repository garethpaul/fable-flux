# Kiki the Joey Tries It Herself

In a comfortable nursery with soft yellow walls, the sun peeked through the curtains. The room smelled like clean blankets and warm milk. Toys rested on a fluffy rug, and tiny books sat on a low shelf. In the middle of the room, a joyful kangaroo joey named Kiki bounced in a gentle circle.

“Boing, boing!” giggled Kiki. Her little paws felt the soft rug. Her ears flicked when she heard birds sing outside. She loved mornings. Mornings felt new and bright.

Today, Kiki’s nanny, Miss Lila, smiled and set a small backpack on a chair. “Good morning, Kiki. Today is Try-It-Yourself Day,” she said in a warm voice. “You can try to do little things all by yourself.”

Kiki’s eyes sparkled. “I can try!” she said. She put her paw on her chest. “I am brave. I am curious.”

Miss Lila nodded. “Curious means you like to learn new things. Independent means you try first, then ask for help if you need it. We can make good choices together.”

Kiki looked around. Her tummy made a tiny rumble. On the low table sat a small bowl of fruit and a little cup of yogurt with a short spoon. Kiki took a breath. “I can do it,” she whispered.

She climbed onto her chair. It felt smooth and cool. “First, I choose,” Kiki said. “Strawberries or bananas?” She tapped her chin. “I choose strawberries today.” She placed three red slices in her bowl. Plop, plop, plop.

She picked up the spoon. It felt a bit heavy in her paw. “Slow and steady,” Kiki told herself. She scooped yogurt. Oops! A little blob fell on the table. “Uh-oh,” said Kiki. Her cheeks grew warm.

Miss Lila was nearby. “What can you try, Kiki?”

Kiki looked at a small cloth. “I can wipe it.” She wiped the blob. Swipe, swipe. “All clean!” she cheered. She tried again—slowly this time. Scoop, lift, taste. “Mmm!” The yogurt felt cool and sweet.

After breakfast, Kiki bounced to the shelf. She wanted a book with a big moon on the cover. She stretched her paw, but two books slid and fell. Thump! The sound was loud. Kiki’s tummy fluttered.

“Oops,” said Kiki. She crouched and peeked at the books. “I can fix this.” She stacked them one by one. “I choose to move slowly.” Her paws felt the smooth covers, and soon the books were neat again. She smiled and picked the moon book. “I did it!”

Miss Lila clapped softly. “You made a plan, you tried, and you fixed a mistake. That is independence.”

Kiki opened the book and traced the moon with her paw. “I like learning,” she said.

Then Kiki saw her small backpack on the chair. “I can pack it!” she declared. She laid things out on the rug: a tiny water bottle, a napkin, and her favorite blue crayon. “What should I bring?” she asked herself. “I will choose three things.” She counted, touching each one. “One, two, three.” She zipped the backpack. Zip! The sound was zippy and fun.

A soft knock came at the door. “Knock-knock.” Kiki’s friend, Pip the little platypus, waddled in with big curious eyes. “Hi, Kiki,” Pip said. “Can we build a block tower?”

“Yes!” said Kiki. “Let’s try it ourselves.”

They sat by the block basket. Kiki chose a wide blue block for the bottom. “This will be strong,” she said. Pip chose a tall yellow block. “This one reaches the sky!”

They stacked: blue, red, yellow. The tower wobbled. Pip’s bill trembled. “It might fall,” he whispered.

Kiki took a slow breath. “We can make a plan.” She put her paw on her heart. “First, I think. Then, I try.” She looked for a flat green block and slid it under the wobbly part. “Try now,” she said.

Pip placed the yellow block again. The tower stood taller. Tap, tap. Still steady! “We did it!” Pip cheered.

A tiny joey named Momo watched with wide eyes. “Can I have a turn?” Momo asked softly.

Kiki looked at the basket. “We can share,” she said. She handed Momo a red block. “You can put this one on top.”

Momo smiled and carefully set it down. The tower reached higher, almost to Kiki’s shoulders. They all clapped. Clap, clap, clap!

Miss Lila brought a small sticker sheet. “I see thinkers and helpers,” she said. “Kiki, what did you learn today?”

Kiki tilted her head. She thought about yogurt, books, backpacks, and blocks. “I learned I can try first,” she said proudly. “I can make choices. I can fix mistakes. I can share and work with friends.”

Miss Lila nodded. “That is independence. You listen to your body, you make a plan, and you ask for help when you need it.”

Kiki hugged her backpack. It felt light, and her heart felt big. “I am curious, and I can do hard things,” she said.

Pip grinned. “And we can do them together, too.”

The nursery felt warm and bright. The tower stood tall. The room smelled like crayons and clean wood. Kiki’s ears twitched with joy. She bounced once—boing!—but very gently, so the tower stayed standing.

“Today I tried it myself,” Kiki whispered, “and that felt good.”

The End.
