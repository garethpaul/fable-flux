# Millie Mouse’s Dinner Time Dance

Millie was a kind little mouse with soft gray fur and bright, curious eyes. She lived in a cozy nook by the wall of a big family’s dining room. Every evening, the dining room smelled like warm soup and fresh bread. The wooden table felt smooth, and the chairs made gentle squeaks when people sat. The clock on the wall went tick-tock, tick-tock, like a calm song.

Millie loved the dining room at dinner time. She had a special routine. Routine means doing things in the same order each day. Her routine helped her feel safe, calm, and ready.

“First, I wash my paws,” Millie said softly to herself. She dipped her tiny paws in a thimble cup of water and dried them on a scrap of soft cloth. The water felt cool. The cloth felt cozy.

“Next, I set my place,” she squeaked. Millie slid a little leaf onto her bottle-cap plate. She put a button spoon on the right side. Tap, tap, tap. She lined it up carefully. It made her whiskers wiggle with pride.

“Then, I wait my turn,” she reminded herself. Millie always waited until the family began dinner. She listened for the chairs to scoot. She listened for the quiet hush when everyone held hands to say, “Thank you for our meal.” Millie did the same thing in her tiny mouse way. She placed her paws together and whispered, “Thank you.”

One evening, Millie heard a faint sniffle. It came from under the table, by the soft rug fringe. She peeked out. There sat Benny Beetle, shiny and blue, with his six little legs tucked tight. His antennae drooped.

“Hello, Benny,” Millie said kindly. “Are you okay?”

Benny shook his head. “I don’t know the dinner time rules,” he said. “I am slow. I am small. I am different. I don’t fit.”

Millie’s heart felt warm and big. “Different is special,” she said. “We can learn together. Would you like to join my dinner time routine?”

Benny blinked. “Really? Even if I click when I walk?” Click, click, went his tiny feet.

“I squeak when I walk,” Millie giggled. “Click and squeak can be friends.”

Just then, a breeze carried the smell of carrot soup. The smell was sweet and earthy. Millie’s tummy rumbled. Benny’s tummy made a little beep.

“First, we wash,” Millie said. She showed Benny the thimble cup. Benny dipped his front feet, then his back feet. Splash, splash. Drip, drip.

“Next, we set our places.” Millie slid a second leaf next to hers. “You can use this shiny coin for a plate,” she said. Benny’s eyes lit up. “It sparkles!” he chirped.

“Now our spoons,” Millie said. “We have one button spoon and one acorn cap spoon. Let’s take turns.” Millie smiled. “Tonight, you stir the soup with the acorn cap, and I will use the button. Tomorrow, we can switch.”

“Taking turns feels fair,” Benny said. He breathed out, and his antennae stood taller.

They both looked at the big table. The family sat down. The chairs made soft squeaks. The clock went tick-tock, steady and friendly. The soup bowls clinked. “Thank you for our meal,” the family said together.

Millie put her paws together. “Thank you,” she whispered. Benny copied her. “Thank you,” he buzzed softly.

The room felt peaceful. It was their time.

Millie dipped the acorn cap spoon into a tiny puddle of soup that had dripped near the table leg. She blew gently. “It’s hot,” she said. Steam curled like a little ribbon. “We wait for three ticks.” Tick. Tock. Tick. They waited together, breathing slowly.

They ate. Sip. Sip. The soup tasted sweet like carrots and warm like hugs. Benny tried a crumb of bread Millie had saved. Crunch. “It is crunchy!” he said, delighted.

A crumb rolled near a spider who was weaving a web in the corner. She had long, careful legs and shy eyes. “Hello,” Millie said. “Would you like to join our dinner routine?”

The spider paused. “I eat little insects,” she said gently. “I am different too.”

Millie nodded. “We all have different foods. We can still share good manners, kind words, and our turns.” The spider smiled and waved a leg. “I will listen to the tick-tock with you.”

Millie showed Benny and the spider her routine once more:
- Wash paws or feet.
- Set places neatly.
- Wait for the signal.
- Say thank you.
- Take turns and help.

Benny beamed. “I can remember that!”

When a pea rolled off the table, Millie caught it. “Here you go,” she said, handing it to a tiny ant who hurried by. “Thank you,” squeaked the ant. Helping felt good.

After dinner, Millie and Benny cleaned their little spot. They wiped the coin plate. They folded the soft cloth. Wipe, wipe. Fold, fold. The floor smelled clean like lemon. The rug felt fluffy under their feet.

Benny looked shy again. “Will you do this tomorrow?”

“Yes,” Millie said. “A routine is our steady friend. It helps us feel ready.”

The clock went tick-tock, tick-tock. The dining room grew quiet and warm. Millie’s eyes felt sleepy. Benny tucked his legs in happily.

“We are different,” Benny said, “and we belong.”

“We are helpful,” Millie said, “and we take turns.”

“And every day,” they said together, “we follow our kind routine.”

The End.
