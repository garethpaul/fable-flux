# Ollie Otter and the Tidy-Time Plan

Ollie was a playful otter with bright eyes and a swishy tail. He loved to splash, roll, and giggle. Today, Ollie had a big job. Nana had asked him to help in the garden shed.

“Ollie,” Nana said with a warm smile, “we have three jobs today. First, sweep the floor. Second, sort the tools. Third, water the little seedlings. We will use a timer to help us.” Nana showed a little red timer that tick-tick-ticked softly.

Ollie sniffed the shed. It smelled like fresh dirt and lemon soap. The wooden shelves felt smooth and cool. Sunlight made dust sparkles dance in the air. Ollie bounced on his paws. “I can do it! How long, Nana?”

“Ten minutes to sweep, ten to sort, and ten to water,” said Nana. “That’s thirty minutes, start to finish. We’ll work, then play.”

Ollie nodded. “Work, then play.” He liked the sound of that. Nana turned the timer. Tick, tick, tick.

Ollie picked up the little broom. Swish, swish went the straw bristles on the floor. “I am sweeping,” he sang. “Swish-a, swish-a!” He saw a shiny blue marble under a basket. “Ooo!” he said. “A treasure!” Ollie reached for it.

The timer kept ticking. Tick, tick, tick.

Ollie rolled the marble. It made a soft clink. He rolled it again. Clink, clink. Then he remembered. “Oh! I am sweeping. Work, then play.” He put the marble on the shelf. “Later,” he told it.

Swish, swish. The floor looked neat and clean. “Nana will be happy,” Ollie whispered. He felt warm and proud inside. The timer dinged! “Sweep time is done,” Nana said, peeking in. “Great job, Ollie. What comes next?”

“Sort the tools,” Ollie said. He smiled and set the timer again. Tick, tick, tick.

Ollie picked up a small rake. The metal teeth felt cool in his paws. He found a hook on the wall. “Rake goes here.” He held a bright yellow trowel. “Trowel goes with the trowels.” He found a tiny watering can. “Not yet,” he told it. “Watering is last.”

On the shelf, a box of stickers winked at him. “Stars!” Ollie gasped. “Shiny stars!” He opened the box and one star stuck to his nose. It felt smooth and a little sticky. He giggled, “I am a star-nosed otter!”

Tick, tick, tick.

Ollie wanted to make a big sticker train, but he stopped. He took a slow breath, in and out. “Work, then play,” he said softly. He closed the box and placed it on the top shelf. “Later.” He hung up the shears, set the gloves in a bin, and lined up the pots, small to big, like a tiny tower.

Ding! The timer rang. Nana clapped. “Ollie, you sorted so well. What is last?”

“Water the seedlings,” Ollie said. He set the timer again. Tick, tick, tick.

The little seedlings lived in a tray by the window. Their leaves looked like tiny green hands. “Hello, little plants,” Ollie said. He filled the watering can. The water sounded like rain, pitter-pat, as it poured. The dirt smelled rich and wet. Ollie watched carefully. “Just a little drink,” he whispered. “Not too much.”

But Ollie got excited. He liked watching the water sparkle. He poured a little more. Then one leaf drooped. “Oh no,” Ollie said. “Too much.” His tummy felt tight.

He ran to Nana. “Nana, I spilled extra water,” he said, voice small. “I did not follow the directions. I am sorry.”

Nana knelt and gave Ollie a gentle hug. “Thank you for telling me the truth,” she said. “That is honest and brave. We can fix it. Let’s use a sponge to soak up the extra.”

They pressed a soft sponge to the soil. Squish, squish. The sponge felt cool and heavy. The leaf perked up a little. Ollie’s shoulders relaxed. He smiled. “Better!”

Ding! The timer chimed. “All done,” Nana said. She looked around the garden shed. The floor was clean. The tools were in their places. The seedlings looked happy again.

“How do you feel?” Nana asked.

“Proud,” Ollie said. “I did my jobs in order. Sweep, sort, water. I used the timer. I told the truth when I made a mistake.”

Nana nodded. “You followed directions and you managed your time. Work, then play.”

Ollie’s eyes sparkled. “Now play?”

“Now play,” Nana agreed, laughing.

Ollie picked up the blue marble. He rolled it along the smooth floor. Clink, clink. He took out the star stickers and gave one to Nana. “A star for you,” he said. He put a small star on the watering can. “A star for trying again.”

As they played, a warm breeze swirled into the shed. It smelled like grass and sunshine. Ollie wiggled his tail. “I like the tidy-time plan,” he said. “Timer, jobs, then play. If I get stuck, I can stop, breathe, and try again.”

Nana smiled. “That’s a smart plan, Ollie.”

Ollie nodded. “I can follow directions. I can be honest. And I can use my time well.”

He tapped the timer gently. Tick, tick, tick. “Tomorrow, we can do more jobs,” he said, “and more play.”

The End.
