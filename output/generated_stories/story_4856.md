# Roo’s Treehouse Tune-Up

Roo was a joyful kangaroo joey with big springy feet and a cozy treehouse. The treehouse smelled like fresh leaves and warm sunshine. Birds chirped on the branches, and the wood felt smooth under Roo’s paws. Roo loved to bounce and sing, “Hop, hop, hooray! It’s a bright, bright day!”

One sunny morning, Roo climbed the ladder with a little thump-thump-thump. Roo opened the round window and took a deep sniff. “Oh!” Roo wrinkled a nose. The air smelled a bit dusty and not-so-fresh. Roo looked around. There were crinkly wrappers on the floor, a tipped-over water bowl, and a pile of old paper and broken crayons in the corner.

“My treehouse feels sad,” Roo whispered. “And the tree is my friend. I want to take care of it.” Roo put a paw on the wooden wall. “I’ll fix it.”

Roo clapped and hummed, “We can clean, we can care, we can make fresh air!” The words made Roo feel brave and busy. But Roo was just one joey. The job felt big.

Roo picked up a wrapper. “I can start with one,” Roo said. Pick. Drop. Plop into a little bucket.

Then Roo had an idea. “Music makes work fun. Music helps us do things together.” Roo grabbed a tiny drum, a blue shaker filled with beans, and a stick that made a tink-tink sound.

Roo tapped the drum softly. “Tup-tup, tup-tup.” The sound bounced around the treehouse like a happy heartbeat. Roo opened the window wider, and the breeze brought a sweet leaf smell.

Roo called out the window, “Friends near and far, let’s care for where we are!”

First, a kookaburra laughed from a nearby branch. “Hoo-hoo-hoo! I can help,” it chattered. A parrot flapped closer. “Squawk! I can help, too!”

Roo smiled. “Let’s make a clean-up band!”

Roo set a rhythm. “Tap-tap, shake-shake, tink-tink!” The birds joined in with chirps and clicks. Roo picked up wrappers to the beat. “One, two, pick and drop. Three, four, don’t let it stop!”

Soon, Roo’s tablet blinked with a little beep. It was a video call! On the screen came faces from all around the world: a panda cub in China with a tiny bell, a squirrel in Canada with wooden spoons, and a child in Kenya with a tin can drum.

“Hi, Roo!” said the child, waving. “We love your song.”

“We are cleaning, too!” squeaked the squirrel, tapping spoons. “Clink-clank!”

The panda rang the bell, “Ding!” and smiled. “We care for bamboo and streams.”

Roo’s heart felt warm like sun on a leaf. “We are a world team,” Roo said. “We care for our homes together!”

They all made the same steady beat. Roo counted, “One, two, ready, do!” The music kept everyone moving in time—good coordination that felt like a dance. Roo swept the floor with a soft swish. The parrot sorted the paper. The kookaburra pulled a string to lift the water bowl and set it upright. The panda on the screen sorted leaves from plastic. The squirrel stacked small sticks for compost. The child placed bottles into a blue bag for recycling.

“This goes in trash,” Roo said, dropping a sticky wrapper into a bin. “This goes in recycling,” Roo said, placing a plastic bottle into another bin. “And these orange peels can feed the soil,” Roo added, patting a compost bucket. The words felt simple and good. Trash. Recycle. Compost.

The treehouse began to shine. Light flowed in, and the air smelled clean. Roo felt proud and calm. “Thank you, team,” Roo said. “Our music helped our hands and paws and wings work together.”

But Roo saw one more problem. The wind blew a paper bird out the window. It fluttered down, down, toward the ground. “Oh no!” Roo gasped. “Litter can hurt the earth.”

Roo took a careful hop down the ladder, drum in one paw. “Tap-tap,” Roo played to stay brave. The paper bird landed by a tiny flower with yellow petals. Roo picked it up gently. “Hello, flower,” Roo whispered. “We will keep you safe.” Roo put the paper bird into the recycling bag.

Back in the treehouse, the friends played a happy ending tune. “Tup-tup, shake-shake, tink-tink!” The birds sang. The child clapped. The panda rang the bell. The squirrel clicked the spoons. Roo danced, tail swish-swish, feet boing-boing.

Roo looked out at the bright sky. “We are global helpers,” Roo said. “We care for our tree, our town, and our earth.”

Roo made a simple plan:
- Keep a trash bin, a recycling bin, and a compost bucket.
- Sing a clean-up song each day.
- Share the song with friends everywhere.

Roo smiled at the treehouse walls. “Thank you, tree,” Roo said softly. “We will keep you healthy.”

The breeze smelled like leaves and lemon soap. The floor felt smooth and dry. The window showed blue sky and a far-away cloud like a soft sheep. Roo hugged the drum and whispered, “We did it together.”

And the lesson was clear and kind: When we clean, sort, and care, we help the earth. Music helps us work together, here and around the world. Little hands can make a big, bright difference.

The End.
