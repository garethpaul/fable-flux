# Tiko the Tortoise and the Pantry Parade

Tiko was a wise tortoise with a shiny, pebble-green shell. He lived in a cozy house with a big, wooden pantry. The pantry was cool and quiet. It smelled like cinnamon, cocoa, and fresh bread. When Tiko walked, his shell made a soft tap-tap on the floor.

One sunny morning, Tiko peeked into the pantry. “Good morning, foods,” he said. “I am curious. Where do you come from?”

He took a deep breath. He smelled sweet banana, crunchy crackers, and salty soup. He touched a bag of rice. It felt smooth and sandy. He listened. The jars didn’t speak, of course, but Tiko was wise. He knew how to ask calm questions and listen with his heart.

“I want to learn about the world,” Tiko said. “I can start right here.”

He wiggled forward and looked at a jar. “Hello, peanut butter,” Tiko said.

The jar was sticky on the outside. Tiko gave it a gentle wipe with a cloth. “Ah,” he said. “We must respect our food. We keep it clean.”

He read the label slowly. “Pea-nut but-ter. From across the ocean.” He blinked. “The world is big. People grow and make food in many places.”

Tiko took initiative. He found a little notebook. He wrote big round letters: “Where Food Comes From.” His handwriting was wobbly, but that was okay. He was trying, and trying is brave.

“Hello, rice,” Tiko said to the bag with tiny white grains. He ran his paw over it. Shh-shh. It sounded like gentle rain. The label said it came from far away too. “Rice grows in wet, green fields,” Tiko whispered. He could almost hear the splash of water and see the sun on the water’s skin.

Next, he met a jar of honey. It was golden and glowed when the light came in. “Bees make you,” said Tiko softly. “Thank you, bees.” He touched the warm glass and imagined buzzing and flowers.

A little neighbor mouse named Mina peeked in. “Hi, Tiko,” she squeaked. “What are you doing?”

“I am learning about the world by learning about the food,” Tiko said with a smile. “Come see.”

Mina’s whiskers twitched. “I like learning!”

They looked together. There were noodles from a place with tall mountains. There were sweet mangoes from a place with warm winds. There were beans from a place with red soil. There was even cocoa powder from a place with deep, green forests.

Mina’s eyes grew wide. “All from different places?” she asked.

Tiko nodded. “Yes. People in many lands plant, pick, and pack. The world is like a big family kitchen. We share.”

Mina sniffed the cocoa. “It smells like chocolate,” she giggled.

Tiko nodded again. “It smells like stories too.”

Just then, they found a problem. A bag of apples had a soft spot. One apple was mushy and smelled a little sour. “Oh dear,” said Mina. “This one is going bad.”

Tiko took a calm breath. “We have a problem. We must find a solution.” He tapped his shell. Tap-tap. “Let’s use our curiosity and our care.”

First, they looked at the good apples. They felt firm and cool. They made a crisp sound when Tiko tapped them. “These are fine,” he said.

Then they looked at the mushy apple. “This one needs help,” said Mina.

Tiko had an idea. “We can save food when we act with care and respect. Let’s wash the good apples, and let’s not waste the mushy one. We can make applesauce.”

Mina clapped. “Yes! Initiative!”

They washed their paws. They washed the good apples and set them in a basket. The basket was lined with a soft cloth that smelled like sunshine. For the mushy apple, Tiko cut away the bad part very carefully. “We must be safe,” he said. “If food is too spoiled, we don’t eat it. But a little soft part we can trim with care.” The safe pieces went into a small pot.

They added a spoon of water and a tiny drizzle of honey. The pot bubbled. Blub-blub. Warm cinnamon scent filled the air. Mina giggled. “It smells like hugs!”

As the applesauce cooked, Tiko drew a simple map in his notebook. He made a big circle for the world. He put tiny dots for rice, cocoa, mangoes, and beans. “This helps us remember,” he said. “Our pantry is part of the world. The world is part of our pantry.”

Mina pointed. “We are all connected!”

Tiko smiled. “Yes. We show respect by learning, by not wasting, and by thanking the people and places that help us.”

They tasted the applesauce. It was warm and sweet. “Thank you, farmer,” said Tiko. “Thank you, bees. Thank you, sun and rain.”

Mina nodded. “Thank you, world.”

They put labels on the jars and baskets: “Rice—wet fields,” “Cocoa—green forests,” “Mango—warm winds.” The labels helped them remember the stories behind the foods. They decided to check the pantry every week, to keep it tidy and to save food before it spoiled.

Tiko looked at the neat shelves. He felt proud and peaceful. “Today we learned about the world,” he said. “We were curious. We took action. We showed respect.”

Mina hugged his shell. “And we found a tasty solution!”

Tiko chuckled. “Yes. When we love the world, the world tastes sweeter.”

The End.
