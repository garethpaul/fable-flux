# Sunny Hops and Helpful Feelings

In a sunny backyard, a joyful kangaroo joey named Kip woke up with a big yawn and a bigger smile. The grass felt cool under Kip’s toes. The sky was bright and blue. Birds sang, “Cheep-cheep!” and the warm sun kissed Kip’s nose.

“Today is my big backyard day,” Kip said, bouncing a tiny hop. “I will help. I will learn. I will be brave.”

Mama Roo waved from the porch. “Have fun, Kip. Check your feelings, and use your words.”

Kip patted his chest. “I can do that.” His heart felt happy and bouncy, like a soft drum.

Kip hopped to the little garden bed. The soil smelled earthy and rich. Mrs. Perez, the neighbor, was there. She wore a bright hat and a bright smile. “Buenos días, Kip,” she said. “That means ‘good morning’ in Spanish.”

“Boon-ess dee-ahs!” Kip tried. His cheeks felt warm. Trying new words was fun.

Mrs. Perez laughed kindly. “Wonderful try. Would you like to water the tomatoes?”

“Yes!” said Kip. He held the small watering can. The water made a shhh sound and tiny rainbows in the sun. The tomatoes glowed red and round.

Suddenly, a bee buzzed close. “Bzzzz!” Kip jumped back. His tummy felt tight. His ears felt hot.

“I feel scared,” Kip said, putting a paw on his belly. “Bee, please give me space.”

The bee floated to a flower and sipped. Mrs. Perez nodded. “You named your feeling. That is brave. The bee is busy and gentle.”

Kip breathed in slow, then out slow. His tummy felt softer. “I can breathe when I feel scared,” he said. He smiled at the bee from a safe step away.

Next, Kip saw a small sign on the grass. It was tilted. It said, “Please keep the gate closed so pets stay safe.” Kip read the words slowly. “This is for everyone,” he said. “It helps the whole street.” Kip pushed the sign into the soil so it stood tall. He clicked the gate closed. Click!

“That is civic caring,” Mrs. Perez said. “You follow rules to help everyone.”

Kip felt proud. Proud felt warm, like sunshine in his chest.

On the other side of the yard, Kip heard a giggle. Mei, a new friend, waved. She wore a red ribbon. “Hi!” she said. “Want to play music?”

“Yes!” Kip said. Mei showed a small drum. Tap-tap. Kip had a shiny bell. Ting-ting.

“Let’s make a kind song,” Mei said. “In my family, we say ‘xie xie’ for thank you. It is Chinese.”

“Shay shay,” Kip tried. He liked the sound. The drum and bell made happy beats that danced with the bird songs. Tap-tap-ting-ting!

As they played, Kip saw a little mess by the sandbox—cups, a shovel, and paper wrappers. His nose wrinkled. “It looks messy,” he said. “I feel a little upset.”

Mei looked too. “We can feel upset,” she said, “and we can help.” She picked up a wrapper. Kip picked up another. The crinkle felt dry and papery in his paws.

Kip said, “I will do my part.” He found the recycling bin with the blue symbol. Together they sorted: paper in recycling, banana peel in compost, and broken bits in the trash. Kip wiped the sand toys and stacked them. Stack, stack, stack.

Kip’s upset feeling shrank. It felt calm now, like quiet water. “Helping made my body feel better,” he said.

“Mine too,” Mei said. “Thank you—xie xie!”

Kip’s tummy rumbled. “I feel hungry,” he said. Mama Roo brought a plate with crunchy carrots, sweet mango, and round crackers. The carrots snapped. The mango was juicy and bright on Kip’s tongue.

“May I pour the juice by myself?” Kip asked.

“Yes,” said Mama Roo. “Use slow hands.”

Kip held the small pitcher. His paws felt a little wiggly. He breathed in, breathed out, and poured. Glug-glug. Only one tiny drop spilled. He wiped it with a cloth. “I did it!” he said.

“That is independence,” Mama Roo said. “You tried, you focused, and you cleaned up.”

Kip’s ears heard a bark. Woof! The neighbor’s puppy stood by the gate, wagging. The gate was closed and safe because Kip had clicked it earlier. The puppy licked the air. “Good gate,” Kip said. His heart felt safe and steady.

After snack, the sun felt warmer, and a little cloud moved over the yard. Kip felt his body slow. “I feel tired,” he said. “I need a rest.”

Mei nodded. “I feel tired too. Let’s make a calm corner.” They put a blanket under the shade. The blanket was soft and cool. They sat and took three slow breaths together. In… out. In… out. In… out.

Kip whispered, “Today I felt scared, proud, upset, hungry, and tired. I used words and I helped.”

Mrs. Perez waved from the garden. “Gracias—thank you—for caring for the yard,” she said.

“De nada,” Kip and Mei said together. Kip grinned. “That means you’re welcome!”

The sun peeked from the cloud, warm and bright again. Kip gave a tiny hop. “My feelings can change, and that is okay. I can breathe, use my words, and help others.”

Mama Roo hugged Kip. The hug felt snug and sweet. “That is the lesson of today,” she said. “You named feelings, cared for your world, and tried things on your own.”

Kip beamed. “I am a joyful joey, and I can care with my heart and my hops!”

The End.
