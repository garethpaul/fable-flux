# Leo the Cub Solves the Laundry Puzzle

In a sunny house with soft rugs and squeaky floors, a brave little lion cub named Leo padded into the laundry room. His paws were tiny, his whiskers wiggled, and his golden ears perked up with curiosity. The room smelled like clean soap and warm towels. The washing machine hummed, “Whirr-whirr,” and the dryer went, “Thump-thump-thump,” like a sleepy drum.

Leo loved to help. He wanted to do a brave job today.

“Hello, laundry room,” Leo whispered. “I am here to solve a problem.”

He looked around carefully. He used his eyes, his nose, and his ears. On the blue laundry basket sat three socks—one green, one blue, and one red. On the floor lay two more socks—one blue with stars and one green with stripes. The socks were all mixed up!

“Oh no,” said Leo. “The socks lost their friends.”

He sat and thought. He did not pounce. He did not rush. He used critical-thinking. “First,” he said, “I will sort by color.” He picked up the blue socks. One was plain blue. One was blue with stars. Then he carefully picked up the green socks. One was plain green. One was green with stripes. He looked for a red friend too, but there was only one red sock.

“Hm,” Leo hummed. “I see a pattern. Some socks match by color and pictures. Some socks do not match at all.” He gently tapped his chin. “What is the best plan?”

Just then, the dryer door squeaked open. Warm air puffed out with a soft, sweet smell. A fluffy towel slid out and said, “Whoops!”

“Hello, Towel,” Leo giggled. “I have a sock problem.”

A little plastic basket rolled closer and squeaked, “Maybe toss them all away!”

Leo shook his head. “We do not throw things away so fast. We can reuse, or we can recycle.”

The basket wobbled. “What does recycle mean?”

Leo stood tall, even though he was small. “Recycle means we use something old in a new way, or we send it to be made into something new, so we do not make extra trash.”

The washing machine hummed, “Whirr-whirr,” like it agreed.

Leo took a deep breath. He made a plan. “Step one,” he said softly. “Find pairs. Step two: If there is no pair, think of a new job for the single sock. Step three: Ask for help and make a deal—negotiate—so everyone feels good.”

He held up the plain blue sock and the blue star sock. “You do not match,” he said kindly. “But you can be friends! You can be happy play socks.” The socks seemed to wiggle with joy.

He paired the green sock with stripes and the plain green sock. “You can be garden socks,” he said. “You can go outside and help with plants.”

Leo looked at the lonely red sock. “You have no partner,” he said. “But you are still special.”

He blinked and used his imagination. He sniffed the soft fabric and rubbed it with his paw. “You are cozy. You can be a puppet, or a dust cloth, or a pouch for crayons!”

The basket squeaked, “I like puppet!”

The dryer thumped, “Thump-thump. Dust cloth is useful.” The towel flapped and said, “Crayon pouch sounds neat!”

Leo put his paw on his heart. “We need to agree. Let’s negotiate. What is fair?”

He held up three ideas on his paws. “Idea one: Puppet for play time. Idea two: Dust cloth for clean time. Idea three: Crayon pouch for art time. Which helps the most today?”

He listened. He did not interrupt. He looked at each helper. “Play, clean, or art?”

The washing machine hummed, “Whirr-whirr,” and shook a little, sprinkling a tiny bit of lint on the floor.

Leo saw the lint and smiled. “I think the house needs clean time.” He looked at the red sock. “Will you be a dust cloth?”

The red sock was silent, but Leo patted it kindly. “If we try this, we can wash you and use you again. That is recycling by reusing.”

He waited. The basket squeaked, “Yes! Yes! Clean time!”

Leo nodded. “We agree. Thank you for talking it out.” He carefully slipped his paw into the red sock and made a dust cloth puppet. “Hello, Dusty,” he giggled.

“Hello, Leo,” he squeaked in a silly voice. Then he gently wiped the lint. Swipe, swipe. The floor looked bright. He wiped the dryer door. Shine, shine. He dusted the shelf. Swish, swish.

“Good job,” the towel cheered.

Leo placed the two “friend pairs” of socks—blue and green—in a small box. He drew a label with a stubby crayon: Play Socks and Garden Socks. He put Dusty the red sock on a hook marked Cleaning.

He sat down and took a slow breath. “I looked. I thought. I made a plan. I talked to others. We found a fair answer. We reused and kept things out of the trash. That is smart and kind.”

His tail swished in happy circles. The laundry room smelled like soap and a little bit of warm sunshine. The dryer gave one last gentle, “Thump,” and the washing machine sang, “Whirr!”

Leo smiled. “I am a brave little lion cub, and I can solve problems.”

The End.
