# Nuts on the Rooftop

Sunny the squirrel lived high above the street on a flat, warm rooftop. The roof felt rough under her tiny paws. The wind tickled her whiskers. She could see chimneys, clouds, and a shiny blue sky. “This is my special place,” Sunny chattered, her tail flicking like a soft brown feather.

One cool morning, Sunny had a big plan. “I want to build a snack station!” she said. “I will sort my nuts and make a neat place to work.” She tapped her little paws together. “I can do it!”

Sunny found many nuts. She found small nuts, big nuts, round nuts, and bumpy nuts. She also found smooth pebbles and dry leaves. “So many things,” she whispered. She carried them up the drainpipe, one by one, feeling the metal cool against her paws.

She made a pile on the roof. Plop, plop, plop. The nuts and pebbles rolled and bumped. “Oh no,” Sunny squeaked, “my pile is messy.” A gust of wind came—whooosh!—and some leaves flew away. Sunny’s ears drooped. “I want to do this right.”

Sunny took a deep breath. She wiggled her nose. “I can solve this,” she said. “I can learn new skills.” She looked around. The roof felt wide and open. The sun was bright and warm on her back. A pigeon waddled nearby and cooed.

“Hello,” said Sunny. “I need to make a snack station. But my things keep rolling away.”

The pigeon tilted his head. “Coo-coo. What is your plan?”

Sunny blinked. “I don’t know yet,” she said. “I need a plan.”

“Coo,” said the pigeon softly. “You can try three steps: sort, choose, and set.”

“Sort, choose, and set,” Sunny repeated. She smiled. “That sounds like a skill.”

Sunny started with sort. She touched each nut and looked closely. “Big nuts in this spot,” she said, patting a sunny corner. “Small nuts over here.” She made two neat circles. The big nuts looked like tiny brown moons. The small nuts looked like shiny buttons. She used a light leaf to brush pebbles into their own pile. Swish, swish. “Pebbles in one place, leaves in another,” she hummed. Sorting felt good. It felt calm.

Next, she tried choose. “What should I use to stop things from rolling?” Sunny asked. She picked up a leaf. It was too light. The wind fluttered it away. She picked up a pebble. It was heavy and cool. “This can help,” she said. She tested it. She rolled a nut toward the pebble. Tap! The pebble stopped the nut. “Yes!” Sunny cheered. She lined pebbles in a gentle row to make a tiny wall. Tap, tap, tap went her paws as she moved each stone.

Now it was time to set. Sunny found a flat, shady spot near a chimney. It smelled a little like warm toast. She set a leaf for a mat. She set big nuts on the left and small nuts on the right. She set pebbles at the edge to hold things in place. She made a little sign with a line in the dust: Big → Small. “There,” she said proudly. “My snack station is ready.”

Just then, the wind blew again—whooosh!—and a small nut rolled. But the pebble wall stopped it. Bump! Sunny giggled. “My plan works!”

A tiny mouse peeked from a crack in the roof. “Squeak! What is this?” he asked. His whiskers twitched.

“This is my snack station,” said Sunny. “I sorted, I chose, and I set. Now everything is neat, and nothing blows away.”

The mouse sniffed the air. He smelled the nutty, warm smell of the snacks. “Squeak. That is smart. How did you learn it?”

Sunny puffed up with pride. “I practiced a skill. I made a plan. When I had a problem, I used steps.”

The mouse smiled. “Can I help?”

“Yes,” said Sunny. “Can you count?” She pointed with her paw. “Let’s count the big nuts: one, two, three. And the small nuts: one, two, three, four.”

They counted together. Their voices sounded happy and light. The rooftop felt friendly and bright. Sunny’s tail swished with joy.

Later, a gray cloud covered the sun. The wind grew cool and quick. “Time to store the snacks,” said Sunny. She looked at her station. “I will use my plan again.”

She sorted the nuts into two leaf baskets she had made. She chose strong leaves for the bottom and pebbles to hold the edges. She set each basket near the chimney where the wind was softer. Rustle, rustle went the leaves. Clink, clink went the pebbles. Soon, everything was safe and tidy.

The mouse clapped his tiny paws. “Squeak! You did it.”

Sunny smiled. “When we sort, choose, and set, we can solve problems. We can stay calm. We can be ready.”

The wind slowed. The cloud moved on. Warm sun touched the roof again. Sunny and the mouse shared one big nut and one small nut. Crunch, crunch. It tasted sweet and toasty.

Sunny looked at her neat station and her new friend. “Today I learned a new skill,” she said softly. “I learned to make a plan, try ideas, and keep things organized. I feel proud.”

The mouse nodded. “Me too. Next time, we can build a water station.”

Sunny’s eyes sparkled. “Yes! We will sort, choose, and set again.”

They both laughed. The rooftop felt like a happy, helpful place.

The End.
