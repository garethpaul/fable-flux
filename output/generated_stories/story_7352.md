# Cubby Makes It Right

In a comfortable nursery, soft and sunny, a friendly bear cub named Cubby woke up with a happy wiggle. The curtains were leafy green, the rug was fuzzy like moss, and the air smelled like warm toast and pine. Cubby’s favorite wooden blocks sat in a neat basket, and his tiny watering can waited beside a row of potted plant friends: Sprout the pea plant, Fern the fern, and Sunny the sunflower.

“Good morning, friends!” Cubby hummed, patting the soft rug with his paw. He loved caring for the plants. He loved the cozy nursery. He loved helping.

Today, Cubby felt extra bouncy. “I will build a big block city,” he said. He poured the blocks with a clatter—clack, clack, clack!—and the sound echoed like little raindrops. He stacked a tall tower. He made a bridge. He made a tiny road for his toy wagon.

“Be careful, Cubby,” whispered Fern, her leaves brushing the window with a swish. “Slow and steady.”

“I can do it!” Cubby chirped. He stood on his tippy toes, reaching, reaching—

Uh-oh! His paw bumped the watering can. The can tipped. Water splashed. “Splash, splish, sploosh!” The water ran across the rug and into the block city. The tower tumbled. The bridge broke. The little road slid apart. Even Sunny’s leaves shivered and shook.

Cubby’s eyes went big and round. “Oh no,” he whispered. His tummy felt wiggly and tight. His ears felt warm. He didn’t mean to spill. He didn’t mean to break his city. He didn’t mean to scare Sunny.

Cubby sat very still on the soft rug. He listened. Drip. Drip. Drip. He could hear the water drops. He could hear his breath going in and out, slow and deep, like ocean waves. His heart slowed down.

He looked at the plants. “I’m sorry, Sunny. I’m sorry, Sprout. I’m sorry, Fern,” Cubby said softly. “I made a mess.”

Sunny lifted her bright face toward the window. “It was an accident,” she said, gentle as sunshine. “But what can you do to make it right?”

Cubby thought. He put his paw on his chest and took another deep breath. “I can fix it,” he said. His voice felt stronger. “I can help.”

First, Cubby found a towel. It was soft and fluffy, and it smelled clean. He pressed it on the wet rug. Squish, squish. He patted and dabbed until the rug felt only a little damp. “I am cleaning up my spill,” Cubby told himself. “I am making amends.”

Next, Cubby checked the plants. He touched the soil with one careful claw. “Not too soggy,” he said. “We don’t want to waste water.” He tipped the can back upright and set it far from the blocks. “Water is precious. We use what we need, not more,” he said, wagging his little tail. Sustainability means taking good care of our things and our world.

Then, Cubby picked up the blocks. “Clack, clack.” He sorted them by shape and size. “Squares here. Triangles here. Long rectangles here.” He hummed a little song: “Clean up, help up, make it right. Bear cub heart feels kind and light.” His hum felt like warm cocoa in his chest.

Sprout swayed in his pot. “Thank you, Cubby,” he whispered. “I feel safe again.”

Cubby smiled. “I am sorry I scared you. I will be more careful.”

He looked around the nursery. The sunlight made golden squares on the rug. The air smelled like pine and soap. Cubby’s tummy felt calm again. He had made amends with kind actions.

“Would you like help rebuilding?” asked Fern, tapping a leaf on a triangle block.

“Yes, please!” Cubby grinned. “We can build a city that is safe for plants.”

Together, they made a plan. Cubby placed a tray under the pots to catch any drips. “This will save water and keep the rug dry,” he said. He put the watering can on a little shelf. “This is its home,” he said. He left a wide path between the block city and the plant corner. “This is a caring pathway,” he told the plants. “No bumps. No spills.”

Cubby set the blocks again—tap, tap, tap—slow and steady. He didn’t rush. He listened to his paws and his breath. When he felt bouncy, he paused and took a deep, slow sip of air. In and out. Calm and careful. His tower stood tall. His bridge was strong. His little road was smooth and dry.

Sunny beamed. “What a lovely city,” she sang. “You fixed the mess, you made a plan, and you showed your caring heart.”

Cubby’s cheeks felt warm in a good way. “I made it right,” he said. “I said sorry with words and with helping.”

Just then, Cubby heard a tiny drip. Plink. A drop fell into the tray under the pots. Not on the rug. Cubby clapped. “The tray works! We are saving the rug and the water.”

He put away the towel to dry and recycled a crumpled paper tag from the plant pots in the blue bin. “We can use things again,” he said. “That helps the Earth.”

At last, Cubby sat on the fuzzy rug and looked at his city and his plant friends. The nursery felt cozy and kind. The sun felt like a hug on his fur.

“I learned something today,” Cubby said. “When I make a mistake, I can stop, breathe, say sorry, and help fix it. I can take care of my things, my friends, and the Earth.”

Sunny nodded. “That is the way of a helpful heart.”

Cubby smiled a big bear cub smile. “I feel proud,” he whispered.

The End.
