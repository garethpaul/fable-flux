# Fox Kit and the Clean Green Art

The creative art room smelled like crayons and pine soap. Sunlight splashed across big tables. Paper rustled like leaves. Paints blinked in bright colors—red, blue, green, yellow. Brushes stood tall like tiny trees.

A curious fox kit named Fern padded in on soft paws. Her ears perked. Her nose twitched. “Oooh,” Fern whispered. “So many colors! So many things to make!”

Ms. Maple, the gentle teacher, smiled. “Welcome, Fern. In our art room, we make beautiful things, and we take care of our room and our Earth.”

Fern’s eyes shone. “I want to make a big picture of a forest!”

“You can,” said Ms. Maple. “Here are paints, paper, and a water cup. And here is a cozy spot for you.” She pointed to a table. “Remember, each friend has their own space. We ask before touching someone else’s art.”

Fern nodded. “I will ask. I will care.”

Next to Fern sat a small raccoon named River. River had a sparkly sticker on his shirt. He was gluing old paper scraps to make a sun. “Hi,” he said softly.

“Hi!” said Fern. “Your sun is shiny!”

River smiled. “Thank you.”

Fern dipped her brush into blue paint. Swish, swish. The brush whispered on the paper like the wind. She dipped into green. Swirl, swirl. Leaves and grass grew on her page. She hummed. “Green is for trees. Trees help us breathe.”

Ms. Maple nodded. “Yes. Trees are helpers. And guess what? We can help trees too. We can use less, reuse, and recycle.”

Fern’s ears wiggled. “Reuse?”

“It means we use something again,” Ms. Maple said, holding up a box of clean, dry bottle caps. “These are from juice bottles. We washed them. We can make art with them instead of throwing them away.”

River clapped. “Cap art!”

Fern giggled. “Cap forest!”

She reached toward River’s glue. River pulled it closer. “This is my glue,” he whispered. His tail flicked. “Please ask.”

Fern blinked and pulled back her paw. Her cheeks felt warm. “Oh! I’m sorry. May I please use the glue when you are done?”

River’s shoulders relaxed. “Yes. I will share when I finish this sun beam.”

Fern breathed out. Her chest felt light again. Ms. Maple smiled. “Good asking and good listening. That is respecting personal space and personal things.”

Fern looked around. She saw paper bits on the floor. She saw a drip of paint sliding toward the edge of the table. Plop! It dropped onto the ground.

“Oh no!” said Fern. “The room looks messy.”

Ms. Maple nodded kindly. “Art can be messy. But we clean as we go. That keeps our room safe, and it helps our Earth. Less waste. Less water to scrub later.”

Fern wanted to hurry and finish her forest, but the drip on the floor made her paws itch. She took a cloth from the clean basket. Wipe, wipe. The floor felt smooth again. She picked up the paper bits and put them in the recycle bin with the blue sign.

“Thank you,” said Ms. Maple. “You are caring for our room and our planet.”

River held out the glue. “Your turn!”

“Thank you,” said Fern. She dabbed glue along a painted branch. Tap, tap. She pressed a green bottle cap onto the glue. It clicked softly. “Listen!” she said. “Click!”

River giggled. “Click, click!”

Soon, Fern’s picture had a blue sky, a tall tree, and cap leaves. It looked like a happy forest. She wanted to add a big river too. She looked at River’s paper. His sun shone bright. A little glitter bottle sat near his paw.

Fern reached for the glitter. River hugged it close. His eyes got big. “This is my special glitter,” he said. “Please don’t touch.”

Fern froze. Her paw hung in the air like a leaf on a still day. She felt a tight ball in her tummy. She wanted to say, “But I need glitter!” Instead, she took a breath. In, out. Her ears drooped a little. “Okay,” she said softly. “I won’t touch. May I please borrow some glitter when you are ready?”

River looked at his sun. He thought. Then he nodded. “We can share. I can pour a little on your paper.”

Fern’s tail swished. “Thank you.” She held her paper out. River sprinkled a tiny bit. It fell like shiny rain. Tinkle, tinkle. The river on her picture sparkled.

Ms. Maple clapped. “I see asking, I see waiting, and I see sharing. You are taking care of each other.”

After they finished, the tables had paint dots and paper scraps again. Ms. Maple rang a gentle bell. “Clean-up time! Caring for our art room helps the Earth. We put caps back, we wash just one cup of water each, and we sort our trash and recycling.”

Fern liked the bell’s soft ring. She hummed as she worked. She poured extra paint back into the jars. Plip, plop. She wiped brushes, slow and careful. She folded a cloth and wiped the table. Wipe, wipe. River picked up scraps and sorted them. Snip edges went to trash. Big, clean pieces went to the reuse box.

Fern pointed to a torn paper corner. “Can we save that?”

Ms. Maple peered. “It’s clean and big enough. Yes. Reuse!”

Fern’s chest warmed. “I can help the Earth,” she said.

“You are helping,” said Ms. Maple. “You are using less, reusing things, and keeping our room clean. You are also asking before touching and listening when a friend says no.”

Fern and River stood by the drying rack. Their pictures glowed in the sun. Fern’s forest sparkled. River’s sun shone.

River looked at Fern. “Thank you for asking.”

Fern smiled. “Thank you for sharing.”

They bumped paws gently. Tap.

Ms. Maple hung a small sign above their art. It read: “We Care for Our Earth. We Care for Each Other.”

Fern’s tail curled with pride. She whispered, “I can make art. I can listen. I can care for the Earth.”

“Yes,” said Ms. Maple. “Little paws can do big, kind things.”

Fern nodded. “I will remember: use less, reuse, recycle. Ask first. Listen to no. Clean as we go.”

And the creative art room felt bright, peaceful, and green.

The End.
