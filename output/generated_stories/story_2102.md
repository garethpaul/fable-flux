# Ellie’s Careful Trunk

Ellie was a gentle elephant calf with soft gray ears and a curious trunk. One sunny afternoon, she visited Grandpop’s garage workshop. The workshop smelled like warm wood and orange oil. Sunlight made dust sparkle in the air. Tools hung in neat rows on the wall—bright wrenches, shiny screwdrivers, and a long tape measure that went zip and zzzip.

Grandpop Elephant hummed a song. “Today we fix the squeaky wagon,” he said. He patted the little red wagon that belonged to Ellie’s friend, Max. “It squeaks when it rolls. We must use careful hands and careful hearts.”

Ellie’s feet did a tiny hop. She loved the workshop. She loved the sounds: clink, tap, whirr. Her trunk swished with excitement.

“I want to help!” Ellie said.

“You can,” Grandpop said, smiling. “But remember our family rule. In the workshop, we slow down. We breathe. We listen. That is our tradition.”

Ellie nodded hard. “I can slow down,” she said. But inside, she felt the wiggles. Her trunk wiggled. Her toes wiggled. Her whole elephant self felt like a wobbly jelly.

Grandpop showed her a small, soft cloth. “First, we wipe the wheels. Gentle circles.”

Ellie tried to make gentle circles, but her trunk swooped fast—whoosh! The cloth bumped the paint. The wagon rocked. “Oops!” Ellie squeaked.

Grandpop held up a big, calm hand. “Workshop pause,” he said softly. “Let’s use our steps. One, two, three.”

“What steps?” Ellie asked. She wanted to hurry and fix the wagon. The squeak was calling to her.

Grandpop took a slow breath. “Step one: Stop. Place your feet like sturdy blocks.” He spread his huge feet. Ellie copied him.

“Step two: Breathe like the bellows.” He puffed in, big and slow, then out, soft and warm. Ellie breathed in, filling her belly like a balloon. She breathed out, letting her shoulders melt.

“Step three: Say what you need,” Grandpop said. “Use words to guide your trunk.”

Ellie whispered, “I need to move slow. My trunk wants to race.” She touched her chest. “I can help it slow down.”

Together, they counted, “One… two… slow.” Ellie felt her wiggles soften. Her trunk settled like a sleepy snake. The dust sparkles in the sun seemed to float slower too.

“Good,” Grandpop said. “Now gentle circles.”

Ellie made small, careful loops with the cloth. Wipe, wipe, wipe. The wheels shone. She smiled. “I did it!”

“Responsible work,” Grandpop said. “You took care of the wagon and your feelings.”

Just then, Max the mouse peeked in the door. “Is my wagon ready?” he chirped. His whiskers trembled with worry. “I need it for the parade. My nana taught me to pull our special drum.”

Ellie looked at Max’s bright eyes. “We’re fixing it,” she said kindly. “We want you to have your drum parade.”

Grandpop nodded. “We will share our tradition too. In our family, we test the wheels. We listen for the squeak.”

He lifted the wagon. “Ellie, bring me the oil can.”

Ellie saw the oil can on a high shelf. Her trunk popped up. She wanted to show she could reach it fast. Zoom! Her trunk swished. The oil can wobbled. A tiny drip fell—plop!—onto the floor.

Max squeaked and hopped back. “Slippy!”

Ellie’s ears drooped. “I was too fast,” she said. “I forgot to pause.”

Grandpop put a gentle hand on her shoulder. “You noticed. That is responsible. Now we fix the drip.”

Ellie took a rag and wiped the oil carefully. “Stop. Breathe. Say what I need,” she murmured. In… out… “I need to move slow.”

Max watched. “I get wiggly too,” he said. “When I drum, I want to go bang-bang-zoom! But Nana says, ‘Tap-tap, tiny paws. Feel the beat.’”

Ellie smiled. “We both can slow down.”

Grandpop lowered the oil can. “Let’s compromise,” he said. “I will hold the can. Ellie, you squeeze tiny drops when I say ‘now.’ Max, you watch for drips and say ‘stop’ if you see any.”

“That’s teamwork,” Max said proudly.

They worked together. “Now,” Grandpop said.

Squeeze… drip. No spill.

“Now,” he said again.

Squeeze… drip. No spill.

Max’s ears perked. “No drips! Stop,” he said when a shiny bead grew too big. Ellie lifted her trunk. “Stopped,” she said, feeling strong and steady.

Grandpop spun the wheels. The wagon rolled—no squeak! It made a soft shh sound on the floor. “We did it,” he said.

Ellie clapped her feet. Max tapped his tiny drum. “Shall we test it outside?” he asked.

They rolled the wagon to the driveway. The warm air smelled like oranges and summer. Birds chirped. Max climbed in with his drum. Ellie pulled the handle slowly. The wheels went shh-shh, smooth and quiet.

“Thank you,” Max said, smiling up at Ellie. “You were gentle and careful.”

Ellie felt proud. “I used my steps,” she said. “Stop. Breathe. Say what I need.”

Grandpop leaned on the doorframe, happy. “That is our family tradition,” he said. “We pass it on. Careful hands, careful hearts.”

Max hugged his drum. “And I will share my drumming tradition,” he said. He tapped a soft beat: tap-tap, pause, tap. Ellie swung her trunk to the rhythm, slow and steady.

When it was time to go, Ellie looked back at the neat tools and the clean floor. She put the rag in the basket, the cloth on the shelf, and the oil can in its safe spot. “Being responsible feels good,” she said.

Grandpop winked. “Self-control helps the work go well, and it helps friends feel safe.”

Ellie took one more slow breath. She felt calm like a quiet pond. She felt strong like a big, steady tree. “I can help my body,” she said softly. “I can help my friends.”

Max waved from the wagon. “See you at the parade! Tap-tap, pause!”

Ellie smiled. “Shh-shh, slow and steady,” she replied.

The End.
