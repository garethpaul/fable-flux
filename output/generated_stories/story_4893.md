# Momo’s Brave Drum Day

Momo the monkey loved the playful living room. Sunlight made warm stripes on the soft rug. Pillows were bright like candy. The couch felt bouncy like a trampoline. The air smelled like oranges from a bowl on the table.

Momo’s tail swished with joy. “Today, I will practice my drum,” Momo said. His drum was small and round, with colorful patterns from Grandma’s home far away. It felt smooth under his furry fingers. Tap, tap, tap. The sound was warm and happy.

Mama Monkey smiled. “Be brave, Momo. Use your big, kind voice if you need it.”

“I can do it,” Momo whispered, touching his drum gently. He took a brave breath. In-out. In-out.

Ding-dong! The doorbell rang. Momo’s friends bounced in. There was Pip the puppy, Zara the zebra, and Nia the bunny. They giggled and wiggled. The living room suddenly sounded like a playground—paws tapping, hooves hopping, ears flopping.

“What are we playing?” Pip asked, sniffing the orange smell.

“I want to practice my drum,” Momo said. “It is from my family. When I play, I remember Grandma’s songs.”

“Cool!” said Nia, clapping. “Let’s listen!”

Zara tilted her head. “I’ve never heard a drum like that.”

Momo felt a small flutter in his tummy. He took a brave breath. Tap, tap… tap-tap-tap. The drum thumped softly, then louder, like footsteps in a happy parade.

Pip wagged. “I like it! But can we also play tag? I want to run!”

Momo hugged his drum. “I need a little time to practice first,” he said, using his kind, clear voice. “Then we can play tag.”

“That’s fair,” Nia said, nodding.

Zara shuffled her hooves. “It is loud. I don’t like loud,” she said softly.

Momo paused. He looked at Zara’s ears. They twitched. “Thank you for telling me,” Momo said. “We can make it softer.” He placed a pillow next to the drum and played with gentle fingers. Tap… tap… ta-taaap. The sound became soft like a whispering heartbeat.

Zara smiled. “That’s better. Thank you.”

Momo felt warm in his chest. He was brave. He used his voice, and he listened too.

Mama brought a tray of orange slices. The sweet smell danced around them. “Here is a practice snack,” she said. “Being brave can make you hungry.”

They giggled and ate. Juice dripped on their fingers. “Sticky!” Pip laughed.

Momo wiped his hands and looked at his friends. “Would you like to try my drum? It is special to my family. Grandma sings, and we drum together.”

Nia’s eyes shone. “I would love to!”

Momo showed them how to sit steady and tap with soft hands. “We say thank you to the drum,” he said. “We take turns.”

Pip tapped too fast. “Oops!” He knocked the drum. It wobbled. Momo caught it and held it close. His cheeks felt hot. “Please be gentle,” he said, voice steady and brave. “This drum is precious to me.”

Pip’s ears drooped. “I’m sorry, Momo.”

Momo nodded. “Thank you for saying sorry. Let’s try again, slowly.”

They did a practice rhythm together: tap, tap, tap-tap. Tap, tap, tap-tap. The living room filled with friendly sound. Nia hummed a soft tune. Zara swayed her tail. Pip kept time with a soft paw. The drum beat felt like a big hug.

Suddenly, the doorbell rang again. It was Mr. Owl from next door. “Hoo-hoo! I heard music,” he said, blinking behind his glasses. “It sounds lovely.”

“It’s my family drum,” Momo said. “We are practicing and taking turns.”

Mr. Owl nodded. “I like how you asked for what you needed and also listened to your friends. That is brave and kind.”

Momo’s heart fluttered in a happy way. “Thank you.”

“May I share a shaker from my home?” Mr. Owl asked, lifting a small gourd with beads inside. “It makes a quiet rain sound.”

“Yes, please!” Zara said.

Momo smiled wide. “We can make a little band. Soft and sweet.”

They played together—drum like a heartbeat, shaker like rain, hummed song like a breeze. The living room felt cozy and bright. The sunlight stripes seemed to dance on the rug.

After the song, they all clapped. Pip wagged so hard his tail thumped the couch. “That was fun! Now tag?”

Momo giggled. “Yes! I finished my practice. Thank you for waiting.”

They played a gentle game of tag around the pillows, laughing and squeaking and hooting. When they were tired, they flopped on the rug in a happy pile.

Mama Monkey handed out napkins. “I saw brave choices today,” she said. “Momo, you used your voice. You cared for your drum. You listened to your friends. Friends, you were patient and kind.”

Momo nodded, feeling proud. “Being brave means I can say what I need,” he said. “And being kind means I listen to others. We can practice both.”

Zara nuzzled his shoulder. “And we can enjoy each other’s special things.”

Pip barked softly. “And we can fix mistakes with a sorry and a try-again.”

Nia smiled. “We are a great team.”

Momo hugged his drum. “Yes,” he said. “Brave and kind, together.”

The End.
