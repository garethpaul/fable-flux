# Benny Beaver’s Balcony Garden Plan

Benny was a busy beaver with bright eyes and a wide, toothy smile. He lived with Mama Beaver in a cozy apartment. Outside their sliding glass door was a small balcony garden. It had a wooden floor that felt warm under Benny’s paws when the sun shone. Pots sat in neat rows. Some held green leaves. Some had tiny sprouts just peeking out. The air smelled like wet soil and mint. Bees hummed somewhere far away, and a soft breeze tickled Benny’s whiskers.

“I want to help!” Benny said one sunny morning. “I want to make the balcony garden big and beautiful.”

Mama Beaver smiled and patted his head. “You are very helpful, Benny. But our balcony is small. We need a good plan.”

Benny puffed his chest. “I can plan! I can do it!”

He ran to the balcony and looked at the pots. He felt excited and a little wiggly inside. Benny grabbed the watering can. “Water makes plants happy,” he said. He poured a lot of water. Splash! Splash! Water spilled over the sides and puddled on the floor. Mud splashed on Benny’s toes. The mint leaves drooped.

“Oh no,” Benny whispered. His ears drooped too. “I wanted to help. I think I messed up.”

Mama Beaver came to the door with a soft towel. “It’s okay,” she said calmly. “Helping means trying. It also means learning. Let’s take a breath together.”

They breathed in and out. The breeze smelled like soil and sunshine.

“I still want to help,” Benny said, his voice small.

“I know you can,” said Mama. “Let’s make a plan. Planning helps busy beavers do their best.”

Benny’s eyes lit up. “A plan! Yes!” He grabbed a piece of paper and a crayon. He drew a big rectangle for the balcony. He drew circles for the pots. He drew a tiny watering can and made a happy sun.

“Step one,” Mama said, tapping the paper. “We check what each plant needs.”

Benny read the little tags in the pots. He sounded out the words the best he could. “Mint likes a little drink. Tomatoes like more drink. Flowers like morning drink.”

“Great reading,” Mama said. “Step two: We choose the right places. Plants like sun or shade.”

Benny looked at the sun patch on the floor. It was warm on his paws. “Tomatoes go in the sunny spot,” he said. “Mint can sit in the shady corner. Flowers get the front row so they can say hello.”

“Step three,” said Mama, “We set a schedule. That means we choose times.”

Benny nodded. “Water in the morning. Check leaves after lunch. Sweep the floor at the end.”

“Step four,” Mama added, “We use the right tools the right way.”

Benny held up the watering can. It felt heavy in his paws. “I will pour slowly,” he said. He practiced with a dry pot, tipping just a little. Drip, drip, drip. He smiled. “I can do slow.”

“Step five,” Benny said, giggling, “We clean up.”

They wrote the steps on the paper. Benny drew little stars next to each step. He taped the plan to the wall by the door. The tape made a little sticky sound, and the paper fluttered like a flag.

The next morning, the sky was pink and gold. “Plan time!” Benny cheered. He checked the sun. He checked the pots. He talked to the plants. “Good morning, tomatoes. Hello, mint. Hi, flowers!” The leaves felt cool and a little bumpy under his paw.

He poured slowly. Drip, drip, drip. The soil grew dark and smelled rich and sweet. No puddles this time. Benny wiggled his tail with pride.

After lunch, Benny went out again. He peered at a yellow tomato flower. “You look thirsty?” he asked. He touched the soil with one careful claw. “Still damp. Not yet.” Benny felt responsible and careful. He gave the mint a tiny sip because the top felt dry. “Just a little drink,” he whispered.

In the evening, Benny swept the floor. Swish, swish, swish. Dry leaves rustled into a neat pile. He wiped the splash marks from yesterday. The wood looked tidy and clean. The balcony smelled fresh.

All week, Benny followed the plan. Some days were windy. The wind whooshed and made the chimes sing. Some days were hot. The sun made the pots warm like toast. Benny adjusted. “A little more water today,” he said on hot days. “A little less today,” he said on cool days. He felt strong and smart inside.

One morning, a tiny red tomato winked at him from the vine. Benny gasped. “Mama! Mama! Look!”

Mama Beaver stepped out and hugged him. “You did it, Benny! Your careful plan helped the plants grow.”

Benny held the tomato in his paws. It was smooth and shiny. He smelled it. Sweet and bright! He took a small bite. Juicy! It popped like a little sun in his mouth. Benny’s heart felt big.

“I can plan. I can help. I can do it,” he said softly.

Mama nodded. “You believed in yourself. You were responsible. You made a plan and followed it. That is self-confidence.”

Benny looked at his paper plan, now dotted with little water spots and crayon stars. He stuck a new star on the top. “I am a busy beaver with a bright plan,” he said.

The plants swayed as the breeze whispered by, like they were clapping with their leaves. Benny smiled at the balcony garden, small and beautiful, just right for a busy beaver who believed he could.

The End.
