# Miko Finds His Brave Voice

In a comfortable nursery with soft rugs, gentle lamps, and a window full of warm morning light, lived a courageous monkey named Miko. His fur was fuzzy and brown, and his tail curled like a question mark. The room smelled like clean blankets and crayons. A tiny fan hummed, “whirr, whirr,” and a clock ticked, “tick-tock.”

Today was puzzle day. Miko loved puzzles. He wiggled his toes and clapped his hands. “I can do it,” he said to himself. He stretched his arms up high—reach, reach—and took a deep belly breath. In the corner, a bright, colorful jungle puzzle waited. The pieces were smooth and cool in his hands.

Lila the lamb bounced over. “Hi, Miko! Can I play too?”

“Yes,” said Miko. “Let’s take turns.”

They sat on the rug. Their knees touched. They could hear the soft swish of the curtains and smell the orange slices Miss Dori had set out for snack. Miko picked up a round, green piece. “This looks like a leaf,” he said.

Lila nodded. “And this one is a tiger tail!”

Just then, Tiko the turtle scooted in. “I want to try,” he said, picking up three pieces at once. His shell made a gentle tap on the floor.

Miko’s belly felt a little tight. He wanted to say something, but his voice felt small. He watched Tiko push the pieces in the middle. The puzzle got crowded.

Miss Dori, their teacher, smiled from a cozy chair. “Friends, remember: soft hands, kind words, and listening ears.”

Miko looked at his hands. They were shaking a tiny bit. He whispered to himself, “I am brave. I can say what I need.” He put his hand on his chest and felt his heartbeat—thump, thump. He took a slow breath in, like smelling a flower. He blew out slowly, like blowing a bubble—whooo.

“Lila, Tiko,” Miko said in a warm, clear voice, “I want a turn. I want to hold one piece at a time. Can we take turns, please?”

Lila nodded right away. “Yes. We can take turns.”

Tiko blinked. He looked at his hands, full of puzzle pieces. “Oh,” he said softly. “I didn’t know you were waiting. I can put some down.” He placed two pieces back with a little clack, clack.

Miko felt lighter. “Thank you,” he said. “I feel better when I have space.”

They made a plan. Lila would go first, then Miko, then Tiko. Miss Dori gave a thumbs-up. “You used your brave words,” she said. “That is self-advocacy. You told your friends what you needed.”

Miko smiled. His ears felt warm and happy. He picked up a green leaf piece and tried to fit it in. It didn’t go. He tried again slowly. He lined up the little bump with the little hole. Click! It fit. He wriggled his shoulders with joy.

“Nice job,” said Lila. “Good matching!”

Miko’s tail made a soft swish. He felt proud. He could do hard things when he took his time. That was self-regulation. He remembered: first breathe, then speak, then try.

They kept playing. Lila’s turn. Then Miko. Then Tiko. Sometimes a piece did not fit. Miko would pause, take one more belly breath, and try with steady hands. He used both hands together—left hand held the puzzle board, right hand turned the piece. That took coordination. He laughed when the tiger’s whiskers matched. “Whisker, whisker!” he giggled.

After a while, a problem popped up. Two friends from the reading corner, Pip and Zara, wanted to join. “Can we play?” Pip asked, bouncing on his toes. Zara nodded, her braids swishing.

The puzzle was small. There was not much room. Miko felt that tight feeling again. Five hands might be too many. He put his hand on his chest, closed his eyes, and did his flower-and-bubble breath. In, smell the flower. Out, blow the bubble.

He opened his eyes and used his brave words. “I want to keep our plan. We are taking turns: Lila, Miko, Tiko. But I also want to include you. We can make more space. Or we can make a new game.”

Pip blinked. “What new game?”

Miko looked at the empty puzzle box. “We can be Piece Finders,” he said. “You can look for pieces we need. We will say, ‘Please find a green leaf,’ and you can bring it to the person whose turn it is.”

Zara smiled wide. “I can do that!”

Miss Dori clapped softly. “That is a peaceful solution. You kept your plan and made room for friends.”

Pip and Zara stood at the side. When it was Miko’s turn, he said, “Please find a monkey face.” Pip searched. The cardboard pieces made a papery shuffle. “Found it!” he said, handing it to Miko with gentle fingers.

“Thank you,” Miko said. He fit the monkey face—click! The puzzle picture was almost done. The room felt cheerful and calm. They could hear the clock tick and the fan hum. Orange slices smelled sweet.

At last, one piece was left: a bright sun. Miko looked at his friends. “Who wants to do the last piece?” he asked.

Lila looked at Miko. “You used your brave voice today. You can do it.”

Miko’s heart felt warm. He put his hand on the sun. He lined it up, steady and slow. Click! The puzzle was complete. Everyone cheered, “Yay!”

Miss Dori handed out orange slices. The juice was cool and tangy. “Friends,” she said, “what did we learn?”

Miko licked orange juice from his fingers. “I learned to use my brave words to say what I need. That is self-advocacy.”

“I learned to breathe and wait,” said Tiko. “That is self-regulation.”

“I learned to use both hands together,” said Miko again, wiggling his fingers. “That is coordination.”

“And we found a peaceful way to include everyone,” said Lila.

Miko curled his tail like a happy question mark. He had a brave voice, and he knew how to use it. The nursery felt extra cozy now, like a soft hug.

The End.
