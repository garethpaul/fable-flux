# Finn the Fox Kit and the Clean Paws Project

In a bright garage workshop, tools hung like shiny fish on the wall. The air smelled like warm wood and a tiny bit like soap. A curious fox kit named Finn peeked in with his big ears up and his whiskers wiggling.

“Hello?” Finn called. His voice echoed a little in the big, cozy space.

“Hi there, Finn!” said Mr. Rivera, the neighbor. He wore blue overalls and a kind smile. His hands were clean, and he had a soft towel on his shoulder. “Today we build something special.”

Finn’s tail swished. “What is it? What is it?”

Mr. Rivera held up a smooth wooden board and a bright red handle. “A hand-washing station for the community garden. Clean paws help healthy veggies!” He winked. “We will follow directions, step by step.”

Finn bounced. “I like steps! I like bouncing, too.”

Mr. Rivera laughed. “First, we wash our paws.” He pointed to a small sink in the corner. Bubbles puffed like tiny clouds. The water went whoosh and felt warm and tickly on Finn’s fur.

Finn giggled. “It’s squishy and soft! The soap smells like oranges.”

“That’s because it is orange soap,” said Mr. Rivera. “We rub for a song, then rinse. No rush.”

They sang a short song together. “Bubbles here, bubbles there, clean paws show we care.”

Finn watched the bubbles sparkle. His paws felt fresh and light. “Ready!” he chirped.

Mr. Rivera opened a picture guide. The pages showed numbers and drawings. “Step one,” he said, tapping the page. “Measure the wood.” He handed Finn a measuring tape. It clicked and whirred.

Finn tried to hurry. He stretched the tape fast. It slipped and snapped back. “Oops!” he squeaked.

Mr. Rivera’s eyes were gentle. “Slow and steady. We follow directions so we stay safe and do good work.”

“Slow and steady,” Finn repeated. He measured again, this time lining the tape with the little black lines. The tape felt cool and smooth in his paws.

“Good,” said Mr. Rivera. “Step two, mark the wood.” Finn used a soft pencil. It made a tiny, grainy sound on the wood. Scratch, scratch.

“Step three,” Mr. Rivera said, “we drill holes.” He gave Finn small orange ear muffs. “These are for your ears. We protect our ears and our paws.”

Finn put them on. They felt puffy. The drill hummed and buzzed like a big bee. Sawdust floated down like brown snow. It smelled like sweet trees.

Finn wrinkled his nose. “It tickles!”

They paused and swept the dust into a bin. Swish, swish. Mr. Rivera showed Finn how to wipe the bench. “Clean space, clean work,” he said.

“Clean paws, clean space,” Finn echoed. He liked the rhyme.

When the wood pieces were ready, Mr. Rivera brought out a small tin. Inside were colorful stickers with swirls and flowers.

“These are from my family’s culture in Mexico,” he said. “They show bright colors for joy. We can add patterns to celebrate working together.”

Finn’s eyes grew round. “They are so pretty!”

“We can also paint a little pattern from your family,” Mr. Rivera said. “What does your family like?”

Finn thought. “We tell moon stories. We like little silver moons.”

“Perfect,” said Mr. Rivera. He opened safe, water-based paints. The paint smelled mild and clean. Finn dipped a tiny brush. Tap, tap. He painted small silver moons along the side while Mr. Rivera added bright flowers. Together, they made the station cheerful and welcoming.

Finn felt proud. His chest felt warm and glowy. “It looks happy,” he whispered.

“Step four,” Mr. Rivera said, pointing to the guide, “attach the soap pump and the water jug.” They clicked the red handle into place. Click! They screwed the pump on tight. Twist, twist.

Finn wanted to try it right away. He reached his paw to the pump.

“Wait,” said Mr. Rivera kindly. “One more step. We read the hand-washing directions.” He posted a simple sign with pictures: wet, soap, scrub, rinse, dry. The pictures showed a little paw turning, scrubbing backs, and cleaning between fingers.

Finn read the pictures out loud. “Wet, soap, scrub, rinse, dry.”

“Now try,” said Mr. Rivera.

Finn pushed the red handle. The water whispered out. He wet his paws, pressed the pump, and rubbed. The soap made creamy clouds. He scrubbed the backs, the wrists, and between each little claw. He sang their bubble song. Then he rinsed and shook, shake-shake, and dried with a clean towel.

“My paws feel sparkly!” Finn said. He lifted them to sniff. “Mmm. Orange moons.”

Mr. Rivera smiled. “You followed directions. You stayed safe. You kept things clean. And we honored our cultures with art.”

Just then, Finn looked at the floor. Little paw prints of dusty brown marched across the cement. “Oh no,” he said, ears drooping. “I made a mess when I came in.”

“That is a problem,” said Mr. Rivera, nodding. “But we can solve it.”

“How?” asked Finn, tail low.

“Step one,” Mr. Rivera said gently, “we notice. Step two, we clean.” He handed Finn a small brush and a damp cloth. “Step three, next time, we wipe paws at the door.”

Finn scrubbed the prints. Swish, swish, wipe, wipe. The floor looked smooth and gray again. He set a little mat by the door and put a sign with a friendly paw and a towel.

“Now we have a Paw Stop!” Finn cheered.

They stood back and admired the hand-washing station. It gleamed with silver moons and bright flowers. The air smelled like oranges and clean wood. Birds chirped outside. Finn felt calm and proud.

“What did we learn today?” asked Mr. Rivera.

Finn thought. “Clean paws keep friends healthy. We follow directions to stay safe. We can share and celebrate our different ways, like flowers and moons together.”

Mr. Rivera patted Finn’s shoulder. “Exactly.”

Finn pressed the pump one more time and made a tiny bubble crown on his paw. He giggled. “I can’t wait to show everyone at the garden!”

The End.
