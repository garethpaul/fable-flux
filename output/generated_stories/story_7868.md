# Tiko the Tortoise Listens to Words

In a quiet house, there was a cozy pantry with jars that shone like little moons. Bags of rice whispered when the door opened. Spices gave warm smells—cinnamon sweet, pepper tickly, and mint cool like a breeze. In the corner lived Tiko, a wise tortoise with a gentle smile and a shell the color of forest leaves.

Every morning, Tiko stretched his legs and listened. He loved words. He loved how words felt in the mouth and in the heart. “Hello,” he would say to the jars. “Hola,” he would try, and then “Bonjour,” and “Jambo.” The pantry hummed with happy sounds.

One day, the pantry door swung wide. In rushed three friends. Pip the mouse, tiny and quick. Bibi the beetle, shiny and brave. And Momo the moth, fluttery and soft.

“We have a problem!” squeaked Pip, his whiskers shaking. “Someone tore the paper bag!”

“It wasn’t me,” buzzed Bibi. “I only nibbled a crumb.”

“I didn’t do it,” whispered Momo, hiding under a tea bag. “I was just brushing my wings.”

On the floor, a paper bag of oats lay open. Oats spilled like little moons across the tiles.

Tiko blinked slowly. “Let’s use calm words,” he said. “Our words can be soft like blankets.”

Pip stamped his tiny feet. “I’m mad!” he squeaked. “Mad like pepper!”

Tiko nodded. “It is okay to feel mad. Let’s breathe.” He took a deep, slow breath. “In… out…” The friends breathed with him. The air smelled like cinnamon. Their bodies felt softer.

“Now,” said Tiko, “we will listen. Words can help us understand. Words are tools. They build bridges.”

Pip looked at the floor. “I was hungry,” he said, small and squeaky. “I pulled the bag, and it tore. I did not mean to.”

“Thank you for telling the truth,” said Tiko. “Brave words.”

Bibi buzzed gently. “I can help clean,” she said. “I like tidy lines.”

Momo peeked out, her wings like soft paper. “I can carry light oats,” she said. “I flutter softly.”

Tiko smiled. “We will work together. We will be kind. We will also be careful. We do not waste food. We care for our home. That is conservation.”

They worked as a team. Pip swept with a tiny brush. Bibi pushed oats into a neat hill. Momo lifted bits like feathers. Tiko held the bag still with his strong shell. The oats swished and tickled their feet. The pantry felt calm again.

When the floor was clean, Tiko said, “Let’s label the bag so it does not tear again.” He pulled out sticky labels and a small crayon. “Words can help us remember. Words can help us care.”

“What should we write?” asked Pip.

“‘Please open gently,’” said Tiko. He wrote it slowly. “Now, let’s add more languages,” he said with a smile. “Words come in many sounds. They are like music.”

He wrote: “Please open gently” and then “Por favor, abre con cuidado,” “S’il te plaît, ouvre doucement,” and “Tafadhali, fungua polepole.”

Pip’s eyes grew wide. “They all mean the same?”

“Yes,” said Tiko. “Different words, same kindness. Language is a bridge.”

Momo fluttered closer. “I like how soft ‘doucement’ sounds,” she whispered. “It feels like a cloud.”

Bibi buzzed, “I like ‘polepole.’ It sounds like walking slowly.”

Tiko nodded. “When we learn words from friends, we show respect. That is tolerance. We say, ‘Your words are welcome here.’ We learn. We grow.”

The pantry door creaked again. A glass jar wobbled. “Clink! Clink!” It almost fell.

Pip gasped. “Oh no!”

Tiko moved quiet and steady. He pressed his shell against the jar. “Safe,” he said. “We use gentle hands. We use gentle words.”

Pip looked up at Tiko. “I am sorry I got mad and shouted.”

Tiko touched Pip’s paw with a slow nod. “Your feelings are real. You used your words to tell us. Then you used calm breaths and helpful hands. That is anger management. That is strong.”

Bibi smiled. “We did not blame. We listened.”

Momo twirled. “We learned new words,” she sang. “Polepole! Doucement!”

They sat together on a soft dish towel. Tiko poured warm tea from a tiny pot into tiny cups made from bottle caps. The tea smelled like mint. It tasted cool and kind.

“Let’s make a word wall,” said Tiko. “A place for helpful words.”

They wrote more: “Thank you.” “Asante.” “Merci.” “Gracias.” “Excuse me.” “Samahani.” They added little drawings—a heart, a leaf, a smiling sun.

Tiko looked around. “Our pantry is a garden of words,” he said. “We will use words to care for food, to care for each other, and to care for our world.”

Pip leaned against Tiko’s shell. “I like words,” he said softly. “They make my heart feel safe.”

Bibi buzzed a happy note. “Words can save oats,” she joked.

Momo giggled. “Words can make wings feel brave.”

Tiko closed his eyes and listened. The pantry whispered with many voices, gentle and sweet.

“Remember,” he said, “when we feel big feelings, we breathe. When we need help, we ask. When we meet new words, we smile and learn. We do not waste. We share. We are kind.”

The friends nodded. The labels looked neat. The oats were safe. The pantry smelled like warm spice and mint. The house felt peaceful.

And Tiko, the wise tortoise, hummed a little song. “Hola, bonjour, jambo, hello. Words can help our kindness grow.”

The End.
