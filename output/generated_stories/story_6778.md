# Nutkin's Honest Tree House

Nutkin the squirrel woke up on a bright, sunny morning. The warm sunshine tickled his fluffy gray tail as he stretched his tiny paws. "What a perfect day to build my new tree house!" he chirped happily.

Nutkin had been dreaming of building the most wonderful tree house in the big oak tree. He wanted it to have a cozy bedroom, a kitchen for storing acorns, and even a little window to watch the clouds.

He scampered down to the sunny backyard and began collecting materials. "I need twigs, leaves, and something to hold it all together," he said to himself. Soon, he had gathered a big pile of brown twigs and colorful autumn leaves that crinkled under his paws.

But there was one problem – Nutkin needed something sticky to hold his tree house together. He looked around the backyard with his bright, curious eyes.

"Oh!" he squeaked with excitement. "Mrs. Robin has a ball of mud by her nest. That would be perfect!"

Nutkin quietly took the mud while Mrs. Robin was away finding worms for her babies. The mud felt cool and squishy between his paws. "This will make my tree house so strong!" he thought.

He climbed up the oak tree and began building. He stuck the twigs together with the mud, making walls that smelled like fresh earth. He arranged the leaves to make a soft, rustling roof. The tree house was looking wonderful!

Just as Nutkin was putting on the finishing touches, he heard a sad chirping sound below.

"Oh no! Where is my mud?" cried Mrs. Robin. "I need it to fix my nest before the rain comes! My babies will get wet!"

Nutkin's tummy felt funny and tight. He knew he had taken Mrs. Robin's mud without asking. His whiskers twitched nervously.

"I should tell her the truth," Nutkin whispered to himself. "Even though I really want to finish my tree house."

Nutkin climbed down the tree, his heart beating fast like a tiny drum. "Mrs. Robin," he said in a small voice, "I took your mud. I'm sorry. I wanted to build my tree house, but I should have asked first."

Mrs. Robin looked surprised, then smiled kindly. "Thank you for being honest, Nutkin. That was very brave of you."

"But now you don't have mud for your nest," Nutkin said sadly, his ears drooping.

Mrs. Robin tilted her head thoughtfully. "Well, maybe we can work together! I know where to find the best mud in the whole backyard. If you help me gather some, we can share it!"

Nutkin's eyes sparkled with joy. "Really? We can both use it?"

"Of course!" chirped Mrs. Robin. "There's plenty for both of us."

Together, they hopped and flew to the muddy spot by the garden hose. The mud was perfect – not too wet, not too dry. They worked side by side, Mrs. Robin showing Nutkin the best techniques for gathering mud.

Soon, Mrs. Robin had fixed her cozy nest, and Nutkin had finished his beautiful tree house. As the golden afternoon sun warmed their backs, they both felt proud of their work.

"Thank you for being honest," said Mrs. Robin. "Good friends always tell the truth."

Nutkin's tail twitched happily. "And good friends help each other too!"

From that day on, whenever Nutkin needed something, he always remembered to ask first and tell the truth. His tree house became the coziest home in the whole backyard, and he and Mrs. Robin remained the very best of friends.

The End.
