# Polly Paints in the Workshop

Polly was a creative parrot with bright green feathers and a red-tipped tail. She loved to make things. One sunny morning, Polly flapped to the garage workshop. The air smelled like fresh wood and orange soap. Sunbeams made the dust sparkle. Tools hung on the wall—hammers, brushes, a tiny saw—and there was a big, friendly workbench.

“Squawk! Time to build!” Polly chirped.

Her friend Max the mouse popped out from a small toolbox. He wore little blue earmuffs. “Hi, Polly! I want to build a picture frame!”

Polly smiled and bobbed her head. “I want to paint a sign that says ‘Friends!’ We can do both.”

On the bench sat a tray with paints—red, blue, yellow—and a cup of water that went swish-swish when Polly dipped a brush. There was also a measuring tape that went zip! and a sandpaper sheet that felt rough like a cat’s tongue.

Polly picked up the paintbrush. “I’ll paint first,” she said.

Max reached for the brush too. “But I want a turn! I want to paint the frame green.”

Both friends held the brush. Their hands bumped. Drip! A dot of blue paint splashed on the bench. Both eyes went wide.

Polly tucked her wings. “Oh.”

Max lowered his whiskers. “Oh.”

From a high shelf, Grandpa Owl hooted softly. “Hoo-hoo. In this workshop, we take turns,” he said, blinking his warm golden eyes. “Taking turns means one friend goes first, then the other friend goes next.”

Polly looked at Max. “We can do that,” she said. “You first, or me first?”

Max took a breath. He listened to the drip-drip in the cup. “You can go first, Polly. I will watch and learn.”

Polly felt proud and also gentle inside. “Thank you, Max.” She dipped the brush in red paint. Swish, swish. “I paint one stroke, then it’s your turn.”

Polly painted a big red F on the wooden sign. The brush made a soft whisper sound. She smiled and passed the brush to Max. “Your turn.”

Max held the brush with tiny paws. He dipped it in green. “I paint the frame,” he said. He made small lines. Tap, tap, tap went the brush.

Polly watched. She loved the soft brush sounds and the fresh paint smell. “You’re doing great!” she chirped. “I like your careful hands.”

Max grinned. “Thank you,” he squeaked. He handed the brush back. “Your turn.”

They went back and forth. One turn for Polly. One turn for Max. The brush went swish, tap, swish. The water cup went ploop when they rinsed. Each time, they shared. Each time, they waited. Each time, they said, “Your turn,” and “Thank you.”

Grandpa Owl nodded. “That is respect,” he said. “Respect means you care about other people’s feelings. You listen. You wait. You use kind words.”

Polly puffed her feathers. “Respect feels nice,” she said.

Max squeaked, “It really does.”

Soon, the sign said FRIENDS in bright letters, and the frame looked smooth and green. But there was still more to do. The wood felt scratchy.

Grandpa Owl fluttered down with the sandpaper. “Next, we sand. Sanding makes the wood smooth. Who will go first?”

Polly looked at Max. She remembered the brush. She remembered the drip. She remembered how good it felt to share. “Max, you go first,” Polly said.

Max’s eyes shone. “Thank you!” He rubbed the sandpaper in little circles. Shh-shh-shh. The frame grew soft under his paws. He stopped and smiled. “Your turn, Polly.”

Polly took the sandpaper. Shh-shh-shh. “It sounds like the ocean,” she giggled.

They kept taking turns. They took turns with the glue—squeeze, press, hold—counting to five together. “One, two, three, four, five!” They took turns with the tiny hammer—tap, tap, tap—wearing safety glasses that made them look very smart. They took turns washing hands in the sink, the water warm and bubbly.

When everything was dry, Polly placed the sign on the wall: FRIENDS. Max slid a drawing of the three of them into the green frame. The drawing showed Polly, Max, and Grandpa Owl smiling.

Polly flapped. “We did it!”

Max hopped. “We did it together!”

Grandpa Owl hooted. “What did you learn in the workshop today?”

Polly thought about the brush, the sandpaper, and the waiting. She felt calm and happy. “I learned to take turns,” she said. “When I take a turn, I do my part. When I wait, I let my friend do their part.”

Max nodded. “Taking turns shows respect. We listen and we share. We both get time. We both have fun.”

Grandpa Owl smiled. “That is good knowledge,” he said. “Knowledge helps us make kind choices.”

Polly turned to Max. “Your turn to choose a snack,” she said.

Max giggled. “Apples and crackers!”

They sat on a little bench. They crunched apples. Crunch, crunch. They nibbled crackers. Nibble, nibble. The workshop smelled like paint and apples. The tools glowed in the sun. The FRIENDS sign shone bright.

Polly patted Max’s paw. “Thank you for taking turns.”

Max patted her wing. “Thank you for waiting and sharing.”

Grandpa Owl tucked a soft cloth over the tools. “Today was a good day,” he said.

Polly and Max nodded. Their hearts felt warm and light.

The clear lesson was simple and true: Taking turns is kind. Taking turns shows respect. Taking turns helps friends learn and play together.

The End.
