# Tippy Fawn Takes a Calm Breath

Tippy was a gentle deer fawn with soft, speckled spots and big listening ears. One sunny afternoon, Tippy stepped into Grandpa Gus’s garage workshop. The air smelled like pine wood and a little bit like lemon oil. The floor felt cool on Tippy’s hooves. Tools hung on the wall like shiny silver fish, and a little fan hummed, whirr, whirr, whirr.

“Hello, Tippy,” said Grandpa Gus, wiping his hands. “I’m fixing Birdy’s mailbox. Would you like to help?”

Tippy nodded. “I want to write a label for the mailbox,” Tippy said. “I want it to say, ‘Birdy’s Mail.’ I know letters!”

Grandpa Gus smiled. “That’s a great job. First we measure. Then we write.” He pointed to a wooden bench. There were pencils, fat crayons, and a small notepad with smooth paper.

Tippy’s legs felt wiggly with excitement. “I want to start now!” Tippy hopped from hoof to hoof. Tap, tap, tap.

Grandpa Gus chuckled. “Let’s use our plan. We move our bodies, then we calm our bodies, then we write.”

Tippy tilted their head. “Move, calm, write?”

“Yes,” said Grandpa Gus. “It helps us do our best.”

Tippy tried to wait, but the workshop had so many curious things! A drill buzzed on the far table. Buzzzz. A wood plane shushed across a plank. Shhhhhh. The smells and sounds made Tippy’s heart beat fast-fast-fast. “I can’t sit still!” Tippy said, ears twitching.

“That’s okay,” said Grandpa Gus in a warm voice. “Let’s solve this together.”

Problem: Tippy felt too wiggly to sit and write.

Solution step one: Use movement to help the wiggles.

Grandpa Gus pointed to a soft mat in the corner. “Let’s do three strong pushes on the wall,” he said.

Tippy placed gentle hooves on the wall and pushed. “Push… one. Push… two. Push… three.” The muscles in Tippy’s legs felt warm and steady.

“Good. Now let’s do slow deer steps,” said Grandpa Gus. “Heel, then toe.”

Tippy stepped heel-toe, heel-toe in a little circle. Clip, clop, clip, clop. The fan’s whirr sounded softer now.

Solution step two: Use calm breaths to help the heart.

Grandpa Gus put a hand on his chest. “Smell the pine,” he said, sniffing in. “Blow the feather,” he said, puffing out.

Tippy copied him. “Smell the pine.” In through the nose. The pine smell tickled. “Blow the feather.” Out through the mouth. A tiny feather on the bench danced. In… and out… three times.

“How do you feel?” asked Grandpa Gus.

“My heart is slow now,” Tippy said, placing a hoof on their chest. “Thump… thump… thump.” The thumps were steady and calm. Tippy’s ears relaxed.

Solution step three: Use words to guide the brain.

Grandpa Gus handed over the notepad. “Let’s make a writing plan. Say it with me.”

Tippy whispered, “First I sit. Then I hold my crayon. Then I write the letters slowly.”

“Excellent,” said Grandpa Gus. “You are using knowledge. Knowledge helps us know what to do.”

Tippy climbed onto a little stool. The wood was smooth and cool. Tippy held a fat blue crayon. “It feels round and a little sticky,” Tippy said, smiling.

“Ready when you are,” said Grandpa Gus.

Tippy said softly, “Move, calm, write.” Then Tippy began. “B…” The crayon made a soft scritch on the paper. “I…” Scritch. “R…” Scritch. “D…” Scritch. “Y’ S…” Tippy paused and looked up. “Grandpa, where does the apostrophe go?”

“After the Y,” Grandpa Gus replied. “You asked a smart question.”

Tippy added the tiny mark. “Mail,” Tippy continued, saying each sound and letter. “M… A… I… L.” The word looked neat and brave on the page.

Just then, the drill buzzed again, louder this time. Buzzzzz! Tippy’s shoulders jumped. The crayon wobbled, making a little squiggle.

Tippy frowned. “Oh no! I messed up!”

Grandpa Gus held up a finger. “Let’s use our plan.”

Tippy nodded. “Move, calm, write.” Tippy pressed palms to the table and did two gentle pushes. Push… push. Then Tippy breathed. “Smell the pine. Blow the feather.” In… out… in… out. The little feather danced again. Tippy smiled. “I can fix it. I can try again.”

Grandpa Gus gave Tippy another sheet. “That’s self-regulation,” he said. “You noticed your feelings and used tools to help.”

Tippy wrote the words again, slow and steady. Scritch, scritch, scritch. This time the letters stood tall, and the apostrophe sat in the right spot. Tippy traced the words with a finger. “I did it!”

They stuck the label onto the small wooden mailbox. Grandpa Gus held it up. The wood felt warm from the afternoon sun shining through the garage door. “Perfect,” he said.

Tippy’s heart felt light like a leaf. “Grandpa, I learned something. When I feel too wiggly, I can move. Then I can breathe. Then I can write.”

Grandpa Gus nodded. “That is wise knowledge. Your body and your brain are good helpers.”

Tippy looked at the tools on the wall. “Thank you, body. Thank you, brain.” Tippy giggled. “And thank you, feather!”

Grandpa Gus laughed. “Let’s celebrate with a calm high-hoof.”

They touched hoof to hand, soft and gentle. Tippy listened to the fan’s quiet whirr and smelled the pine again. The workshop felt friendly and safe.

From then on, Tippy used the plan whenever things felt too loud or too wiggly: move, calm, write. Tippy knew that taking care of feelings helped make room for learning and doing.

The End.
