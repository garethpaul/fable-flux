# Owl Hoots for Space

High in a cozy treehouse, tucked among rustling green leaves, lived a wise old owl named Olive. Her feathers were soft and speckled, like tiny stars. Her treehouse smelled like pine needles and warm wood. Sunlight made gentle patterns on the floor, and a little bell by the door went tinkle-tinkle in the breeze.

Olive loved to host friends. She had a basket of sweet berries, a pile of soft pillows, and a shelf of picture books. But Olive also loved quiet time to rest her wings and think big thoughts. She called it “Owl Space.” It meant she could sit, breathe, and feel calm.

One bright morning, Olive was sipping warm tea that tasted like flowers. She hummed and rocked in her chair. Knock-knock! The little bell went tinkle-tinkle.

“Come in,” Olive hooted kindly.

In burst Squeak the squirrel, with fluffy tail and busy paws. “Play! Play! Play chase!” Squeak squealed, bouncing on Olive’s pillows and scattering berries.

Olive’s feathers fluffed. Her heart thumped fast. “I was resting,” she said softly. “I need Owl Space.”

Squeak didn’t hear. He zoomed in circles. “Catch me!” he chattered.

Olive took a slow breath. She used a strategy she had practiced: Stop, Breathe, Speak. She stopped moving. She breathed in, then out. Then she spoke clearly.

“Squeak,” Olive said, calm and kind, “I like playing with you. Right now, I need quiet. My body needs space.”

Squeak froze mid-bounce. “Space? Like the sky?” he asked, tilting his head.

“Space means I need room and time to myself,” said Olive. “My wings and my mind need rest. Please use a soft voice and gentle feet.”

Squeak’s ears drooped. “Oh. I didn’t know,” he whispered. “I can try.”

Olive smiled. “Thank you. Let’s make a plan.”

They set a little sand timer on the table. Swish, swish, the sand began to fall. “When the sand is gone,” Olive explained, “we can play a quiet game. Until then, I will have Owl Space.”

Squeak tiptoed to the window. He watched leaves dance and listened to birds tweet-tweet. A breeze brushed his whiskers. He wiggled, but he stayed gentle.

The sand slid down. When it was done, Olive opened her wings. “Thank you for waiting, Squeak. Let’s build a pillow nest.”

They stacked soft pillows. They laughed. They whispered. It felt cozy and calm.

Knock-knock! Tinkle-tinkle!

“Come in,” Olive hooted.

In clattered Tilda the turtle, dragging a toy wagon. “Move! Big parade!” Tilda boomed, bumping the pillow nest with a thud.

Olive’s eyes went wide. Her belly felt tight. She put a wing over her heart. Stop, Breathe, Speak.

“Tilda,” Olive said, “please pause. You bumped our nest. My body needs safe space. My home needs gentle voices and slow feet.”

Tilda blinked. “But I want a parade,” she said, frowning.

“We can have a parade later,” Olive offered. “Right now, we are playing quiet. Let’s make a boundary sign.”

“What’s a boundary?” Tilda asked.

“It’s a line to keep us safe and comfy,” Olive said. “It tells others what is okay and what is not okay for our bodies and our feelings.”

Olive took a piece of cardboard. She drew a circle and a little owl. She wrote in big letters: Quiet Time. Please Knock. She hung the sign by the door, right under the tinkle-tinkle bell.

Squeak read it slowly. “Quiet. Time. Please. Knock.”

Tilda nodded. “I can do slow feet and soft voice,” she said, lowering her head.

They tried again. Tilda rolled her wagon softly. Squeak tiptoed. Olive smiled and felt her belly relax. The treehouse felt warm and safe.

But soon, the wind picked up. Whoosh! Leaves rattled. The bell went tinkle-tinkle-tinkle! The sign flipped and fluttered. A flock of chirpy finches peeped in. “Party! Party!” they sang, flapping and hopping and pecking at the berry basket.

Oh no! Too much noise. Too many wings. Olive wanted to shout. Instead, she remembered her plan. Stop, Breathe, Speak.

“Friends,” Olive hooted, firm and kind, “I care about you. This is my home. My body needs calm. Please line up at the door. Soft wings. Quiet beaks.”

She pointed to the sign. She put a gentle wing across the doorway, like a friendly fence. “One at a time,” she said. “We will take turns.”

The finches blinked, then shuffled into a line. Peep. Peep. Soft and small.

Olive showed a new strategy: the Calm Corner. She spread a blanket that smelled like sun and soap. She set out three choices: a fuzzy ball to squeeze, seashells to listen to the sea, and a picture book with big, slow pages.

“Choose calm,” Olive invited.

Squeak squeezed the fuzzy ball. Squish. Tilda listened to a shell. Shhhhh. The finches turned pages with tiny clicks.

The treehouse grew quiet again. Olive felt her heart slow. She smiled at her friends. “Thank you for respecting my boundary,” she said. “Now I feel safe and happy. When we listen to each other’s words and bodies, everyone can feel good.”

Tilda lifted her head. “I like the Calm Corner,” she said.

Squeak nodded. “I like the sign.”

A brave little finch piped, “I like taking turns.”

Olive hooted softly. “Boundaries are kind. They help us be friends.”

When the sun grew golden, Olive set the sand timer one last time. “After quiet,” she said, “we can have a small, gentle parade around the pillows. Slow feet, soft voices, happy hearts.”

They waited. Swish, swish. Then they marched slowly, one by one, around the nest. Tap-tap went Tilda’s wagon. Shuffle-shuffle went Squeak’s feet. Flutter-flutter went the finches’ wings—soft as whispers.

Olive’s treehouse glowed with peace. Her friends smiled. Olive felt strong and calm. She had set her boundary, used a good plan, and solved the problem with kind words.

The lesson was clear: Your body and your feelings are important. You can say what you need. You can make a plan. You can be kind and firm. And good friends will listen.

The End.
