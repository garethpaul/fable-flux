# Squeaky Clean Squirrel

Pip was a small, resourceful squirrel with bright eyes and a fluffy tail. He loved to explore. One sunny morning, Pip peeked into a shiny, tiled place he had never seen before—the bathroom. It smelled like soap and mint. Drip, drip, drip went the faucet. Swish, swish went a soft blue towel.

Pip wiggled his whiskers. “What is this place?” he whispered. “It looks like a rain room!”

On the counter stood a big mirror. Pip climbed up, pat-pat-pat, and saw his face. His cheeks had a bit of sticky jam from breakfast. His paws had dotty dirt from playing in the garden. “Oh my,” Pip said. “I want to be clean. But how?”

Pip’s curiosity buzzed like a little bee. He reached for a round, smooth bar. It smelled like oranges. “Hello, orange stone,” he said. “Do you make me clean?” He rubbed it on his fur. Nothing happened. His fur got sticky and slippery. “Oh no! I am a slippery squirrel!” he squeaked, wobbling.

He heard another drip. Pip turned to the shiny faucet. “Maybe the silver nose gives rain,” he said. He tried to twist the handle. It was big for tiny paws. “I need help,” Pip decided. Pip was brave and kind, so he used peaceful words.

“Excuse me,” he called softly toward the hallway. “Can someone help me, please?”

Maya, a gentle girl with warm eyes, peeked in. “Hi, Pip,” she said in a calm voice. “Are you exploring the bathroom?”

“Yes,” said Pip. “I want to be clean, but I do not know how. I tried the orange stone. It made me slippery.”

Maya smiled. “That orange stone is soap. Soap helps when we add water. Would you like me to show you?”

“Yes, please,” Pip said. “I am curious and a little bit scared.”

“It’s okay to feel that way,” Maya said. “We can be brave together.”

Maya lifted Pip carefully. The sink was cool and smooth. She turned the handle. Water sang, hisssss, then made a warm, gentle stream. “Can you feel it?” Maya asked.

Pip reached out a paw. The water tickled. “It’s warm! And it sounds like a little river,” he giggled.

“First we make our paws wet,” Maya said. “Then we use a small bit of soap.” She pressed the pump on a bottle. Squish! A soft bubble cloud landed on Pip’s paws.

“Oooh!” Pip sniffed. “It smells like oranges and sunshine.”

“Now rub, rub, rub,” said Maya. “Rub your paws together. Rub the backs, rub between fingers, rub your thumbs. Sing with me.”

They sang a simple song:

“Rub, rub, rub,
Bubbles on my paws.
Rub, rub, rub,
Clean with gentle laws.”

Pip laughed. Bubbles popped—pop! pop!—like tiny stars. “Between fingers,” he said, wiggling. “Around thumbs!” He rubbed for a little while. “Now rinse.”

Maya guided his paws under the water. The bubbles slid away. “Look,” Maya said. “Clean paws!”

Pip beamed. “My paws are not sticky now. They are squeaky!” He tapped the sink. Tap, tap. “What about my jammy cheeks?”

“We can use a soft cloth,” Maya said. She ran warm water over a washcloth. It was fuzzy and cozy. She wrung it out—squeeze, squeeze. “Close your eyes,” she said. “I will wipe gently.”

Pip closed his eyes. The warm cloth touched his cheeks. Swish, swish. The jam was gone. “That feels nice,” Pip sighed.

“Now we dry,” Maya said. She dabbed him with the blue towel. Pat, pat, pat. The towel smelled like clean air and lavender. Pip’s fur puffed up like a little cloud.

Pip looked in the mirror again. “Hello, clean Pip!” he cheered. “I feel fresh. I feel brave. I learned something new.”

Maya nodded. “You tried something hard. You asked for help with kind words. That shows courage and good communication. And now you know about hygiene—keeping our bodies clean.”

Pip thought for a moment. “Hygiene means washing paws, face, and fur. It means soap with water. It means drying after.” He counted on his tiny toes. “One: wet. Two: soap. Three: rub. Four: rinse. Five: dry.”

“Perfect,” Maya said. “We also clean our teeth.” She pointed to a toothbrush. It had soft bristles. “We brush in the morning and at night.”

Pip’s eyes sparkled. “Can I try?”

“Sure,” Maya said. She put a tiny dot of toothpaste on a small brush. “Open wide.”

Pip opened his mouth. The minty paste felt cool and tingly. “It tastes like snow!” he said, giggling. Maya showed him small circles—round and round, front teeth and back teeth. After a little rinse, Pip smiled. His teeth felt smooth.

“I did it!” Pip cheered. “Thank you for helping me learn, Maya.”

“You’re welcome,” Maya said. “You were curious and careful. That’s the best way to learn.”

Pip hopped to the counter edge. “When I get dirty, I will wash my paws. When I eat jam, I will wipe my face. I will brush my teeth, too. I can teach my friends!”

“That’s a great plan,” Maya said. “Clean bodies help us stay healthy. Clean hands help us not spread germs.”

Pip waved his tail. “I like being a squeaky clean squirrel.”

The bathroom was quiet now. Drip, drip, drip slowed to stop. The towel hung straight. The mirror shone. Pip took a deep, happy breath. He felt safe, smart, and cared for.

“Good-bye, rain room,” he whispered. “See you after playtime.”

He scampered out, ready for new adventures—with clean paws, a bright smile, and kind words in his heart.

The End.
