# Mango’s Porch of Kind Words

Mango the monkey loved the front porch. It was a peaceful porch with a soft blue rug, two wooden chairs, and a big pot of mint that smelled cool and sweet. The porch was warm in the sun and quiet except for birds that went tweet, tweet. Mango felt safe there.

Every morning, Mango swept the porch with a straw broom. “Sweep, sweep,” went the broom. Mango smiled. “I keep the porch clean. That is my job,” Mango said. Mango was small but courageous. Mango tried new things, even when they felt big.

One gentle morning, Mango set out a basket of books on the porch table. The pages felt smooth. Some books had big letters. Some had tiny letters. Mango traced a letter with a furry finger. “Hello, A,” Mango said. “You look like a ladder.” Mango giggled.

Grandma Turtle slowly climbed onto the porch. “Good morning, Mango,” she said in a soft, low voice.

“Good morning!” Mango chirped. “Would you like mint tea?”

“Yes, please,” said Grandma Turtle. She sat in the shade. Mango poured tea very carefully. “Pour, pour,” went the tea. The air smelled like fresh leaves.

Grandma Turtle sipped. “Mmm. Thank you for the careful pour,” she said.

Mango puffed up with pride. “I am responsible,” Mango said. “I take care of the porch.”

Just then, a breeze brought voices. Two sparrows landed on the railing. “Chirp! Tweet-tweet!” they sang. Another bird, a robin, landed too. “Cheer-up! Cheerily!” it called.

Mango clapped. “I love your sounds! Different birds, different songs,” Mango said. “Language is like music.”

Grandma Turtle nodded. “Languages help us share feelings,” she said. “Words can be hugs for ears.”

On the little path, a neighbor rabbit named Nia hopped closer. “Hi, Mango,” Nia said, ears up. “Can I play on the porch?”

“Yes!” said Mango. “Let’s make a word game.” Mango brought out crayons and paper. “We will listen. We will look. We will find gentle words.”

Nia smiled. “I like gentle words,” she said.

They started to draw. Mango drew a banana. Nia drew a carrot. “Banana!” Mango said. “Carrot!” Nia said. The sparrows chirped. The robin sang. The mint leaves fluttered. The porch felt happy.

Then, a tiny chipmunk scampered in a hurry. “Squeak-squeak!” it cried, tail shaking. “I lost my nut! I can’t find it!” Its eyes were wet.

Mango felt a pull in the chest. Mango wanted to help. “We can help you,” Mango said. “But let’s use peaceful words first.”

The chipmunk sniffled. “Please help me,” it said more softly.

“Thank you for asking kindly,” Mango said. “Words can calm stormy feelings. Let’s start by naming what we need.”

The chipmunk took a breath. “I need my nut. I am scared.”

Grandma Turtle said, “Good sharing. That is brave.”

Mango looked around. “We will listen with our ears and look with our eyes,” Mango said. “Nia, you check under the chair.”

“Okay,” Nia said. She bent down. “No nut here. But I see dust bunnies!” She giggled.

Mango knelt by the mint pot. The leaves tickled Mango’s nose. “Achoo!” Mango sneezed. “Mint smells strong,” Mango said.

The robin hopped to the porch step. “Cheer-up!” it called and pecked at something shiny. It was a bottle cap, not a nut.

Mango looked at the broom. “I swept today,” Mango said. “Did I sweep the nut away?”

Mango’s heart felt heavy. “If I did, I will make it right,” Mango said. “That is responsibility.”

Mango lifted the straw broom and swept gently. “Sweep, sweep,” went the broom. Something rolled. “Tink-tink!” A small brown nut popped out from under the rug.

“My nut!” squeaked the chipmunk. It hugged the nut. “Thank you!”

Mango smiled but still spoke clearly. “I am sorry if my sweeping hid your nut,” Mango said.

The chipmunk blinked. “Thank you for saying sorry,” it said. “I feel better.”

Grandma Turtle nodded. “See how words help? ‘Please,’ ‘Thank you,’ and ‘I’m sorry’ are strong, peaceful words.”

Mango turned to Nia. “Let’s add those words to our game,” Mango said. Mango wrote BIG letters on a paper: PLEASE, THANK YOU, SORRY. Then Mango added more: HELLO, MAY I?, I CAN HELP.

Nia traced the letters with a crayon. “These words feel nice,” she said. “Like soft blankets.”

Mango smiled. “Words can be soft,” Mango said. “They can help when we are brave, scared, or mad.”

The sparrows chirped together. Mango listened. “I hear your bird words,” Mango said. “I don’t speak Bird, but I can still listen with my heart.”

Grandma Turtle chuckled. “Listening is part of language, too,” she said.

Mango handed out tiny paper cards. “Let’s make a Porch Word Garden,” Mango said. “We will plant our kind words.”

They tucked the cards around the mint pot and the chairs. The wind wiggled the papers. The porch looked like it was smiling.

Suddenly, a gust pushed the door. Bang! The cards fluttered. The chipmunk grabbed one. Nia held another. Mango reached for the rest, tail swishing. “I can do it,” Mango said, steady and brave. Mango caught the last card and put them all back in place.

“You were so calm,” Nia said.

“I used my words inside,” Mango said. “I told myself, ‘I can do this.’ That is self-reliance.”

Mango poured more tea for everyone and passed out apple slices. The mint smell was cool. The apples were crisp. Birds sang. The porch glowed in late sun.

Mango raised a cup. “To kind words,” Mango said. “They help us solve problems. They help us be gentle.”

“To kind words,” everyone said together.

Mango looked at the big letters again and spoke slowly. “Hello. Please. Thank you. I’m sorry. May I? I can help.”

The chipmunk hugged the nut. “I feel safe,” it said.

Grandma Turtle patted Mango’s hand. “You used language with love,” she said. “That takes courage.”

Mango’s heart felt warm and light. On the peaceful front porch, with songs and mint and friends, Mango learned that words are tools, music, and hugs all in one.

And Mango promised to use them, every day.

The End.
