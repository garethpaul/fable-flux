# Bubbles, Beeps, and Taking Turns

The curious fox kit, Fina, padded into the bright bathroom on soft paws. The tiles felt cool on her toes. Drip, drip, went the faucet. Plink, plink, went the drops in the shiny sink. The room smelled like mint and orange soap.

“Today I will learn everything!” said Fina, her tail swishing with excitement.

Her big brother, Rook, a kind fox with a white-tipped tail, was already there. He held a tiny toothbrush with a blue stripe. “Hi, Fina,” he said. “Let’s brush our teeth.”

Fina bounced. “Me first! Me first!”

Rook smiled. “We can take turns,” he said. “Taking turns means you go, then I go, then you go again. Like a little line. It’s fair.”

Fina tilted her head. “A line?”

Rook pointed to two toothbrushes in a cup. “See? Two brushes. Two foxes. One sink. We make a plan.”

He drew three dots with his claw on a bath towel: Fina dot, Rook dot, Fina dot. “First dot is you,” he said. “Second dot is me. Third dot is you again for rinsing.”

Fina’s ears perked. “A plan helps us wait.”

“Right,” said Rook. “Wait and watch. That’s logical thinking. We think about what comes next.”

Fina nodded. She liked plans. They felt neat and safe, like a snug den.

Rook turned the faucet. Whoosh! Warm water ran over their paws. It felt like soft rain. He put a little pea of toothpaste on Fina’s brush. It smelled like sweet mint.

“First turn: Fina brushes,” Rook said.

Fina made little circles on her teeth. “Round and round,” she sang. “Front teeth, side teeth, back teeth.” The bubbles tasted tingly. Her whiskers wiggled. She counted slowly, “One… two… three…” Rook tapped the counter with a gentle beat to help her keep time. It sounded like “tap, tap, tap.”

When Fina finished, she looked up. “Your turn!” she said, proud to remember.

Rook took his brush. “Thank you,” he said. He brushed with the same little circles. “Round and round.” Fina waited. Waiting felt hard. Her paws wanted to bounce. Her tail wanted to swish fast.

She took a deep breath. She listened to the tap, tap, tap. She watched the mirror fog a little like a cloud. She counted Rook’s taps in her head. “One… two… three…” Counting helped. Making a plan helped. Waiting began to feel like part of the game.

Rook spit. “All done,” he said. “Your turn to rinse.”

Fina giggled. She took a sip of water and swished. “Swish, swish! Bubble boat!” She spit into the sink and watched the foam swirl like tiny white waves.

“Good turn-taking,” Rook said, and he ruffled her ears.

Next, Fina spotted a bath toy boat and a rubber duck on the edge of the tub. “Can we play?” she asked.

“Yes,” said Rook. “But still with turns. First we fill the cup, then pour. We make a pattern.”

He held up a clear cup. “Listen to the water,” he said.

Drip, drip became splish, sploosh as the cup filled. “Full,” said Rook. “Your turn to pour.”

Fina poured slowly. The water made the duck bob. “Quack!” she said for the duck. “My turn is pour.”

“Now my turn to pour,” said Rook. He filled, then poured. The boat sailed toward the drain stopper.

Fina reached for the cup. She remembered. “We are taking turns,” she said, pulling her paw back. “I can wait and watch.”

She watched the boat move. She thought. “If you pour on the side,” she said softly, “the boat goes this way.” She pointed.

Rook’s eyes smiled. “Let’s test your idea. That is practice and thinking.”

He poured on the side. The boat drifted to the left. “It worked!” laughed Fina. Her tail swished happily. “Now my turn.” She poured on the other side, and the boat went right. “I like our plan.”

The mirror was foggier now. Fina drew a little line with her paw on the steam. She drew two dots again. “Me, then you.”

Rook nodded. “You’re getting very good at taking turns.”

At the towel rack, two soft towels waited. Fina touched them. “Fluffy,” she said. “Can I pick first?”

Rook thought. “We can practice fairness,” he said. “Today you pick first, and I pick second. Tomorrow I pick first, and you pick second. That’s a turn too.”

Fina picked the orange towel. It smelled like sunshine. Rook took the green one. It smelled like trees.

They dried their faces. Fina peeked at her reflection. Her fur looked smooth and shiny. She felt proud inside, like warm cocoa.

“Rook,” she said, “taking turns helps us both.”

“Yes,” said Rook. “Taking turns makes time feel kind. We both get a chance. We make plans. We wait. We watch. We try again. That is how we learn.”

Fina hugged him. The towels were soft between them. “I can take turns with the sink, the cup, and the toys,” she said. “I can take turns with you and with friends.”

Rook hugged back. “And when waiting feels wiggly,” he said, “you can count, breathe, or make a little plan in your mind.”

Fina took a slow breath. In through her nose. Out through her mouth. She smiled. “I am ready for more turns tomorrow.”

They turned off the faucet together. Click. The bathroom was quiet. The mirror cleared a little. The boat and duck rested side by side.

Fina waved to them. “Good night, boat. Good night, duck. We will take turns again.”

Rook switched off the light. “Good night, Fina.”

In the cozy dark, Fina felt calm. She had brushed, she had waited, she had thought, and she had played. She had practiced taking turns, and it felt good.

The End.
