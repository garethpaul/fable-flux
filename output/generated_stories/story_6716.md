# Fawn's Calm-Corner Workshop

A gentle deer fawn named Fawn liked to visit Grandpa’s garage workshop. The air smelled like pine wood and orange soap. Sunlight fell in square patches on the floor. Tools hung in neat rows on the wall: shiny wrenches, a blue tape measure, and a big, round clock that went tick-tock, tick-tock. There were jars of buttons and bolts, and a shelf of paints that looked like a rainbow.

Fawn’s small hooves made tiny tap-tap sounds on the floor. “Grandpa,” Fawn said in a soft voice, “may I make art today?”

Grandpa wiped his hands on a clean rag. He smiled, his eyes warm and kind. “Yes, little one. Today, you can paint a wooden sign for the garden. It will say ‘Welcome.’ We will use colors and careful hands.”

Fawn’s ears perked up. “I love colors!” Fawn said. “I want red and blue and yellow and sparkles!”

Grandpa chuckled. “We can try some of that. But first, we plan. In the workshop, we follow steps. That keeps us safe and calm.”

Fawn nodded. The word calm felt smooth, like a gentle breeze. Fawn wanted to be calm and careful.

Grandpa set the wooden board on the table. “Step one,” he said, pointing to a paper with simple pictures. “We put on aprons and tie them. Step two: we lay out brushes. Step three: we paint slowly.”

Fawn listened, but the bright paints looked so exciting. The shiny lids clicked, and the colors peeked out. Fawn’s heart went thump-thump faster. “I want to paint now!” Fawn said, grabbing two brushes at once.

“Hold on, little one,” Grandpa said kindly. “Let’s put on aprons first.”

Fawn tried to tie the apron strings but got tangled. The strings brushed Fawn’s fur and tickled. Fawn huffed. “This is hard!” The brushes wobbled and a small drip of blue fell—plip!—onto the table.

Fawn felt warm and fizzy inside, like a soda bottle shaking. “I can’t do it!” Fawn said, voice getting loud.

Grandpa held up a hand and took a slow, deep breath. In… and out… Fawn watched his chest rise and fall.

“This is our calm-corner,” Grandpa said, pointing to a soft mat near the workbench. “When big feelings come, we pause. We breathe. We count. We can do it together.”

Fawn’s ears lowered. “But I want to make the sign now.”

“We will,” Grandpa said gently. “Let’s help your body be ready. Try my calm steps: Smell the flower, blow the candle.”

Fawn closed eyes. Smell the flower—inhale. Blow the candle—exhale. Fawn did it again. Smell the flower. Blow the candle. The fizzy feeling started to fade. Fawn’s shoulders softened.

“Better?” Grandpa asked.

“Better,” Fawn said, a little smile returning. “Let’s try again.”

“Good plan,” Grandpa said. “That is self-regulation. It means you help your body and feelings work together.”

Fawn’s eyes grew wide. “Self-regu-la-tion,” Fawn repeated, giggling at the long word. “That is big knowledge!”

“It is,” Grandpa agreed. “And artists use it every day.”

Grandpa helped tie Fawn’s apron. The knot felt snug and safe. They set out three brushes: big, medium, and tiny. Grandpa held the blue paint. “May I open this?”

“Yes,” Fawn said calmly. “Slowly.” The lid turned with a soft creak. The paint smelled a little sweet and a little sharp.

Fawn dipped the big brush into yellow. “Swish,” went the bristles. Fawn spread smooth sunshine across the board. The wood felt cool under the brush. “This is fun,” Fawn whispered.

Next, Fawn used the medium brush for blue dots. “Plip, plip, plip.” Then the tiny brush for red letters. Fawn’s tongue poked out a little in concentration. The clock went tick-tock, tick-tock, steady and soft.

Halfway through the letter C, the brush slid too far—oops! A red streak ran down the board. Fawn’s chest got tight again. The fizzy feeling tried to come back.

Fawn looked at Grandpa. Grandpa nodded and tapped his own chest. “What do we do when we feel fizzy?”

Fawn put the brush down. “We pause,” Fawn said. “We smell the flower. We blow the candle.”

In… out… in… out…

The tight feeling loosened. Fawn looked closely. “I have another idea,” Fawn said. “I can paint a red ladybug over the streak.”

“That’s creative problem-solving,” Grandpa said with a grin. “Art can turn mistakes into new ideas.”

Fawn painted a round red ladybug with tiny black dots. It looked cozy next to the blue dots and yellow sunshine. Fawn finished the letters: W-e-l-c-o-m-e. Each stroke was slow and careful. The sign looked warm and bright.

Grandpa clapped softly. “You did it. What helped you today?”

Fawn stood tall. “I used steps. I paused when feelings got big. I breathed. I fixed my mistake with art. I used self-regu-la-tion and knowledge.”

Grandpa put a gentle hand on Fawn’s shoulder. “Yes. Your calm helped your art shine.”

They set the sign to dry on a rack. It smelled like paint and pine and a little bit like happiness. The clock ticked, the wrenches gleamed, and the sun made the colors glow.

Fawn nuzzled Grandpa’s sleeve. “Next time I feel fizzy, I will pause, breathe, and try again.”

Grandpa smiled. “That is a wise plan, little artist.”

The End.
