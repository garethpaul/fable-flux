# Tiko the Tortoise and the Treehouse Tasks

High in a friendly oak, a cozy treehouse smelled like fresh leaves and warm wood. A little bell tinkled in the breeze. Inside, Tiko the wise tortoise wore a tiny blue scarf. He moved slowly, and he smiled softly.

“Good morning, treehouse,” Tiko said. His voice was calm and kind. “Today we have tasks to do. We will practice, take turns, and finish together.”

A list hung on the wall. It said:
1. Sweep the floor.
2. Hang the bell.
3. Sort the crayons.

Tiko took a deep breath. He liked the sweet smell of the crayons. He heard birds chirp, chip-chip, outside. He touched the smooth broom handle.

Knock, knock.

A small squirrel named Suri peeked in. “Hi, Tiko!” she squeaked. “Can I help?”

“Hello, Suri,” Tiko said. “Yes, you can help. We will be fair. We will talk. We will practice. We will finish our tasks.”

Suri’s tail swished. “I like bells! I want to hang the bell right now!”

Tiko nodded. “We will hang the bell. First, let us sweep the floor. The floor is sandy. Sand makes tiny crunch sounds under my feet. Listen.” He pushed the broom. Crunch, crunch. “We practice sweeping, and the floor will feel smooth.”

Suri wiggled. “Sweeping is slow!”

Tiko smiled. “I am slow, too. Slow is okay. Slow helps us notice. We can talk while we sweep.”

They began. Suri tried to sweep fast. The sand jumped into the air and fell back, tap-tap-tap. “Oh no,” she said. “The sand is everywhere.”

Tiko showed her small steps. “Try a little sweep. Then pause. Then sweep again.” He moved the broom gently. Swish, pause. Swish, pause.

Suri copied. Swish, pause. Swish, pause. The sand gathered in a neat little pile. Suri grinned. “I did it!”

“You practiced,” Tiko said. “Practice is doing a thing again and again to get better.”

They scooped the sand into a bin. The floor felt smooth. Tiko placed his foot down and nodded. “Task one is done.”

Suri clapped. “Now the bell?”

Tiko looked at the list. “Yes, hang the bell is task two. But the hook is high. It is near the window. We must think.” He tapped his chin, tap-tap. “How can we reach it safely?”

Suri looked around. “We can stack the cushions!”

Tiko shook his head gently. “The cushions are soft. They may wobble. That is not safe. Let us think more.”

Suri sniffed the air. The wood smelled like sunshine. “We have a step stool,” she said. “It is solid.”

“Good thinking,” Tiko said. “We used words. We listened. We found a safe way.”

Tiko held the stool steady. Suri climbed. Her little paws felt warm wood. She lifted the bell. It felt cool and smooth. Tink! The bell sang a tiny silver sound when she touched it.

“Almost there,” Tiko said. “One more step.”

Suri took a breath. She hung the bell on the hook. Tink-a-tink. The sound was bright and happy.

“I did it!” Suri beamed.

“We did it together,” Tiko said. “We talked. We took turns. We were fair.”

Suri hopped down. “Now the crayons!”

They sat on a rug. Crayons were everywhere—reds, blues, greens, and one tiny gold crayon that sparkled. The box had three cups: Warm, Cool, and Bright. Tiko read the labels aloud. “Warm colors feel like sun. Cool colors feel like water. Bright colors stand out like stars.”

Suri picked up a red crayon. She sniffed it. It smelled a little like wax and berries. “Warm?”

“Yes,” Tiko said. “Red is warm.” He handed her blue. “Blue is cool. Where should it go?”

Suri frowned a little. “Cool,” she said, and dropped it in the Cool cup. Plink.

They worked. Plink, plink, plink. Sometimes Suri wasn’t sure. She held up purple. “Warm or cool?”

Tiko asked, “What do you think? Why?”

Suri closed her eyes. “Purple feels like night. Night is cool.”

“Good thinking,” Tiko said. “You used your mind. That is called critical thinking.”

They practiced until all the crayons were sorted. The cups looked neat and colorful. Suri sighed with happy pride. “We finished the list!”

Tiko rang the bell. Tink-a-tink-tink. “We did our tasks. We practiced. We talked. We were fair. We took turns.”

Suri tilted her head. “Fair?”

“Fair means everyone gets a chance,” Tiko said. “You swept, I held the stool, and we sorted together. We shared the jobs.”

Suri’s tail curled. “I like fair.”

Just then, a soft breeze came through the window. The leaves whispered shhhh. The treehouse felt calm and bright. Tiko stretched his neck and looked at the tidy floor, the happy bell, and the sorted crayons.

Suri said, “At first, I wanted to rush. But slow was better. Practice helped me do it right.”

Tiko smiled. “Practice makes us strong and steady. When a task feels big, we can:
- Stop and talk.
- Think of safe ways.
- Try small steps.
- Practice again.”

Suri nodded. “Tomorrow, can we practice drawing?”

“Yes,” said Tiko. “We will practice lines and circles. We will finish our picture. We will be fair and take turns with colors.”

Tiko rang the bell one more time. Tink! The sound danced like a little star. Suri giggled.

“Thank you, Tiko,” she said.

“Thank you, Suri,” Tiko replied. “Together, we can do hard things, one gentle step at a time.”

The End.
