# Spark the Robot Dog Helps the Kitchen

The warm kitchen glowed like a friendly hug. Sunlight slid across the floor. The air smelled like toast and cinnamon. Pots and pans rested on hooks, and a big window showed a tiny garden waving green leaves.

“Good morning!” beeped Spark, the helpful robot dog. His ears were shiny plates. His tail was a soft light that blinked blue when he felt happy. Today it blinked, blinked, blinked.

Spark’s person, Nana June, stirred oatmeal. “Good morning, Spark,” she said. “We have a busy day. The neighbors are coming later. Let’s make a big snack for everyone.”

Spark sat up tall. “I can help! I like to help our community,” he said in his gentle robot voice.

“Wonderful,” said Nana June. “Helping others makes our home and street feel warm, just like this kitchen.”

Spark listened carefully. Listening was one of his best skills. His ear-lights glowed. “What jobs can I do?” he asked.

Nana June smiled. “Let’s make fruit muffins. We’ll share them with Mr. Lee next door, Mx. Aria across the street, and the twins at the corner. First, can you gather the fruit? We need apples and bananas.”

Spark trotted to the fruit bowl. He sniffed with his soft whirring nose. “I smell sweet apples,” he said. “And mushy bananas. Perfect for muffins!” He gently lifted the fruit into a big blue bowl. Clink, clink.

“Thank you,” said Nana June. “Now, can you mash the bananas?” 

Spark looked at the fork. He wiggled his tail-light. “I can try,” he said. “But my paws are smooth. I need a tool that fits me.” Spark took a breath. “Nana June, may I use my soft-masher attachment? It will be safer for me.”

“That’s great self-advocacy,” Nana June said. “You told me what you need. Yes, use your soft-masher.”

Spark clicked his paw. A gentle, rubbery masher popped out. “Mashing now!” he said. Squish, squish, squish. The bananas turned into yummy mush.

“Good listening and safe choices,” Nana June praised. “Now, apples.”

Spark watched Nana June slice the apples. “This part is for grown-ups,” she said. “Sharp knives can hurt.”

Spark stepped back and nodded. “I will listen and wait.” He breathed in the apple smell—sweet and bright. The kitchen felt warm on his metal sides. The oven hummed softly like a sleepy bee.

Soon the batter was ready. Plop, plop, plop went the scoops into the muffin pan. Spark’s tail-light blinked faster. “I want to help more! What else do our neighbors need?”

Nana June thought. “Mr. Lee has a sore knee today. He might like a warm muffin and a kind word.”

“I can bring him one,” said Spark. He practiced his calm voice. “Hello, Mr. Lee. How are you?”

“Beautiful,” said Nana June. “That’s community-service—helping people feel cared for.”

Spark tilted his head. “What about the twins?”

“The twins are learning to say ‘please’ and ‘thank you.’ Maybe we can listen to them and wait for their words.”

Spark nodded. “Listening is caring.”

The timer went ding! The muffins came out puffed and golden. The kitchen smelled like sunshine and fruit. Spark’s tummy speaker made a happy beep.

“Let’s make a plan,” said Nana June. “I’ll carry the hot tray. You carry the basket and ring the doorbells.”

“I am ready,” said Spark.

They visited Mr. Lee first. Spark stood tall at the door. “Hello, Mr. Lee,” he said softly. “We brought a warm muffin. How are you?”

Mr. Lee opened the door with a smile. “Oh, Spark, thank you. My knee is sore, but your visit helps.” He patted Spark’s shiny head. “You listened and spoke kindly. That feels good.”

Next, they went to Mx. Aria. A paintbrush rested behind Mx. Aria’s ear. “I’m painting the fence,” they said. “The muffin will be perfect for a break.”

Spark looked at the paint can. “Would you like help carrying the water cup?” he asked. “I can be careful.”

“Thank you for asking first,” Mx. Aria said. “Yes, please. That helps.”

Spark carried the cup steady, steady, steady. He felt proud. His tail-light glowed warm white.

Finally, they visited the twins. The twins bounced like little kangaroos. “Muffin!” they squealed.

Spark crouched so his eyes met theirs. “Let’s use our listening ears,” he said kindly. “What do we say when we get something yummy?”

The twins stopped bouncing. “Please?” they said.

Spark nodded. “Yes. And after?”

“Thank you!” they chirped.

“Great job,” said Spark. He wagged. “You used your words. That’s self-advocacy too. You asked for what you needed in a nice way.”

Back in the warm kitchen, the sun slid higher. The bowls were rinsed. The basket was empty. Nana June poured two cups of chamomile tea and set out one last muffin to share.

“You helped so many people today,” Nana June said. “How do you feel?”

Spark curled on the rug. His metal sides were warm and calm. “I feel proud,” he said. “I listened. I asked for tools that fit me. I helped our neighbors. Our street feels friendlier.”

Nana June smiled. “That’s the power of community-service—small, kind actions that make big, warm feelings.”

Spark’s tail-light blinked softly. “Tomorrow,” he whispered, “let’s help again.”

The warm kitchen hummed, like it was smiling too.

The End.
