# Joey Hops to Help

The sun was warm on the tin roof of the garden shed. Inside, it smelled like fresh soil and lemon soap. A joyful kangaroo joey named Kip hopped in place, boing, boing, boing! His soft ears wiggled, and his tail thumped gently on the wooden floor.

“I want to help today!” Kip said, eyes bright. “I want to volunteer!”

Grandma Roo smiled from a corner of the shed. She wore yellow gloves and a big straw hat. “What a kind idea,” she said. “The garden club needs help. Let’s listen first, and then we’ll start. Listening helps us help.”

Kip nodded, ears up. He took a deep breath. He smelled mint leaves and dusty rakes. He heard birds peep-peep outside and felt the cool shade of the shed.

Mr. Wren, the garden leader, stepped in with a list. “Hello, helpers! Today we will sweep, stack pots, and bring water. We can make the shed tidy for everyone.”

Kip’s feet bounced. “I can hop fast! I can sweep!”

Grandma Roo touched Kip’s shoulder. “Let’s listen to the steps,” she whispered.

Mr. Wren pointed. “First, sweep the floor. Next, stack the small clay pots. Last, bring water to the thirsty plants outside.” He smiled. “Let’s make it fun!”

Kip held the small broom. Swish, swish, swish. Dust danced in the sunlight. “The broom tickles the floor,” he giggled.

Grandma Roo swept with a big broom. “Swish, swoosh,” she sang. “Sweep, sweep, sweep.”

Kip hopped a little, then remembered: listening first. He looked at the floor. He swept all the corners, even behind the green wheelbarrow. He found a shiny button. “Treasure!” he whispered, and he put it on a shelf so the owner could find it. Volunteering means caring for other people’s things.

When the sweeping was done, Kip clapped. “What’s next?” he asked.

“Stack the pots,” Mr. Wren said kindly. “Small ones together. Big ones on the bottom. Gentle hands.”

Kip picked up a pot. Cool, smooth clay brushed his paws. “I will be gentle,” he said. He tapped the pots softly together. Tap, tap. He listened to the tiny clinks and made a neat, safe tower.

A little pot wobbled. Kip steadied it with two paws and a careful tail. “It’s okay, little pot,” he whispered. “We will keep you safe.”

Grandma Roo smiled. “You’re doing great. You’re listening with your eyes and your ears.”

Kip puffed up with pride. Helping felt warm inside, like sunshine in his tummy.

“Now water for the plants,” Mr. Wren said. “We will bring the watering can outside. It is heavy.”

Kip’s eyes sparkled. “This is good exercise! My legs are strong for helping.”

He tried to lift the full watering can. “Oof!” It was heavy. He wiggled and huffed. The metal can felt cool and slippery.

Grandma Roo crouched beside him. “Let’s do it together. Helpers work as a team.”

“One, two, three, lift!” Kip said. Together, they carried the can. Their steps made soft thud-thud sounds on the shed floor. The water sloshed, whoosh, whoosh. A few drops splashed on Kip’s toes. “Cold!” he squeaked, then giggled.

Outside, the plants were quiet and thirsty. The soil was dry and crumbly. Kip tilted the can and watched the water sparkle in the sun. Splash, sprinkle, sip. The plants seemed to sigh, “Ahh.”

Mr. Wren clapped. “Wonderful work, volunteers!”

Kip’s chest rose and fell. He felt tired and happy. Exercise made his legs tingle in a good way. Volunteering made his heart feel big.

Back in the shed, the floor was clean. The tools were lined up. The pots were safe and neat. It smelled like wet earth and sweet tomatoes. The air felt calm and proud.

Kip looked around. “We did it! We helped!”

Grandma Roo put a gentle paw on his head. “You listened. You used your strong body. You worked kindly. That is real helping.”

Kip stood tall. “Listening first. Then helping. Then moving my body to do the job!” He grinned. “Volunteering is fun.”

Mr. Wren handed Kip a little green sticker shaped like a leaf. “Thank you for your time and your care,” he said. “Volunteers make things better for everyone.”

Kip stuck the leaf on his shirt. It crinkled and felt smooth under his paw. “I will come again,” he said. “I can sweep, stack, and water. I can share my hops!”

Grandma Roo nodded. “Next time, we can also tidy the seed packets. We will listen to the plan, move our bodies, and help our friends.”

Kip took one more look at the shiny floor and the happy plants. He listened. He heard birds, soft wind, and his own calm breath. He smiled a big, warm smile.

“Today I learned,” Kip said, “that volunteering means giving our time to help. Listening helps us know what to do. Exercise helps our bodies be strong for helping. When we help together, we all feel good.”

Grandma Roo hugged him close. “Yes, my joyful joey.”

Kip hopped once, light and soft. “Boing,” he whispered, full of pride and peace.

The End.
