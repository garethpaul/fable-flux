# Tessa the Brave in the Treehouse

Tessa was a shy turtle with a smooth green shell. She liked quiet things: soft leaves, gentle breezes, and tiny puddles that felt cool on her toes. One sunny afternoon, Tessa and her friends visited a wooden treehouse that smelled like pine and sunshine. The ladder was tall and the tree leaves whispered, shhh, shhh, in the wind.

“Let’s take turns going up!” chirped Poppy the sparrow, fluttering to the rail.

“I can go first,” said Max the squirrel, twitching his tail.

Tessa looked up. The ladder seemed very high. Her heart felt thump-thump, like a little drum. Her tummy felt wiggly, like jelly. She tucked her head a bit into her shell.

“It’s okay, Tessa,” Poppy sang. “We’ll wait and cheer.”

Max scampered up. Tap-tap, tap-tap went his paws on the rungs. “I made it!” he called. “The treehouse smells like cinnamon and wood!”

Poppy flew up next. Whoosh, whoosh. “It’s cozy up here!” she chirped. “There are soft pillows!”

Tessa took a small breath. She touched the first rung. It felt warm from the sun. “I’m not sure,” she whispered.

“You can try,” said Max, peeking down with bright eyes. “We’re taking turns. It’s your turn now, but only when you feel ready.”

Tessa nodded. She put one foot up. Tap. Then the other foot. Tap. She climbed one rung. Then two. But when she looked down, the ground looked far away. Her head felt floaty, and her legs felt shaky.

“I think I need to go slow,” Tessa said, her voice very tiny.

“That’s okay,” Poppy called. “Slow and steady is strong.”

Tessa tried one more rung. Tap. The bark smelled sweet. A soft breeze brushed her cheeks. But her tummy flipped again. She climbed back down, one gentle step at a time. Thump. She reached the ground and let out a breath.

“I couldn’t do it,” Tessa said, eyes shiny.

Max climbed down too. “You did try,” he said kindly. “Trying is brave.”

Poppy landed beside her and gave a gentle peep. “What helps you feel brave, Tessa?”

Tessa thought. She remembered how she felt calm when she listened to leaves and counted pebbles. “Maybe… I can practice on the bottom rungs. And I can take deep breaths.”

Max nodded. “We can all take turns practicing!”

They made a little plan. They drew a chalk line on the first rung and another on the second. “First line today,” said Poppy. “Second line after a rest. That’s a growth plan!”

Tessa smiled a tiny smile. “A little bit at a time.”

Poppy went first this time and touched the first line. “My turn!” she chirped, then flew to a branch to wait.

Max went next and tapped the first line with a happy pat. “My turn!” he said, then stepped back.

Tessa waited patiently. Waiting felt hard, but she remembered sharing turns is kind. When it was her turn, she took a deep breath. In… out. The air smelled like green leaves. She tapped the first line. Tap. Her shell felt lighter.

“Yay, Tessa!” Poppy sang.

“Your turn, Poppy,” Tessa said, smiling wider.

They kept going, slow and steady. Tap. Wait. Tap. Wait. Each time Tessa tried, she felt a little braver. Her legs felt stronger. Her heart still went thump-thump, but softer now.

After a snack of crunchy apples—crunch, crunch—and a cool sip of water, Tessa touched the second line. Tap. She blinked. “I did it!”

“You did it!” Max cheered, clapping his tiny paws.

Tessa looked up at the next rungs. The sun made little dots of light on the wood. She took another deep breath and whispered, “I can try one more rung.” She climbed. Tap. Tap. The breeze kissed her face. The bark felt friendly under her toes.

Halfway up, she paused. Her tummy wiggled. “I feel worried again,” she said.

Poppy fluttered closer. “When I worry, I say a brave thought. Like, ‘I can learn. I can try.’”

Tessa nodded. “I can learn. I can try.” She touched her shell gently. “Slow is okay.”

Step by step, with Poppy cheering and Max waiting for his turn, Tessa reached the top. She peeked into the treehouse. Inside, there were soft pillows, a small bell, and a window where sunlight poured in like warm lemonade.

“I made it,” Tessa whispered. Her eyes felt sparkly. The wood felt smooth under her hands. She rang the bell—ding!—and laughed.

“Welcome, Tessa!” Max squeaked, hopping in beside her.

“You were brave,” Poppy chirped, landing on a pillow.

Tessa smiled a big, warm smile. “I was brave because I kept trying. I went slow. I took turns. And I didn’t give up.”

They played a game in the treehouse, taking turns ringing the bell. Ding for Max. Ding for Poppy. Ding for Tessa. The air filled with happy sounds.

When it was time to climb down, Tessa led the way. Tap, tap, tap. She felt steady and proud.

On the ground, the tree leaves whispered again. Shhh, shhh. Tessa looked up at the ladder and patted it kindly. “Thank you for helping me grow,” she said.

That night, Tessa curled into her shell and felt cozy. She remembered the steps she took, the breaths she breathed, and the friends who cheered. She knew that next time, if something felt big, she could try a little at a time. She could be brave and keep going.

Lesson: Resilience means trying again, going slow, taking turns, and believing, “I can learn. I can try.”

The End.
