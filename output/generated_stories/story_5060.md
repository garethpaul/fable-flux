# Melody's Paper Nest

Melody was a little yellow bird who lived in a cozy nursery. She loved to sing sweet songs while perched on the windowsill. The warm sunlight felt good on her soft feathers.

Today, Melody wanted to make something special. She had seen the baby in the nursery playing with colorful paper. "I want to make my own pretty nest!" chirped Melody.

Melody flew down to the craft table. There were sheets of bright paper everywhere - red, blue, green, and yellow. They felt smooth and crisp under her tiny claws.

"This looks easy," sang Melody. She grabbed a piece of red paper with her beak. But when she tried to fold it, the paper tore. "Oh no!" she chirped.

Melody tried again with blue paper. She wanted to make it round like a nest. But her little claws couldn't grip it right. The paper slipped and fell to the floor.

"Tweet, tweet!" Melody felt frustrated. Her wings flapped fast. "Why can't I make a pretty nest like I want?"

Melody hopped around the nursery floor. She was so upset that she forgot to sing her happy songs. The room felt too quiet without her music.

Then Melody heard a gentle voice. "What's wrong, little bird?" It was Mrs. Robin, an older bird who lived in the tree outside.

"I can't make a paper nest," Melody chirped sadly. "My claws are too small. The paper keeps tearing."

Mrs. Robin flew through the open window. Her brown feathers looked wise and kind. "Making things with your hands - or claws - takes practice," she said softly.

"But I want it to be perfect now!" Melody said.

"Let me show you something," said Mrs. Robin. She picked up a small piece of yellow paper. "Start with something tiny. Use just the tip of your beak."

Mrs. Robin showed Melody how to make gentle folds. "Slow and careful," she cooed. "Don't rush."

Melody watched carefully. She saw how Mrs. Robin moved slowly and breathed deeply when the paper didn't fold right away.

"Now you try," said Mrs. Robin.

Melody picked up a small piece of green paper. This time, she moved her beak very slowly. When she felt frustrated, she took a deep breath like Mrs. Robin showed her.

Fold. Breathe. Fold. Breathe.

"I did it!" sang Melody. She had made a tiny paper cup! It wasn't perfect, but it was hers.

"Beautiful!" chirped Mrs. Robin. "Now try another piece."

Melody made another small fold, then another. Each time got a little easier. Her claws learned to hold the paper just right. Soon she had made five little paper cups.

"Look!" Melody sang happily. She arranged her paper cups in a circle. "It's like a nest made of cups!"

The paper cups caught the sunlight and made pretty shadows on the nursery floor. Melody felt proud and happy.

"When we take our time and practice, we can make wonderful things," said Mrs. Robin. "And when we feel frustrated, we can take deep breaths and try again."

Melody nodded and began to sing again. Her voice filled the nursery with joy. She had learned that making beautiful things takes patience, practice, and gentle movements.

From that day on, whenever Melody wanted to craft something new, she remembered to start small, move slowly, and breathe deeply when things felt hard.

The End.
