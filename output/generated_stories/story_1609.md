# Gigi the Giraffe and the Clean-Hands Club

Sunlight peeked through soft curtains in the comfortable nursery. The room smelled like fresh soap and warm blankets. Colorful blocks sat in tidy baskets, and picture books rested on a low shelf. In the middle of the play rug stood Gigi, a thoughtful giraffe with long eyelashes and a gentle smile.

Gigi loved to think. She tilted her head and listened to the quiet tick-tock of the clock. “Hmm,” she hummed, “what will we learn today?”

Her friends tumbled in. There was Benny the Bunny, Mia the Mouse, and Pip the Puppy. Their teacher, Miss Naya, wore a bright apron with tiny suns on it. She clapped her hands softly. “Good morning, friends,” she said. “Today we will play, share, and learn. But first, we wash our hands.”

Gigi looked at her hooves. They felt a little dusty from blocks. She sniffed and wrinkled her nose. “I think they smell like crayons,” she said.

“Crayons are for coloring, not for germs,” Miss Naya said kindly. “Let’s go to the sink.”

The sink was low and shiny. The soap smelled like oranges. Swish, swish, went the water. “Wet, soap, scrub, rinse, dry,” Miss Naya sang.

Gigi sang back, “Wet, soap, scrub, rinse, dry.” She rubbed her hooves together. The bubbles felt tickly. She scrubbed the tops, the bottoms, and in between. “I’m washing for twenty,” she said, counting in a soft voice. “One, two, three...” The warm water rushed like a tiny waterfall.

Benny hopped from hoof to hoof. “Do I have to?” he asked.

Gigi smiled. “Yes, Benny. It’s kind to your body. Clean hands keep us safe.”

Benny sniffed the orange soap. “Mmm. Okay,” he said, and he scrubbed too.

After drying their hands on fluffy towels, the friends sat for story time. Miss Naya held up a book with shiny pages. “This book is about a Clean-Hands Club,” she said. “In the club, everyone helps everyone. That is fair.”

Gigi’s ears perked up. “Fair means justice,” she said thoughtfully. “Everyone follows the same kind rules.”

“That’s right,” said Miss Naya. “It’s fair to keep our germs to ourselves. We do that with soap and water.”

They read and giggled at the pictures of silly soap bubbles wearing crowns. Then it was snack time. Apples, crackers, and little cups of water waited on the table. Gigi touched her hooves together and paused. “Wait,” she said. “Did we wash before snack?”

Mia the Mouse squeaked, “We washed before, but then we touched blocks again.”

Gigi nodded. “We should wash again. That is responsible.”

“Responsible means doing what is right, even when it is hard,” Miss Naya added.

The friends lined up. Swish, swish went the water. “Wet, soap, scrub, rinse, dry,” they sang together. The room filled with happy voices and orange-scented steam. Pip the Puppy wagged his tail so fast that droplets sprinkled like tiny rain. “Oops!” he barked.

Gigi laughed. “It’s okay, Pip. Let’s wipe it up.” She grabbed a towel and patted the water until the floor was dry. “We keep our nursery safe.”

After snack, they built a tall block tower. Gigi stretched her long neck to place the last block. Tap, tap, tap. The tower shimmered in the sunlight. Then, a little sneeze squeaked from Benny. “Achoo!”

Mia blinked. “Bless you,” she said.

Benny looked worried. “Did I do it right?”

Gigi spoke gently. “Next time, cover your sneeze with your elbow,” she said, bending her long leg to show him. “Like this. Then wash your hands.”

Benny tried. “Achoo,” he pretended into his elbow. “Like that?”

“Perfect,” said Miss Naya. “You are learning. That is education!”

Benny smiled. “Education helps me be fair and safe,” he said, proud.

A little while later, Pip found a shiny sticker under a chair. It was shaped like a star. “I want it!” he yipped, nose twitching.

Mia blinked. “I think it fell off the soap chart,” she said. “It might belong to everyone.”

Gigi thought for a moment. She felt the smooth sticker with her hoof. “Let’s be fair,” she said. “We can earn it together. We can make a Clean-Hands Club chart. When we remember to wash, we get a star.”

“That sounds just,” Miss Naya said, smiling. “Justice means the rules help everyone. No one is left out.”

So they made a chart with names and little boxes. Each time someone washed before eating, after sneezing, or after the potty, they got a tiny star. The chart filled up slowly. The nursery smelled sweet and clean. The towels felt warm and soft. The friends’ hands looked shiny and dry.

At the end of the day, Miss Naya looked at the chart. “You all were so responsible,” she said. “You washed with care. You reminded each other with kind words. That is fair and just.”

Gigi felt her heart glow like sunshine. “We did it together,” she said. “Clean hands show love. Clean hands keep us safe.”

Benny nodded. “And clean hands taste better,” he giggled, taking one last sip of water.

Miss Naya gave each friend a small paper star. “You are all in the Clean-Hands Club,” she said. “Sing your song at home too.”

As the sun turned golden and the clock went tick-tock, Gigi leaned against a big pillow. She smelled the soft soap on her hooves and smiled. “Wet, soap, scrub, rinse, dry,” she whispered. “I can be thoughtful, I can be fair, and I can be clean.”

The lesson felt cozy inside her, like a warm blanket. She knew she could help her friends, her family, and herself by washing, learning, and caring every day.

The End.
