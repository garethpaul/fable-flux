# Joey Roo Helps the Backyard Bloom

The sun shone bright in the big, sunny backyard. Warm light tickled the grass. Bees hummed. The air smelled like sweet flowers and fresh dirt. A joyful kangaroo joey named Joey Roo bounced out the back door. Boing! Boing! Boing!

“Good morning, backyard!” Joey Roo giggled. His tail swished with happy excitement.

Nana waved from a wooden bench. She wore a big straw hat. “Hello, Joey Roo,” she called. “Our neighbor, Mr. Wren, needs help. His garden is messy. The wind scattered leaves and sticks everywhere.”

Joey Roo’s ears perked up. “We can help! I love to help.”

Nana smiled. “That’s community-service, dear. We help our neighbors. We make our place bright and kind.”

Joey Roo took a deep breath. He smelled lemony soap and flower petals. He felt the warm sun on his fur. He listened to the bees buzz and a faraway dog bark. “I’m ready,” he said.

Nana pointed to a tidy basket of tools. “Let’s plan. Good helpers use coordination. That means we work together, step by step.”

Joey Roo bounced closer. Inside the basket were little gloves, a tiny rake, a soft brush, and a bright blue watering can.

“What’s step one?” Joey Roo asked.

“Step one is look and listen,” Nana said. “We see what the garden needs.”

They hopped to the fence. Mr. Wren peeked over with a gentle smile. “Oh, thank you,” he said softly. His glasses sparkled in the sun. “The wind made a mess, and my knees are tired today.”

Joey Roo waved. “We will help, Mr. Wren!”

Joey Roo and Nana opened the gate. Crunch, crunch went the dry leaves under their feet. A little robin chirped, “Cheep-cheep!” The garden bed was full of scattered sticks and a leaning watering can. A tiny wagon lay on its side.

Joey Roo put on the little gloves. “My paws are ready,” he said. He felt the snug, safe squeeze.

Nana nodded. “Step two is sort. Can you sort sticks and leaves?”

“Yes!” Joey Roo said. He used the tiny rake with careful moves. Scritch, scritch, scritch. He pulled leaves into a happy pile. He stacked sticks in a neat bundle. “Leaves here. Sticks there,” he sang. “Leaves here. Sticks there.”

“That’s good coordination,” Nana said. “Eyes watching, hands working, feet steady.”

Joey Roo smiled. “I can do it.” His tail helped him balance. He used slow steps and steady breaths. He stood the wagon up. “Ready, little wagon?” he whispered. The wheel squeaked, eeeek!

Mr. Wren brought out a small broom. “Sweep-sweep,” he said. Joey Roo copied him. “Sweep-sweep!” The patio stones felt bumpy under Joey Roo’s feet. The broom made soft whooshing sounds.

“Step three is care,” Nana said. “Let’s check the plants.”

They walked to a droopy sunflower. The petals felt silky and warm. Joey Roo touched the soil. “It’s dry,” he said.

Nana nodded. “Water time. Slow and gentle.”

Joey Roo lifted the bright blue watering can. It felt heavy. “I will go slowly,” he said. He tilted the can just a little. Drip, drop, dribble. The dirt turned dark and happy. The sunflower lifted its head, just a little. Joey Roo’s eyes sparkled. “It’s smiling!”

Mr. Wren clapped quietly. “You have a kind touch, Joey Roo.”

Joey Roo giggled. “I’m learning a new skill. Water slow, not splash.”

“Step four is teamwork,” Nana said. “We can move the leaf pile together.”

Joey Roo held one side of a burlap sack. Nana held the other side. “Ready, set, lift,” Nana said.

“Lift!” Joey Roo echoed. They counted, “One, two, three,” and poured the leaves into the big compost bin. Rustle, rustle! A fresh, earthy smell puffed up.

The robin hopped closer. “Cheep-cheep!” It found a tiny worm and flew away.

Joey Roo looked around. The garden was not messy anymore. The path was clear. The wagon stood straight. The watering can sat by the tap. The sunflower stood taller, bright and brave.

Mr. Wren’s eyes were shiny. “Thank you, helpers,” he said. He opened a tin and shared sweet orange slices. “For kind hearts.”

Joey Roo tasted one. Juicy! Cool! “Mmm,” he hummed. “Helping tastes sunny.”

Nana patted Joey Roo’s shoulder. “You did community-service today. You used coordination. You built new skills.”

Joey Roo nodded. “I looked and listened. I sorted. I cared. I worked as a team.” He touched his chest. “My heart feels big.”

Mr. Wren pointed to a small sign by the gate. It said, “Thank you, helpers!” with a little painted flower. “You may add a sticker,” he said.

Joey Roo chose a star sticker and pressed it on the sign. “A star for the team,” he said. His paws were a bit dirty, and he didn’t mind. They smelled like leaves and sunshine.

The bees still hummed. The breeze was soft. “Nana,” Joey Roo said, “Can we make a Helping Plan for every sunny day?”

Nana smiled. “Yes. We can help in small ways every day. Pick up a stick. Share water. Sweep a step. Say kind words. Each small help makes a big bright place.”

Joey Roo bounced once, twice, three times. Boing! Boing! Boing! He waved to Mr. Wren. “See you soon!”

As they walked back, Joey Roo whispered, “Helping is my favorite adventure.”

“And today,” Nana said, “you learned that helping together, step by step, makes our world bloom.”

Joey Roo grinned. “I can help. I can learn. I can do it with you.”

The End.
