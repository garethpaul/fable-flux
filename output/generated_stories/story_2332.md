# Benny Beaver’s Balcony Big Feelings

Benny was a busy beaver with bright front teeth and a soft, flat tail. He lived in a cozy home with a tiny balcony garden. The garden had pots of mint, basil, and little tomatoes on a vine. Bees hummed. Leaves smelled sweet and green. The sun felt warm on Benny’s fur.

“Good morning, garden,” Benny said. “Let’s work together.”

Benny loved to build and tidy. He liked to pat soil with his tail and line up pebbles in neat, little rows. Today, he wanted to make a tiny wooden arch for the tomato vine. He had sticks, string, and a small watering can that went tip-tip-tip.

He started to tie a knot. The string slipped. The knot fell open.

“Oh, fiddly sticks!” Benny huffed. His cheeks felt hot, and his tail thumped the floor. Thump. Thump.

Just then, his neighbor, Maya, peeked over the railing. She wore a sun hat with a blue ribbon. “Hi, Benny! It smells like mint tea out here.”

“It does,” said Benny, sniffing. “But my knot does not. My knot will not! It keeps falling.”

Maya smiled softly. “That sounds frustrating.”

Benny crossed his arms. “I feel grumpy. My tummy feels squirmy like a worm.”

Maya nodded. “Let’s use our words. You can say, ‘I feel frustrated.’ Can you try?”

Benny took a small breath. “I feel… frustrated.”

The word felt big and round, but it fit. He said it again, slowly. “Fru-stra-ted.”

Maya clapped quietly. “Good using words! Words can help our feelings feel smaller.”

Benny watched a bee float by. He listened. Bzzzz. The sound was soft and steady, like a tiny song. The air smelled like wet dirt and basil. He felt the sun tickle his whiskers.

Maya pointed to a leaf. “Let’s do a mindful minute. We can use our five senses. What do you see?”

“I see shiny tomatoes,” Benny said, peeking at the little red dots. “I see a blue sky.”

“What do you hear?”

“I hear the city cars far away. Vrrmm. And I hear the bee. Bzzzz.”

“What do you smell?”

“Mint!” Benny giggled. “And basil. It smells like pizza.”

“What do you feel?”

Benny rubbed his paws together. “I feel the smooth string. I feel the warm sun.”

“What do you taste?”

Benny took a tiny nibble of a basil leaf. “Spicy!” he chirped.

Maya smiled. “Good noticing. Now take three belly breaths. In through the nose, out through the mouth.”

Benny puffed his cheeks in. And out. In. And out. In. And out.

His tummy felt calmer. His shoulders went soft.

“I still have a knot to tie,” he said, “but I don’t feel stormy now.”

“Let’s try together,” Maya said. “That’s cooperation.”

They sat knee to knee on the balcony. Benny held the sticks. Maya held the string.

“First, we loop,” Maya said. “Can we make up a little string song to help us remember?”

Benny’s eyes lit up. He loved words. “Loop and swoop,” he sang. “Under and over, pull and stay.”

They sang together, quiet and happy.

“Loop and swoop,
Under and over,
Pull and stay,
Good knot today!”

Benny pulled, gently. The knot held tight. The tomato vine sighed as if it was happy too, resting on the tiny arch.

“We did it!” Benny cheered. He clapped his paws, pat-pat-pat. His tail thumped, but this time it was a happy thump. Thump! Thump!

Then the watering can tipped, and water splashed on Benny’s toes. Cold! He jumped. A pebble rolled out of line.

Benny’s face scrunched. “Oh no! Not again!”

Maya touched his shoulder. “Big feelings can come back. What can we do?”

Benny put a paw on his tummy. “I can say my feeling. I feel… frustrated.” He took one belly breath. In. Out. He looked at the pebble. “I can fix it slowly.”

He picked up the pebble and set it back. He wiggled his toes in the cool water. “It’s splashy,” he said, smiling a little. “It feels funny.”

“That’s noticing,” Maya said. “Mindfulness helps us see small good things.”

Benny nodded. “Words help too. Fru-stra-ted. And ‘I can try again.’”

They finished watering together. Tip-tip-tip. The pots glistened. The air smelled like rain and leaves.

Benny held up a small sign he had made from a stick and a card. He had written a word in big, careful letters: GROW. He planted the sign near the tomatoes.

Maya read it aloud. “Grow.”

Benny grinned. “Grow is a good word for plants. And it is a good word for me.”

Maya tilted her head. “How so?”

“I can grow my patience,” Benny said. “I can grow my calm. I can grow my words.”

They high-fived. Slap!

As the sun began to rest, Benny looked over his balcony garden. The arch stood tall. The string held. The pebbles lined up. Bees hummed a good-night song.

“Thank you for helping,” Benny said.

“You helped too,” Maya replied. “You used your words. You breathed. You tried again. That is cooperation.”

Benny stretched and took one more mindful breath. He whispered to the plants, “Good job, team.”

He felt proud and peaceful. His tummy felt still, like a quiet pond.

Today, Benny learned that when frustration comes, he can stop, breathe, use words, and try again. He learned that being outside, noticing smells and sounds, helps feelings settle. He learned that songs and kind friends make hard things easier. And he learned that growing takes time.

The End.
