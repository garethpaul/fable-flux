# Sprout, the Helpful Robot Dog

Sprout, the helpful robot dog, lived in a cozy garden shed. The shed smelled like fresh soil and sweet wood. Sunlight peeked through the little window and made tiny dust sparkles dance in the air. Tools hung on hooks. A watering can, a trowel, and seed packets sat on a shelf. Sprout’s metal paws went click-clack on the floor, and his round eyes glowed a gentle blue.

One morning, Sprout’s antenna beeped. “Ping! New message,” he said in a warm, friendly voice. He wagged his metal tail. “Hello! Who needs help today?”

A tablet on the wall lit up. Faces appeared—children from many places. A girl with bright braids smiled. A boy with a red scarf waved. Another child with curly hair held up a small plant. They spoke in kind voices, each with a different sound.

“We are making a Garden of Friendship,” the girl said. “But we have a problem.”

“What is the problem?” Sprout asked, tilting his shiny head. He listened carefully. He always listened carefully.

“Our seeds are from all around the world,” the boy with the scarf said. “But we don’t know how to plant them the right way.”

Sprout sniffed the air. He could smell the peppery scent of seeds and the damp smell of soil. “We can learn together,” he said. “We can share knowledge. We can be good neighbors to our big world.”

He tapped his paw on the floor. Whirr. Click. A drawer opened. Out came a soft brush, a tiny shovel, and a notebook with a bright green cover. On the cover, Sprout had drawn a picture of Earth with lots of smiling faces.

“Let’s meet in the garden shed,” Sprout said. “We will make a plan.”

The children gathered at the shed door. They took off muddy shoes and giggled. “It smells like rain in here,” the curly-haired child said.

Sprout nodded. “Rain is good for seeds.”

He gently poured the different seeds onto a tray. There were small black seeds like pepper, long thin seeds like tiny boats, and round gold seeds like little suns.

The girl with braids touched the round gold seeds. “These are from my grandma,” she said softly. “She says they love warm sun.”

The boy with the red scarf lifted the long thin seeds. “These are from my uncle,” he said. “He says they drink lots of water.”

The curly-haired child held the tiny black seeds. “These are from my neighbor,” they said. “They like cool mornings.”

Sprout’s ears whirred. “We each have a piece of the puzzle. Let’s put our knowledge together.”

He opened his green notebook. “Step one: Ask. Step two: Listen. Step three: Share. Step four: Care.”

The children nodded. “Ask, listen, share, care,” they echoed.

“Ask,” said Sprout. “What do these seeds need?”

“They need warm sun,” the girl said.

“They need lots of water,” the boy said.

“They need cool mornings,” the curly-haired child said.

“Listen,” said Sprout. He closed his eyes and listened to the garden. Birds chirped. Leaves rustled. A distant bell rang. “Our garden has sun in the afternoon, shade in the morning, and a rain barrel for water.”

“Share,” said Sprout. “We can share space and time. We can plan a bed for sun, a bed for shade, and a path for water.”

They moved together. Sprout showed them how to scoop soft soil. “Scoop, scoop,” went the trowel. The soil felt cool and crumbly in their hands. They smelled the earth—rich and clean.

“Warm sun seeds go here,” the girl said, patting the sunny spot. She hummed a song her grandma taught her. It sounded like sunshine, bright and gentle.

“Water-loving seeds go near the rain barrel,” the boy said, filling the ground with little rivers from the watering can. Splish, splash. He sang a tune that sounded like a flowing stream.

“Cool morning seeds go under the tall tree,” the curly-haired child said, smiling. A breeze brushed their cheeks, light and sweet.

“Care,” said Sprout. He pressed a button on his chest. A soft light glowed, and a tiny fan blew a gentle wind. “I can help watch the air and water. You can help with songs, stories, and kind hands.”

Days passed. The children visited the garden shed. Sprout greeted them with happy beeps. “Ping! Good morning, friends!”

They checked the beds. Green sprouts peeked up. The garden smelled like mint and tomatoes and wet leaves. Bees buzzed softly. A butterfly landed on Sprout’s shiny nose. He giggled. “That tickles!”

One afternoon, a cloud covered the sun. The warm-sun seeds drooped.

“Oh no,” the girl said. “They look sad.”

Sprout blinked. “This is our next problem. How can we help?”

The boy thought. “We can reflect light,” he said, holding a shiny spoon. The curly-haired child found a piece of clean foil. Sprout gently angled a mirror from the shelf.

Together, they bounced light to the plants. The leaves lifted. “We did it!” the children cheered.

Sprout wagged his tail. “We solved the problem by sharing ideas from many places. That is global citizenship. We care for each other and our world.”

The children smiled. “Ask, listen, share, care,” they said again.

By the end of the week, the Garden of Friendship was full. Red tomatoes, tall beans, tiny purple flowers, and bright green herbs waved in the breeze. The garden shed felt warm and happy. The air smelled like life.

The children sat in a circle on the shed floor. Sprout brought small cups of mint water. Sip, sip. It tasted cool and sweet.

“We learned so much,” the girl said.

“We helped together,” the boy said.

“We used ideas from all around the world,” the curly-haired child said.

Sprout looked around with bright eyes. “When we share knowledge and respect our many ways, everyone grows.”

They all hugged Sprout. His metal fur felt smooth and safe.

The End.
