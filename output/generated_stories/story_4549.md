# Rosie's Fair Share Journal

Little Rosie the mouse sat at the big dining room table. The wooden surface felt smooth and cool under her tiny paws. Sunlight streamed through the window, making the table shine like gold.

Rosie had a special red notebook and a small pencil. She loved to write down important things that happened each day. Today felt extra special because her family was having a big dinner together.

"Rosie, can you help set the table?" asked Papa Mouse, carrying a stack of tiny plates.

"Of course!" squeaked Rosie cheerfully. She put down her pencil and hopped off her chair.

As they worked together, Rosie noticed something that made her whiskers twitch with worry. Her little brother Pip was getting three cookies on his plate, but her sister Lily only had one cookie.

"Papa," said Rosie gently, "I think there's a mistake. Pip has more cookies than Lily."

Papa Mouse looked at the plates. His ears perked up with surprise. "Oh my! You're absolutely right, Rosie. Thank you for noticing."

Papa Mouse moved one cookie from Pip's plate to Lily's plate. Now they each had two cookies - exactly the same amount.

"That's fair!" said Lily with a big smile.

Pip's face scrunched up. "But I wanted three cookies!" he whined.

Rosie felt her tummy get tight. She wanted to help Pip understand, but she also knew what was right. She took three deep breaths, just like Mama had taught her when feelings got big.

"Pip," said Rosie kindly, "when we share things fairly, everyone feels happy. It's like when we play together - we all get the same turns."

Rosie picked up her red notebook and wrote carefully: "Today I helped make dinner fair for everyone."

"What are you writing, Rosie?" asked Lily, peeking over her shoulder.

"I'm writing about being fair," explained Rosie. "When I write things down, it helps me remember important lessons."

Papa Mouse smiled proudly. "That's very wise, Rosie. Writing helps us think about what we learn."

During dinner, something else happened. Mama Mouse served the soup, but she accidentally gave herself a much smaller bowl than everyone else.

Rosie noticed right away. "Mama, your bowl is too small! You need more soup to feel strong and healthy."

"You're such a caring mouse, Rosie," said Mama Mouse. "You always notice when things aren't fair."

Rosie helped pour more soup into Mama's bowl until it matched everyone else's. She felt warm and happy inside, like sunshine was glowing in her chest.

After dinner, Rosie wrote in her notebook again: "I helped Mama get enough soup. Fair means everyone gets what they need."

"Reading is fun, but writing is special too," said Rosie to her family. "When I write about fairness, it helps me remember to look out for others."

Papa Mouse hugged Rosie tight. "You have a big heart and a smart mind, little one. You help our family stay happy and healthy."

That night, as Rosie snuggled in her cozy bed, she felt proud of her day. She had used her eyes to notice unfairness, her voice to speak up kindly, and her writing to remember important lessons.

"Tomorrow I'll keep watching for ways to be fair," whispered Rosie to herself. "And I'll write about them too."

The dining room grew quiet and peaceful. Rosie's red notebook sat on the table, filled with words about kindness and fairness - ready for tomorrow's adventures.

The End.
