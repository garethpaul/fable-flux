# Ollie Otter’s Brave Brush

In a cozy bedroom with soft blue curtains and twinkly star stickers on the ceiling, a playful otter named Ollie wiggled his whiskers. The room smelled like clean blankets and fresh crayons. His bed was a little boat-shaped bed, and it made gentle creak-creak sounds when he climbed in. On the wall, a big paper hung from a tape, waiting for a painting.

“I want to paint a big picture,” Ollie said, patting his round belly. “I want to make a brave lion!”

He opened his paint box. The box clicked open and showed bright colors—sunny yellow, ocean blue, apple red, and grassy green. Ollie dipped his brush into the yellow paint. Drip. Drip. The brush felt cool and tickly in his paw.

But when he started, the lines went wiggly. The lion’s mane looked squiggly. The nose was smudgy. The colors smooshed together into a brownish swirl.

Ollie’s tummy felt tight. His tail drooped. “Oh no,” he whispered. “It’s not right. I can’t do it.”

He wanted to hide under his soft blanket. He wanted to stop.

Just then, Mama Otter poked her head into the cozy bedroom. She smelled like warm soap and hot chocolate. “Hi, Ollie,” she said gently. “How does your painting feel?”

Ollie shook his head. “It feels hard,” he said. “I’m scared I can’t paint a brave lion. My lines are wiggly.”

Mama sat on the rug, which was fluffy like a cloud. “Sometimes brave is wiggly,” she said. “Brave can look like trying again. Brave can sound like a deep breath. Can we try a brave breath together?”

Ollie nodded. They breathed in—slow and long. They breathed out—soft and slow. Whoosh.

Ollie’s shoulders relaxed. His whiskers stopped trembling.

Mama smiled. “Courage lives in little steps,” she said. “Let’s make little steps. First, a circle face. Then, a nose. Then, a mane. One step at a time. I’ll sit with you.”

Ollie dipped his brush again. Plop. He made a circle. It wasn’t perfect, but it was a circle. “Circle,” he said.

“Good step,” said Mama. “Now a nose.”

Ollie dotted a tiny triangle. Tap. “Nose,” he said, feeling a tiny glow in his chest.

He looked at his painting. He still felt a little scared. “What if I make a mistake?” he asked.

Mama pointed to a small cup of water. “We can wash the brush. We can try again. Patience is waiting and trying. Kindness is being gentle to yourself.”

Ollie dipped the brush into the water. Swish-swish. The water turned light orange. It sounded like rain in a teacup. He wiped the brush on a soft towel—dab, dab.

He chose the apple red and made little lines for the mane. One, two, three. The lines went around the circle like sunshine. Some lines were long, some were short, and some went a little crooked.

“I did it!” Ollie giggled. His heart felt warm. He wiggled in his boat bed and peeked out again. “I did a brave try!”

Mama clapped softly. “That’s courage, Ollie. You kept going.”

Just then, a little paint drip slid down—plop! It landed right on the lion’s cheek. “Oh!” Ollie blinked. “Now it looks like a tear.”

Ollie felt the tight tummy again. He pressed his paw on his chest and took a brave breath. In. Out.

“I can fix it,” he said slowly. “I can try again.” He took the grassy green, dipped carefully, and painted a tiny leaf near the tear. Now it looked like the lion was in grass. “Hello, lion,” Ollie whispered. “You are brave too.”

A small squeak came from the doorway. Ollie’s stuffed mouse, Mr. Nibble, sat propped against the door, smiling with his stitched eyes. Ollie tucked Mr. Nibble under his arm. “We are brave together,” he said.

He added sunny yellow to the lion’s face. He added a soft pink tongue. He made two friendly eyes. Dot. Dot.

The cozy bedroom felt extra warm. The star stickers seemed to glow. The paint smelled sweet and a little tangy. Ollie hummed a happy tune. His brush made soft scratch-scratch sounds on the paper.

When he finished, Ollie stepped back. The lion looked kind and strong. It had a big mane and a gentle smile. It was not perfect. It was Ollie’s.

“I’m proud of you,” Mama said. “You showed courage, kindness, and patience. You tried, you waited, and you were gentle with yourself.”

Ollie’s tail swished. “I felt scared,” he said. “But I kept going.”

Mama nodded. “That is what brave looks like.”

Ollie looked at the little brownish swirl from before. He touched it lightly with a clean brush and turned it into a small tree next to the lion. “Mistakes can be art,” he said, eyes bright.

He took a crayon and wrote, “BRAVE LION” in big, careful letters at the bottom. He added a tiny heart for kindness. He added a clock with slow hands for patience. He added a little bandage for resilience, to show healing and trying again.

Ollie hugged Mama. Mama hugged back, warm and tight. “Tomorrow,” Ollie said, “I will paint a brave river.”

“Tomorrow,” Mama said, “you will be brave again.”

Ollie yawned. The room felt snug. The boat bed creaked softly as he climbed in. He tucked Mr. Nibble close. The painting dried with a gentle papery whisper.

“I can be brave,” Ollie whispered to the stars. “I can be kind. I can be patient.”

And the cozy bedroom felt full of courage.

The End.
