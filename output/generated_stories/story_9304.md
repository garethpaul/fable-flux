# Tilly Turtle Takes Her Time

Tilly was a shy turtle. She liked quiet places. One day, Tilly climbed the small wooden stairs to the quiet attic. The attic was soft with dust and smelled like old books and warm wood. Sunlight slid through a little round window and made golden lines on the floor. It was peaceful. It was safe.

Tilly heard a soft hum. “Hello?” she whispered.

“Hello!” sang a tiny voice. A moth fluttered by the window. “I’m Mo the moth. I like light.”

Tilly smiled a small smile. “I’m Tilly. I like quiet.”

On the old rug, Tilly saw a little drum, a tin triangle, and a shiny spinning top. A faded sign leaned against a box. It said, “Fair Fun Band.” The letters were bouncy and bright.

Tilly touched the drum. Tap-tap. It made a gentle thump. “Oh,” said Tilly, “I want to play.”

Mo fluttered closer. “We can make a band! I can hum. You can drum. And look—a metronome!” He pointed to a small wooden triangle with a swinging arm. Tick-tock, tick-tock.

Tilly’s eyes went big. “What does it do?”

“It helps us keep time,” said Mo. “It’s fair for all. We take turns. We listen. We go slow. We practice.”

Tilly took a deep breath. She lifted the drumstick. Tap—tap—tap. The sound was wobbly. The metronome went tick-tock, tick-tock. Tilly tried to tap with it, but her taps were too fast. Then too slow. Her head tucked a little into her shell. “I can’t do it,” she said.

Mo landed on the drum. “It’s okay,” he said softly. “We can be patient. We can try again. We will take our time.”

Tilly listened to the ticks. She felt her heart beat. She tapped again. Tap—tap—tap. Still wobbly. Her cheeks felt warm. “I am too slow,” she said.

Mo hummed. “Slow is okay. Slow is good. You are a turtle. Taking time is your superpower. Let’s count together. One, two, tap. One, two, tap.”

They counted. They tapped. The attic felt cozy. Dust sparkles danced in the sun. The drum sounded like soft rain on leaves. Tilly’s shell felt less tight. Her taps started to match the tick-tock. Tap… tap… tap.

From a box, a plush bear peeked out. “May I listen?” the bear asked in a sleepy voice.

“Yes,” said Tilly. “But I am still learning.”

“That is fair,” said the bear. “We all learn.”

They tried the triangle next. Ting! It sang like a tiny star. Tilly smiled more. “I like that sound.”

“Let’s practice taking turns,” said Mo. “I hum. You ting on two.” He hummed, “Mmm-one, mmm-two—”

Tilly waited. Her tummy fluttered. She wanted to ting right away. But she squeezed her eyes, listened for the count, and waited. “Mmm-one, mmm-two—” Ting! The sound rang clear and bright.

The bear clapped his paws. “That was patient,” he said. “You waited. You were fair. You let Mo lead.”

Warm pride bubbled in Tilly’s chest. “Thank you,” she said.

They played again. Sometimes Tilly tinged too early. Sometimes too late. Each time, she stopped, breathed in, and tried again. Mo said, “We can pause. We can practice.” The metronome kept a steady tick-tock, like a kind friend saying, “I am here.”

Soon, the attic filled with little sounds: tap, ting, hummm, tick-tock, the soft swish of Mo’s wings, the creak of the wooden floor. It felt like a calm song. Tilly’s shy felt smaller. Her brave felt bigger.

Then a breeze slid in through the window. The spinning top rolled off the shelf. Clack! It bumped the drum and fell with a wobble. “Oh no!” said Tilly. “We have a problem!”

The top would not spin. It wobbled and stopped. Tilly picked it up. “I want to make it go now,” she said.

Mo shook his head kindly. “Let’s be patient. Let’s try step by step. We can practice.”

Tilly set the top down. She pushed it too hard. It fell. She pushed it too soft. It did not move. Her eyes felt wet. “It’s not fair,” she whispered. “Mo is fast. I am slow.”

The bear leaned closer. “Fair is not fast,” he said. “Fair means we all get time. Fair means we all get help. You get time. You get help.”

Mo nodded. “Let’s count together. Hold the top. One, two, three—spin. Gentle and steady.”

Tilly wiped her eyes. She held the top. She waited for the count. One… two… three—spin. Her paw moved smooth and slow. The top began to turn. Whirr. Round and round it went, humming like a bee. Tilly laughed, a sweet small laugh. “I did it!”

“You did it because you were patient,” said Mo.

“You did it because you practiced,” said the bear.

“And we were fair with each other,” said Tilly. “We took turns. We used the tick-tock. We waited.”

The top spun until it slowed and rested. The attic was quiet again. Tilly felt calm and proud. She hugged the drum. She tapped once more. Tap. It matched the tick-tock.

Tilly looked at her friends. “When I take my time, I can do new things,” she said. “I can be shy and still be brave.”

Mo smiled. “Patience helps our hearts feel steady.”

The bear yawned. “Practice makes us strong.”

Tilly nodded. “And being fair makes friends.”

They put the toys back neatly. The sun made one last golden line on the floor. Tilly whispered, “Thank you, attic. Thank you, friends.”

Then they went down the little stairs, slow and happy, taking their time together.

The End.
