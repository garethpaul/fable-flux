# Bouncy Roo Listens

In a comfortable nursery, soft yellow light glowed on the walls. Pillows were plump, the rug was fuzzy like a cloud, and tiny wooden blocks waited in neat baskets. A joyful kangaroo joey named Roo bounced in, his little tail wiggling.

“Good morning, Roo,” said Miss Maple, the gentle teacher. Her voice was warm like cocoa. “Today we will play, snack, and sing. We will follow our classroom rules to stay safe and fair.”

Roo clapped his small paws. “I love to play! I love to bounce!”

Miss Maple smiled. “Wonderful! First, let’s listen. Ears open, eyes watching, body calm. That helps us follow directions.”

Roo’s ears perked up. He tried to hold still, but his feet did a tiny hop-hop.

Miss Maple pointed to a colorful chart. “We have three steps. One: Use walking feet inside. Two: Take turns and share. Three: Stop and listen when you hear the chime.” She held up a silver chime that made a soft, twinkly sound.

“Ting-ting,” she rang it. The sound was sparkly like stars.

Roo giggled. “Ting-ting! I can do that!”

Playtime began. Roo saw a big tower of blocks. It was taller than a chair, with red, blue, and yellow squares. “Wow!” he breathed. “Bouncy tower!”

He bounced closer. His paws tingled to touch. But Miss Maple’s voice floated over the rug. “Remember, Roo: walking feet.”

Roo took a breath. He felt the fuzzy rug under his toes. He set his feet down gently. “Walking feet,” he whispered, and he walked to the blocks. His heart felt proud.

“Can I add a block?” he asked, peeking at his friends, Mia and Theo.

“Yes,” said Theo. “But we are taking turns.”

Mia smiled. “You can go after me.”

Roo’s tummy wiggled. He really wanted to go now. He watched Mia place a blue block very carefully. His paws twitched. When Mia stepped back, Roo reached up fast, bouncing just a little—boing! His elbow bumped the tower.

“Oh no!” Roo gasped.

Clatter-clack! The tower tumbled like rain. Blocks scattered across the rug with a wooden rattle.

Mia’s eyes grew wet. “I was still thinking,” she said softly.

Theo looked down. “We worked hard,” he murmured.

Roo’s ears drooped. His belly felt squishy and sad. “I’m sorry,” he said in a tiny voice. “I forgot to wait for my turn.”

Miss Maple came over, kneeling so her eyes were kind and level. “Thank you for saying sorry, Roo. Let’s practice the directions again. We take turns so it’s fair. And we use gentle bodies so we stay safe. What can we do now?”

Roo sniffed. He looked at Mia and Theo. “We can fix it together,” he said. “I can wait. You tell me when it’s my turn.”

Mia wiped her eyes. “Okay. My turn, then Theo, then Roo.”

Roo folded his paws and watched. He felt the soft air and heard the click of each block. He hummed the chime sound in his head—ting-ting—to help him wait. When it was finally his turn, he took one yellow block. He breathed in slow, placed it carefully, and stepped back.

“There,” he said, eyes wide.

“Good listening,” said Theo.

The tower grew again, steady and tall. Roo’s smile came back like sunshine.

Soon, the silver chime rang—ting-ting! Everyone froze like gentle statues. Roo wanted to place one more block, but he remembered the rule. Stop and listen when you hear the chime. He put the block down and looked at Miss Maple.

“Time for snack,” she said. “Walking feet.”

Roo looked at the snack table. Bowls of apple slices smelled sweet and crisp. Cups of cool water sat in a neat row. He felt the urge to bounce to the table, but he whispered, “Walking feet,” and stepped softly across the rug.

At the table, Miss Maple spoke again. “We have enough for everyone. Please wait until I say, ‘Begin.’ That keeps it fair.”

Roo’s tummy rumbled like a tiny drum. He licked his lips. The apple slices shone like little moons. But he folded his paws and waited.

“Begin,” said Miss Maple.

Crunch! The apples were juicy and cool. Roo took small bites. “These are so yummy,” he said with a happy sigh.

After snack, it was time for music. Miss Maple gave out small shakers. Roo lifted his shaker high. “Shake-shake!” he sang, ready to go wild.

Miss Maple raised a finger. “Shakers in your lap until the music starts,” she said. “That keeps our ears safe.”

Roo put the shaker in his lap. He felt it cool against his fur. He wiggled, but he waited. When Miss Maple started the song, he shook it to the beat—shake, shake, shake! The sound was bright and cheerful. Everyone danced with their shoulders and toes, but they kept their bodies safe and their space fair.

At cleanup, Roo saw a shaker under a chair. Mia reached for it at the same time. “My turn?” Mia asked.

Roo nodded. “You saw it first. I can wait.” He stepped back. Mia smiled and handed it to Roo after she put hers away. “Now your turn,” she said.

“Thank you,” Roo said. His chest felt warm. Waiting felt good, like a cozy blanket.

When all was tidy, Miss Maple rang the chime one more time—ting-ting. “Friends,” she said, “you followed directions. You stayed safe. You used peaceful solutions and were fair.”

Roo lifted his paw. “I learned to stop and listen to the chime. I learned walking feet. I learned to take turns and wait.”

Miss Maple nodded. “And how did that feel?”

Roo looked at his friends, at the tall block tower, at the shiny, clean room. “It felt happy,” he said. “Following directions helped everyone.”

Miss Maple smiled. “That is our lesson.”

Roo gave a little, gentle hop—just one. “Ting-ting,” he whispered with a grin.

The End.
