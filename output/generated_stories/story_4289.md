# Bubbles in the Busy Garage

Bubbles was a playful dolphin with bright, curious eyes and a giggle that sounded like tiny splashes. One sunny morning, Bubbles visited Nana Lila’s garage workshop. The garage smelled like warm wood and orange oil. Sunlight made dust sparkles float in the air. There were tools on hooks, jars of shiny buttons, and a big workbench that was smooth and cool.

“Click, clack, zing!” sang the tools when the door opened.

Bubbles flapped her tail and squeaked, “Hello! I want to help!”

Nana Lila smiled, her hands gentle and strong. “Hello, Bubbles. Today we fix old toys and share old ways,” she said. “This is our family tradition. We fix, we care, and we share.”

Bubbles loved to help. She bounced and splashed in her tiny tub of water by the bench. “I can do it! I can do it!” she sang.

Just then, two friends came to the garage. Tiko the turtle wore a tiny cap. Zara the zebra had a bright scarf. Their feet made soft sounds on the floor. Tap, tap, tap.

“I found my grandpa’s wooden boat,” said Tiko. He held a small boat with a broken sail. “Grandpa taught me to sail slow and steady. It’s our tradition.”

“I found my aunt’s colorful spinner,” said Zara. “It twirls fast with wind. We spin and dance together. That’s our tradition.”

Bubbles looked at the boat and the spinner. “Let’s fix both! I’m fast! Splash-dash fast!” she squeaked.

Nana Lila nodded. “We will help each other. We will listen. We will be gentle. That is integrity. It means doing the right thing, even when it is hard.”

Bubbles bobbed her head. “I will try!”

They started with Tiko’s boat. The sail was torn. The wood was scratched. Bubbles wanted to go fast. She grabbed the brush with her flipper. “Paint! Paint!” she sang.

“Wait, Bubbles,” said Tiko softly. “My grandpa sands the wood first. Slow and small. Swish, swish. Then paint.”

Bubbles blinked. Her tail swished. She felt a bubble of anger rise, hot and fizzy. “But I want to paint now!” she squeaked. Her splash made little waves in the tub.

Nana Lila placed a calm hand on the bench. “Let’s pause,” she said in a warm voice. “Feel your breath. In and out. One, two, three.”

Bubbles took a breath. In. Out. The anger bubble got smaller. “Okay,” she said softly. “I can wait. I can learn your way.”

“Sanding is like giving the boat a hug,” said Tiko. “You can try.” He held the sandpaper out.

Bubbles took the sandpaper carefully. Swish, swish. The sound was soft. The wood smelled sweet. “It feels nice,” Bubbles said. “I like your tradition.” She smiled at Tiko. “Thank you for sharing.”

Next was Zara’s spinner. Its stick was wobbly. Its paper petals were torn.

“I can glue!” said Bubbles. “Gloop, gloop!”

Zara nodded. “Yes, and we line up the colors. Red, yellow, blue. We say a little rhyme. Spin, spin, spin, let the colors dance again!”

Bubbles felt excited. She wanted to say her own song. Her tail splashed too big. “Oops!” Glue dripped. A petal stuck to her nose.

Zara giggled. “It’s okay. Sometimes things stick. We fix. We improve.”

Bubbles scrunched her nose and gently pulled the petal free. “I’m sorry,” she said. “I will try again.”

“That is integrity,” Nana Lila said. “You told the truth and fixed the mistake.”

Together they lined up the colors. Red, yellow, blue. They said the rhyme. Spin, spin, spin, let the colors dance again! The spinner whirled and hummed. Whirr, whirr! It made the air feel cool on their cheeks.

Tiko clapped slowly. “Now my boat?” he asked.

“Yes,” said Bubbles, calmer now. “We sand first. Then we paint.” She looked at Tiko. “Will you show me your brush steps?”

Tiko smiled. “Short and steady,” he said. “Tap-tap, slide. Tap-tap, slide.”

Bubbles copied. Tap-tap, slide. The boat began to shine. It looked loved. It looked ready.

As they worked, Nana Lila told stories. “My mother taught me to fix toys for the town fair. We shared what we knew. People brought old things. We made them new. That is tradition-sharing.”

Bubbles listened. She liked the word. Share. She looked at Tiko and Zara. “I can share too,” she said. “I know a calming song for angry times.”

“What is it?” asked Zara.

Bubbles sang softly. “Bubble up, bubble slow, let the big feelings go.” Her voice was like quiet waves.

They all sang together. Bubble up, bubble slow, let the big feelings go. The garage felt warm and kind.

By afternoon, the boat had a new sail that fluttered like a little cloud. The spinner twirled bright like a rainbow wheel. The tools were quiet now. The floor smelled like orange oil and a tiny bit of glue.

Tiko hugged his boat. Zara hugged her spinner. They both hugged Bubbles. “Thank you,” they said.

Bubbles smiled. “Thank you for showing me your ways. I learned to wait. I learned to listen. I learned to tell the truth and fix mistakes. I learned to be gentle with my feelings.”

Nana Lila nodded. “Today you practiced tolerance. You respected different ways. You let your friends be themselves, and you tried their traditions. That helped everyone improve.”

Bubbles splashed a happy splash. “Tolerance means we are kind to different ways,” she said. “And when I feel mad, I breathe, I sing, and I try again.”

They opened the garage door. A soft breeze moved in. The spinner went whirr. The boat’s sail went flutter. The sunlight felt warm on their faces.

“See you tomorrow,” said Tiko.

“Let’s fix more things,” said Zara.

“Yes!” said Bubbles. “We will share, we will listen, and we will help.”

And the busy garage felt full of care, color, and calm.

The End.
