# Cleo Cat Finds Her Calm

Cleo was a clever cat with bright green eyes and soft gray fur. She loved to explore the garden shed. The shed smelled like fresh wood, dusty straw, and sweet flower pots. Sunlight made tiny beams through the little window, and dust sparkles danced in the light.

One warm morning, Cleo pushed the door with her paw. Creak. The garden shed opened. Inside, there were boxes, a watering can, a broom, and a basket of shiny tools. Cleo’s whiskers twitched. “I will find something new today,” she purred.

She hopped onto a wooden stool. She sniffed a coil of rope. It smelled like rain. She poked a stack of small pots. Tap, tap, tap. The pots wobbled but did not fall. “Careful,” Cleo told herself. “Slow paws.”

Then Cleo saw it. A red ball of string! It was big and round and bright like a tomato. “Oh!” she meowed. “A perfect toy.” She patted it. The ball rolled and bumped the wall. Thump.

Cleo patted again, a little harder. The ball zoomed across the floor and whooshed under a low shelf. It stopped there, just out of reach.

Cleo’s ears drooped. “Oh no,” she sighed. She lay down and stretched her paw. Swipe, swipe. “Come here, string.” But the ball did not move.

Cleo tried again. She pushed her whiskers close to the shelf. She could smell the string. It smelled like warm wool. She pushed and grunted. “Mmmf.” Her paw bumped a tiny screw. Tink! The sound was sharp and small.

Cleo felt a hot bubble in her tummy. Her tail flicked fast. “This is too hard!” she meowed. “I want it now!”

Her heart beat quicker. Her fur felt prickly. Cleo stomped her back paw. She wanted to swat and hiss. She wanted to give up.

Just then, a soft breeze slipped through the window. It smelled like mint leaves from the garden. The breeze tickled Cleo’s nose. She paused. “Sniff… that smells nice.”

Cleo remembered something wise her friend, Mr. Toad, had once croaked by the pond. “When you feel hot and fizzy, stop. Take a slow breath. Be calm, then try again.”

Cleo sat down on the floor. She wrapped her tail around her toes. “Okay,” she told herself. “I can calm my body.” She closed her eyes for a moment.

“In through my nose,” Cleo whispered. She breathed in, slow and gentle. “Out through my mouth.” She breathed out, soft and long. Her chest puffed and sank. In… out… in… out. She counted softly. “One… two… three.”

Her body felt softer. The fizz went away. Her tail stopped flicking. “Better,” Cleo purred. “I am calm now.”

Cleo opened her eyes and looked around. “Maybe I can find a helper tool,” she said. She scanned the shed. There was a wooden spoon in a box. There was a long garden stick near the door. There was the broom, tall and friendly.

Cleo smiled. “A gentle helper, not a hard shove.” She picked the wooden spoon with her mouth and set it on the floor. Then she nudged the spoon under the shelf. The spoon touched the string. Tap.

“Come on, little red ball,” Cleo said kindly. She gave a tiny push. The string rolled a bit, then stopped. “Almost!”

Cleo took another slow breath. In… out. “Try one more time,” she told herself. She used the broom handle this time, pressing softly, slowly, steadily. Scritch… scritch… The red ball rolled out from under the shelf and bounced into the light. Boing!

Cleo laughed. “I did it!” She hugged the string with her paws. The wool felt warm and tickly. She batted it left and right, but gently now. The ball swished and shushed on the wooden floor. Swish, shush, swish.

A little friend peeked through the doorway. It was Nia the ladybug, with red wings dotted with black. “Hello, Cleo,” Nia buzzed. “I heard a boing! Are you okay?”

“I was frustrated,” Cleo said. “My tummy felt hot and fizzy. I wanted to shout. But I stopped. I took slow breaths. I looked for a helper tool. Then I tried again.”

Nia smiled. “That is a healthy habit. Calm first, then solve.”

Cleo nodded. “Yes. Calm paws do smart things.” She batted the string to Nia, who rolled on top of it and giggled. “It tickles!”

They played for a little while, moving slowly so nothing fell. When they finished, Cleo put the string back in a small basket. “A tidy shed is a happy shed,” she said. She sipped some cool water from her bowl and stretched long, toes spread wide. “Ahhh.”

Nia waved a tiny wing. “Good-bye, Cleo. Remember your calm breaths.”

“I will,” Cleo purred. “When things feel hard, I can pause. I can breathe. I can think. Then I can try again.”

The sunbeam slid across the floor, warm and golden. Cleo curled up in it, feeling proud and peaceful.

The End.
