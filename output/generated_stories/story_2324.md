# Pippa Penguin’s Balcony Garden

Pippa was a cheerful penguin who loved to waddle, wiggle, and hum. She lived in a cozy home with a little balcony garden. The balcony had a sunny corner, a shady corner, and a small blue watering can. The air smelled like mint and lemons, and the leaves made soft whisper sounds when the wind blew, shhh, shhh.

One bright morning, Pippa clapped her flippers. “Today, I will grow a big, happy garden!” she chirped. “I will plant seeds and make a place for everyone.”

Pippa waddled outside with a basket. Inside were tiny seed packets: lettuce, strawberries, and sunflowers. She read the pictures carefully. “Lettuce likes shade. Strawberries like sun. Sunflowers like to stand tall.” She was curious about how they would grow, and her eyes sparkled.

Pippa took a deep breath. “I will start!” she said with initiative. She filled pots with soft soil. She poked little holes with her spoon. Pat, pat, pat went the soil. She planted the lettuce in the shady corner, the strawberries in the sunny spot, and the sunflowers along the railing. She hummed a garden song. “Grow, little seeds, grow and glow!”

Just then, neighbors peeked from their balconies.

Milo the mouse squeaked, “Hi, Pippa! What are you doing?”

“I’m making a garden,” Pippa said. “It will be a friendly garden. Everyone can visit.”

Mina the sparrow fluttered closer. “I love seeds,” she chirped. “Can I look?”

Pippa smiled. “Yes, let’s look together.”

Mina landed softly. Milo scampered over the railing. They sniffed and peeped and tilted their heads. The mint smelled tingly, and the lemon leaves smelled bright and sweet.

Later that day, more friends came by. Nia the snail slid slowly. “Good day,” she said in a small voice. “I like shade.”

Jax the kitten padded in with quiet paws. “I like sun,” he purred.

Pippa clapped again. “Welcome! I want everyone to feel happy here.”

But when she looked at the balcony, her smile faded a little. There was not much room. The sunflower pots were in a line. The strawberry pots were close together. The lettuce pots took up the whole shady corner. There was hardly a space to sit or play. A breeze swished. Pippa’s tummy felt twisty.

Milo tugged his whiskers. “I can’t see over the sunflower pots,” he said.

Mina peeked. “I’m small,” she chirped. “I need a place to land.”

Nia blinked slowly. “I need shade that is not too crowded,” she whispered.

Jax looked around. “Where can I curl up?” he asked.

Pippa felt worried. “Oh no,” she said softly. “I wanted a garden for everyone, but now it feels too full. What should I do?”

She sat on a cushion and thought. She listened to the leaves, shhh, shhh. She watched the sun slide across the floor. She felt the cool shade on her feet. Her curiosity bubbled. Could she move things? Could she share spaces in a better way?

“I have an idea!” Pippa said, bright again. “Let’s make a plan together. We can move the pots so everyone is included.”

Her friends nodded. “Let’s try!” they said.

Pippa showed initiative. “Milo, can you help me push the sunflower pots into two small groups, one on each side? Then there will be a window to see through in the middle.”

“Sure!” squeaked Milo. He pushed and puffed. The pots slid, scrape, scrape, scrape.

“Mina,” Pippa said, “can you help me find a safe landing spot? Maybe a little stick perch?”

Mina fluttered up and came back with a smooth twig. “Here!” she chirped. Pippa set the twig between two pots. “A landing perch!” she said. Mina tried it. “Perfect!”

“Nia,” Pippa asked softly, “would you like a quiet shade nook?”

“Yes,” Nia whispered. Pippa moved two lettuce pots apart and put a tiny shell in the space. “A snail seat,” she said. Nia sighed happily. “Thank you.”

“Jax,” Pippa smiled, “there is a sunny spot left by the strawberries. Would you like a soft towel there?”

“Yes, please,” Jax purred. He curled up and closed his eyes. The sun felt warm, like a blanket.

Pippa stepped back and looked. The balcony now had shapes for everyone: a sunny corner with strawberries and a nap towel; a shady nook with a shell seat; two groups of tall sunflowers with a window between; and a little perch for Mina. There was even a small path down the middle where Pippa could waddle and water.

Pippa filled the blue watering can. Drip, drip, drip. “We will share the space,” she said. “We will take turns. We will take care of each other.”

“Can I water one pot?” Milo asked.

“Yes,” said Pippa. She handed him the can. “We share.”

“Can I sing a growing song?” Mina asked.

“Yes,” said Pippa. “We include voices.”

“Can I rest here every afternoon?” Jax purred.

“Yes,” said Pippa. “We make room.”

“May I nibble fallen leaves?” Nia asked.

“Yes,” said Pippa. “We help each other.”

Days passed. Little green sprouts popped up, boop, boop, boop. Red strawberries peeked like tiny hearts. The sunflowers stretched tall and turned their faces to the light. The mint smelled cool and fresh, and the lemon leaves shimmered.

One evening, everyone gathered. Pippa picked a ripe strawberry. “Let’s share,” she said, cutting it into tiny pieces. Everyone tasted. “Mmm!” they cheered.

Pippa’s tummy felt warm and calm. She smiled wide. “We solved our problem by planning together,” she said. “Inclusion means we make space, we listen, and we share. When everyone belongs, the garden grows happy.”

All the friends nodded. The leaves whispered, shhh, shhh, like a soft clap.

The End.
