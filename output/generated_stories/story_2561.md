# Leo’s Little List of Big Help

Leo was a brave little lion cub with soft golden fur and bright, curious eyes. He loved his cozy bedroom. Sunlight made warm stripes on the rug. His bed was fluffy like a cloud, and his pillow smelled like fresh cotton. On his small desk sat crayons, paper, and a blue pencil that felt smooth in his paw.

One morning, Mama Lion peeked in. “Good morning, Leo,” she purred. “Today, our town needs helpers. We can be good neighbors.”

Leo’s ears perked up. “Helpers? Like heroes?” he asked, tail swishing.

“Yes,” Mama said, smiling. “Civic responsibility means we care for our home and our neighbors. We follow directions. We recycle. We use kind words. We do our part.”

Leo sat straight. “I want to help!” he roared softly.

Mama placed a small notebook on his desk. “Let’s make a list,” she said. “Writing helps us remember.”

Leo held his blue pencil. It felt cool and firm. Scratch, scratch. He wrote, with Mama’s help:
1. Follow directions
2. Recycle paper
3. Use gentle words
4. Put toys away
5. Say thank you

Leo read the list aloud. “Follow directions,” he said carefully.

“First,” Mama said, pointing to the laundry basket, “please put your soft socks in here.”

Leo nodded. “Yes, Mama!” He picked up his tiny socks. They were warm and smelled like lemons. He dropped them into the basket. Plop! He smiled. “I followed directions!”

“Good job,” Mama said. “That helps our home.”

Next, Leo looked at the trash and the recycling bin. One bin had a blue circle. The other was for trash. Leo saw a crumpled paper drawing on the floor. It was an old picture of a green tree.

He picked it up. “Is this for recycling?” he asked.

Mama nodded. “Paper goes in the blue bin.”

Leo opened the bin. It made a soft creak. He slipped the paper in. Rustle. “I recycled!” he cheered.

“Recycling keeps our world clean,” Mama said. “That helps our town.”

Leo looked back at his list. “Use gentle words,” he read.

Just then, his little toy truck rolled under the bed. “Oh no!” Leo said. He wanted to roar loudly, but he took a deep breath. He remembered his list. He bent down, reached his paw, and pulled the truck out. “It’s okay,” he told himself gently. “I can try again.” His voice was calm and kind.

Mama smiled. “You used gentle words with yourself. That is strong and brave.”

Leo liked the warm, proud feeling in his chest. He wrote a star next to “Use gentle words.” He liked writing stars. They looked shiny even in crayon.

“Number four,” he said. “Put toys away.”

Leo gathered blocks, a soft lion plush, and his toy truck. The blocks clicked together as he stacked them in the box. The plush felt fuzzy on his cheek. He placed everything on the shelf. Click, squish, slide. His room looked neat, and the air smelled clean like sunshine.

Mama clapped softly. “You are taking care of your space. That helps everyone move safely. That is civic responsibility.”

Leo stood taller. “I like helping.”

Mama pointed to the last line. “Five. Say thank you.”

Leo thought. He looked at his notebook. He remembered writing together. He hugged Mama. “Thank you for helping me write my list,” he said.

“You’re welcome,” Mama purred, nuzzling his head.

A soft knock sounded at the window. Tap-tap. It was Bree Bird, their neighbor, on the sill. “Chirp! Good morning!” Bree sang. “Our street is having a Clean-Up Day. Can Leo share his list with us?”

Leo’s whiskers twitched. He felt shy, but brave. “Yes,” he said. He took his notebook. The pages smelled like paper and crayons. He opened the window just a bit. A cool breeze tickled his nose.

Leo read clearly, “We follow directions. We recycle paper. We use gentle words. We put toys away. We say thank you.”

Bree chirped happily. “Chirp! That is a good plan for our town. Thank you, Leo!”

Mama whispered, “Would you like to make copies?”

Leo nodded. He traced each line again on new sheets with his blue pencil. Scratch, scratch. He drew a big blue circle next to “Recycle” and a smiling sun next to “Follow directions.” He made three lists and handed them to Mama.

Together, they placed one list by the door, one by the recycling bin, and one on Leo’s desk. Leo read them again. The words felt friendly and strong.

Mama said, “Leo, you showed civic responsibility. You cared for your room, you followed directions, and you helped our neighbors. Your writing helped you share.”

Leo’s chest felt warm and proud. “I am a helper,” he said.

Bree chirped from the window, “Chirp! We will all help together. Clean and kind!”

Leo looked around his cozy bedroom. It was neat. It smelled fresh. The sunlight was soft on the rug. He took a deep breath and smiled.

“I can help every day,” he said. “I can follow directions. I can recycle. I can use gentle words. I can say thank you. That is how I take care of my home and my town.”

Mama hugged him close. “That is how we all make a better world,” she said.

Leo tucked his blue pencil back on the desk. Tap. He set his notebook beside it. Pat. He felt ready, brave, and kind.

“Time to help the street,” Mama said.

“Ready!” Leo giggled.

And they stepped out together to share Leo’s little list of big help.

The End.
