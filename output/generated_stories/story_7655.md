# Bunny Bright Ideas

In a cozy study corner by the window, a determined rabbit named Rollo sat at a little desk. The desk smelled like crayons and clean paper, and the sunlight made warm squares on the floor. Rollo’s ears stood tall. His whiskers wiggled with a big idea.

“I will make a picture for our wall!” Rollo said. “I want it to be bright and happy.”

Rollo opened his art box. He had crayons, glue, and a pile of old paper scraps. There were empty toilet paper tubes, a cereal box, and a button that had lost its shirt. A basket labeled “Recycle” sat beside his chair, full of safe, clean bits to reuse.

Rollo’s mama peeked in. “Good morning, Rollo,” she said softly. “Time for your study corner routine: tidy, plan, create, and share.”

Rollo nodded. “I can do it!” he said. He took a deep breath. He straightened the crayons by color, stacked the paper, and washed his paws. “Tidy, done!” he chirped.

Next, he planned. He closed his eyes. “I see a rainbow garden,” he whispered. “With a paper tube tree and a button bird.”

He began to draw. Scratch, scratch went the green crayon. Swish, swish went the blue. He snipped the cereal box into leaves—snip, snip, snip. He glued a toilet paper tube for the tree trunk. “Stick, stick,” he murmured, pressing with gentle paws.

Just then, his little sister, Mina, bounced in. “Rollo, can I use a crayon?” she asked. Her eyes were wide and shiny.

Rollo held a red crayon tight. He remembered the routine: create, and share. He felt a tiny pinch of frustration inside his chest. “I want to finish my picture,” he thought. His ears drooped for a moment.

He took another deep breath. In and out. He remembered respect. “Yes, Mina,” he said kindly. “You may use red after I use it. Here is purple for now.” He passed her the purple crayon with a smile.

“Thank you,” said Mina, smiling back. She sat on the rug and drew squiggly lines that looked like worms dancing.

Rollo went back to work. He made a sun from a round yellow lid. “Recycle and reuse,” he sang softly. “Old things can be new.”

But the glue would not stick the lid. It slid off—plop!—and rolled under the chair.

Rollo’s nose twitched. He felt his cheeks get warm. “Oh no,” he huffed. Frustration bubbled up again, like a little pot of soup. He wanted to stomp. He wanted to say, “This is too hard!”

He put his paws on his belly. In… out… in… out. His ears relaxed. Mama’s voice floated back into his mind: “When your feelings grow big, pause, breathe, and try a new way.”

Rollo looked around. He saw a roll of tape in the basket. He saw tiny paper squares. “I can try tape,” he said. He made tape loops—sticky and soft. He pressed the sun lid again. Press, press. It stayed!

“Yes!” Rollo whispered. He felt a warm happy feeling, like cocoa.

Mina waddled over. “That is bright,” she said. “Can my purple cloud go in your sky?”

Rollo thought. The picture was his idea. He also wanted to show respect for others. He looked at the big blue space at the top. “Yes,” he said. “We can share the sky.”

Mina carefully glued her purple cloud next to the sun. “Puffy,” she giggled.

Rollo added the button bird. He glued the button for the belly and a paper triangle for the beak. “Tweet,” he chirped. He added a path made from a brown paper bag. He drew tiny flowers with tiny dots. Pink. Orange. Blue.

Soon the page was full of color and shape. The study corner smelled like paste and crayons. The window breeze tickled Rollo’s whiskers.

“Create, done!” he said. “Now share.”

Rollo and Mina picked up the picture together. It was a rainbow garden with a tube tree, a button bird, and a purple cloud. They carried it to Mama.

Mama clapped softly. “What a bright idea garden,” she said. “Tell me about it.”

Rollo’s eyes sparkled. “We used old things. The tube is a tree, the lid is the sun, and the button is a bird belly. We recycled! When the glue did not stick, I breathed and tried tape. We shared the sky, too.”

Mina nodded. “I made a cloud. It is friendly.”

Mama smiled. “I see creativity. I see respect. I see careful hands and calm breaths. I also see how you used your routine.”

Rollo stood taller. He felt proud. He wrote his name at the bottom. “Rollo and Mina,” he said. “We made it together.”

They hung the picture above the desk. The sunlight lit it up, and the colors looked like candy.

Rollo cleaned the desk. He put the scraps in the recycle basket. He stacked the crayons. “Share, done,” he said. “Clean, done.”

Mina handed him a tiny paper heart. “For you,” she said.

Rollo put the heart on the corner of the picture. “For us,” he said. His ears wiggled.

Mama hugged them both. “What did we learn today?” she asked.

Rollo thought. “When I feel stuck, I can breathe and try a new way,” he said. “Old things can be new with creativity. And sharing makes space for others.”

Mina added, “Respect is kind.”

Rollo smiled. “Tomorrow, I will make a rocket from a box,” he said. “But today, I will look at our garden and feel happy.”

They all looked at the bright garden in the sunny study corner. The room felt warm and peaceful, like a soft blanket.

The End.
