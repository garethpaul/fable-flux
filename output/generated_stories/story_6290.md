# Joey Roo and the Helpful Pantry

Joey Roo was a joyful kangaroo joey with bright eyes and a bouncy tail. He loved to help. Today, he peeked into the pantry. The pantry smelled like warm oats and sweet honey. There were crunchy crackers, soft bread, and shiny apples in a basket.

“Mama, can I help?” Joey Roo asked, hopping in place.

Mama Kangaroo smiled. “Yes, little helper. We’re sorting food for our neighbors today. That’s community service. We share what we can.”

Joey’s heart thumped with happy beats. “I can do it! I’m a good helper!”

“Remember,” Mama said gently, “we use safe hands. We lift small things. We ask for help with heavy things.”

Joey nodded. “Safe hands. Ask for help.”

He felt proud as he stacked boxes of pasta. They made a soft swish-swish sound. He lined up cans of beans. Clink, clink, clink. He sniffed a bag of oranges. They smelled bright and sunny.

“Hello, Joey,” said Mrs. Wombat from the doorway. She was their neighbor. “I brought extra rice for the pantry project.”

“Thank you!” Joey said. “We’re helping the whole street!”

Mrs. Wombat chuckled. “You are a star.”

Joey’s chest felt warm. He liked being called a star. He wanted to do more.

On the highest shelf sat a tall jar of honey with a golden lid. It gleamed. Joey could almost taste the sweet sticky smell.

“I’ll bring the honey down,” Joey said.

Mama looked up. “That shelf is too high for you, Joey. We will use the step stool, and I will hold it. That is the safe way.”

Joey wanted to show he could do it. “I am big and strong,” he whispered to himself. His tail wiggled. He stretched on his toes. He reached one paw up, then the other.

His paws slipped. The jar wobbled. Wobble, wobble, wobble.

“Uh-oh!” Joey squeaked.

Mama moved quickly. She steadied Joey with a firm, gentle paw. “Pause, Joey. Safety first.”

Joey’s ears drooped. “I wanted to help.”

“You are helping,” Mama said kindly. “Helping means being safe and asking for help when we need it.”

Joey took a slow breath. He listened to the quiet hum of the fridge. He felt Mama’s paw on his shoulder, warm and steady.

“Okay,” he said. “Let’s do it together.”

Mama placed the step stool on the floor. “We check that the stool is flat. Feet apart. Hold the rail.” She climbed up and passed the honey to Joey. Joey held it with two paws, close to his belly, like a hug.

“You did it the safe way,” Mama said.

Joey’s smile came back. “I did it the safe way!”

Mr. Pelican arrived next with a box. “I heard you are sorting food,” he said. “I brought cans of corn.”

“Thank you!” Joey waved. The box looked big and heavy. Joey remembered: safe hands, ask for help.

“Mr. Pelican, is it heavy? I can help carry one can at a time,” Joey said.

“That is a smart idea,” Mr. Pelican said. He opened the box, and Joey carried one can, then another. Clink. Clink. He walked slow and steady. He felt useful. He felt brave.

Soon, the pantry looked neat and kind. There were baskets of fruit, rows of pasta, and a shelf just for baby food. A little sign read, “Sharing is Caring.”

Mrs. Wombat clapped her paws. “This looks wonderful.”

Joey felt his cheeks glow. He stood a little taller. “I helped. I did it safely.”

A soft knock came at the door. Little Possum peeked in. “My tummy is rumbly,” she said shyly.

“Come in,” Mama said. “We have a bag for you.”

Joey wanted to make the bag special. He picked an apple that smelled like sunshine, a box of pasta that made a soft swish, and a jar of sauce that made a tiny glug sound when he moved it. He added a small note with a smiley face. “For you,” he said to Little Possum. “We care.”

Little Possum beamed. “Thank you, Joey. You are kind.”

Joey’s heart felt big, like a warm balloon. He thought about the honey jar wobble. He thought about asking for help. He thought about carrying one can at a time. He had listened. He had been safe. He had helped his community.

Mama kneeled so her eyes were level with Joey’s. “How do you feel?”

Joey put a paw on his chest. “I feel proud inside. I used safe hands. I asked for help. I helped our neighbors. I can do good things.”

Mama hugged him. “That is self-esteem,” she said softly. “That is believing in yourself because you make good choices.”

Joey liked that word. “Self-esteem,” he repeated, stretching the sounds. It felt strong and gentle at the same time.

He hopped once, twice, not too high, just right. He looked around the tidy pantry. It smelled like oats and oranges and kindness.

“Today,” Joey said, “I was a careful helper. Tomorrow, I can help again.”

Mama smiled. “Yes. Safe, kind, and proud.”

Joey took one last look at the shining shelves and the “Sharing is Caring” sign. He put his paw to his heart. “I can do big help in little steps,” he said.

And Joey Roo bounced softly, full of courage and calm, ready for more good choices and more kind helping.

The End.
