# Ellie’s Soft Steps

Ellie was a gentle elephant calf with big, floppy ears and a tiny, twitchy tail. She loved music. Every afternoon, she went to the musical practice room at the community center. The room smelled like polished wood and lemon soap. Sunlight made warm squares on the floor. There were drums, bells, and a shiny piano. There were soft mats, music stands, and a big mirror.

Ellie wanted to learn the xylophone. It was a rainbow of wooden bars that went “ting, ting, ting!” when she tapped them. Her teacher, Mr. Pip, was a small, smiling parrot with bright green feathers. He wore round glasses that slid down his beak.

“Hello, Ellie,” chirped Mr. Pip. “We will practice soft steps today. Gentle taps make sweet sounds.”

“Okay,” said Ellie. She lifted the mallet with her trunk and tried a soft tap. Ting!

“That was lovely,” said Mr. Pip. “Soft and steady.”

But when Ellie tried the next bar, her mallet bumped too hard. BONK! The sound was too loud. The bar buzzed like a bee. Ellie’s cheeks felt hot.

“It’s okay,” said Mr. Pip. “Let’s try again.”

Ellie tried again. BONK! The loud sound made her ears twitch. Her trunk felt tight. Her little elephant heart beat fast.

“I can’t do it,” Ellie huffed. “I want it to be perfect right now.”

Mr. Pip fluttered close and spoke softly. “Sometimes our bodies feel big feelings. That’s normal. What can we do when we feel mad?”

Ellie stomped one foot. Thump. “I don’t know,” she said, sniffling. Her eyes felt watery.

Mr. Pip pointed to a poster on the wall. It had pictures of a turtle, a cloud, and a stop sign. “We have calm tools,” he said. “Stop. Breathe. Choose.”

Ellie looked at the poster. “Stop. Breathe. Choose,” she whispered.

“Let’s try,” said Mr. Pip. “First, we stop.” He tapped the red stop sign on the poster.

Ellie put the mallet down. She made her body still. Stop.

“Now, breathe,” said Mr. Pip. “Smell the flower. Blow the candle.”

Ellie closed her eyes. She pretended to smell a flower. In through her trunk—sniff. She blew out like a candle—whooo. She did this three times. Sniff, whooo. Sniff, whooo. Sniff, whooo.

Her chest felt softer. Her trunk felt loose. Her ears relaxed.

“Now, choose,” Mr. Pip whispered. “Choose a helpful thought.”

Ellie thought, I can try again. I can learn with little steps. She opened her eyes and smiled a tiny smile.

“Ready?” asked Mr. Pip.

“Ready,” said Ellie.

She picked up the mallet. Tap—ting! Soft and sweet. Tap—ting! She remembered to breathe. Sniff, whooo. Tap—ting!

“Beautiful,” said Mr. Pip, clapping his wings. “That is improvement.”

Just then, the door creaked open. Squeak. A penguin named Poppy waddled in with a drum. “Hi! Can I practice too?” she asked.

“Of course,” said Mr. Pip. “Please share the room.”

Poppy set the drum near Ellie and started to play. BOOM-BOOM-BOOM! The drum was loud. The floor buzzed. The mirror shook a little.

Ellie jumped. Her mallet slipped. BONK! Her ears drooped. “I was doing it!” she cried. “Now I can’t hear my ting!”

Poppy stopped and blinked. “Oh,” she said softly. “I didn’t know.”

Ellie felt the big feeling coming back. Her trunk curled tight. Her tummy felt twisty. She remembered the poster. Stop. Breathe. Choose.

She put her mallet down. Stop.

She breathed. Sniff, whooo. Sniff, whooo.

She chose a helpful thought. I can use my calm words. I can find a peaceful plan.

Ellie turned to Poppy. “Poppy,” she said in a gentle voice, “your drum is very big and strong. I like your beat. But it is hard for me to hear my ting. Can we take turns?”

Poppy smiled. “Yes! We can count to five. I play five, you play five.”

Mr. Pip nodded. “That is a peaceful solution. Good talking, Ellie.”

They tried. Poppy played five beats—BOOM, BOOM, BOOM, BOOM, BOOM. Ellie waited and breathed—sniff, whooo. Then Ellie played five tings—ting, ting, ting, ting, ting. Poppy waited and tapped her drum with soft, soft fingers.

Soon, they made a pattern. Boom-ting, boom-ting. The room filled with music that felt like a friendly heartbeat. The sunlight warmed Ellie’s back. She felt calm and proud.

“Listen,” said Mr. Pip. “You are working together. You stopped. You breathed. You chose. You found a way.”

Ellie laughed. Her laugh sounded like bubbles in a stream. “I did it. I used my calm tools.”

Poppy flapped her little wings. “I learned, too! I can play soft. I can listen.”

They practiced their song again. Soft taps. Gentle booms. They even added a bell that went “ding!” Mr. Pip counted, “One, two, ready, play!” and the music danced in the air.

When it was time to go, Ellie put the mallet back and patted the xylophone. “Thank you for the music,” she said.

Mr. Pip gave Ellie a small sticker with a turtle on it. “For slow and steady,” he said. “For using your calm tools.”

Ellie stuck it on her practice book. “Stop. Breathe. Choose,” she said, touching each word.

As she left the room, Ellie felt tall inside, even though she was a small calf. She had learned how to calm her anger. She had found a peaceful plan. She had improved with little steps.

The clear lesson floated with her like a song: When big feelings come, stop, breathe, and choose. Use calm words. Make a plan. Try again.

The End.
