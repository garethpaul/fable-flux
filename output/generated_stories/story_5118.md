# Otto Otter’s Sparkly Story

Otto was a playful otter with big bright eyes and quick little paws. He lived near a cozy house with a garage workshop. The workshop smelled like warm wood and lemon soap. Sunlight slipped through the window and made dust sparkles dance.

Today, Otto had a big goal. “I want to write my very first story!” he chirped. His whiskers wiggled with excitement.

He waddled into the garage workshop. He heard soft humming from the old fan. He felt the cool floor under his paws. On the workbench, he found paper, thick crayons, a pencil, and a tiny jar of silver glitter. He also saw a small toolbox, a soft cloth, and a pump bottle of foamy soap near a sink.

Otto smiled. “Perfect! I will write about the river and the stars.”

He climbed onto a stool. He placed the paper flat. He picked up the pencil. “Once upon a river…” he began to say.

Just then, Otto noticed little paw prints on the paper. His paws were dusty from the floor. The prints looked smudgy and gray.

“Oh no,” Otto frowned. “My paper looks messy. My words will be hard to see.”

Otto tried again with a crayon. Squeak, squeak went the crayon on the paper. But the dust made the crayon roll over lumps. The lines got bumpy. The letters looked wiggly and not very clear. Otto felt a little flutter in his tummy. He wanted his story to be neat.

He looked around. He saw a broom. He saw the soft cloth. He saw the foamy soap and the small sink. He saw the glitter shining in the jar. He also saw a little spider in the corner, quietly building a web.

Otto took a breath. “I can solve this. First, I will get clean.”

He climbed down, turned on the tap, and pumped the foamy soap. Squish! The soap felt soft like a cloud. It smelled like oranges. Otto scrubbed his paws, tops and bottoms, between his little fingers, and around his thumbs. He sang, “Scrub, scrub, scrub, clean paws for writing!” He rinsed with water that felt cool and tickly. Then he dried his paws with the soft cloth. “Ahh,” he said. “So fresh!”

He wiped the workbench with the cloth, gentle circles, wipe, wipe, wipe, until it looked smooth and dry. He swept the floor around the stool. Swish, swish went the broom, and the dust was gone.

Otto climbed back up. He smiled at his clean paper. He touched it with his clean paws. No smudges! He picked up his pencil. Tap, tap. “Once upon a river,” he whispered, and he wrote slowly. His letters were big and simple. He said each word out loud as he wrote it. “Once… upon… a… river…”

The workshop felt quiet and cozy. Otto heard the soft whirr of the fan. He could smell lemon and orange and a tiny hint of sawdust. He felt calm and proud.

Suddenly, the tiny spider tumbled from its web. “Oh!” Otto gasped. The spider landed near the edge of the table and wiggled its little legs.

Otto’s heart was gentle. “Are you okay, little friend?” he asked. He remembered compassion means being kind and helping others.

He looked for a way to help. He did not want to squish or scare the spider. He found a small paintbrush in the toolbox. He held the brush like a tiny bridge. “Climb on,” he whispered.

The spider climbed on the soft bristles. Otto slowly moved the brush to a safe spot near the corner, away from his paper and the fan breeze. “There you go,” he said. The spider sat still. It looked calm.

Otto smiled. “Back to writing!” He wrote, “A brave little otter found a fallen friend and helped with care.” He added, “He washed his paws and kept his paper clean.” Otto liked how true it felt.

He drew a big river with a blue crayon. He added swirls. He drew stars with sparkly dots. He opened the glitter jar. “Just a little,” he told himself. He dabbed glue in tiny spots, then sprinkled a pinch of glitter. The dots twinkled like a night sky. He waited for it to dry. “Patience,” he said softly. “Good writing takes time.”

When the glitter dried, Otto read his story out loud, slowly and proudly:
“Once upon a river,
Otto wrote with clean paws.
He helped a tiny friend,
He used gentle hands and kind words.
His page stayed neat,
His stars shone bright,
And his heart felt light.”

Otto felt warm and happy. He washed his paws again to clean off the last glitter dust. Squish, scrub, rinse, dry. He put the tools back in their places. He waved at the little spider. “Have a good day, friend.”

He looked at his story. It was clear and easy to read. The pictures sparkled. The workshop was tidy. Otto giggled. “I did it!”

Otto learned three big things:
- Clean paws help neat writing.
- Kindness helps little friends.
- Careful work and patience make a story shine.

He hugged his paper and whispered, “I love writing.”

The End.
