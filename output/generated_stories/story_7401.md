# Sunny Letters in the Backyard

Benny was a small, determined rabbit with soft gray fur and bright, curious eyes. He lived beside a sunny backyard filled with warm grass, yellow dandelions, and a gentle breeze that smelled like sweet clover. Birds sang, bees buzzed, and the sun made everything glow.

One morning, Benny hopped into the sunny backyard and took a big sniff. “Today,” Benny said, wiggling his nose, “I will write my very first story!”

Benny found a flat stone under the shade of a big sunflower. He spread out his paper. He held a short, stubby pencil in his little paws. The pencil smelled like wood and felt smooth. Benny’s ears stood tall with excitement.

“I can do this,” he whispered.

His friend Mia, a kind mouse with tiny pink ears, peeped from under a leaf. “What are you doing, Benny?” she asked.

“I’m writing a story,” Benny said. “A good one!”

Mia clapped her tiny paws. “I love stories! What is it about?”

Benny tapped the paper. Tap, tap, tap. “Hmm,” he said. “I don’t know yet.”

He pressed the pencil to the paper. He made a line. Then another. The line looked wiggly. It didn’t look like a word. Benny’s whiskers drooped.

“I can’t think,” he said softly.

Mia sat beside him. “What do you see? What do you hear? What do you feel?” she asked. “Start with that.”

Benny looked around. The sun felt warm on his fur. The grass felt tickly on his toes. He heard birds sing, “Cheep-cheep!” and a bee hum, “Bzzz!” He smelled the fresh, green leaves.

“I see the sun,” Benny said.
“I hear the birds,” he said.
“I feel the soft grass,” he said.

He wrote one word: sun. Then he wrote: bird. Then: grass. The letters were big and a little crooked. But they were his.

Benny smiled. “I wrote words!”

“Good job!” Mia squeaked. “What happens next in your story?”

Benny thought and chewed the end of his pencil. He tried to write a sentence. But the pencil slid, and the letters squished together. He frowned. A breeze lifted his paper, and whoosh! The paper flipped and skittered across the yard.

“My story!” Benny hopped after it. The paper stuck to a flower, then flopped into a shallow puddle by the hose. Plop!

Benny’s heart sank. “Oh no,” he whispered. “My words.”

Mia ran close. “It’s okay,” she said gently. “We can fix it.”

Benny took a deep breath. In. Out. The air smelled wet and cool. He carefully lifted the soggy paper and set it on the warm stone to dry. He patted it softly with a clean leaf.

“I will keep trying,” Benny said. “I won’t give up.”

He found a tiny rock and placed it on the corner of the paper so it would not blow away again. He moved his paws slowly and held the pencil steady. He spoke his words out loud as he wrote them.

“My… name… is… Benny,” he said, writing each word one by one. “I… hear… birds. I… feel… the… grass.”

The letters were clearer now. Benny felt proud.

Just then, a puff of dust made him sneeze. “Ah-choo!” A little smudge landed on his page.

“Oh no,” Benny said.

Mia laughed kindly. “Smudges are okay,” she said. “Writers fix things. You can cross it out and try again.”

Benny nodded. He drew a small line through the smudge and slowly rewrote the word. He was using problem-solving, step by step.

A bright blue butterfly fluttered by and landed on his ear. It felt light like a whisper. Benny giggled. “Butterfly,” he wrote. “Blue.”

He read his words out loud to see how they sounded. “Sun. Bird. Grass. My name is Benny. I hear birds. I feel the grass. Blue butterfly.”

“It sounds lovely,” Mia said. “What is your story about now?”

Benny looked up at the sunny sky. He listened to the happy yard. He felt calm and brave. “My story is about me,” he said, “and the sunny backyard.”

He added more: “The bee goes bzzz. The bird sings. I write and smile.”

When he finished, he looked at his page. The letters were not perfect. Some were big. Some were small. But every word was his. He had tried, fixed, and tried again.

Benny took a deep breath. The paper smelled like sunshine and a little like puddle. He read his story to Mia in a clear, warm voice. Mia clapped and squeaked, “Bravo! You did it!”

Benny’s ears wiggled with joy. “I did it because I kept going,” he said. “I looked. I listened. I fixed my mistakes. I did not give up.”

Mia nodded. “That is what writers do.”

Benny smiled at the bright day. “Tomorrow, I will write again,” he said. “I will use my eyes, ears, and heart.”

The sun slid lower, and the birds sang a soft, good-night song. Benny carefully tucked his paper in a dry, safe spot. He patted it gently.

“Thank you, sunny backyard,” Benny whispered. “You help me write.”

Then Benny and Mia shared a crunchy carrot snack. The carrot was sweet and crisp. Benny felt proud, peaceful, and ready to try again tomorrow.

The lesson was clear: When you write, look and listen. Use your words. Fix little mistakes. Keep trying. You can do it too.

The End.
