# Nutmeg’s Attic Story Show

Nutmeg was a small, resourceful squirrel with bright eyes and a fluffy tail. She lived in a quiet attic, where sunbeams made little dust sparkles dance in the air. The attic smelled like old wood and warm blankets. It was soft and still, like a secret fort.

Every morning, Nutmeg woke up to the tiny tick-tock of a sleepy clock and the gentle creak of the attic floor. Today, she wiggled her whiskers with a brave idea. “I will make a story show!” she said. Her voice sounded brave and happy in the quiet room.

Nutmeg looked around. She saw boxes, buttons, ribbons, and a blue scarf. She saw a shoebox, a shiny spoon, and a lamp with a soft glow. She touched the scarf. “So smooth,” she giggled. She tapped the spoon. “Ting!” The attic made little music.

Nutmeg set the shoebox on its side. “This will be my stage,” she said. She pulled the blue scarf across two sticks. “This is my curtain!” The scarf swished and felt cool on her paws. She drew a smiling moon on a piece of paper and taped it to the back wall. “This is the sky,” she whispered.

Nutmeg made puppets from socks and buttons. One puppet had a big smile. One had tiny ears. She named them Pip and Dot. “Hello, Pip,” she said in a high voice. “Hello, Dot,” she said in a squeaky voice. She giggled at the sounds.

Just then, a friendly mouse named Tully peeked from a crack. “What are you doing, Nutmeg?” he asked, sniffing the air. He smelled dust and a hint of cinnamon from the old cookies in a tin.

“I am making a story show,” Nutmeg said. “Do you want to watch?”

Tully nodded. “Yes, please. I love stories.”

Nutmeg took a deep breath. Her heart went pitter-pat. “I feel a little wobbly,” she said.

Tully smiled. “You can do it. Your story is yours.”

Nutmeg stood tall. “Yes,” she said softly. “My story is mine.” She felt warm and brave inside.

She pulled the scarf-curtain. “Welcome to the Moonlight Adventure!” she announced. Her voice was clear. “Tonight, Pip and Dot will find a song.”

Nutmeg made Pip hop. “I want to sing,” Pip said. Nutmeg made Dot wiggle. “I am shy,” Dot whispered.

Nutmeg tapped the spoon again. Ting. Ting. It sounded like stars. She crinkled paper to make wind sounds. “Whoooo,” she said, making the wind gentle and kind.

Pip looked up at the paper moon. “I will sing to the moon,” Pip said. Dot looked down at her tiny feet. “I am not sure,” Dot said.

Nutmeg paused and listened to her heart. “Be brave,” she told herself. “Tell it your way.” She picked up a ribbon and made it dance. “The moon likes any song,” Nutmeg said as the narrator. “Big songs, small songs, silly songs, and new songs.”

Tully clapped softly. “I like that.”

Nutmeg made Dot breathe in. “I can try,” Dot said. “La, la, la.” The little song was quiet, like a whisper. Nutmeg made Pip smile. “Beautiful,” Pip said. “Sing your way.”

The scarf-curtain brushed Nutmeg’s paw. It felt silky and cool. She smiled. “Pip and Dot can sing together,” she said. She hummed a gentle tune. “Hmm, hmm, hmm.” She shook a jar with buttons to make soft rain sounds. Click-a-click. The attic filled with music.

Suddenly, a big pigeon landed on the windowsill with a flurry of feathers. “Coo?” he asked. His wings went whoosh, whoosh. He tilted his head. “What is this noise?”

Nutmeg stood straight. “Hello. This is my story show,” she said. Her voice was calm. “You can watch if you like.”

The pigeon puffed. “I like quiet,” he cooed.

Nutmeg nodded. “I understand,” she said kindly. “I can make soft sounds.” She tapped the spoon very lightly. Ting. She shook the buttons like tiny rain. Click. “We can share the attic. I will be gentle, and you can listen.”

The pigeon blinked. “Coo,” he said, which meant, “Okay.”

Nutmeg felt proud. She had been polite and firm. She had used her voice and her idea. The show went on.

Pip and Dot sang their moon song, soft and sweet. “La, la, la,” Nutmeg sang with them. The attic felt warm and safe. The lamp glowed like honey. Tully swayed his tail. The pigeon tucked in his wings and watched.

At the end, Nutmeg bowed. “Thank you for coming,” she said. Tully clapped. The pigeon cooed. The clock ticked happily.

Tully squeaked, “I liked how Dot tried. I liked your soft sounds.”

Nutmeg’s tail flicked with joy. “Thank you,” she said. “I learned I can tell my own story. I can be brave and kind. I can use my voice and still listen.”

She packed her puppets with care. She folded the scarf-curtain. She put the spoon by the jar of buttons. The attic was quiet again, like a deep breath.

Nutmeg looked at the paper moon. “Tomorrow, I will make a new story,” she said. “Maybe a sunny dance. Maybe a rain parade. I can create with what I have.”

She felt calm, clever, and proud. She had shared her ideas. She had asked for space kindly. She had made something beautiful from simple things.

Nutmeg smiled. “My story matters,” she whispered. “And so does yours.”

The End.
