# Pippa Penguin’s Rooftop Song

Pippa was a cheerful penguin with bright eyes and a bouncy waddle. She lived in a tall building by the sea. One sunny morning, Pippa climbed up to the flat rooftop. The sky felt big and blue. The wind tickled her feathers. She could smell salty air and warm tar. Seagulls glided by, calling, “Kee-ya! Kee-ya!”

Pippa clapped her little flippers. “Today, I will write a song!” she said. “I will make music that makes hearts smile.”

She set down her tiny notebook and a stubby blue crayon. She hummed, “La, la, la,” and tapped her feet on the roof. Tap-tap… tap. But something was not quite right. Her tune sounded wobbly, like jelly.

Pippa frowned a little. “Hmm. My music needs a steady beat. How can I find it?” She looked around. There were flower pots, a rain barrel, and a shiny metal vent. The vent made a soft whoosh as warm air puffed out.

“Listen first,” Pippa told herself. “Listening is part of music.” She closed her eyes. She heard the wind, the gulls, and the city below—cars humming, a bike bell ringing ding-ding, a dog giving a happy woof.

Pippa opened her eyes and tried again. “Tap-tap, tap-tap.” She tapped the rain barrel with her flipper. It sounded like a deep drum. Thoom… thoom. She smiled. “That is a good beat!”

She wrote in her notebook, big and simple:
- Beat: thoom thoom
- Wind: whoosh
- Gull: kee-ya

“Now I need a melody,” she said. “A melody is the part you sing.” She sang, “Thoom-whoosh, kee-ya, laaaa!” The notes slipped and slid away. Pippa giggled, but then felt unsure. “Oh no. It still does not sound like a real song.”

Her tummy fluttered like a tiny kite. She wanted to give up. “Maybe I can’t do it,” she whispered.

Just then, a small voice came from the edge of the roof. It was Miri the mouse, peeking from a flower pot. “Hi, Pippa! I like your drum sounds.”

Pippa’s eyes brightened. “Thank you, Miri! I am trying to write a song, but it is hard.”

Miri tilted her head. “What do songs need?”

Pippa tapped her beak, thinking. “Songs need a beat. Songs need a melody. Maybe words too. Words we can sing.”

“That’s smart thinking,” Miri said. “Let’s solve it step by step.”

Pippa took a deep breath. The air tasted like salt. She felt brave again. “Step one: Make a steady beat.” She tapped the barrel with a gentle pattern: thoom-thoom, pause, thoom. She counted, “One, two, rest, three.”

“Step two: Pick a tiny tune.” Pippa tried small notes, like baby steps. “La, la, laaa. La, la, laaa.” She kept it simple.

“Step three: Write the words.” Pippa pressed her crayon to paper. Big letters, short words:
- Hello, sky
- Hello, wind
- I can sing
- I can try

She read the words out loud. “Hello, sky. Hello, wind. I can sing. I can try.” The words felt brave and kind. They fit like a snug scarf.

“Now put it together,” Miri said softly.

Pippa stood tall. She tapped the drum. Thoom-thoom, pause, thoom. She sang her tiny tune with the words:
“Hello, sky, hello, wind,
I can sing, I can try.
Tap my feet, flap my fins,
Music lifts me to the sky.”

Her voice rang clear in the open air. The wind gave a happy whoosh. The gulls called back, “Kee-ya!” A warm puff from the vent made a gentle hum, like a friendly cello.

Pippa grinned. “It works! My song works!” She wrote it all down. She drew little pictures beside the words—clouds for “sky,” swirl lines for “wind,” little feet for “tap.”

But on the second try, Pippa slipped on a pebble. Her beat went fast-fast, then slow. “Oh no!”

Miri squeaked, “It’s okay! Try again. You can fix it.”

Pippa nodded. “I can fix it.” She looked at her notes. She counted slow and steady. “One, two, rest, three.” She tried again. Thoom-thoom, pause, thoom. Sing. Smile. It sounded smooth and strong.

Just then, a neighbor turtle named Tavi poked his head up from the stairwell. “I heard music,” he said in a low, cozy voice. “May I listen?”

“Yes!” Pippa said. “And you can help with the beat.”

Tavi tapped the flower pot with a gentle tick-tick. Miri shook a tiny seed pod—shaka-shaka. Pippa led them. “Count with me: one, two, rest, three!” They played, listened, and stayed steady. When they finished, the three friends laughed. The rooftop smelled like sunshine and sea, and their hearts felt full.

Tavi smiled. “Your song made my shell tingle.”

Miri clapped. “You solved the problem step by step. You listened. You wrote. You kept trying.”

Pippa hugged her notebook. “Music is listening, counting, and sharing. Writing my song helped me remember my ideas. Thinking in steps helped me solve the hard parts. And trying again made me stronger.”

She looked up at the big blue sky and sang one more time, a little louder:
“Hello, sky, hello, wind,
I can sing, I can try.
With friends’ beats and notes that blend,
Music helps us reach the sky.”

Pippa’s eyes shone. “I learned that music grows when we listen, plan, and practice. When something is hard, we can try again. That is resilience!”

Miri and Tavi nodded. “And it’s fun!”

They played until the sun turned golden and the rooftop felt warm like toast. Pippa tucked her notebook in her bag, ready for tomorrow’s song.

The End.
