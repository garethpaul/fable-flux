# Bubbles, Buttons, and Saying "I’m Sorry"

Max was a courageous monkey with bright eyes and quick little hands. Every morning, Max had a routine. He woke up, stretched his arms, and whispered, “Today I will be kind and brave.” He put a sticker on his goal chart. His goal was simple: be helpful every day.

One sunny morning, Max tiptoed into the laundry room. The room hummed softly. The washing machine whirred, “Whirr, whirr,” like a sleepy song. Warm light made the clean towels look fluffy like clouds. Max loved the smell—soapy and sweet, like bubbles and fresh rain.

Mama was folding clothes. “Good morning, Max,” she said. “Want to help with our routine?”

“Yes!” Max said, tail swishing. “Today my goal is to help you!”

Mama smiled. “Wonderful. First, we sort. Whites here, colors there.”

Max nodded. He liked sorting. Whites in this basket. Colors in that basket. Socks together like best friends. Max felt proud. “I am a good helper,” he whispered.

Then Max saw a shiny red button on the washer. It looked like a cherry candy. It glowed softly. “Boop,” it seemed to say. Max loved buttons. His fingers wiggled. He wanted to push it.

“Max,” Mama said, “please do not press buttons without asking.”

Max wanted to be brave and do the right thing. He took a deep breath. But the button was so shiny. He reached out anyway. “Just one tiny boop,” he whispered. His finger tapped the button.

Beep! The washer stopped. The humming song ended. The room felt too quiet.

“Oh!” Mama said. “Max, did you press the button after I asked you not to?”

Max’s heart slid down like a sock that lost its pair. “I… I did,” he said. His cheeks felt hot. He wanted to hide in the towel basket. He knew he had not kept his goal. He had not listened. That was not honest. That was not brave.

Max took a slow breath. He remembered his sticker chart. He remembered being helpful. He remembered that brave monkeys tell the truth. He stood tall and looked at Mama. “I am sorry,” he said. “I pressed the button. I did not listen. I made the washer stop. I want to fix it.”

Mama knelt down. Her eyes were kind. “Thank you for telling the truth, Max. That is integrity. An honest apology means a lot.”

Max nodded. “How can I make it better?”

Mama smiled softly. “First, we can turn the washer back on together. Then, we can add one more step to our laundry routine. We can write a ‘Do Not Press’ note.”

Max’s ears perked up. “We can write?”

“Yes,” Mama said. She took a small notepad and a big blue marker from the drawer. The marker smelled like blueberries. “Let’s set a goal. Our goal is: listen before touching buttons.”

Max liked this plan. He held the marker carefully. “What do we write?” he asked.

Mama guided his hand. Together they wrote: “Ask First.” Max drew a little picture of his own monkey hand with a question mark. He giggled. “It looks like my hand is thinking!”

They taped the note above the red button. The tape made a soft rip-rip sound. The paper fluttered and then sat still.

“Now,” Mama said, “press this green button with me.” They pressed together. The washer hummed again. Swish, swish. The soapy smell filled the air. Max felt his chest calm down, like a quiet drum.

“I am still sorry,” Max said. “I did not listen.”

“I forgive you,” Mama said, giving him a warm hug. “Saying ‘I’m sorry’ and making a plan helps our family.”

Max wanted to do more to fix things. He looked at the basket of socks. “I will match every sock,” he said. “That is my helping goal for today.”

He made pairs—stripe with stripe, polka-dot with polka-dot. He found one tiny blue sock and one tiny green sock. No match! Max frowned. “Where is your friend?” he asked the blue sock.

He searched under a towel. He peeked behind the laundry basket. He listened. Quiet. Then—rustle, rustle—he heard a soft sound near the dryer. Max crouched low, like a brave explorer, and reached behind. His fingers touched something soft and warm. “Aha!” He pulled out the tiny blue sock’s match. “Found you!”

Mama clapped. “Great problem-solving, Max!”

Max felt proud again, but in a calm way. He had told the truth. He had said “I’m sorry.” He had made a plan. He had helped.

Before they left the laundry room, Max took the marker. On his goal chart, he drew a small bubble with letters inside. He said them slowly, “S-O-R-R-Y.” He smiled. “This bubble reminds me I can fix mistakes.”

Mama nodded. “Everyone makes mistakes. The brave thing is to apologize and try again.”

Max looked at the red button. He looked at the note: Ask First. “Mama,” he said, “next time I want to press a button, I will ask.”

“That sounds like a strong plan,” Mama said.

The washer sang a happy beep-beep at the end of its cycle. Warm steam puffed out when they opened the door. Max hugged a warm towel. It felt like sunshine. He whispered to the towel, “Today, I was kind and brave.”

And he was.

The End.
