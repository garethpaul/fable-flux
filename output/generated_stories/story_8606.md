# Dolly the Dolphin Hears with Her Heart

In a comfortable nursery with soft rugs and twinkly lights, toys rested on low shelves, and tiny chairs made a neat circle. The air smelled like clean blankets and warm crayons. A gentle song drifted from a little speaker. It went, “La-la-la,” like a feather on the air.

Splash! In a big, low tub filled with warm water sat Dolly the Dolphin. She was gray and shiny, with bright, curious eyes. She liked to click and squeak and make tiny waves that kissed the edge of the tub.

“Good morning, Dolly!” said Ms. Bloom, the teacher with a kind smile and a flower pin that smelled like oranges. “Today we will clean up, then make music together!”

Dolly flapped her tail. “Eee-eee! Music! I love music!” she squeaked, spraying a small rainbow of droplets.

Children giggled. There was Max with the red socks, Lina with the braid, and Theo with big round glasses. They all sat in the circle, wiggling with happy feet.

Ms. Bloom clapped a gentle rhythm. “First, we tidy our nursery. Then, we play the band game.” She pointed to a chart with pictures. Step 1: Clean up toys. Step 2: Wash hands. Step 3: Choose instruments. Step 4: Play together.

Dolly hopped a little in her tub. “I want to play drums!” she chirped.

Max raised his hand. “I want drums too,” he said softly.

Lina hugged a soft panda. “I want the bell,” she said.

Theo whispered, “I like the quiet shaker.”

Ms. Bloom nodded. “We will be fair. First we finish our tasks. Then we take turns.”

Dolly swished her tail and made a splashy clap. She was so excited that she did not hear Max’s small voice. She did not see Lina’s shy smile. She was dreaming about boom-boom drums.

“Okay, friends,” said Ms. Bloom. “Cleanup time!”

Blocks clacked as children put them in bins. Crayons rolled into a box with a soft rattle. Dolly had a job too—she used her nose to nudge floating bath toys into a net. Plop, plop, plop went the yellow duck, the blue boat, and the tiny frog.

But Dolly was in a hurry. “Drums! Drums!” she sang, and she splashed faster and faster. Some water spilled onto the rug.

“Oops,” said Theo, backing away from the puddle. His socks got a little wet.

Max sighed and picked up extra blocks near the spill. Lina got a towel and patted the rug.

Ms. Bloom crouched beside Dolly. “Slow and gentle, please,” she said. “Look at Max’s socks.”

Dolly looked. Max’s red socks were dark at the toes. His cheeks were pink. He didn’t say anything.

Dolly felt a little twist in her tummy. “I wanted drums so much,” she thought. “I forgot about my friends.”

She took a deep breath. She listened. She could hear the soft hum of the air, the rustle of blocks, the tiny drip of water, and the quiet, quiet sound of Max’s breath.

Dolly looked at Max. “I’m sorry I splashed your socks,” she said in a small, honest voice.

Max glanced up. “It’s okay,” he said, but he still looked a little sad.

Dolly wanted to help. She had an idea. “I can use my tail to move the towel!” She swished carefully, pushing the towel toward the puddle. Swoosh. She nudged it right to Max’s feet. “Can I help dry?” she asked.

Max smiled a little. “Thank you,” he said.

“Thank you for showing empathy, Dolly,” Ms. Bloom said. “Empathy means we listen and care about how others feel.”

Dolly whispered, tasting the new word like a bubble. “Em-pa-thy.”

Soon the nursery was tidy. The chart said Step 2: Wash hands. Everyone washed and dried. Step 3: Choose instruments.

Dolly saw the shiny drums. She opened her mouth to shout, “Mine!” but then she saw Max look at them. He looked at the drums the way Dolly looked at the ocean—full of hope.

Dolly paused. Her heart felt warm. She clicked softly. “Max, do you want the drums first?” she asked.

Max’s eyes grew big. “Really?” he asked.

Dolly nodded. “I can take the shaker. It sounds like rain.” She shook it gently—shhh-shhh. It made a cozy whisper, like bedtime stories.

“That is fair,” Lina said, picking up the bell that chimed like stars. Theo chose the quiet shaker, and Ms. Bloom took a small guitar.

“Band game!” said Ms. Bloom. “We will start soft and slow, and we will listen to each other.”

Dolly closed her eyes for a moment. She felt the cool shaker in her fin. She heard Max’s drums tap-tap, Lina’s bell ding-ding, Theo’s shaker hush-hush, and Ms. Bloom’s guitar strum-strum. Dolly listened with her ears and her heart.

“Ready?” asked Ms. Bloom.

“Ready!” everyone said.

They played together. The music was gentle at first, like a sleepy morning. Then it grew brave and happy. Dolly kept the rhythm with soft shakes and careful splashes that stayed inside her tub. She watched Max smile as he kept a steady beat. She rang her shaker when Lina chimed her bell and paused when Theo made a quiet ocean sound.

Between songs, Dolly said, “Max, after your turn, can I try the drums?”

Max nodded. “Yes! We can share.”

They traded. Max showed Dolly, “Tap-tap, not splash!” Dolly giggled and tried. Tap-tap. It sounded just right. Fair turns felt good.

When they finished, the room felt warm and proud. Ms. Bloom clapped. “You all worked hard. You cleaned up, you listened, and you showed empathy. That made our music beautiful.”

Dolly’s heart felt big. She looked at Max, Lina, and Theo. “Thank you for helping me learn,” she said.

Max wiggled his dry red socks. “Thank you for caring,” he said.

Lina rang the bell once, like a happy star. Theo smiled and hugged his shaker.

Dolly took a tiny bow in her tub. “When we listen to feelings, our songs sound better,” she said.

“Yes,” Ms. Bloom said. “Empathy helps us be fair, finish our tasks, and make joyful music together.”

Dolly swished her tail gently. No splashes this time—just happy ripples. In the comfortable nursery, the band packed up their instruments, put them away, and sang a soft goodbye tune.

La-la-la. Shhh-shhh. Tap-tap. Ding.

The End.
