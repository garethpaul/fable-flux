# Melody Mouse and the Mixed-Up Music Room

Melody Mouse loved her cozy home in the corner of the music practice room. Every morning, she would wake up to the beautiful sounds of children playing piano, violin, and drums. The room smelled like fresh flowers from the window box, and sunlight danced across the wooden floor.

But today was different. When Melody peeked out of her tiny door, she gasped. "Oh my whiskers!" she squeaked. "What a mess!"

Sheet music was scattered everywhere like fallen leaves. Broken pencils rolled across the floor. Old water bottles sat forgotten on the piano bench, and crumpled papers filled every corner.

Melody's friend, Mrs. Robin, flew in through the open window. "Good morning, Melody! My goodness, what happened here?"

"I don't know," said Melody, twitching her pink nose. "But the children won't be able to practice music in this messy room. I need to think about this problem step by step."

Melody sat on her favorite cushion and put her tiny paw to her chin. "Let me think logically," she said to herself. "First, what needs to be done? Second, what order should I do things? Third, how can I make this room stay clean?"

She grabbed a small piece of paper and a pencil stub. With careful paws, she wrote down her plan:
1. Pick up trash
2. Organize sheet music
3. Sharpen pencils
4. Make recycling bins

"Writing down my plan helps me think clearly!" Melody told Mrs. Robin.

First, Melody collected all the crumpled papers and old water bottles. "These bottles can be recycled," she said, feeling proud about helping the Earth. She made a neat pile by the door.

Next, she gathered all the scattered sheet music. "Let me sort these by instrument," she thought. "Piano music here, violin music there, and drum music over here. That makes sense!"

Then Melody found all the broken pencils. Using her sharp teeth, she carefully sharpened each one. "Musicians need good pencils to write notes," she said.

Finally, Melody had her biggest idea yet. She found three empty boxes and made labels with her neat writing: "Paper to Recycle," "Bottles to Recycle," and "Pencil Shavings for Compost."

Just as she finished, the first music student arrived. "Wow!" said Emma, a little girl with pigtails. "This room looks amazing! And look - recycling bins! That's so smart."

"Thank you for keeping our practice room clean," said Mr. Chen, the music teacher. "Someone was very thoughtful."

Melody smiled from her hiding spot. She felt warm and happy inside, like drinking hot cocoa on a cold day.

Mrs. Robin chirped softly, "Melody, you solved that big problem by thinking step by step. You made a plan, wrote it down, and followed it through!"

"Yes!" squeaked Melody proudly. "When I have a big problem, I can break it into small steps. Writing helps me remember what to do. And now the room is clean, the Earth is happier with recycling, and everyone feels good in this tidy space."

From that day on, the music room stayed neat and organized. The children learned to use Melody's recycling system, and they always put their music sheets back in the right places.

Melody learned that logical thinking, planning, and caring for others - and the Earth - made every day better. And whenever she faced a new problem, she would sit down, think step by step, and write down her plan.

The End.
