# Owl Hoots at Sunrise

On a quiet rooftop, a wise old owl named Ori lived beside a shiny weather vane. The rooftop felt cool at night and warm in the morning. The city below hummed softly. Ori loved the soft breeze, the smell of fresh bread from the bakery, and the twinkle of little lights far below.

Ori had a routine. He would hoot at sunrise, stretch his wings, hop three times, and greet the day. Then he would watch and help the neighbors on the street. Ori liked routines because they made him feel calm and strong.

One morning, Ori blinked his big, golden eyes. “Hoot-hoo! Time to stretch,” he said. But the city felt extra busy. Drums beat. A flute played. Laughter bounced up from the street. The smells were new—spicy and sweet.

Ori peered over the edge. On the street, families from many places were setting up a big morning dance. He saw bright scarves, small drums, and colorful ribbons. A little girl in a red dress waved up at him.

“Hello, Owl!” she called. “I’m Asha. We dance every morning. Will you join us?”

Ori smiled. “Hoot-hoo! Thank you, Asha. But I stretch on the rooftop every sunrise. It’s my routine.”

Asha nodded. “We have a routine too. We dance before breakfast. It’s our family exercise.”

Just then, another child waved. “Hola! I’m Mateo,” he said. “We clap to the rhythm. Clap-clap, clap-clap!”

A boy with a green hat added, “Ni hao! I’m Lee. We breathe in and out. Slow and steady.”

Ori wanted to join, but he also wanted to keep his rooftop routine. He felt a little worried. His feathers ruffled. “If I leave my routine, I may forget it,” he whispered.

A soft wind brushed his wings. Ori took a deep breath. He looked at the weather vane and had an idea.

“Hoot-hoo! Maybe we can fix this together,” Ori said. “I can keep my routine and be kind to my friends.”

He stood tall and called, “Friends below! Let’s share a morning routine. I will begin on the rooftop. Then I will hoot for you to start on the street. We can do it together, but in our own places.”

Asha smiled. “Yes! We can follow your hoots.”

“Clap-clap!” said Mateo.

“Breathe slow,” said Lee.

Ori began. “Step one: Stretch.” He reached his wings wide. The cool air felt fresh. His feathers tickled in the breeze. He counted, “One, two, three.”

He hooted, “Hoot!” Asha and the children stretched below. They reached for the sky. “Up, up, up!” Asha giggled.

“Step two: Hop,” Ori said. He hopped three times across the rooftop. His claws clicked softly: click, click, click.

“Hoot!” The children hopped on the street. Shoes tapped: tap, tap, tap. Ribbons fluttered. Drums went boom-boom in a gentle beat.

“Step three: Breathe,” Ori said. He breathed in through his beak and out slowly. The city smelled like bread, flowers, and cinnamon.

“Hoot!” Lee led the breathing. “In and out,” he said. “It feels good.”

“Step four: Kind words,” Ori said. “We always say kind words in our routine. We say hello to the day.”

He hooted once more. The children called, “Good morning, sun! Good morning, friends! Good morning, Owl!”

Ori felt warm and proud. But then a small problem popped up. A little boy named Sami stood still. He looked shy. He held a tiny drum close to his chest.

Ori tilted his head. “Hoot-hoo? What’s wrong, friend?”

Sami whispered, “I don’t know the steps. I am new here.”

Ori blinked kindly. “We can help. Routines are like songs. We learn them together.”

Asha walked to Sami. “Hold my hand. We can hop side by side.”

Mateo clapped softly. “We can clap slow.”

Lee breathed with him. “In…out…”

Ori gave a gentle hoot and started again, slower this time. “Stretch…hop…breathe…kind words.”

Sami smiled and tried. He stretched a little. He hopped once. He breathed in and out. “Good morning,” he said in a tiny voice.

“Good morning, Sami!” everyone cheered.

Ori’s heart felt light as a feather. He realized his problem had a solution: he could keep his routine and share it, too. Sharing made it stronger.

The sun peeped over the buildings. The rooftop glowed warm under Ori’s feet. The city’s music mixed with the wind. Ori stretched one more time and gave a final hoot.

Asha waved. “Thank you, Owl! That was fun and kind.”

Mateo grinned. “Your routine helped us move our bodies.”

Lee bowed. “We can do this every morning.”

Sami beat his tiny drum, tap-tap. “I like the slow steps.”

Ori nodded. “Hoot-hoo! Every sunrise, we will follow our morning routine together. We will stretch, hop, breathe, and say kind words. We will be gentle with new friends and help them learn.”

He looked at the shiny weather vane. It spun softly, like a little dance in the sky. Ori felt calm. He kept his routine, and he made new friends from many places.

He whispered to the sun, “Routines help us grow. Kindness brings us close. Moving our bodies makes us strong.”

And every morning after, the rooftop and the street moved as one. Stretch, hop, breathe, kind words. Together.

The End.
