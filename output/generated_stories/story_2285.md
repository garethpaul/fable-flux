# Tiko Takes Turns on the Porch

Tiko was a gentle elephant calf with big soft ears and a curious trunk. He sat on the peaceful front porch with Mama, watching the morning sun paint the sky peach and gold. The wooden boards felt warm under Tiko’s little feet. The air smelled like fresh bananas and clean rain.

“Today is porch day,” Mama said with a smile. “We will tidy the porch and share snacks. We will take turns and use our thinking.”

Tiko’s trunk wiggled with excitement. “I love porch day!”

On the porch sat a small broom, a tiny watering can, a blue bucket of soapy water, and a basket of fruits from faraway places—yellow bananas, red apples, and green little limes. A soft breeze whispered through the leaves, and a bird chirped, “Cheep-cheep!”

“First job,” Mama said. “We sweep the porch. Who goes first?”

Tiko wanted to grab the broom right away. His trunk reached out fast. Then he remembered Mama’s words: take turns. He took a deep breath. He felt his excitement like bubbles in his tummy.

“I can wait,” Tiko said softly. “Mama, you can go first. Then me.”

Mama’s eyes shone. “That is kind. Thank you.”

Swoosh, swoosh went the broom as Mama swept leaves into a neat pile. Tiko watched carefully. He listened to the scratchy sound. He smelled the clean soap from the bucket. When Mama finished, she smiled and passed the broom to Tiko. “Your turn.”

Tiko held the broom and pushed gently. Swoosh, swoosh. A tiny spider sat on a leaf. Tiko paused. “Hello, little spider,” he said. “I will sweep around you.” He thought about safety—gentle steps, careful moves. He made a small path with the broom so the spider could find its way home. The spider wiggled happily and climbed down the porch rail.

“All done!” Tiko said. He felt proud. He had waited, watched, and worked.

“Next is watering the porch flowers,” Mama said, pointing to a row of pots. The flowers smelled sweet and looked like tiny suns and stars. “Who goes first?”

Tiko tilted his head. “We can count the pots,” he said. “You water one, I water the next. We take turns.”

“Good thinking,” Mama said.

Mama picked up the little watering can and poured a gentle rain on the first pot. Tiko held the can with his trunk for the second pot. Tip, tip, tip. The water sparkled in the sunlight. A ladybug crawled on a leaf. “Hello, little traveler,” Tiko whispered. “We share the world.”

They kept going—Mama, Tiko, Mama, Tiko—until each flower had a drink. The dirt looked dark and happy.

“Now we wash the porch rail,” Mama said. She dipped a cloth in the soapy water. “This job is slippery. We use safety.”

Tiko nodded. “Slow feet. No running.” He held the rail with one foot and wiped with his cloth. Swish, swish. Bubbles popped on his nose. Pop! Tiko giggled. “It tickles!”

When the rail was clean and dry, Mama clapped quietly. “We worked as a team. We took turns. We used safety. Last, we share a snack.”

Tiko’s eyes opened wide at the basket of fruits. “From near and far,” Mama said. “Bananas from our garden. Apples from over the hills. Limes from warm islands far away.”

Tiko touched each fruit with his trunk. Smooth apple. Soft banana. Bumpy lime. “So many homes,” he said. “The world is big.”

“Yes,” Mama said. “People and animals everywhere grow and share food. We say thank you to helpers around the world.”

Tiko felt happy and careful at the same time. “How do we share the snack?” he asked.

Mama smiled. “Let’s make a plan.”

Tiko thought. He looked at the basket. He looked at Mama. He listened to the bird still singing. “First, we peel one banana. You take a bite. Then I take a bite. We take turns. We finish the banana before we start the apple. That’s completing our task.”

“That is a clear plan,” Mama said.

They peeled the banana. The peel made a soft rip sound. The sweet smell made Tiko’s tummy rumble. Mama took a small bite. Tiko waited, feeling the warm porch boards under his feet. “My turn?” he asked.

“Yes, your turn,” Mama said.

Tiko took a bite. The banana was creamy and sweet. “Yum!” he said. They passed the banana back and forth until it was gone. Then they shared the apple the same way—crunch, crunch. For the lime, Mama squeezed a tiny bit on the apple pieces. “Tart!” Tiko giggled as his tongue tingled.

When the basket was empty, Tiko helped gather the peels and cores. He put them in the compost bucket. “We are not done until we clean up,” he said. “That completes our task.”

“Exactly,” Mama said.

They sat side by side. The porch was tidy. The flowers were bright. The air was fresh. Tiko’s heart felt calm and big.

“Mama,” Tiko said, “today I learned to take turns, to think of a plan, to be safe, and to finish what we start. I can do that every day.”

Mama hugged him with her trunk. “Yes, little one. Taking turns makes room for everyone. Careful thinking keeps us safe. Finishing tasks helps our world.”

The bird sang a happy song. The breeze kissed Tiko’s ears. The sun felt warm like a hug.

Tiko smiled. “Next porch day, you can go first,” he said.

Mama smiled back. “And then it will be your turn.”

The End.
