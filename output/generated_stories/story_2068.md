# Fawn’s Brave Bedtime Plan

The moon was round and soft like a scoop of ice cream. A gentle deer fawn named Fawn curled up in a cozy bedroom. The bed had leaf-print sheets, a warm quilt, and a little night-light that glowed like a firefly. Fawn’s window showed the night sky, where stars twinkled like tiny bells. The air smelled like clean blankets and pine.

Fawn tucked her legs under the quilt. “Good night, room,” she whispered. “Good night, stars.”

But then—thump. Tap-tap. Rustle.

Fawn’s ears twitched. Her heart went pitter-pat. “What is that sound?” she whispered.

She sat up. The quilt felt heavy and safe. Still, the sounds made her feel wiggly and unsure.

Mama Deer peeked in the door. “Do you need help, little one?” she asked in a soft voice.

Fawn took a deep breath. She remembered what Mama always said: “You can try first. You are strong and smart.” Fawn wanted to be brave. She wanted to help herself.

“I will try, Mama,” Fawn said. “I can do a plan.”

Mama smiled. “I’m near if you need me. You can ask for help with your words.”

Fawn nodded. Her ears calmed. “I will use my plan,” she told herself.

She made three steps with her gentle hoof on the quilt.

“Step one,” Fawn said. “I breathe.” She breathed in slow, smelling pine and clean quilts. She breathed out slow, watching the night-light glow.

“Step two,” she said. “I look and listen.” Fawn opened her eyes wide. Tap-tap came from the window. Rustle came from under the bed. Thump came from the closet door.

“Step three,” she whispered. “I use my words and tools.”

Fawn slid out of bed. The floor felt cool, smooth like river stones. “Hello, sounds,” she said in a brave, small voice. “I hear you. I want to know you.”

She clicked on her little flashlight. The beam was warm and round, like a golden pancake.

First, Fawn padded to the window. She pulled the curtain. Tap-tap! The window showed a tiny branch touching the glass. The wind was playing a gentle drum.

“Oh!” Fawn giggled. “It is just the tree saying hello.” She moved a soft pillow to prop the curtain so it did not knock the glass. The tap-tap grew quiet.

Next, Fawn knelt by the bed. Rustle-rustle. She shined the light. “Hello?” she said. “Is someone there?”

Under the bed sat a box of dress-up scarves. The scarves were silky and bright. Red like cherries. Blue like the river. Gold like the sun. They rustled when air moved.

Fawn smiled. “You are my play scarves.” She tucked the box in snug. “Rest now.” The rustle stopped.

Then, Fawn stood by the closet. Thump. Her heart bounced again. “I can do this,” she told herself. “I am brave and careful.” She used her words to call, “Mama, I am checking the closet now.”

“Okay, dear one,” Mama said from the door. “I am here if you want.”

Fawn opened the door. Thump! A wooden drum rolled out and tapped her hoof. The drum was painted with bright patterns—dots, lines, and stars. Fawn had learned at school that friends drum in different ways. Some drums say dum-dum like thunder. Some sing tap-a-tap like rain. Her friend Ayo played a djembe from his family’s home. Her friend Mei used bamboo sticks. Fawn’s drum was from a traveling market, with colors from many places.

“Oh, drum,” Fawn laughed. “You were the thump.” She hugged the drum and felt the smooth wood. It smelled a tiny bit like spice.

Fawn set the drum on a soft rug so it wouldn’t roll. She tapped it gently—dum, dum. The sound was friendly, like a heartbeat. “Thank you, drum,” she said. “I know you now.”

Fawn felt warm and proud. She had used her plan. She had helped herself. She had also used her words to tell Mama what she was doing. That made her feel safe and strong.

Mama stepped in, eyes kind. “You did it, Fawn. You listened, you looked, and you solved the problem.”

Fawn’s tail wiggled. “I breathed. I checked. I talked. I used tools,” she said. “I can do hard things.”

Mama nodded. “Yes. And when you need help, you can ask. That is strong, too.”

Fawn climbed back into bed. The quilt was soft and warm, like a hug. The night-light glowed steady. The room was quiet, with only a tiny whisper of wind. She placed the flashlight on the table, next to her drum.

“Good night, room,” Fawn said. “Good night, tree, scarves, and drum. Thank you for teaching me.”

“Sleep well, brave Fawn,” Mama whispered, giving a nuzzle.

Fawn closed her eyes. Her breath went in and out, slow and sweet. She felt safe because she had a plan. She felt proud because she tried by herself. And she knew, if she needed, she could call for help with her words.

Lesson: You can be brave and try first. Breathe. Look. Listen. Use your words and tools. Ask for help when you need it. You are strong and smart.

The End.
