# Mimi Mouse and the Gentle Breath

Mimi was a kind little mouse who lived near a sunny garden shed. The shed smelled like fresh soil and warm wood. Sunbeams slipped through tiny cracks, making golden lines on the floor. Outside, birds sang, “Chee-chee-chee!” and the wind whispered through tall grass.

One bright morning, Mimi tugged on a twine rope. “Today I will build a leaf-cart,” she squeaked. “I will help carry big leaves to the compost pile.” Mimi loved helping. Helping made her whiskers tingle with joy.

She scampered into the garden shed. Inside, there were soft rakes, shiny cans, and a little wooden box of nails. Mimi found four bottle-cap wheels, two straight sticks, and a small plank. “Perfect,” she said. “This will be my leaf-cart!”

Tap, tap, tap went Mimi’s tiny hammer. Clink, clink went the bottle caps. The shed smelled like mint from a nearby pot. Mimi’s ears heard a bee hum outside. She felt cozy and proud.

Then—uh-oh! One wheel rolled away. It spun across the floor and plunked into a crack between two boards. “Oh no,” said Mimi, her voice getting hot and tight. She tugged the wheel. It did not move.

Mimi’s face felt warm. Her tail flicked. “This isn’t fair,” she squeaked. “I worked so hard!” Her tummy felt like a little storm. She wanted to yank and yank and stomp her tiny feet.

Just then, Mr. Robin peeked in. “Good morning, Mimi,” he chirped. “I heard a clatter. Are you okay?”

Mimi crossed her paws. “I am mad,” she said. “My wheel is stuck. I want to shout.”

Mr. Robin fluttered onto a rake handle. “It is okay to feel mad,” he said. “Feelings are like weather. They come and go. Would you like to try the Gentle Breath?”

Mimi blinked. “What is the Gentle Breath?”

“It helps the storm calm down,” said Mr. Robin. “Smell the flower.” He took a slow, deep breath in through his beak. “Then blow the wind.” He let the air out slowly. “Let’s do it together.”

Mimi nodded. Her paws felt shaky, but she tried.

“Smell the flower,” said Mr. Robin.

Mimi breathed in. The shed smelled like soil and mint.

“Blow the wind.”

Mimi breathed out. Her tummy softened.

Again. “Smell the flower.” In. “Blow the wind.” Out. Her tail stopped flicking. The storm felt smaller.

“How do you feel?” asked Mr. Robin.

“Better,” Mimi whispered. “The mad is still there, but it is gentle now.”

“Good,” said Mr. Robin. “Now we can think. Do you need help?”

Mimi looked at the crack. She wanted to say, “No, I can do it!” But she remembered another feeling in her chest—honesty. “Yes,” she said. “I do need help. I was too mad to say so before.”

“That was brave and honest,” said Mr. Robin. “Let’s try together.”

They pushed the plank to lift the board a tiny bit. “One, two, three,” said Mimi. She slid a twig under the wheel. The wheel wiggled free and rolled into her paws.

“You did it,” cheered Mr. Robin.

Mimi smiled. “We did it. Thank you.”

Outside, the sun was higher and warmer. Mimi finished the leaf-cart with gentle taps. Tap, tap. Clink, clink. She set the wheels on tight. She pulled the cart outside and breathed the sweet garden air. The grass felt cool under her feet.

“Time for outdoor work,” she said. “Let’s carry leaves to the compost.”

She loaded big, crunchy leaves. The leaves made a soft rustle. She pulled the cart along the little path. Butterflies fluttered by, yellow and white. A ladybug crawled on her finger. “Hello, little friend,” said Mimi, giggling.

On the way, a bump in the path made the cart wobble. A leaf fell out. “Oh,” said Mimi. For a moment, the storm in her tummy peeked back in. Mimi stopped. She placed her paw on her chest.

“Smell the flower,” she told herself. In. “Blow the wind.” Out. The storm grew quiet again.

Mimi picked up the leaf and tucked it in. “I can be gentle,” she said. “I can be calm.” She kept walking to the compost pile.

When the cart was empty, Mimi turned to Mr. Robin, who had hopped along beside her. “I feel proud,” she said. “I felt mad, but I did not shout or stomp. I told the truth when I needed help. And I finished my outdoor job.”

Mr. Robin nodded. “You used your Gentle Breath. You were honest. You did kind work. That is a strong mouse!”

Back in the shed, Mimi put the tools away. She looked at the little crack in the floor. “I will make a tiny cover for that later,” she said. “So wheels won’t fall in.”

She picked up her cart and chuckled. “And next time, I will check the path for bumps.”

Mimi curled her tail around her paws and took one more slow breath. The shed felt peaceful now. The sun made warm stripes on the floor. Outside, the birds sang, and the garden smelled green and good.

Mimi whispered, “When I feel mad, I can stop. I can breathe. I can tell the truth. I can try again.” Her whiskers tingled with joy.

The End.
