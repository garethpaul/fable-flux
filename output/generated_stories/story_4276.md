# Ellie the Fair Helper

In a sunny town, there was a cozy garage workshop. It smelled like fresh wood and warm rubber tires. Tools hung on the walls like shiny silver fish. In the middle stood Ellie, a gentle elephant calf with soft gray ears and bright curious eyes.

Ellie loved to help. She loved to move her strong legs and stretch her trunk. “Helping is good exercise,” she said with a giggle. She took deep breaths and felt the cool air tickle her nose.

Today was a busy day. The workshop door creaked open. Three friends stepped inside.

First came Amir, a boy with curly hair and a green scooter. “Hello!” he said. “My scooter squeaks. Can you help?”

Next was Mei, a girl with a red bicycle bell. “Ni hao!” she greeted. “My bell is stuck.”

Then Kofi arrived with a small blue skateboard. “Akwaaba!” he said with a wide grin. “My wheels wobble.”

Ellie’s ears flapped happily. “Welcome! I like your words,” she said. “Let’s fix things, one by one.”

Ellie stretched her legs. She marched in place—step, step, step. “March with me!” she invited. The children laughed and marched too. Their feet made soft thump-thump sounds on the floor. It felt fun and strong.

Mr. Santos, the kind mechanic, smiled from his workbench. He wore bright gloves and smelled like orange soap. “Ellie is my helper today,” he said. “We will make things fair.”

“What does fair mean?” asked Amir.

“Fair means everyone waits their turn,” Ellie said. “Fair means we tell the truth. Fair means we make things right.”

Amir nodded. Mei and Kofi nodded too.

Ellie lifted Amir’s green scooter first. “Squeak, squeak,” went the wheel. “I hear you,” Ellie whispered. She found a tiny drop of oil. Drip! The scooter sang a soft whirr instead of a squeak. Amir grinned. “Xie xie,” he tried in Mei’s language. They all giggled.

Mei held up her bell. “It is stuck,” she said. Ellie tapped gently with a small rubber mallet. Tap, tap. “Ding!” The bell rang bright and clear, like sunshine. Mei clapped. “Thank you!”

Kofi rolled his skateboard. “Wobble, wobble,” it went. Ellie crouched low and did a trunk stretch, like a big slow bow. “Stretching helps me focus,” she told them. She checked the wheels and tightened the tiny nuts. Click, click. Now the board glided smooth.

“Everyone is happy,” said Mr. Santos.

But just then, a loud voice called, “I want my scooter fixed now!” A tall older kid, Nico, stomped in with a shiny black scooter. He pushed in front of the others. The door banged against the wall. The room felt tight and quiet.

Amir stepped back. Mei hugged her bell. Kofi held his board close. Nico’s face looked stormy.

Ellie took a deep breath. She felt her heart go thump-thump. Then she stood tall. Her voice was soft and steady. “Nico,” she said, “in this workshop, we are fair. We wait our turn. We do not push.”

“But I’m bigger,” Nico said. “I should go first.”

Ellie shook her head gently. “Being big is not the rule. Justice means we treat everyone the same way. You came after Amir, Mei, and Kofi. Your turn will come after theirs.”

Nico frowned. His scooter wheel squeaked like a tiny mouse. He looked at the line. He looked at his feet. He shuffled. “I don’t like waiting,” he muttered.

Ellie nodded. “Waiting is hard. Let’s do waiting exercises.” She lifted her knees high. “Up, down, up, down.” The children copied. Nico watched. Ellie swung her trunk side to side. “Sway, sway, sway.” Mei giggled and swayed too. Mr. Santos hummed a calm tune.

Nico’s shoulders relaxed. “Can I do the exercises too?” he asked.

“Yes,” said Ellie. “Everyone can join. We all move. We all breathe.”

They marched in a little circle. Step, step, step. Their shoes made tiny taps. Ellie’s feet made soft pads on the floor. The smell of orange soap mixed with the clean oil, and the air felt warm and friendly again.

When they finished, Ellie smiled. “Now, it is your turn, Nico.”

Nico handed over his scooter. “Thank you for being fair,” he said. “I shouldn’t have pushed.”

Ellie checked the wheel. It needed a cloth wipe and a small drop of oil. Drip, wipe, spin. “All better,” she said.

Nico grinned. “Gracias,” he said in Spanish. “I will wait next time.” He looked at Amir, Mei, and Kofi. “I’m sorry.”

“It’s okay,” said Amir. “We all learned.”

“We shared words,” said Mei. “Ni hao, xie xie, gracias.”

“We shared turns,” said Kofi. “That is justice.”

Ellie felt warm and proud. “Today we learned that justice means fair turns, kind words, and gentle bodies,” she said. “We can move, breathe, and help each other.”

They all rolled outside together—scooter, bike, and board—ready for a smooth ride. The sun shone bright. The wheels hummed. Their hearts felt light.

The End.
