# Dippy the Dolphin in the Garden Shed

The garden shed smelled like fresh wood and sweet soil. Sunlight peeked through tiny windows and made golden squares on the floor. Shelves held shiny tools, soft gloves, and bright pots. Drip, drop, went a little leak into a metal pail.

Inside the shed, a small kiddie pool sat in the middle. It was full of clean, cool water. Splash! A playful dolphin named Dippy popped up, clicked happily, and waved his smooth gray fin.

“I love to splash!” Dippy sang. “I love to zoom! I love to pop and whoosh with a boom!”

“Hello, Dippy,” said Nana June, who kept seeds in jars and labels in neat rows. She wore a sun hat with a big yellow bow. “Remember, the shed is cozy and small. Be gentle. Be kind. Ask before you bump or splash.”

Dippy wiggled with joy. “Okay, Nana June! I can do gentle. I can do kind. I can ask.”

The door creaked, and two friends peeked in. Pip the hedgehog waddled carefully, his tiny feet patter-pattering. Lola the ladybug buzzed in, red wings bright like candy.

“Hi, Dippy!” chirped Lola. “We heard your happy song!”

Pip sniffed the air. “It smells like mint and wet wood. I like it.”

Dippy splashed once. “Come see my pool! We can play tag. We can twirl. We can—”

“Wait,” Pip said softly. “I like slow. I don’t like big splashes on my quills.”

Lola hovered near a sunny spot. “And I like warm light. I don’t like cold drops on my wings.”

Dippy blinked and bobbed. “Oh. I feel so excited. My tummy is fizzy. I want to play big! But I hear your words.”

Nana June smiled and dried a pot with a towel. “We all have bodies. We all have feelings. We can say what we like and what we do not like. Those are our boundaries. We ask. We listen.”

Dippy took a deep breath. In through his blowhole, out with a gentle puff. “Pffoooo.” The sound was soft like a sleepy whistle.

“Can I splash a little?” Dippy asked. “Just tiny drops?”

Pip looked at his quills. “Tiny drops are okay, but not on me.”

Lola twirled near the sunbeam. “I will sit in the light. No drops here, please!”

“Okay!” Dippy said. He made one teeny plip. The water sounded like a whisper: plip.

Pip smiled. “That is nice.”

Lola hummed. “Thank you for asking.”

They began a game. “Copy the Sound!” said Nana June. Dippy clicked, click-click. Pip made a gentle tap-tap with a tiny trowel. Lola buzzed bzz-bzz. Dippy copied, click-click, tap-tap, bzz-bzz. Everyone laughed.

Then Dippy’s excitement grew again. His tail itched to swish. His heart thumped like a little drum. He forgot and gave a big swoosh. Splash! Water sprayed. A cold drop landed right on Lola’s wing.

Lola gasped. “Eep! That feels bad for me!”

Pip jumped back and puffed his quills. “Too much! Too fast!”

Dippy froze, eyes wide. His tummy felt twisty. “Oh no. I didn’t mean to. I forgot. I am sorry.”

Nana June knelt by the pool. “It is okay to make a mistake, Dippy. We notice. We name. We fix.”

Dippy took another breath. “I feel excited, and I went too big. I want to try again. Lola, what helps?”

Lola fluttered to the warm spot and spread her wings. “I need sunshine to feel better. And I need to know I can say stop.”

Dippy nodded. “You can say stop. And I will stop.”

Pip stepped close. “I like a quiet game. Maybe roll a ball?”

Dippy’s eyes lit up. “Yes! A soft garden ball.” Nana June rolled a small rubber ball to Dippy. It was green like a pea and squishy like a sponge.

“Can I roll it to you, Pip?” Dippy asked.

“Yes,” Pip said. “Roll it slow.”

Dippy pushed the ball with his nose. It made a tiny squeak and rolled in a gentle circle. Pip giggled and tapped it back with his paw. Lola landed on the ball and rode for a moment, like a little boat in calm water.

They played “Yes-No-Maybe,” a game Nana June taught them. Dippy asked, “Do you want a tiny spray, Pip?” Pip said, “No.” Dippy said, “Okay,” and did not spray. Dippy asked, “Lola, do you want a puff of air?” Lola said, “Maybe a small warm puff.” Dippy made a gentle whooooo that rippled the sunbeam but not the water. Lola smiled.

The shed felt safe and bright. The tools were tidy. The mint leaves smelled sweet and clean. Drip, drop, went the pail, soft like a lullaby.

Soon it was time to rest. Pip curled on a glove like a little bun. Lola tucked her wings. Dippy floated in the pool, still and happy.

“I learned today,” Dippy said. “I can feel big feelings and still be gentle. I can ask before I splash. I can listen to a yes, a no, and a maybe.”

Nana June patted his fin. “That is personal boundaries. Your body is yours. Their bodies are theirs. Asking and listening keeps everyone safe and joyful.”

Pip lifted his head. “We are different, and that is good.”

Lola chimed in. “We can celebrate how we each like to play.”

Dippy smiled. “Different is special. When I listen, we all have fun.”

They all took one more breath together, slow and calm. In. Out. The shed felt warm and friendly, like a hug.

The End.
