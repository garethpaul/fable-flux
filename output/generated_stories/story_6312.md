# Tully Turtle and the Treehouse Tune

Tully was a shy turtle with a smooth green shell and gentle eyes. He lived near a big oak tree with a wooden treehouse high in the branches. The treehouse smelled like warm wood and pine cones. Sunlight slid through the window like golden ribbons.

Every afternoon, friends gathered there to make music. Bees hummed outside. Leaves whispered. Inside, there were tiny drums, a triangle, little bells, and a small toy piano with bright red keys. Tully loved the sounds, but he was shy to play.

One sunny day, Tully peeked into the treehouse. “Hi, Tully!” chirped Pip the squirrel. Pip’s fluffy tail twitched. “We’re making a song. Do you want to try?”

Tully tucked his head a little. “I’m not very good,” he whispered.

Mina the mouse squeaked kindly, “That’s okay. We can practice. Practice helps us grow.”

Tully saw a soft, round drum. He touched it with one finger. Tap. Tap. The drum made a gentle sound, like raindrops on leaves. Tully smiled a tiny smile.

Pip shook a bell bracelet. Jingle-jingle! Mina pressed the red piano key. Plink! The sounds were bright and happy. The friends laughed and clapped. Tully’s shy heart felt warm.

But then, a problem began.

Pip wanted the drum. Mina wanted the drum too. They both reached for it at the same time.

“I had it first!” Pip said, his voice squeaky and fast.

“No, I did!” Mina said, her whiskers twitching.

The treehouse grew very quiet. Even the breeze paused. Tully held the drum close to his shell. He didn’t like loud arguing. His tummy felt tight.

Tully took a deep breath. He remembered what his grandma turtle always said: “Slow and steady. We can talk it out.”

In a small, soft voice, Tully said, “We can share. We can take turns.”

Pip and Mina looked at each other. Pip’s ears drooped. “I’m sorry,” Pip said.

“I’m sorry too,” Mina squeaked. “Let’s take turns.”

Tully’s tummy felt better. He nodded. “Let’s count to five when we play. Then we switch.” He held up his little turtle hand. “One… two… three… four… five!”

Pip giggled. “Five is fair!”

They made a plan. Pip would play the drum for five counts. Mina would play the piano for five counts. Then they would switch. Tully would keep time with the triangle. Ting! Ting! Ting! Ting! Ting!

They tried it. Pip tapped the drum: tap-tap, tap-tap. Mina played the piano: plink-plink, plink-plink. Tully counted with the triangle. Ting! Ting! The music felt like sunshine and skipping feet.

But Tully still felt shy about playing the drum by himself. He wanted to try. He wanted to get better. He wanted to practice.

Pip noticed. “Do you want a turn, Tully?”

Tully nodded slowly. “Yes. I want to practice.”

Mina smiled. “We will listen. We will cheer for you.”

Tully placed the drum in front of him. His hands were small and careful. He closed his eyes. He heard the wind. He heard the bees. He felt the warm wood under his feet. He tapped the drum, very softly at first. Tap… tap…

He tried again. Tap-tap… tap-tap-tap. His taps made a little pattern. It sounded like tiny turtle steps. He smiled a bigger smile. “I can do it,” he whispered.

Pip clapped. “You can do it!”

Mina nodded. “Practice makes strong music.”

Tully kept practicing. He tried loud taps and soft taps. He tried fast taps and slow taps. Tap-tap-tap. Tap… tap. The drum felt friendly now. It felt like a heartbeat in the treehouse.

Suddenly, Pip tapped too fast and bumped Mina’s paw. Plink! The piano made a funny sound. Pip frowned. Mina frowned. The music got messy.

Tully remembered their plan. “Let’s breathe,” he said. He counted, “One… two… three.” They breathed in and out. The tight feelings melted away.

“Let’s try again,” Tully said. “Slow and steady. We will practice together.”

They started over. Tully kept the beat on the triangle. Ting… ting… ting. Pip waited his turn and played the drum with careful tap-taps. Mina waited her turn and pressed the red key, then the blue one, plink-plonk. They shared the instruments. They shared the time. Their song grew smooth and sweet, like honey on toast.

When they finished, birds outside chirped as if clapping. The friends laughed. Tully stood a little taller. He felt brave. He felt proud.

Pip grinned. “Tully, your beat was perfect!”

Mina squeaked happily. “You practiced, and you got better!”

Tully touched the drum and the triangle. “We practiced, we shared, and we used our words. That made our music nice.”

They made a new rule for the treehouse. The rule was simple:
- We practice.
- We share.
- We talk and listen.

They drew the rule on a big leaf with a berry pen. The leaf smelled sweet and purple. They hung it by the window where the sunlight shone.

Every day after that, Tully came to the treehouse. He wasn’t so shy anymore. He still moved slow and steady, but his heart felt strong. He practiced the drum. He practiced waiting and sharing too. And every time, the music sounded a little better.

The friends learned that practice helps us grow, sharing keeps us happy, and kind words solve problems. Their treehouse was full of songs, giggles, and soft, steady beats.

The End.
