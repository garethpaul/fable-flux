# Milo Makes a Colorful Plan

Milo was a courageous monkey with bright eyes and quick hands. One sunny morning, he bounced into the garage workshop. The air smelled like wood and a little like paint. Sunlight made dust sparkle in the air. Tools hung neatly on the wall. Brushes sat in a jar. Cans of paint were lined up like a rainbow.

Milo clapped his hands. “Today, I will make art!” he said. His tail curled with excitement. “I will paint a big, happy mural for everyone to see.”

He looked around. “First, I need a plan,” he whispered. He remembered what Mama Monkey always said, “A plan helps you finish a task.” Milo nodded. He took a piece of chalk and drew a simple picture on the floor: a big sun, a tall tree, and a friendly blue bird.

“Step one,” he said, tapping the chalk plan, “draw my idea. Step two, gather my tools. Step three, paint slowly. Step four, clean up.”

Milo smiled. “I can do this.”

He found a wide board standing against the wall—smooth and perfect for a mural. He ran his hand over it. It felt cool and a little bumpy. “Hello, board!” he giggled. “You will be my art.”

Milo opened the paint cans. “Red,” he said. He sniffed. The smell was strong but not bad. “Blue.” “Yellow.” “Green.” He found small brushes, a big brush, and a sponge.

He put on a tiny apron. It was soft and had little bananas on it. “Ready!” he cheered.

Milo dipped the big brush into yellow paint. “Swish, swish.” He painted a round, warm sun. The brush felt tickly in his hand. Yellow drops fell—plip, plip—on the floor. “Oops,” he said, and he wiped them with a rag. “That’s okay. I can fix small oops.”

He switched to a smaller brush for the tree. “Stroke, stroke.” The tree trunk stood tall and brown. Milo hummed a tune. “I am calm. I take my time.”

Then he wanted green leaves, but the green can was stuck. He pulled. He tugged. “Hmm,” he said. “What can I try?” He looked around the workshop. He saw a rubber jar opener. He wrapped it around the lid and twisted. “Pop!” The lid came off.

“Yay!” said Milo. “I used my tools. I can solve problems.”

He dabbed the sponge into green and patted the board. “Pat, pat, pat.” Leaves! The sponge made soft, round shapes. It sounded like gentle rain. Milo grinned.

Next, he wanted a blue bird. But there was no blue paint left in the can. “Oh no,” he said. “I need blue.” He looked at the yellow, and he looked at the red. He remembered something from art time. “Colors can mix. I can make new colors.”

Milo poured a little red and a little yellow into a cup. “Stir, stir.” The paint turned orange. “Orange!” He clapped. “What makes blue?” He thought hard. He spotted a tiny bottle of paint powder on a high shelf. The label said, “Sky Blue.” It was too high for him.

Milo took a deep breath. “I am a courageous monkey, but I must be safe.” He moved a small step stool close to the shelf. He checked that it did not wobble. He called out, “Is this safe?” The workshop was quiet, but Milo listened to his own careful heart. “Yes, go slow.”

He climbed up, one step, two steps. He reached, reached, and gently took the little bottle. “Got it!” He climbed down slowly. His feet went “tap, tap” on the stool.

Milo sprinkled a little blue powder into water and stirred. “Swirl, swirl.” The water turned bright blue. He mixed in a bit of white paint to make it smooth. “Now I have blue!”

He picked up his smallest brush and painted the bird. “Tweet,” he chirped as he worked. The bird looked friendly and proud. Milo added a tiny eye and a tiny beak. “Hello, blue bird,” he whispered. “Thank you for waiting.”

Suddenly, the lights flickered. The sun went behind a cloud. The garage felt dim. Milo blinked. “It’s a little dark,” he said. He did not stop. He rolled the big door open a bit more. Warm light sighed into the room. “Ahh,” he said. “Much better.”

Milo checked his plan. “Step three, paint slowly.” He stepped back and looked. He saw a smudge on the sun. He got a clean brush and smoothed it. “Fix, fix.” He added grass at the bottom. “Swish.” He dotted flowers with the tip of his finger. “Dot, dot, dot.”

At last, he put his brush down. “I think I am done,” he said. He took a deep breath. The mural was bright and happy. The sun smiled. The tree stood tall. The blue bird spread its wings.

Milo clapped softly. “Step four, clean up.” He washed the brushes in warm, soapy water. “Swoosh.” He put the lids back on the cans. He wiped the table and the floor. He folded his apron and hung it on a hook.

Mama Monkey peeked in. “Oh, Milo!” she said. Her eyes were shiny. “You made beautiful art.”

Milo’s heart felt warm. “I made a plan. I used my tools. I tried new things. I finished my task.”

Mama Monkey nodded. “You were careful, brave, and patient. Art can teach us to look, think, and finish. And art brings joy.”

Milo smiled at the mural. The blue bird seemed to smile back. “I learned that colors can mix, problems can be solved, and taking my time helps me do my best,” he said.

Mama Monkey gave him a hug. “Yes, little artist.”

Milo waved to his mural. “Thank you, workshop,” he said. “Tomorrow, I will make more art.”

The End.
