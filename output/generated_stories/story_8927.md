# Momo Waits for Morning

Momo was a courageous monkey who loved stories, especially the special family story his Grandma told every year. It was a bedtime tradition, soft like a blanket and warm like cocoa. Tonight was the night.

Momo sat in his cozy bedroom. A moonbeam slid across the floor like silver paint. The curtains swished softly, swish-swish. Momo’s quilt smelled like clean sunshine. His favorite stuffed banana, Nana, tucked under his arm, felt squishy and safe.

“Grandma, is it time now?” Momo asked, eyes bright.

“Almost,” Grandma said, smiling. Her voice was gentle like a lullaby. “We will tell the family story when the clock says seven. That is our special time.”

Momo bounced on the bed. “But I want it now.”

Grandma shook her head kindly. “I hear you, Momo. Waiting is hard. Our family tradition needs patience. We prepare, we wait, and then we share.”

Momo let out a little sigh. “Hmm.”

Grandma sat beside him. “We can get ready while we wait. Preparation helps time go by.”

“What can we do?” asked Momo, tail curling with hope.

“First, cozy,” Grandma said. “Fluffy pillows in place?”

Momo patted the pillows. Poof, poof. “Cozy!”

“Second, snack,” Grandma said. “Would you like a tiny bowl of warm banana oatmeal?”

Momo’s eyes popped wide. “Yes, please!”

They carried the bowl back to the bedroom. The oatmeal smelled sweet and cinnamon-y. Momo blew gently. “Huuu.” The steam tickled his nose.

“Third, boundaries,” Grandma added kindly. “We will keep our room calm. We use inside voices. We ask before hugging, and we stop when someone says stop.”

Momo nodded. “I can do that.”

Momo looked at the clock. “Is it seven?”

Grandma smiled. “Not yet.”

Waiting felt like a slow turtle. Momo’s hands wiggled. His feet wanted to jump. His heart said, “Now, now!” But the clock said, “Tick… tock…”

“I can’t wait,” Momo burst out. He slid off the bed and reached for Grandma’s story basket.

Grandma gently put her hand on the basket. “Pause, Momo. My basket is special to me. Please ask first.”

Momo stopped. He remembered the boundary. He took a deep breath. In… and out. “Grandma, may I see the basket?”

“Thank you for asking,” Grandma said. “Not yet. Let’s wait for seven. You can hold Nana while you wait.”

Momo hugged Nana. He squeezed tight, then less tight. He whispered, “I can wait.” His voice sounded brave, even if his tummy fluttered.

To help, Grandma taught a waiting game. “Let’s play the Patience Parade. We march slow, and we count to five.”

They marched tiny steps around the bed. “One… two… three… four… five.” Their feet went tap… tap… tap.

Momo giggled. The giggle was like bubbles.

They sat again. The clock said, “Tick… tock…” Momo felt the want burst up like popcorn. He looked at Grandma. “I still want it now.”

Grandma nodded. “Feelings are welcome here. You can want it now and still choose to wait. That is patience.”

Momo put a hand on his chest. “I can want and wait.” He felt his heart thump-thump, strong and steady.

Just then, his little cousin Pip peeked in. “Story time?” Pip asked loudly, feet thumping.

“Almost,” Grandma said softly. “Inside voice, please.”

Pip bounced closer and tried to grab Nana. “Mine!”

Momo held Nana close and said, “Stop, please. Nana is my toy.” His voice was calm and clear.

Pip froze, then looked at Grandma. Grandma nodded. “Momo set a boundary. Pip, you can ask.”

Pip tried again, softer. “Can I hold Nana?”

Momo thought. He liked sharing, but he also liked holding Nana. “You can hold Nana after the story,” he said. “Right now, I need Nana.”

Pip nodded. “Okay.” He picked up a pillow instead and hugged it. Momo felt proud. He had been kind and brave.

The clock clicked. The little hand touched the seven. Ding! It was time.

Grandma smiled. “You waited with patience. You prepared. You respected boundaries. Now we share our tradition.”

She opened the story basket. It smelled like paper and lavender. She took out the old, soft book. The cover felt smooth under Momo’s fingertips. Everyone snuggled close—Momo, Pip, Nana, and Grandma.

Grandma began. “Long ago, our family had a brave monkey who climbed a tall tree to share bananas with friends…”

As Grandma spoke, Momo listened. He felt calm, like floating on a warm cloud. The words were sweet and gentle. Pip leaned on Momo’s shoulder. Momo patted Pip’s hand, and Pip smiled.

When the story ended, Momo breathed out, happy. He looked at the clock and at Grandma. “I did it. I was patient.”

Grandma kissed his forehead. “Yes, Momo. Patience helps us enjoy special things. Preparation makes waiting easier. Resilience helps us try again when waiting is hard. And personal boundaries keep everyone safe and comfy.”

Momo hugged Nana and whispered, “I can want and wait. I can be brave and kind.”

Outside, the moon glowed. Inside, the cozy bedroom felt even warmer.

Momo closed his eyes and smiled. He was a courageous monkey, and he knew a big truth: patience helps good moments grow.

The End.
