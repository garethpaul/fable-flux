# Polly Paints the Laundry

Polly was a creative parrot with bright green feathers and a sunny yellow beak. She loved colors, stories, and helping. One morning, Polly flew into the laundry room. It smelled like clean soap and warm towels. The washing machine hummed, “whirr, whirr,” and the dryer made a gentle “thump, thump.”

“Good morning, Laundry Room!” Polly chirped. “Today, I will do my tasks. Then, I will make art and tell a story!”

On the wall was a simple list with stars:
1. Sort clothes.
2. Put clothes in washer.
3. Press safe buttons with help.
4. Fold and put away.

Polly nodded. “First things first!”

She spread her wings wide and looked at the clothes. “Soft socks here, color shirts there, white towels over here,” she said. The socks were fluffy like clouds. The shirts were red, blue, and gold, like a parade. The white towels were bright as moonlight.

Polly found a tiny safety sign. It said, “Ask for help with machines.” Polly remembered the rule. “Safety first,” she said. “I will not climb inside. I will not lean on doors. I will ask for help.”

Polly called, “Mrs. Rivera? Can you please help me?” Mrs. Rivera had warm eyes and kind hands. She wore a bright scarf from her home far away. It had swirls of pink and green.

“Hola, Polly,” she said with a smile. “I can help. Thank you for asking.”

Polly flapped happily. “I sorted the clothes! Now we wash.”

Together, they placed clothes in the washer. The shirts felt cool and smooth. The towels felt thick and cozy. Mrs. Rivera poured soap that smelled like lemons in sunshine.

“May I press the safe button?” Polly asked.

“Yes,” said Mrs. Rivera. “I will show you which one.”

Polly pressed the big round button with a gentle tap. The washer sang, “Beep!” Then it spun. “Swish, swish, swish,” went the water.

“Task two is done!” Polly cheered.

While they waited, Polly saw clothespins in a bowl. Clicky, clacky, little wooden smiles. She had an idea. “Art time while we wait!”

She set the clothespins on a towel. She used colorful fabric scraps from the mending basket. She clipped the scraps to the pins to make tiny flags. Red, blue, yellow, and green danced like birds.

Mrs. Rivera clapped softly. “What a lovely idea. These flags look like the flags from many places.”

Polly puffed with pride. “Each flag can tell a story,” she said. “This red one is for a warm sunset. This blue one is for a calm sea. This green one is for a tall forest. This yellow one is for sweet mangoes.”

Mrs. Rivera nodded. “In my country, we dance when we see bright colors. Colors tell stories of home.”

The washer stopped. “Beep!” Polly remembered. “Time for task three.”

Mrs. Rivera opened the door. Steam puffed out, warm and soft. The clothes smelled like lemony rain. Polly helped move them to the dryer, one gentle piece at a time.

“Safety,” Polly said. “I keep my wings back. I wait for your go-ahead.”

“Very good,” said Mrs. Rivera. She set the dryer. “We will check when it is done.”

While the dryer tumbled, “whump-a-thump,” Polly used chalk on a small blackboard on the wall. She drew her flags and a big smiling sun. She told a story in a happy voice:

“Once there was a parrot who liked to help. She did one task, then the next. She waited. She listened. She stayed safe. And because she finished her tasks, she had time to make art and share stories with friends from many places.”

Mrs. Rivera smiled. “That is a wise story.”

“Thank you,” said Polly. “I feel proud when I finish things.”

“Beep!” went the dryer. Warm air whispered out. Polly and Mrs. Rivera folded the clothes. The towels were soft and huggable. The shirts smelled fresh. Polly stacked the socks in neat pairs, two by two.

“Task four is done!” Polly chirped. “We did it all.”

Mrs. Rivera gave Polly a gentle pat. “You were careful, kind, and creative. You asked for help. You waited for the beeps. You finished each job.”

Polly hooked her tiny flags on a string by the window. The colors shone in the sun. “Look,” she said, “our art is ready.”

They both sat for a moment, quiet and happy. The laundry room felt cozy and bright. Polly’s heart felt as warm as the towels.

Mrs. Rivera said, “What did we learn today, Polly?”

Polly lifted her wings. “We learned to finish tasks, one by one. We learned to be safe with machines. We learned that art and stories can come from many places and many colors. And when we complete our work, we have time for fun.”

“Sí,” said Mrs. Rivera. “Work first, then play. And both can be full of joy.”

Polly chirped a little song. “Sort, wash, dry, fold—then create and share!” She giggled. “I will remember that.”

The flags fluttered. The laundry gleamed. Polly looked at the neat stacks, the bright art, and her kind friend.

“I did my tasks,” Polly said softly, “and now my heart feels done, too.”

The End.
