# Tiko the Turtle Shares the Porch

Tiko was a shy turtle with a smooth, green shell. He loved the peaceful front porch. The porch smelled like warm wood and fresh lemonade. The wind was soft, and the sky was a gentle blue. Birds sang, “Chee-chee,” and a little bell on the door went, “ding!”

Tiko liked quiet. He liked to watch the world from the porch step. He tucked in his head when new sounds came. “It is calm here,” Tiko whispered.

One sunny morning, Tiko heard tap-tap-tap. New neighbors were moving in. A small girl named Lina waved from the path. She wore bright red shoes and a hat with a yellow sun. “Hello!” Lina said. “May I draw on your porch with chalk?”

Tiko pulled his head inside his shell. He felt shy. The porch was his safe place. He peeped out and said, “Maybe… maybe just a little.”

Lina smiled kindly. “Thank you,” she said. She sat and drew a big circle. “This is the Earth,” she said softly. “My grandma lives far away on this circle. She says hello in Spanish: ‘Hola.’” Lina looked at Tiko. “How do you say hello?”

Tiko blinked. “I say, ‘Hello,’” he whispered. He listened to Lina say “Hola,” and tried, “O-la.” The word felt round and warm in his mouth.

A friendly breeze moved the wind chimes. Ting-ting. The porch felt a little less scary. Tiko watched Lina draw oceans and tiny houses. “I like your Earth,” he said. “It looks like a big hug.”

“It is a big hug,” said Lina. “Everyone lives here together.”

Just then, a squirrel named Pip scampered up the steps. “I found a shiny acorn!” Pip chirped. “Can I keep it here?” Pip put the acorn right in the middle of the chalk Earth.

Tiko felt a squeeze in his belly. “That is the middle,” he said softly. “It covers the drawing.”

Pip’s tail drooped. “Oh,” he said. “I didn’t know. I just wanted to share my shiny.”

Lina looked at Tiko. Tiko took a slow breath. He remembered how he felt when new sounds came. He remembered being shy. He looked at Pip’s eyes. They were hopeful and bright.

Tiko said, “Pip, I think your acorn is special. Maybe we can find a place for it that is good for all of us.”

Pip perked up. “Like where?”

Tiko pointed with a gentle claw. “How about here, near the tree Lina drew? Acorns like trees.”

Pip smiled. “That is fair,” he said, and put the acorn by the chalk tree. The porch felt peaceful again.

A little later, a breeze carried the smell of ginger cookies. Mrs. Kim from next door came by with a plate. “Annyeong,” she said. “That means hello in Korean.” She set the cookies down. “May I sit for a moment?”

Tiko tucked his feet and nodded. “Ann-yeong,” he tried. Mrs. Kim’s eyes crinkled in a smile.

Lina offered a cookie to Tiko. “Do you want one?”

Tiko looked at the plate. He wanted a cookie, but he worried there would not be enough. He looked at everyone’s faces. He saw Lina’s kind eyes, Pip’s twitchy nose, Mrs. Kim’s gentle hands. He felt their hopes and their hunger.

Tiko said, “Let’s share so everyone gets one. If there are extra, we can split them.”

“That is fair,” said Lina.

“That is kind,” said Mrs. Kim.

“That is yummy!” said Pip.

Crunch, crunch. The cookies were soft on the inside and warm. Cinnamon tickled Tiko’s nose. He felt brave. He felt part of the porch.

Lina picked up the chalk again. “Tiko, do you want to draw?”

Tiko blinked. “I don’t know how.”

Lina handed him a blue piece. “You can show a feeling,” she said. “Draw the wind, or the song you hear.”

Tiko listened to the chimes. Ting-ting, ting. He listened to the birds. Chee-chee. He felt the porch boards under his feet. Smooth. Safe.

Slowly, Tiko drew soft swirls for wind. He drew tiny dots for birds. He drew a small turtle near the big Earth. “This is me,” he said. “I am small, but I am here.”

Lina clapped. “Your drawing talks,” she said. “It says, ‘I am shy, and I am brave.’”

Pip tilted his head. “It also says, ‘I like cookies.’”

Everyone laughed. The laughter sounded like bells and bubbles.

A mail carrier walked by and waved. “Hello!” he called. “That is a beautiful Earth.”

“Thank you,” said Tiko, a little louder this time. He felt warm inside. He had used his voice. He had listened, too.

The sun began to glow orange. The porch felt cozy. Lina drew little hearts around the Earth. Tiko added a tiny house for Mrs. Kim’s family and a tree for Pip. He drew a small heart for Lina’s grandma far away.

“Look,” Tiko said. “We made space for everyone.”

Lina nodded. “We used kind words and kind ears.”

Tiko took a deep breath. He looked at his friends. “I learned something,” he said. “When we listen to how others feel, we can share, we can be fair, and we can make something beautiful together.”

Mrs. Kim smiled. “That is empathy,” she said. “Seeing with your heart.”

Tiko touched his shell. It felt smooth and strong. “My heart can see,” he whispered, happy.

The porch was still peaceful, but it was full now—full of color, laughter, and care. Tiko was still a shy turtle, but he was not alone.

The End.
