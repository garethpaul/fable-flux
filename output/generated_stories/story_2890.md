# Mango the Brave and the Busy Bins

Mango the monkey woke up to warm sunshine on his fur. He stretched his long arms and sniffed the air in the sunny backyard. It smelled like fresh grass and sweet oranges. Birds chirped, “Tweet-tweet!” The leaves whispered, shhh, in the gentle breeze.

Mango was a courageous monkey. He loved to help. He loved to learn. Today, the backyard looked a little messy. There were bottles near the flower bed, a paper cup by the sandbox, and a banana peel on the step.

“Oh no,” Mango said. “The yard is not happy. I will help!”

Mango hopped to the bins by the fence. There were three bright bins. One was blue, one was green, and one was brown. The blue bin had a picture of bottles and cans. The green bin had a picture of leaves and food. The brown bin had a picture of trash that could not be used again.

Mango touched each bin. The blue bin felt cool. The green bin smelled like apples and soil. The brown bin made a soft thump when he tapped it.

“Blue for recycle. Green for compost. Brown for trash,” Mango said, tapping his chest. “I can do this!”

Just then, Mango’s friend Lina peeked over the fence. Lina had warm brown eyes and a bright scarf. She waved and called, “Hola, Mango!”

“Hola means hello!” Mango grinned. “Hola, Lina!”

Lina laughed. “Bonjour!” she said.

“Bonjour means hello, too!” Mango clapped. “I like words. Words are like little hugs.”

Lina smiled. “Can I help you clean?”

“Yes, please,” said Mango. “We will make the yard smile. We will recycle!”

They made a plan. Mango would pick up items and show them to Lina. Lina would help say where each one goes.

Mango picked up the paper cup. He rubbed it. It felt smooth and crinkly. “Where does this go?”

Lina looked at the cup. “Is it clean?”

Mango sniffed. It smelled like nothing. “Yes!”

“Then it can go in blue. We can recycle clean paper,” said Lina.

Mango dropped the cup into the blue bin. Clink! He felt proud. His tail swished. “I did it!”

Next, Mango found a shiny can by the slide. The sun made it sparkle. “Blue bin?” he asked.

“Sí! That means yes!” said Lina. “Aluminum cans go in the blue bin.”

“Sí!” Mango echoed. He tossed the can in. Clonk! He smiled. “I am smart and brave.”

They found the banana peel on the step. It was squishy and smelled like sweet banana. Mango wrinkled his nose. “Yuck. Where does this go?”

“Green bin,” said Lina. “Food scraps can be compost. Compost turns into good soil.”

“So the peel will help flowers grow?” Mango’s eyes grew wide.

“Exacto! That means exactly,” Lina said with a giggle.

Mango placed the peel into the green bin. Plop. He felt the warm sun on his face. He felt strong. He felt kind.

They kept going. Mango found a glass bottle. It was cold and smooth. He held it up. “Blue bin,” he said. Clink! In it went.

He found a plastic bag. It fluttered like a little ghost. “Hmm,” Mango said. He was not sure. “Blue? Green? Brown?”

Lina thought. “Plastic bags don’t go in blue here. They can break the machines. Let’s put it in brown or take it to a special store later.”

Mango nodded. “Today, brown bin.” He put the bag in the brown bin. Thump.

A little girl named Amara walked in with her grandma. They brought a basket of muffins. “Jambo!” Grandma said. “That means hello in Swahili.”

Mango’s ears perked up. “Jambo!” he said happily.

Amara pointed to the muffins. “We brought snacks. After we clean, we share.”

Mango smiled big. “Thank you! Sharing is kind.”

They all worked together. They found a sticky jar. Mango washed it with warm water from the hose. Splash, splash. He laughed at the bubbles tickling his fingers. Then he placed the clean jar in the blue bin. Clink.

They found leaves and grass clippings. The pile smelled like rain. Mango scooped it into the green bin. Swish.

Soon the sunny backyard looked bright and tidy. The flowers lifted their faces. The birds sang louder. Mango took a deep breath. The air felt fresh and happy.

Grandma clapped. “You used your brain and your heart.”

Amara said, “You were brave, Mango. You asked questions. That is smart.”

Mango’s cheeks felt warm with pride. “Thank you. I like learning new words, too. Hola, bonjour, jambo, sí, exacto!” He giggled. “Different words, same hello. Different bins, same Earth!”

They sat on a blanket. The muffins were soft and sweet. Crumbs stuck to Mango’s fingers. He licked them and laughed. “Mmm!”

Lina raised her cup of water. “To recycling,” she said.

“To helping the Earth,” said Amara.

“To feeling proud,” Mango added. “When we sort blue, green, and brown, we help the ground, the sky, and each other.”

Grandma nodded. “Little hands can make big change.”

Mango looked around the sunny backyard. It glowed with color. He felt brave, smart, and loved. “Tomorrow,” he said, “we can teach more friends. We can say hello in many ways. And we can recycle every day.”

Everyone cheered. “Yes!”

The End.
