# Tiko’s Tap-Tap Tidy Dance

Tiko was a gentle elephant calf with soft gray ears and a tiny, curious trunk. One sunny morning, Tiko trotted into the bright, shiny bathroom. The tiles felt cool on Tiko’s feet. Drip, drip, drip went the faucet. Squeak, squeak went the clean mirror. It smelled like soap and warm towels.

Tiko loved to dance. “Tap-tap, swish-swish!” he hummed, wiggling his toes on the tiles. He lifted his trunk and twirled. The water dripping made a beat. Drip, drip. Tap, tap. Tiko began a bathroom dance.

Mama Elephant called from the hallway, “Tiko, remember our Bathroom Rules!”

Tiko smiled. “Yes, Mama!” he said. But Tiko was excited. He turned the faucet on to make more rhythm. WHOOSH! Cold water splashed. He stomped in a little puddle. Splash, splash! He swirled his trunk like a ribbon. He forgot to turn the water off.

Soon, there was a big puddle on the floor. It was shiny and fun. Tiko giggled. “Tap-tap, splash-splash!” He hopped again. But then—slip! His foot slid. Tiko wobbled.

“Whoa!” Tiko squeaked, flapping his ears. He caught himself on the sink. His heart thumped fast. Thump-thump. The puddle spread to the rug. The water ran and ran. Drip turned to stream, and the stream made a soft rushing sound.

Mama Elephant came in. She looked at the water. She looked at Tiko. “Oh, Tiko,” she said gently. “What happened?”

Tiko’s cheeks felt warm. He looked down. “I was dancing,” he whispered. “I wanted a beat. I left the water on. I made a big mess.”

Mama took a deep breath. She was calm. “Thank you for telling the truth,” she said. “Let’s think. What is our civic responsibility at home?”

Tiko blinked. The word was big. “Civic… re-spon-si-bil-i-ty?” he said slowly.

“It means we take care of our shared spaces,” Mama said. “We help each other. We keep the bathroom safe and clean. We save water, because water is precious.”

Tiko touched the cool, shiny faucet. “Other friends need water too,” he said softly. He remembered a book from story time. The book showed children around the world carrying clean water. He saw smiling faces, blue lakes, and dry places.

“That’s right,” Mama said. “We share our Earth. We are global citizens. We do small things that help everyone.”

Tiko nodded. He took a deep breath. “I want to help,” he said. He turned the faucet handle off. Drip, drip… stopped. The bathroom grew quiet. Tiko listened. He heard the soft flap of towels and his own slow breaths.

“What can we do next?” Mama asked.

“We can clean up,” Tiko said. He rolled up a towel with his trunk. Swish! He pressed it on the puddle. Squish! He pushed the water toward the drain. He used a small sponge. Squeeze, squeeze. Mama held the rug so it could dry. The bathroom smelled fresh again.

Tiko smiled a little. He liked helping. “This is our bathroom,” he said. “We all use it. I can take care of it.”

Mama’s eyes shone. “Yes. That’s civic responsibility,” she said. “And that helps our whole world.”

Tiko had an idea. “I still want to dance,” he said, “but I want to dance responsibly.”

Mama nodded. “We can make a Tap-Tap Tidy Dance. A dance that helps!”

Tiko clapped his feet softly. “Tap-tap!” He made steps with his trunk and toes.

“Step one,” Tiko sang, “Turn off the tap.” He reached up and gently turned the handle. Click!

“Step two,” Mama sang, “Use just a little soap.” She pressed a tiny pump. Plip!

“Step three,” Tiko sang, “Wipe up the splashes.” He danced with a cloth. Swish, swish!

“Step four,” Mama sang, “Hang up the towels.” They lifted towels together. Fluff, flop!

“Step five,” Tiko sang, “Share and care.” He patted his heart. Thump, thump.

They danced the Tap-Tap Tidy Dance again. It was fun. It was safe. The bathroom looked bright. The floor was dry. The water was quiet, waiting for when it was needed.

Just then, Tiko heard a soft drip, drip from the shower. He looked closely. The knob was not tight. Tiko reached out and gently turned it. Click. The drip stopped.

He felt proud. “I can help at home,” Tiko said. “I can help the Earth, too.”

Mama hugged him with her trunk. “Little acts make a big difference,” she said. “You are a caring helper.”

Tiko’s ears wiggled happily. He practiced his dance one more time. Tap-tap, swish-swish, click. He imagined children far away turning off taps, wiping splashes, and smiling. He felt connected. He felt kind.

Before bed, Tiko put a small sign near the sink. The letters were big and friendly. “Tap Off, Wipe Up, Share Care,” it said. He traced the words with his trunk.

Mama read the sign with him. “This will help everyone remember,” she said. “That is good citizenship.”

Tiko yawned. He smelled the clean towels. He heard the quiet room. He felt safe and proud. He whispered, “I am a helper. I am a global citizen.”

Mama kissed his forehead. “Yes,” she said. “And tomorrow, we can teach the Tap-Tap Tidy Dance to our friends.”

Tiko smiled and closed his eyes, dreaming of kind dances, quiet taps, and a big world where everyone helped.

The End.
