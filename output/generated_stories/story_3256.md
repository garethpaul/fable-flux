# Tiko the Tortoise and the Tidy Time Plan

Tiko was a wise tortoise who wore tiny round glasses. He stayed in the guest room at Mia’s house. The guest room was quiet and cozy. It smelled like clean sheets and warm cookies. Sunlight made soft squares on the rug. A clock on the wall went tick-tock, tick-tock.

One sunny morning, Mia ran in and waved a bright kite. “Tiko, let’s fly this at the park!” she said.

Tiko smiled. “That sounds wonderful,” he said in a slow, warm voice. “But first, we have things to do.”

Mia looked around the guest room. There were blocks on the floor, crayons on the pillow, and books under the chair. The kite string was tangled like spaghetti. The clock said tick-tock, tick-tock.

“Oh no,” Mia said. “We will be late! The sky is windy now. Wind is good for kites!”

Tiko nodded. “Wind is good,” he said. “We can do it all, if we make a plan.”

“A plan?” Mia asked.

“Yes,” said Tiko. He pointed to the clock. “Time helps us. We can give each job a little bit of time. We can share the jobs. That is called time-management. It means we take care of time, so time takes care of us.”

Mia took a breath. The room felt big and messy. Her tummy felt wiggly. “I want to go to the park with everyone,” she said softly. She could hear children laughing outside. She could smell grass through the window.

Tiko put a gentle paw on her hand. “We will go,” he said. “Let’s make our plan.”

He took a paper and drew simple pictures. He drew a block, a crayon, a book, and a kite. He made little boxes next to each picture.

“Step one,” Tiko said. “We clean blocks for two songs.”

Mia smiled. “I like songs!”

“Step two,” Tiko said. “We put crayons in the tin for one song.”

“Step three,” Tiko said. “We make a book stack for one song.”

“Step four,” Tiko said. “We untangle the kite for two songs.”

Mia nodded. “I can do that.”

Tiko looked at the door. “And step five,” he said, “we invite friends. Everyone is welcome. We can include big kids and little kids, walkers and wheelers.” He thought of their neighbor, Sam, who used a wheelchair. “Inclusion means we make space for everyone to play.”

Mia clapped. “Yes!”

Tiko set the clock timer. “Ready?” he asked.

“Ready!” said Mia.

They started with the blocks. Tiko sang a counting song in his gentle voice. “One block, two block, red block, blue.” Mia hummed and scooped blocks into the bin. Click, clack, clunk. The rug felt soft under her knees. The timer ticked. When the song ended, the floor looked clearer.

“Great job,” Tiko said. “Next, crayons.”

They sang a rainbow song. Mia picked up crayons: yellow like the sun, blue like the sky, green like grass. Scritch-scratch went the tin as the crayons slid in. The room began to smell like wax and paper. Mia smiled. Her tummy felt calm.

“Books next,” said Tiko.

They stacked books by size. Big, medium, small. Thump, thump, thump. The chair was free again. The guest room seemed to shine.

“Now the tricky part,” Tiko said, touching the kite string. It was a nest of loops. “Slow and steady.”

Mia watched his careful paws. She held the kite tail still. “I can help,” she said. She saw a knot. She loosened it with gentle fingers. The string whispered, slip, slip. The clock said tick-tock, tick-tock.

“Teamwork,” Tiko said. “We share the job and the time.”

When the timer chimed, the kite was smooth and ready. Tiko looked around. “We did it, and we still have wind!”

Mia jumped up. “Park time!”

Tiko smiled. “One more step,” he said, and opened the door. “Let’s include friends.”

They rolled the kite to the porch. “Sam!” Mia called. “Do you want to fly our kite?”

Sam waved from his chair. “Yes!” he said. “I can hold the reel.”

“Great,” Tiko said. “We can take turns. Taking turns is part of our plan.”

They reached the park. The air smelled like fresh grass and flowers. The breeze kissed their cheeks. Whoosh, whoosh. Children laughed. Birds chirped. The sun felt warm like a hug.

Mia ran a few steps. “Ready, Sam?” she asked.

“Ready!” Sam held the reel. The kite rose, flutter flutter, then soared. The tail danced like a ribbon. People clapped. A little kid pointed and giggled. “Pretty!”

Tiko watched with happy eyes. “We used our time well,” he said. “We cleaned, we planned, we included friends, and now we play outside.”

Mia handed the reel to a tiny neighbor. “Your turn,” she said. The neighbor grinned. Everyone cheered. The kite dipped and climbed. The wind sang. The clock back home ticked, but now the time felt just right.

On the way home, Mia looked at Tiko. “I liked our plan,” she said. “It helped me feel calm.”

Tiko nodded. “A little plan makes big feelings smaller,” he said. “Time-management means we choose what to do first, we give each job a little time, and we share. Then we have time for fun.”

Mia hugged him. “Next time, we can make a plan again.”

“Yes,” said Tiko. “Slow and steady wins the time.”

Back in the cozy guest room, the rug was clear, the books were neat, and the crayons clicked softly in their tin. The room smelled sweet and calm.

Mia smiled. “We cleaned up, we included friends, and we flew a kite.”

Tiko winked. “That is the lesson. Make a plan, use time kindly, and include everyone. Then there is time for outdoor fun.”

The End.
