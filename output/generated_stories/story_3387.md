# Joey Roo's Breakfast Routine

In a warm kitchen that smelled like toast and sweet banana, a joyful kangaroo joey named Joey Roo bounced on his soft tail. The morning sun made little golden dots on the floor. The kettle hummed. A clock tick-tick-ticked on the wall.

“Good morning, Joey Roo,” said Mama Roo, smiling as she stirred oatmeal. “It’s breakfast time. Let’s follow our morning routine.”

Joey Roo wiggled his ears. “Routine?” he asked. “Can I bounce first?”

“We can bounce later,” Mama Roo said gently. “First we wash paws, then set the table, then eat together.”

Joey Roo sniffed the warm air. He heard the oatmeal bubble and pop. His tummy rumbled like a little drum. “I’m hungry now,” he said. He darted toward the table and reached for the honey.

“Pause,” said Mama Roo in a calm voice. “First step: wash paws.”

Joey Roo sighed. He wanted honey. He wanted to bounce. He didn’t want to stop. “Do I have to?” he asked, his tail drooping a bit.

Mama Roo nodded. “Yes, dear. Our routine helps us. Clean paws keep us healthy. Then we set the table together. That’s how we get ready.”

Joey Roo looked at his paws. They had a smudge of crayon blue and a spot of garden dirt. He scrunched his nose. “They are a little messy.”

“Let the warm water tickle,” Mama Roo said with a grin.

Joey Roo hopped to the sink. The water felt cozy on his fur. He rubbed, rub-rub, with the soapy bubbles. The bubbles smelled like lemons and made tiny rainbow circles. “Look, Mama! Rainbow bubbles!” he giggled.

“Lovely,” Mama Roo said. “Now step two: set the table.”

Joey Roo looked at the stack of cups and the small plates. He felt a tiny flutter in his belly. “That is hard,” he whispered. “What if I drop a cup?”

Mama Roo tapped her chin. “Let’s make it a game. You place the napkins. I’ll hand you one light cup at a time. We can share the job.”

Joey Roo’s ears perked up. “A sharing game!”

He took a napkin. It felt soft like clouds. He laid it carefully by a spoon. “One napkin,” he said. He placed another. “Two napkins.” He counted slowly, his voice warm and steady. He liked the soft swish of napkins on the table. He liked the clink of the cup when he set it down gently with Mama’s help.

Soon the table looked neat and friendly: two bowls, two spoons, two cups, and a honey jar that gleamed like sunshine. Joey Roo’s chest felt proud. “We did it!”

Mama Roo nodded. “You used your skills. Washing, setting, counting. That’s our routine working.”

Joey Roo hopped once, happy. Then his eyes sparkled. “Now honey?”

Mama Roo tilted her head. “There’s one more step. Oatmeal first, then honey on top. That’s our plan.”

Joey Roo’s tail twitched. He wanted to scoop the honey right now. He looked at the jar and licked his lips. He could almost taste the sweet golden drip.

He took a deep breath. He remembered the warm water, the soft napkins, the counting. The steps helped him. Maybe they could help again.

“I have an idea,” Joey Roo said. “Compromise?”

Mama Roo smiled. “Tell me.”

“I will take three bites of oatmeal,” Joey Roo said, holding up three fingers, “and then I get a little honey swirl.”

Mama Roo nodded. “That sounds fair. We will share the plan.”

Joey Roo took a spoonful. The oatmeal was creamy and cozy, like a hug in a bowl. “Mmm.” He took a second bite. He heard the clock go tick-tick-tick. He took a third bite. “Three!”

Mama Roo dipped the spoon into the honey. It made a tiny, sticky thread. She drew a small swirl on top. The honey smelled like warm flowers. Joey Roo giggled. “Honey spiral!”

He tasted it. Sweet and smooth. “Yum!” He wiggled with joy.

When the bowls were empty, Joey Roo wiped his mouth. He listened to the quiet kettle, now done. He felt calm and strong. “Mama, routines help me know what to do,” he said. “Wash paws, set the table, eat, then honey.”

“And we shared the work,” Mama Roo added. “You built new skills. You counted. You placed things carefully. You made a smart compromise.”

Joey Roo nodded. “I can do it again tomorrow.”

“Every morning,” Mama Roo said, giving him a gentle nose nuzzle. “Routine makes our day smooth.”

Joey Roo looked at the table. “Can I help clean up?” he asked.

“Yes,” Mama Roo said. “You carry the napkins. I’ll carry the bowls. We’ll do it together.”

The napkins made a soft rustle as Joey Roo picked them up. He liked the sound. He liked the feeling of helping. His heart felt big and bright.

“Next time,” Joey Roo said, “I can set three napkins by myself.”

“That’s growing,” Mama Roo said. “One step at a time.”

Joey Roo smiled. “Hop, wash, set, eat, sweet swirl,” he sang softly. “That’s our routine.”

They shared a little laugh, warm like the kitchen. Joey Roo imagined tomorrow’s sunshine dots on the floor and felt ready.

The End.
