# Beaver Benny’s Table-Time Plan

Benny was a busy beaver with bright eyes and a big, flat tail. Today, he sat at a shiny wooden table in the dining room. The room smelled like warm pancakes and sweet apples. Sunlight slid across the floor like a golden ribbon.

On the table sat Benny’s learning placemat. It had numbers, letters, and shapes. There were crayons in a little cup. There was also a stack of tiny picture cards. His chair squeaked as he wiggled.

“I want to learn it all right now!” Benny said, tail thump-thump-thumping on the chair.

Mama Beaver brought a small bowl of apple slices. “Learning is yummy for your brain,” she said with a smile. “Remember, slow and steady. One thing at a time.”

Benny nodded fast. He grabbed three crayons at once. He tried to trace letters, count apples on a card, and sort shapes too. His crayons skidded. His paper wrinkled. His tail thumped again, thump-thump-thump.

“This is hard,” Benny grumbled. “Too many things!”

His tummy felt twisty like a knot. He scrunched his nose and squeezed his eyes. A crayon rolled off the table and plinked on the floor. The dining room felt too bright, too noisy, too much.

Mama Beaver sat beside him. She spoke softly. “I see your big feelings. Put your paw on your belly and take a slow breath with me.”

Benny put his paw on his soft tummy. “In… one, two,” he whispered. He blew air out slowly. “Out… one, two.”

The room felt quieter. The sunlight felt warm, not too bright. The apple slices smelled sweet again.

“I want to learn,” Benny said. “But I don’t know where to start.”

Mama Beaver tapped the table gently. Tap, tap. “Let’s make a plan. Plans help busy brains. You can do it.”

Benny’s ears perked up. “A plan! Yes!”

He looked around the dining room. He saw his napkin. It was clean and white. He saw the crayon cup. He saw the tiny picture cards.

“I will use the napkin as my list,” Benny said. “I will draw three little boxes. One for letters, one for numbers, one for shapes.”

He took a blue crayon and drew three simple squares on the napkin. He drew a tiny A in the first square, a 1 in the second, and a triangle in the third.

“Now,” he said, “I pick one box first.”

He touched the A box and smiled. “Letters first.”

Mama nodded. “Good choice. Pick a tool.”

Benny chose just one crayon—green. It felt smooth and cool in his paw. He traced the letter A on his placemat. He said, “A says a, a, a.” He traced B. “B says buh.” He took one more slow breath when his tail wanted to thump. “Slow and steady,” he whispered.

When he finished five letters, he put a tiny green check in the A box. “Check!” he cheered. He felt a warm glow in his chest.

“Now numbers,” Benny said. He picked two apple slices. “I will count by placing apples.” The apples were crisp and juicy. Crunch! He lined them up and counted, “One, two.” He added more slices. “Three, four, five.” He tapped each slice softly. Tap, tap, tap.

His tail started to wiggle too fast. He paused. Paw on belly. “In… one, two. Out… one, two.” He smiled and finished counting to ten. He put a yellow check in the 1 box. “Check!”

“Shapes next,” he said. He took the picture cards and sorted them into neat piles: circles, squares, triangles. He traced the edges with his finger. The circle felt smooth and round. The square had four little corners. The triangle had three points like tiny mountain peaks.

A loud clatter came from the kitchen—spoons in the sink. Benny jumped. His crayon slid. For a moment, his eyes felt watery.

“You’re okay,” Mama said. “What helps your busy body?”

“My plan,” Benny said. He hugged himself gently. “Squeeze, breathe, do one thing.” He sorted the last few cards, slow and careful.

He drew a red check in the triangle box. “Check, check, check! I did it!”

Mama clapped softly so the spoons wouldn’t clatter again. “How did your plan help?”

Benny sat up tall. “My plan helped me learn. I picked one thing. I used one tool. I took slow breaths. I finished each box.”

He looked at the napkin list, now colorful with checks and tiny smudges. “My list is my helper,” he said. “I can learn new things when I make a plan and calm my body.”

Mama placed a small sticker on his napkin—a shiny star. “You were resourceful,” she said. “You used what you had: a napkin, a crayon, and your good ideas.”

Benny giggled. “And my belly breaths!”

They shared the last apple slice. Crunch! The dining room felt cozy and bright. The table was tidy. Benny’s heart felt proud and peaceful.

He tapped the napkin gently. “Next time, I will make a new list. Maybe I will learn sounds for S and T. Maybe I will count spoons. I can do it, step by step.”

Mama smiled. “Education is like building a beaver dam,” she said. “Stick by stick.”

Benny nodded. “Stick by stick. Box by box. Breath by breath.” His tail gave a happy, quiet tap.

He tucked the napkin into the crayon cup like a little flag. “I’m a busy beaver with a smart plan,” he said.

The End.
