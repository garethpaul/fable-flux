# Rocky Raccoon’s Spin-Cycle Song

In a cozy little house, there was a bright, warm laundry room. The air smelled like clean soap bubbles. The floor was cool and smooth. Shirts and socks hung like little flags on a drying rack. The washing machine hummed, “whirr, whirr,” and the dryer rumbled, “rumble, rumble.”

Rocky Raccoon lived nearby. Rocky wore a tiny red scarf and loved music more than anything. He could tap a beat on pots. He could strum a rubber band like a guitar. And today, Rocky’s nose twitched with curiosity.

“What is that whirr?” Rocky asked. His ears perked up. His striped tail swished. “Is the laundry room playing a song?”

Rocky tiptoed inside. The washing machine had a round, shiny window. Bubbles swirled inside like a snow globe. Shirts danced in circles. “Oh!” said Rocky. “A spinning dance!”

He tapped the side of the machine. Ting-ting! It made a drum sound. He strummed a clothesline. Boing-boing! It made a string sound.

“Hello, laundry room!” Rocky sang. “I want to learn your song.”

He pressed his ear to the machine. The whirr tickled his cheek. “Whirr, whirr, swish,” he hummed back. He smiled. “I can make music from anything!” Rocky’s heart felt light and bouncy.

Then Rocky saw a big, shiny button that said START. His eyes grew wide. “What does this button do?” he wondered. His curiosity buzzed like a busy bee. He reached out a paw.

Suddenly, the dryer beeped. Beep-beep! The sound made Rocky jump. He bumped the basket of clean socks. Plop! Socks spilled everywhere—polka-dot socks, tiny socks, long stripy socks.

“Oh no,” Rocky whispered. His shoulders drooped. “I made a mess.” His tummy felt tight. He wanted to hide under the drying rack.

Rocky took a deep breath in, then out, like a slow flute. “I can fix it,” he told himself. “I can learn.” He remembered a little song he made for big feelings. He sang it softly:

“When things go wrong,
I breathe and wait.
I try again,
I can be great.”

He picked up one sock. It felt warm and fluffy. The warmth made his paws feel calm. He smiled a little. “Sock first, then song,” he said. He picked up another sock, and another, matching pairs like puzzle pieces.

As he worked, Rocky’s curiosity came back, gentle and kind. “Where do socks go?” he wondered. “Maybe not the dryer every time. Maybe some should hang to dry to save energy.” He looked at the tiny socks. “These can hang,” he decided. He clipped them onto the rack with little clothespins. Click, click. The clothespins looked like tiny smiles.

A friendly voice came from the doorway. It was Mia, the house helper. She wore soft slippers and had a basket on her hip. “Hello, Rocky,” she said kindly. “I see you found my singing machines.”

Rocky’s cheeks felt warm. “I spilled the socks,” he said. “But I’m helping. And I’m listening. The laundry room has music!”

Mia nodded. “It does! Machines sing when they work. Do you want to learn how to help them and help the Earth?”

Rocky’s tail flicked with joy. “Yes, please!”

“First,” said Mia, “we only wash when the basket is full. That saves water and energy.” She showed him the basket. “See? Full. Now it’s time.”

Rocky peered in. “Full,” he echoed. He liked that word. It felt round and complete.

“Second,” Mia said, “we use cold water when we can. Cold water is a gentle friend to clothes and to the planet.”

“Cold water,” Rocky sang. “Gentle friend.”

“Third,” said Mia, “we hang some clothes to dry. The sun and the air help us. It takes patience.”

“Patience,” Rocky whispered. He remembered his song. Patience was like breathing slow.

Mia smiled. “Would you like to make a laundry song to help us remember?”

Rocky’s eyes sparkled. “Yes!” He tapped a rhythm on the edge of the basket. Tum-tum, tum! He shook a box of clothespins, chika-chika! He hummed with the machine, whirr-swish, whirr-swish.

Then Rocky sang:

“Full basket, then we start,
Cold water, kind and smart.
Hang socks high, let breezes fly,
We help the Earth, you and I.”

Mia clapped. “Wonderful!” she said. “Now, press this button, the one that says ECO.”

Rocky read the letters slowly. “E-C-O.” He pressed it gently. The machine purred softer, “purrrrr.” It sounded like a sleepy cat.

They worked together. Rocky sorted the clothes by color—blues with blues, reds with reds. He matched socks with care. He hung tiny clothes on the rack. He watched the washer window and counted spins, “One, two, three,” like a drum beat. He breathed when the machine beeped, and he did not jump this time.

When they were done, the room felt tidy and bright. The air smelled like sunshine and soap. Rocky’s heart felt big and happy.

Mia handed Rocky a small wooden spoon. “A drumstick for your laundry song,” she said. “For a curious musician who cares.”

Rocky held the spoon and giggled. “Thank you! I learned that being curious helps me learn how things work. I learned to breathe when I feel mad or scared. And I learned that saving water and energy is a kind song for the Earth.”

He tapped a soft beat, tap-tap, and sang his chorus one more time. Mia joined in, and even the machines hummed along.

“Full basket, then we start,
Cold water, kind and smart.
Hang socks high, let breezes fly,
We help the Earth, you and I.”

Rocky twirled, tail swishing, scarf fluttering. The laundry room’s song was now his song. He smiled wide.

“I can be curious and careful,” he said. “I can make music, fix mistakes, and help the planet, one sock at a time.”

The End.
