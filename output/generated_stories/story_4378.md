# Kiki the Calm Koala Finds a Fair Way

In a sunny, comfortable nursery, soft rugs hugged the floor and little potted plants made the air smell fresh and green. Gentle music tinkled like tiny bells. Children stretched and swayed, and so did Kiki the calm koala. Kiki’s fur was fuzzy and gray, and her eyes were kind and curious. She loved quiet cuddles, soft pillows, and nice, slow stretches.

“Good morning, friends!” sang Miss Nia, the teacher, clapping softly. “Today we will share the big, bouncy mat!”

The big blue mat was thick and squishy. It felt like a springy cloud. Everyone wanted a turn to jump. The line grew long, longer, longest. Some children got many turns. Some got none.

Kiki watched. She waited. She held her paws together and breathed in and out, slow like the sway of the trees. But her tummy felt wiggly, like jelly.

“Is it my turn?” asked little Ben.

“Again, again!” shouted Lina, hopping on the mat for the third time. Her feet thumped—thump, thump—like little drums.

Kiki looked down at her paws. “I have not had a turn,” she whispered.

Miss Nia smiled, busy tying a shoelace. “We will all have a turn,” she said. But the line got jumbled. Some forgot. Some rushed.

Kiki felt a tiny squeeze in her chest. She liked to be calm, but she also liked things to be fair.

She took a deep, slow breath. In. Out. Calm and clear.

“Kiki, do you want a turn?” asked Jae, noticing her quiet face.

“Yes,” said Kiki in her soft voice. “I want a turn too.”

Lina bounced again. “I’m fast! I’m fast!” she chirped, hair flying.

Kiki stepped closer to Miss Nia. “Miss Nia,” she said, “I feel sad. It is not fair. Some friends had many turns. I had none. Can we make a fair plan?”

Miss Nia knelt so her eyes were level with Kiki’s. “Thank you for telling me, Kiki. That was brave. You used your voice.”

Kiki nodded. “I want to help,” she said. “We can share.”

“That’s a wonderful idea,” Miss Nia said. “How can we make it fair?”

Kiki looked around the cozy room. She saw the art shelf. She saw bright paper circles—red, yellow, blue, and green. She saw a quiet timer shaped like a sandcastle.

“I have an idea,” Kiki said, her eyes warm and bright. “We can make Turn Circles. One circle for each friend. We can put them in a line. The timer can go ‘shh’ with sand. When the sand is gone, it’s the next circle’s turn.”

“That’s innovation!” said Miss Nia. “That means a new way to solve a problem.”

Kiki’s ears perked up. She liked new ways.

They spread the circles on the rug. The paper felt smooth under Kiki’s paws. She wrote names with a chunky crayon, slow and careful: Kiki, Lina, Jae, Ben, Pia. The crayon made soft scratch-scratch sounds.

“Can I help?” asked Lina, bouncing less now.

“Yes,” said Kiki. “Helping is fair too.” She handed Lina the green circle.

Miss Nia set the sandcastle timer. The sand whispered down—shh, shh, shh.

“One turn each,” Kiki said kindly. “When the sand is done, we switch. We all move our bodies. We all wait. We all cheer.”

Jae hopped on the mat first. Boing! Boing! Everyone clapped.

The sand slipped away. “Switch!” Kiki sang.

Ben stepped on. Boing! He smiled big. “My turn!”

“Switch!” sang the class when the sand ran out.

Now it was Kiki’s turn. Her paws touched the cool, bouncy mat. She bent her knees and felt the soft whoosh of air. Boing! She jumped and giggled. The mat hugged her feet and pushed back like a friendly trampoline. She spread her arms wide. She felt brave and light.

“You did it, Kiki!” Miss Nia cheered. “How does it feel?”

“It feels fair,” said Kiki. “And fun!”

When the timer whispered again, Kiki stepped off and tapped the next Turn Circle. “Your turn, Lina,” she said.

Lina hopped up and smiled at Kiki. “Thank you for the plan,” Lina said. “I like the circles.”

Kiki’s heart felt warm and steady. She noticed something else. “Some friends want to move more,” she said, looking at Jae’s wiggly toes. “Maybe we can add Mini Moves. When we wait, we can do tiny jumps on our own circles.”

Miss Nia clapped softly. “Another smart idea! Movement helps our bodies and our feelings.”

They tried it. While one friend bounced on the big mat, everyone else did small, quiet Mini Moves on their own circles—little knee bends, tiny toe taps, gentle arm swings. The room was calm and busy at the same time. No one pushed. No one rushed. The air felt even sweeter, like warm cookies and sunshine.

At the end, Miss Nia rang a tinkly bell. “How did our plan go?”

Jae said, “It was fair.”

Lina said, “I got a turn and I waited too.”

Ben said, “I liked the Mini Moves!”

Kiki hugged her Turn Circle and smiled. “We listened. We used our voices. We shared. We moved. We made it fair.”

Miss Nia nodded. “Kiki, you showed self-advocacy by speaking up, and innovation by making Turn Circles and Mini Moves. You helped everyone.”

Kiki’s calm smile grew. “Fair is when everyone gets a turn and everyone feels okay,” she said.

Miss Nia placed a soft sticker on each Turn Circle—a tiny koala with a kind smile. “This reminds us: We can make fair plans together.”

Kiki took one more deep breath. In. Out. The room felt peaceful and bright. The big blue mat waited for another day of fair turns.

And the nursery learned something important: fairness is kind, fairness is smart, and fairness is a plan we can make together.

The End.
