# Pebble the Penguin Says “Please Stop”

On a peaceful front porch, Pebble the cheerful penguin sat in a little rocking chair. The porch smelled like warm biscuits and lemon tea. A soft breeze tickled Pebble’s feathers. The sun made tiny sparkles on a bowl of shiny pebbles by the door. Pebble loved those pebbles. They were smooth and cool. They clicked and clacked like tiny bells when Pebble touched them.

“Good morning, porch!” Pebble chirped. “I’m ready for a happy day.”

Pebble’s best friends waddled up the steps—Mimi the mouse, Benny the bunny, and Tiko the turtle. Their feet went tap-tap-tap on the wooden boards. The boards felt warm from the sun. Birds sang from a nearby tree, “Chee-chee-chee!”

“Let’s play Pebble Shop!” said Benny.

“Yes!” Pebble said. “I’ll be the shop helper. You can choose pebbles. We take turns and we use gentle hands.”

Mimi clapped. “Gentle hands! I like that.”

Pebble set three pebbles on a little mat: one blue like the sky, one gold like the sun, and one pink like cotton candy. The pebbles felt smooth and cool. Pebble’s heart felt happy and calm.

Mimi went first. “I would like the blue one, please.”

Pebble smiled. “Yes, Mimi. Thank you for asking kindly.”

Tiko went next, slow and careful. “I would like the gold one, please.”

Pebble nodded. “Yes, Tiko. That is kind and calm.”

Then Benny bounced. His big feet went boing-boing. He grabbed the pink pebble fast, and he bumped Pebble’s wing.

“Ouch,” said Pebble softly. Pebble felt a tight twist in the tummy. Pebble took a slow breath. In… and out. The porch breeze felt cool on Pebble’s cheeks.

Benny waved the pink pebble. “I got it! I got it!”

Pebble held up a flipper. “Benny, please stop.” Pebble used a strong, clear voice, not loud, not mean. “I don’t like it when you grab and bump me. That hurts. I want gentle hands. We take turns.”

Benny blinked. He looked at the pebble. He looked at Pebble’s wing. “Oh,” he said. His ears drooped. “I was too fast. I didn’t see. I’m sorry, Pebble.”

Pebble felt the tight twist untwist. “Thank you for your apology,” Pebble said kindly. “Let’s try again. You can ask. I will listen.”

Benny took a tiny step back. He held the pink pebble with two careful paws. “Pebble, may I have the pink pebble, please?”

“Yes,” said Pebble. “That is kind and calm. Thank you for asking.”

Benny smiled. The sun warmed their faces. The birds sang again. “Chee-chee-chee!”

They kept playing. Pebble added more pebbles—green like leaves, purple like grapes, and a silly speckled one like cookies and cream. The pebbles made soft clinks. The rocking chair creaked in a friendly way. Creak, creak.

Mimi tried the speckled pebble and rolled it across the mat. It wobbled and stopped by Tiko’s shell.

Tiko chuckled. “It tickled my shell!”

They all laughed. Pebble’s porch felt safe and happy again.

Soon, Benny started bouncing fast once more. Boing, boing. He bumped the bowl of pebbles. The pebbles tumbled. Clink-clink-clatter! One pebble rolled toward the edge of the porch.

Pebble stood up tall and steady. Pebble put both flippers on the belly and took a breath. In… and out. Then Pebble used the same strong, clear voice.

“Benny, please stop bouncing near the bowl,” Pebble said. “I feel worried it will fall. I want you to bounce on the grass instead.”

Benny froze. He looked at the edge, then at Pebble. “Oh! I didn’t think about the bowl,” he said. “I’m sorry again. I will bounce on the grass.”

Pebble nodded. “Thank you for listening.”

Benny hopped down to the soft green grass. Boing-boing… boing. Mimi and Tiko helped Pebble pick up the pebbles. The porch boards felt smooth under their paws and flippers. Pebble’s heart felt light.

When all the pebbles were safe, Pebble poured lemonade for everyone. The cups were cool and sweet, with tiny lemon smells that made their noses wiggle.

“To gentle hands,” said Pebble.

“To kind words,” said Tiko.

“To listening,” said Mimi.

“And to saying, ‘Please stop,’ when we need to,” said Benny. “I will practice.”

Pebble smiled big. “That is being assertive,” Pebble said. “Assertive means I can use my voice to say what I need. I can be clear and kind at the same time.”

Benny sipped his lemonade. “Clear and kind,” he repeated.

Mimi wiped her whiskers. “When you told Benny to stop, you were not mean,” she said. “You were calm. I liked that.”

Tiko nodded slowly. “And Benny said sorry. That was kind too.”

Pebble felt proud and cozy. The porch breeze hummed. The rocking chair went creak, creak. The sun glowed soft and gold on the bowl of pebbles.

They played one more round of Pebble Shop. Everyone asked. Everyone waited. Everyone used gentle hands. When a pebble rolled near the edge, Benny reached out carefully and saved it. “Got it!” he said. He looked at Pebble. “Is this okay?”

Pebble gave a thumbs-up with a flipper. “Yes. Thank you for checking.”

At the end of the day, Pebble stacked the pebbles back into the bowl. Click, click, click. Pebble’s friends gave warm, soft hugs. The porch felt peaceful again.

Pebble waved. “Today I learned this: I can speak up with a clear, kind voice. I can say, ‘Please stop. I don’t like that. I want…’ And I can listen when friends talk to me.”

Benny waved back. “And I can say sorry and try again.”

Mimi and Tiko chimed, “Kindness makes the porch feel safe.”

Pebble rocked once more in the little chair and smiled at the sunset. The sky turned pink like cotton candy, just like the pebble. Pebble’s heart felt warm and proud.

The End.
