# Tilly Turtle’s Soft Song

Tilly was a shy turtle who loved her cozy bedroom. It smelled like fresh laundry and warm tea, and the lamp made a golden puddle of light. Her bed was a soft shell-shaped nest with a quilt that felt like a hug. On her shelf sat a tiny blue ukulele. Sometimes, when the house was quiet, Tilly picked it up and strummed, ting-ting-ting, very softly.

One rainy afternoon, the gentle patter on the window sounded like a heartbeat. Tap, tap. Tap, tap. Tilly’s tummy fluttered. “I want to play my song,” she whispered, “but I’m scared.” Her cheeks felt warm. Her shell felt extra heavy.

Mama Turtle knocked gently. Knock-knock. “May I come in?”

“Yes,” Tilly said in a small voice.

Mama stepped into the cozy bedroom and sat on the rug. “I heard your pretty ting-ting. What’s on your mind, little shell?”

Tilly hugged her ukulele. “I made a song,” she said. “But… what if it sounds silly? What if I play the wrong note? My heart feels wiggly.”

Mama nodded. “Thank you for telling me your feelings,” she said. “That is brave. Let’s name them. Is it worry?”

Tilly blinked. “Yes. It’s worry. And a little shy.”

Mama smiled. “Worry says, ‘I don’t want to make a mistake.’ But practice says, ‘I can try and try.’ Let’s help your body feel calm. Smell the flower… blow the candle.”

Tilly closed her eyes. She breathed in slow, smelling the flower. She breathed out gentle, blowing the candle. In… and out. In… and out. Her tummy felt softer. The rain kept a steady rhythm on the window. Tap, tap. Tap, tap.

“I want to practice,” Tilly said. She plucked a string. Ting. She tried again. Ting-ting. Her fingers felt less sticky. “Would you like to listen?”

“Yes,” said Mama. “I will be your quiet audience with big ears and a kind heart.”

Tilly began to sing in a whisper voice. “Hello, little day, it’s rainy but okay…” Her song was soft as a blanket. The ukulele made sweet sounds like sugar sprinkles. She missed a note and frowned. “Oops.”

Mama touched her shoulder. “That was a brave ‘oops,’” she said. “Every note teaches us. Try again.”

Tilly tried again. Ting-ting-ting. The song grew a little louder, like a flower opening.

Just then, her little brother Toby poked his head in. “Whatcha doing?” His voice was bouncy, and his feet were stompy on the rug.

Tilly’s shell pulled in a bit. “I’m playing,” she whispered.

Toby hopped closer. “Play louder! Play fast!” He clapped off-beat. Clap! Clap-clap!

Tilly’s face scrunched. Her cheeks buzzed. “I don’t like loud right now,” she said, but her voice was teeny tiny.

Mama raised a hand. “Pause,” she said gently. “Let’s use our feeling words. Tilly, what are you feeling?”

Tilly put her hand on her chest. “I feel overwhelmed,” she said slowly. The word felt big, but it fit. “I need quiet to practice.”

Mama nodded. “Toby, what are you feeling?”

“I feel excited!” Toby said. “Music makes me jumpy!”

Mama smiled. “We have two feelings. Excited and overwhelmed. We can solve this together. That is called conflict resolution.”

Tilly looked at Toby. “I want to share my song,” she said, a little louder. “But I need quiet. Can we make a plan?”

Toby’s eyes sparkled. “A plan! Okay!”

Mama suggested, “How about Tilly plays her song softly two times. Toby listens with quiet hands in his lap. Then Toby can have a turn to clap gently, and Tilly can play a fun fast part.”

Tilly nodded. “I like that.”

Toby sat crisscross on the rug. He placed his hands on his knees. “Quiet hands,” he whispered.

Tilly took a deep breath. Smell the flower. Blow the candle. Then she played. Ting-ting-ting. “Hello, little day…” The rain tapped along like tiny drums. Her voice filled the cozy bedroom, soft and sweet. She finished, and her heart felt warm and proud.

Toby whispered, “Again?” Tilly played one more time, still soft and strong. When she was done, she smiled. “Now your turn,” she said.

Toby clapped gently. Clap… clap. “Can you do a fast part?” he asked.

Tilly giggled. “I can try.” She strummed quicker. Ting-ting-ting-ting! Toby swayed instead of stomping. Mama hummed a low, happy note. Mmmm.

When the song ended, the room felt like a cookie just out of the oven—warm and safe.

“Tilly,” Mama said, “how does your body feel now?”

Tilly put a hand on her tummy. “Calm and proud,” she said. “My worry is smaller. Practice helped. Sharing helped too.”

Toby leaned in. “Your song was not silly. It was cozy.”

“Thank you,” Tilly said. “When I feel worry next time, I can name it, breathe, and ask for what I need.”

Mama nodded. “That’s emotional awareness. You listened to your feelings and used words. You made a plan with Toby. You practiced, and your music grew.”

The rain slowed to a soft hush. Tilly set her ukulele on the shelf. She gave Toby a gentle high-five. “We made music together.”

Toby grinned. “Team music!”

Tilly curled on her shell-shaped bed, the quilt hugging her. Mama tucked them in for a quiet rest. The cozy bedroom glowed like a golden shell. Tilly whispered, “Hello, little day… it’s rainy but okay,” and felt the happy beat of her brave, steady heart.

The End.
