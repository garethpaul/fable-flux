# Kiki Koala’s Kind Kitchen Art

The warm kitchen smelled like cinnamon and toast. Sunlight made soft squares on the floor. Kiki the calm koala sat at the round table, her fuzzy ears perked up. The kettle sang a tiny song. The oven hummed. Pots and pans hung like shiny moons.

Kiki held a crayon and a big sheet of paper. “Today I want to make kitchen art,” she whispered. “I want to help decorate for the snack share at the community center.” Her tummy fluttered. “I’ll volunteer my art. I can be brave.”

Mama Koala poured warm tea that smelled like honey. “What is your goal, Kiki?” Mama asked kindly.

Kiki tapped her chin. “Goal: make one big poster with happy food faces,” she said. She drew a small circle with a smile. “But… what if no one likes it?” Her voice was a tiny squeak.

Mama touched Kiki’s paw. “Your art is your heart. Be brave. Try. You can do it.”

Kiki took a slow breath. In and out. She looked around the warm kitchen. She saw a shiny red apple in a bowl. She saw a soft loaf of bread on a wooden board. She smelled sweet banana and toasty oats. The refrigerator hummed like a quiet bee.

“I will draw what I see,” Kiki said. “I will use my own style.”

She began. “Hello, Mr. Apple,” she giggled, drawing a circle with rosy cheeks. “Hello, Miss Banana,” she sang, adding a long yellow curve with a wink. “Bread Bear!” She drew a fluffy brown loaf with tiny paws.

Her crayons made gentle scratches on the paper. Swish, swish. Color slipped across the page like warm butter. Kiki’s paws moved steady and calm.

Suddenly, Kiki frowned. The crayon snapped. “Oh no!” She held the little broken piece. “My line looks wiggly.”

Mama Koala smiled. “Sometimes wiggly is wonderful,” she said. “What can you do?”

Kiki thought. “I can turn the wiggle into a ribbon,” she said. She drew a pink bow on Bread Bear. “Ta-da!” She laughed. “I solved a problem.”

Kiki kept working. She added tiny hearts and stars. She pressed her nose close and smelled wax and paper. She tasted the air—sweet like cinnamon tea. She heard the clock go tick-tock, tick-tock, like a gentle drum.

The front door knocked. Knock, knock.

Papa Koala peeked in with a basket. “The community center needs signs,” he said. “They need helpers. Volunteers set up snacks in one hour.”

Kiki froze. “One hour?” Her ears tingled. “My poster is not done.”

Mama knelt beside her. “Let’s use a plan,” she said. “Step one: finish the faces. Step two: write kind words. Step three: pack and go help.”

Kiki nodded. “Goal set,” she said. “I can be brave.”

She drew faster, but not too fast. Smooth and steady. She gave Banana a tiny blue hat. She gave Apple star eyes. She wrote big, friendly words: “Welcome, Friends!”

Her paw shook. The letters looked bumpy. Kiki felt a lump in her throat. “What if someone laughs?” she whispered.

Mama held her gently. “Brave means trying, even when you feel a little scared,” Mama said. “And you are helping. That is kind.”

Kiki placed a bright green crayon on the table. “I will sign my name,” she said softly. She wrote K-i-k-i in big wobbly letters. Then she drew a small koala paw print. “It is mine,” she said with a sleepy smile.

At the community center, the kitchen smelled like warm muffins and orange slices. People talked and giggled. Cups clinked. Kiki hugged her poster to her chest. Her heart went thump-thump, like a soft drum in her fluffy fur.

A little duckling waddled over. “I like your banana hat,” the duckling said, pointing with a tiny wing. “It makes me giggle.”

Kiki’s eyes sparkled. “Thank you,” she said. “I made it to help.”

“Will you hang it here?” asked Mr. Bear, the center helper, with a gentle growl. “We need a sign by the snack table.”

Kiki stood on a small step stool. She taped the poster to the wall. Rip. Press. Rip. Press. The paper crinkled a little. The colors shone bright in the warm light. The happy food faces smiled at everyone.

“Welcome, Friends!” read the children. They clapped. “We love it!”

Kiki’s shoulders relaxed. Her tummy felt warm, like toast. “I was brave,” she said. “I shared my art. I volunteered to help. I met my goal.”

Mr. Bear handed her a tiny sticker shaped like a star. “Thank you for your creative heart,” he said.

On the way home, Kiki held Mama’s paw. “I learned something,” Kiki said. “My art is my way to speak. I can be brave and share. I can help others with my ideas.”

Mama smiled. “What is your next goal?” she asked.

Kiki looked up at the soft clouds. “Tomorrow I will make a tiny cookbook with silly food faces,” she said. “I will show it to the class. I will keep creating and keep helping.”

Mama squeezed her paw. “That sounds wonderful.”

Kiki took a deep, happy breath. The world smelled like oranges and sunshine. She felt calm and proud.

The End.
