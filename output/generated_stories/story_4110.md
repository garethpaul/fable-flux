# Fawn's Quiet Corner

Little Fawn loved helping others. Her soft brown spots sparkled when she smiled, and her tiny hooves made gentle tap-tap sounds wherever she went. Today, she was volunteering in the community center's busy basement, where many families came for help.

The basement was very, very busy! *CRASH!* went the boxes. *RING-RING!* went the phones. *STOMP-STOMP!* went people's feet on the stairs. Poor Fawn's ears twitched with all the loud noises.

"Fawn, can you help sort these toys?" called Mrs. Robin, the volunteer leader. Her voice was kind but rushed.

"Yes!" said Fawn, wanting to help. But when she tried to pick up a toy truck, someone bumped into her. When she reached for a doll, another volunteer took it first.

Fawn felt dizzy. Her heart beat fast like a little drum. The noise made her head hurt, and she couldn't think clearly. She wanted to help, but everything felt too much!

"I... I..." Fawn started to say something, but her voice was too quiet. Nobody heard her over all the noise.

Fawn remembered what her mama had taught her about feeling overwhelmed. "When things get too busy, little one, find a quiet space and breathe like the trees do - slow and steady."

Fawn looked around the busy basement. In the corner, she spotted a small, empty space behind some soft cushions. She walked over on her gentle hooves and sat down.

"I need a quiet moment," Fawn whispered to herself. She closed her eyes and took a deep breath through her little black nose. *In... and out...* The air smelled like lavender from the donated blankets nearby.

As Fawn breathed slowly, the noises seemed less scary. She could still hear them, but they didn't hurt her ears as much. Her heart stopped racing like a rabbit.

After a few calm breaths, Fawn opened her eyes. She felt better! Now she could think clearly about how to help.

Fawn stood up tall on her hooves and walked back to Mrs. Robin. This time, she spoke up clearly: "Mrs. Robin, may I please have a special job? The noise makes it hard for me to help with the toys."

Mrs. Robin smiled warmly. "Of course, dear Fawn! What would help you feel better?"

"Could I make welcome cards for the families?" asked Fawn. "I could draw pretty pictures in my quiet corner, and it would still help everyone!"

"What a wonderful idea!" said Mrs. Robin. "That's very thoughtful."

Fawn set up her quiet corner with crayons and paper. She drew beautiful pictures of flowers, rainbows, and smiling suns. Each card said "Welcome!" in her careful writing.

Other volunteers noticed Fawn's peaceful corner. When they felt overwhelmed, they would take deep breaths near her quiet space too.

"Thank you, Fawn," said Mrs. Robin at the end of the day. "Your cards made families feel so welcome, and your quiet corner helped everyone remember to breathe calmly."

Fawn's spots seemed to sparkle even brighter. She had learned that helping others was important, but taking care of herself was important too. When she spoke up about what she needed, she could help even better!

From that day on, Fawn always remembered: take deep breaths, find quiet moments, and don't be afraid to ask for what you need.

The End.
