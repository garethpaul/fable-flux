# Hop, Stop, and Brush!

Benny was a determined rabbit with soft gray fur and bright, bouncy ears. He loved to hop. He hopped in the yard. He hopped in the kitchen. He even liked to hop in the hall. Today, Benny hopped into the bathroom.

Drip, drip, drip went the sink. Swish, swish went the shower curtain. The room smelled like mint soap and warm towels. Benny took a big breath. “I can brush my teeth all by myself,” he said. “I am a big bunny. I can do it!”

Mama Rabbit peeked in. “Remember, Benny,” she said kindly, “bathrooms can be slippery. Use slow, careful hops. And we share the space with others.”

“I will, Mama,” Benny replied. His whiskers twitched. He was excited.

Benny set his toothbrush by the sink. He looked at the step stool. It was a little wobbly. He wanted to hop right up fast.

“I can do it!” Benny whispered. He bent his knees. He wiggled his toes. Hop!

The stool wobbled. Benny wobbled too. His paws slipped a tiny bit. “Whoa!” he said, feeling a flutter in his tummy.

From the tub came a soft voice. It was his little sister, Bea, playing with bath bubbles. “Hi, Benny! Splash, splash!” she said. Water plink-plinked onto the floor.

Benny looked at the shiny tiles. They looked smooth and wet. His ears drooped a little. “I want to move fast,” he said, “but I also want to be safe.”

Mama Rabbit nodded. “Try slow hops. Try strong paws. Try eyes watching where you go. Use your body and your brain together. That is called coordination.”

“Co-or-di-na-tion,” Benny repeated, smiling. He liked new words. He took a slow breath in. He placed one paw, then the other, on the stool. He pressed down to feel if it was steady. “Steady,” he said softly.

Step. Step. He moved up, not with a wild hop, but with a careful climb. His body felt different—strong and steady. He reached the sink. “I did it!” he cheered.

Bea clapped. “Good job, Benny! Splash clap!”

Benny turned on the water. Whoosh! Cool water ran over his paws. He squeezed a tiny bit of minty toothpaste. “Just a pea,” he said, remembering Mama’s words.

He started to brush. “Top teeth, bottom teeth. Little circles,” Mama sang. Benny tried to move his paw in small circles. It tickled his gums. “Hee hee!” he giggled. The mint smelled fresh. Bubbles foamed like little clouds.

Then, a bubble popped near his nose. Pop! Benny laughed and wiggled. He wanted to dance. He lifted one foot to hop. The stool wiggled again.

“Uh-oh,” said Benny. “I want to move, but I don’t want to fall.”

Mama pointed to the floor. “Look, Benny. There’s water near your feet. Let’s pause and wipe.”

Benny nodded. He set the toothbrush down. He bent his knees low and reached for a towel. He moved slowly, one paw at a time. Swipe, swipe. He dried the wet spot. The floor felt less slippery. “Better,” he said, feeling proud.

Bea looked up with big eyes. “Benny, can I have some sink water for my toy boat?”

Benny saw his sister’s boat. He wanted to help, but he was brushing. He remembered Mama’s words: we share the space with others. “Yes, Bea,” he said kindly. “I will move over a little. Then you can have a turn.”

He scooted his stool to the side, tiny bit by tiny bit. Scoot, pause. Scoot, pause. He watched his paws and watched the room. He made space for Bea. She smiled and filled her little cup. “Thank you, Benny!”

“You’re welcome,” Benny said. He felt warm and happy inside. Sharing felt good.

Benny picked up his brush again. He counted slowly. “One… two… three…” He brushed in little circles, front teeth and back teeth. He moved his arm like a small windmill—round and round. He stood tall and steady, feet apart like a tripod. He could feel his body working together: eyes looking, paws holding, legs balancing, tummy strong. “This is coordination!” he said proudly.

When he finished, he took a sip of water and swished. Swish, swish. He spit into the sink. “All done!”

Mama smiled. “You used careful movements. You respected Bea’s turn. You kept your body safe.”

Benny’s ears popped up. “I learned hop, stop, and brush!” he said. “First I wanted to hop fast. Then I stopped and looked. I moved slow and strong. I shared the space. I brushed with little circles.”

Bea waved her boat. “Captain Benny keeps the bathroom safe!”

Benny laughed. He hopped once, just a tiny hop. “Tiny hop for happy,” he said. Then he placed both feet down again.

He helped Mama wipe one more splash. He put the toothbrush away. He gave Bea a gentle high-paw. Tap!

Benny looked at his shiny smile in the mirror. “I can move my body in smart ways,” he said. “I can be careful and kind.”

Mama gave him a soft towel hug. “Yes, you can.”

Benny felt proud, clean, and calm. The bathroom sounded quiet now—just a soft drip and a cozy rustle of towels. He took one last deep breath of mint. “Hop, stop, and brush,” he whispered, “and share.”

The End.
