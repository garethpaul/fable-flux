# Ellie and the Fair Fix

Ellie was a gentle elephant calf with soft gray skin and a happy swish-swish tail. One sunny morning, Ellie trotted to the garden shed. The shed smelled like fresh soil and lemons. A little window let in warm light. Tools hung on hooks—rakes, trowels, and a tiny watering can. Ellie loved the shed. It felt cozy and safe.

Outside, birds chirped, “Cheep, cheep!” Inside, Ellie heard, “Oh no!” It was Pip the chipmunk. Pip’s whiskers trembled. His cheeks were puffed with worry.

“What’s wrong?” Ellie asked in her soft, rumbly voice.

Pip pointed to a muddy spot. “The watering can is gone! I need it to water the tomato plants.”

Ellie blinked. “Gone?”

Pip nodded fast. “Someone took it. That’s not fair.”

Ellie’s big ears flapped. “We will find out what happened,” she said. “We will be fair. We will be just.”

“Just?” Pip asked.

“Just means we listen to everyone,” Ellie said. “We tell the truth. We make things right.”

They looked around. Ellie’s trunk sniffed. She smelled damp earth, sweet lemons, and… carrots? She saw little muddy footprints on the floor. They led to the door.

“Let’s follow the prints,” Ellie said.

They stepped outside. Sunlight warmed their backs. The garden was bright with marigolds and green beans. Bees buzzed like tiny humming fans.

The footprints led to the carrot patch. Benny the bunny sat there, ears up, nose twitching. Next to him sat the missing watering can!

“Benny!” Pip squeaked. “You took our can!”

Benny’s eyes went wide. “I… I borrowed it,” he said in a small voice. “I didn’t ask. I was in a hurry. My carrot sprouts were thirsty.”

Pip crossed his arms. “That’s not fair. I needed it, too.”

Ellie took a deep breath. She wanted to be calm and kind. “Let’s use good words,” Ellie said. “Let’s listen. Then we will make it right.”

She pointed gently at her ear. “Listening is part of justice.”

Benny swallowed. “I was wrong not to ask,” he said. “I felt rushed. I thought, ‘I will bring it right back.’ But I forgot.”

Pip looked at the tomatoes. Their leaves drooped a little. He sighed. “I felt sad,” he said. “I thought someone didn’t care.”

Ellie nodded. “Thank you for telling your feelings. Now, we find a fair way to fix this.”

Ellie loved words. She loved how words could help. She said, “Let’s say our feelings and our needs.”

Pip said, “I feel sad. I need to water my tomatoes.”

Benny said, “I feel sorry. I need help asking next time.”

Ellie smiled. “Good words. Kind words. Clear words.”

She looked at the sky. A cloud drifted by like cotton. She had an idea. “We can share and take turns,” she said. “We can make a plan.”

“Plan?” Benny asked.

“A plan is a promise with steps,” Ellie said. “Here is the plan: First, we water the tomatoes with the can. Next, Benny returns the can. Then we water the carrots together. Last, we hang the can back in the shed.”

Pip and Benny nodded. “Yes,” they said.

They walked to the tomatoes. Ellie held the can with her trunk. Water splashed and sparkled, splish-splash, onto the plants. The leaves perked up. Pip smiled.

“Now my turn,” Benny said. He carried the can carefully. Ellie and Pip walked with him to the carrot patch. They watered together, swoosh-swoosh. The soil smelled rich and cool.

“Thank you,” Benny said. “I will ask next time.”

Ellie said, “Let’s practice asking.” She stood tall and friendly. “Benny, say, ‘May I borrow the watering can, please?’”

Benny said, “May I borrow the watering can, please?”

Pip answered, “Yes, and please bring it back right away.”

Benny said, “I will. Thank you.”

Ellie clapped her big feet softly. “That is the language of fairness,” she said. “When we use kind words, we make justice.”

They walked back to the garden shed together. The shed felt cool and lemony again. Ellie wiped the can with a soft cloth. She hung it on its hook. Click!

Pip took a small marker and drew a sign. “Watering Can: Please Ask.” He drew a happy face, too.

Ellie read the sign aloud, slowly and clearly. “Wat-er-ing Can: Please Ask.” She smiled. “Words can guide us.”

Benny looked at the sign. “I can read ‘Please,’” he said, proud. “Please is a good word.”

“It is,” Ellie said. “Please and thank you help us be fair.”

Outside, the birds sang a bright song. The bees hummed. The tomato leaves shone. The carrot tops swayed. The garden felt calm and right.

Pip turned to Ellie. “Thank you for helping,” he said.

Ellie’s tail swished. “Justice is for everyone,” she said. “We listen. We tell the truth. We make it right.”

Benny nodded. “And we ask before we borrow.”

Ellie giggled. “And we enjoy the garden together!”

They stepped into the sunshine. They watered the marigolds, too, just for fun. Splish, splash, smile!

The End.
