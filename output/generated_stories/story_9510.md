# Dipper the Dolphin in the Garden Shed

The afternoon sun made the garden shed smell like warm wood and sweet soil. Dust danced in the light. Soft straw poked from a broom. On a shelf sat paint cans, rubber boots, and a shiny blue hose.

Splash! A playful dolphin named Dipper peeked in through the open door. He lived in the big pond behind the garden. He loved to play, to wiggle, and to squeak, “Eee-eee!” Dipper’s skin was smooth and cool, like wet pebbles. He flicked his tail and waved a fin. “Hello, Shed!” he sang. “Can I play here?”

Inside the shed, Mia the Mouse popped out from a flowerpot. She wore a tiny purple scarf. Gus the Garden Gnome stood by the rake. His red hat leaned to one side. A sleepy Tortoise named Tulla rested on a mat, eyes half closed.

“Hi, Dipper!” squeaked Mia. “We were about to do some stretches. It’s our exercise time.”

“I love to move!” Dipper chirped. “Let’s play ocean games!”

Mia clapped her little paws. “We can imagine the shed is a big blue sea!”

Gus smiled. “I can be a sea captain,” he said in a low, rumbly voice.

Tulla blinked slowly. “I can be a cozy rock,” she whispered.

Dipper splashed his tail in a bucket. Water sprinkled the air. “Eee-eee! The sea is ready!” he said. He swam—well, he wiggled—across the floor, making swishy sounds. He bumped the broom. It rattled. He pushed the hose. It curled like a sleepy snake.

“Careful, Dipper,” Mia said kindly. “The shed is small.”

“I can be small,” Dipper promised. He tried to wiggle quietly. His fin brushed the paint cans. Clank! Clank!

Gus held up his tiny hand. “Captain’s rule,” he said. “We move with gentle bodies and kind hearts.”

Dipper took a deep breath. He smelled dirt, paint, and a little lemon from a soap bar. He moved softer. He listened to the creak of the floor and the tick of a garden timer.

“Let’s use our imagination,” Mia said. “I see waves.” She swayed side to side.

“I see clouds,” Gus added. He pretended to steer a ship. “We sail slow and steady.”

Tulla peeked out. “I feel the sun,” she murmured, pressing her shell to a warm patch of light.

Dipper closed his eyes. He pictured fish with silver scales. He pictured treasure, shells, and sea grass. The shed became a sea in his mind. He wiggled and hummed a whale song. “Mmm-oooo.”

Mia sniffed. “I smell sea air!” she giggled. “And carrots.”

Gus pointed at the hose. “Sea snake ahead! We step and stretch.”

They stretched long like tall seaweed. They curled small like tiny clams. They hopped like happy crabs. Dipper made bubbly sounds and puffed his cheeks. Everyone laughed.

“Tag!” Dipper tapped Mia gently with his fin tip.

Mia froze. Her whiskers twitched. “Dipper, please ask before tagging,” she said softly. “I like to be asked. That is my body.”

Dipper blinked. He felt a little wiggle of worry in his tummy. “I’m sorry,” he said at once. “May I tag you in our game?”

Mia smiled. “Yes, thank you for asking.”

Gus nodded. “Good manners, good fun.”

They played again. Before tagging, Dipper asked, “May I?” He waited. Sometimes Mia said yes. Sometimes she said, “Not now.” Dipper learned to stop when someone said no. He discovered he could tap the broom instead. He could tag the hose. He could even tag the sunlight with a fin shadow. That felt silly and good.

Soon the shed felt crowded again. Dipper’s tail wanted a big swish. He looked at Tulla. She was very close. “I don’t want to bump,” he said. “But I want to move.”

Mia had an idea. “Imagination time! Let’s make lanes,” she said. “Like a pool!”

Gus moved the boots into a neat line. “Captain Gus will draw sea lanes,” he declared, marking paths with chalk bits and old string.

Tulla lifted her head. “I need a quiet corner,” she said. “Loud splashes make me tired.”

Dipper nodded. “We can help.” He and Mia made a cozy, soft spot with a towel and a small hat for shade. “Is this okay?” Dipper asked.

Tulla smiled. “Yes. Thank you for respecting my space.”

They tried the lanes. Dipper slid along one string path, small and smooth. Mia scurried in another lane. Gus marched in his. They counted steps. One-two, swish-swish. They used big arms, then tiny arms. They moved fast, then slow. They took deep belly breaths. In-breathe. Out-breathe.

“Wow,” Dipper said, feeling proud and calm. “We can all move, and we can all be safe.”

He looked at his friends. “I like being different,” he said. “I am splashy. Mia is speedy. Gus is steady. Tulla is cozy.”

“And we all belong,” Mia added.

The sun went lower. The shed turned golden. It was time to rest. Dipper whispered, “Thank you for playing with me.”

“Thank you for asking first,” Mia replied.

“Thank you for making lanes,” said Gus.

“Thank you for my quiet corner,” Tulla murmured.

Dipper felt warm in his heart. He learned that imagination can make small places big and make new rules that help everyone. He learned to ask before touching and to listen when friends say yes or no. He learned that being different is something to celebrate, and moving our bodies can be fun and kind.

He gave a small splash in the bucket—plip!—and grinned. “Eee-eee! Today was a good day.”

The End.
