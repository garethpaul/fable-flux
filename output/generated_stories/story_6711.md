# Roo's Gentle Hops

In a sunny yard by a little house, there was a garage workshop that smelled like pine wood and lemon soap. The door was open. Sunlight made warm rectangles on the floor. Tools hung on the wall like shiny silver fish. A fan hummed softly—whir, whir, whir.

A joyful kangaroo joey named Roo peeked in. Roo’s ears twitched. His tail swished. “Boing-boing!” he giggled. “This looks fun!”

Inside, Grandpa Gus wore blue overalls and a smile. He was sanding a small wooden box. Scratch-scratch, smooth-smooth. On the workbench, there were markers, paper, and a jar of buttons. A bright green helmet and a tiny pair of safety goggles sat nearby.

“Hi, Roo,” said Grandpa Gus. “We will build and write today. We will make a sign for the garden trail!”

Roo clapped his little paws. “A sign! I love outside. I love hopping on trails!”

Grandpa Gus nodded. “Yes. But first, we use calm bodies in the workshop. We look with our eyes. We touch with gentle hands. We listen with our ears.”

Roo bounced higher. “I can hop and hop!” he sang.

“Slow, Roo,” said Grandpa Gus kindly. “Let’s put on safety gear.”

Roo slipped on the tiny goggles. They felt cool on his nose. “I look like a space roo!” he squeaked. He hopped again—boing! His tail bumped a stack of soft wood pieces. Clatter! The pieces slid and made a loud sound.

Roo froze. His cheeks felt hot. His tummy fluttered. “Oh,” he whispered. “I didn’t mean to.”

Grandpa Gus took a slow breath. “It’s okay. The wood is fine. Let’s practice our pause.”

“Our pause?” Roo asked.

“Yes,” said Grandpa Gus. “When our bodies feel bouncy-bouncy, we stop. We put a paw on our tummy. We breathe in, like smelling a sweet flower. Sniff. Then we breathe out, like blowing a gentle bubble. Whooo.”

Roo put a paw on his warm belly. He smelled an imaginary flower. Sniff. It smelled like strawberries in his mind. He blew out like a bubble. Whooo. The fan’s whir sounded quieter now.

“How do you feel?” asked Grandpa Gus.

“Soft,” said Roo. “Like a pillow.”

“Good,” said Grandpa Gus. “That is self-regulation. That means you can help your body and feelings be calm.”

Roo nodded. “I can help my body.”

They picked up the wood together. Tap-tap, stack-stack. Then they looked at the markers and paper.

“We will write the words for the sign first,” said Grandpa Gus. “Words help people know where to go.”

Roo loved words. He picked a purple marker. It smelled like grapes. “What should it say?” Roo asked.

Grandpa Gus said, “It could say, ‘Trail This Way.’ And ‘Please Walk Kindly.’ That shows respect for the trail and for others.”

Roo smiled. “I like kind walking.”

He started to write. T… R… The letters were wiggly like worms. “Oops,” said Roo. “My paw is too jumpy.”

“Let’s try our pause again,” said Grandpa Gus. “Smell the flower. Blow the bubble.”

Roo breathed. Sniff. Whooo. His paw slowed down. The marker felt smooth, sliding over the paper. He whispered the letters as he wrote. “T-R-A-I-L. Trail this way.” He drew an arrow. He added a little sun.

Grandpa Gus clapped softly. “Nice work, Roo. You used your calm.”

Roo felt proud. His chest felt warm like toast. “Can we add ‘Please Respect Nature’?” he asked.

“Yes!” said Grandpa Gus. “Respect means we care. We stay on the path. We are gentle with plants and bugs.”

Roo wrote slowly: Please Respect Nature. He drew a tiny leaf and a ladybug with a smile.

“Now we glue the paper to the wood,” said Grandpa Gus. “And we will add a clear coat to keep it safe.”

Roo picked up the glue. He squeezed too hard. Squish! Glue blorped out in a big lump. It dripped onto the bench. Sticky, cold, and gooey.

Roo’s eyes went wide. “Oh no! I messed up again.”

Grandpa Gus opened a cloth. “Pause,” he reminded. “We clean, we breathe, and we try again.”

Roo placed his paw on his tummy. Sniff. Whooo. He felt the cool glue on his paw. He felt the breeze from the fan on his ears. The hum was like a song. He dabbed the glue gently this time—dot, dot, dot. They smoothed the paper—pat, pat, pat. No bumps. Just neat and snug.

“You did it,” said Grandpa Gus.

Roo grinned. “I did it with my breath.”

When the sign was ready, Roo and Grandpa Gus went outside together. The yard smelled like cut grass and sunshine. Birds chirped—cheep-cheep! A butterfly floated by. They walked to the start of the garden trail. Leaves whispered in the wind—shhh-shhh.

Roo held the sign while Grandpa Gus tapped it into the ground. Tap-tap-tap. Roo read the words. “‘Trail This Way. Please Walk Kindly. Please Respect Nature.’”

A family came by—a duck, her ducklings, and their person friend with a hat. “What a nice sign,” the friend said. “We will walk kindly.”

Roo felt his heart hop. “Thank you,” he said. “I used my calm to write it.”

The ducklings lined up and waddled slowly on the path. Roo wanted to hop fast, but he remembered. He put a paw on his tummy. Sniff. Whooo. He hopped gently beside them. Boing… pause… boing.

Grandpa Gus smiled. “You used self-regulation outside too.”

Roo beamed. “When my body goes big-bounce, I can pause. I can breathe. I can choose gentle.”

“And that,” said Grandpa Gus, “shows respect—for the workshop, for the trail, and for friends.”

Roo looked at the sign, bright in the sun. He felt proud and calm and happy. “I can help my body and my choices,” he said. “I can be a gentle hopper.”

They took one more slow breath together. Sniff. Whooo. Then they followed the trail, listening to birds, feeling the warm sun, and reading their sign as they passed.

The End.
