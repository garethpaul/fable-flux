# Benny Beaver’s Calm-and-Create Plan

Benny was a busy beaver with bright teeth and a bouncy tail. Today, he padded into the creative art room. The room smelled like crayons and school glue. Sunlight sprinkled across jars of colorful buttons. Paints gleamed like rainbows. Paper rustled like tiny waves.

Benny’s eyes grew wide. “So many things! I want to do it all!” he squeaked.

He grabbed a paintbrush, then a glue stick, then a handful of buttons. Plop, plop! Buttons rolled off the table. Splot! Red paint dripped on his paw. The glue cap went missing.

Benny’s heart thumped fast. “Too much,” he whispered. His tail twitched. He wanted to rush. He wanted to do everything at once.

Ms. Maple, the gentle teacher, knelt beside him. “Hello, Benny. I see big ideas in your bright eyes,” she said softly. “Let’s make a calm-and-create plan.”

“A plan?” Benny blinked.

“A simple plan helps busy bodies slow down,” Ms. Maple said. “When we plan, we feel steady and strong.”

Benny took a breath in—slow and cool. He let it out—soft and warm. “Okay,” he said.

Ms. Maple held up three fingers. “Step one: Stop and look. Step two: Pick one job. Step three: Try, pause, and check.”

Benny wiggled his whiskers. “I can try that.”

He looked around. The paints smelled like sweet berries. The buttons clicked together like tiny claps. The paper felt smooth under his paws. Benny put the glue stick back and wiped the paint drip with a damp paper towel. He stopped and looked.

“I pick… painting a river,” Benny decided. He loved rivers, and he loved his strong beaver dam at home.

“Great choice,” Ms. Maple smiled. “What do you need?”

Benny tapped his chin. “Blue paint, a brush, water cup, paper, and a rag.” He gathered just those things. Plop went the paper on the table. Swish went the brush in water. Squeeze went the blue paint.

He dipped and swooped the brush. “Swish, swish,” he hummed. Blue slid across the paper like cool water. Benny smiled. His heart felt slow and steady.

“Pause and check,” he whispered. He put the brush down and looked. The river needed banks. “Brown for the muddy sides!” He added just a little brown. Tap, tap. He paused and checked again.

His friend Dot the ladybug buzzed by. “Ooh! Buttons!” Dot chirped, pointing to the shiny jar.

Benny’s paws twitched. “Buttons would be fun,” he said. Then he remembered. “Step one: Stop and look.” He looked at his river. “I am painting right now. I will finish this job first.”

Dot smiled. “Good plan! I’ll watch.”

Benny painted carefully. When his paw felt wiggly, he took a belly breath in, counted to three, and blew out slowly. “I can be calm. I can wait,” he told himself. The words felt like a blanket.

Soon the river was wide and blue. Benny paused and checked. “It needs a dam!” he said. He planned again. “I need small brown rectangles. I can make them from paper.”

Snip, snip went the safety scissors. Clip, clip went the little paper logs. Benny dabbed a tiny bit of glue. “Dot, can you help me count?”

Dot tapped each paper log. “One, two, three, four…”

“Thank you,” Benny said. “I’m working. I can do this.” He pressed the logs on the river. Press, hold, breathe. Press, hold, breathe. The dam looked sturdy and neat.

“Pause and check,” Benny said once more. He stepped back. He looked with slow eyes. His river painting felt just right.

Now he wiped his brush, capped the glue, and put tools back in their spots. The water cup went in the sink with a gentle clink. The rag went in the laundry bin. Benny liked the clean, quiet table. It made his chest feel light.

“Finished,” he told Ms. Maple.

Ms. Maple beamed. “How did your calm-and-create plan help?”

Benny thought. “I stopped and looked. I picked one job. I tried, paused, and checked. When I felt wiggly, I breathed. I waited. I finished my river all by myself.”

“That is self-regulation,” Ms. Maple said. “You made a plan and followed it. You stayed calm and used kind body control.”

Benny’s tail thumped softly—thump, thump. “I feel proud.”

Dot buzzed again. “Now can we do buttons?”

Benny smiled. “Yes! I will make a button sun. Step one: Stop and look. Step two: Pick one job. Step three: Try, pause, and check.” He gathered yellow buttons, a small glue bottle, and a round paper. Just what he needed.

As he worked, he hummed, “Breathe in, breathe out.” The buttons clicked into a bright, sunny circle. Click, press, wait. Click, press, wait.

Soon, two art pieces shone on the drying rack: a blue river with a strong dam and a golden button sun.

Ms. Maple clapped quietly. “Benny, you planned your work. You kept calm. You were independent.”

Benny nodded. “When I feel busy inside, I can stop, look, pick one job, and do it step by step.”

He patted his chest. “I can be my own calm helper.”

Dot twirled in the air. “And I can be your counting friend!”

Benny laughed. The art room smelled like clean brushes and lemon soap now. The tables felt smooth, and the room sounded soft and happy.

“Tomorrow,” Benny said, “I will plan again.”

The End.
