# Pockets’ Postcards on the Porch

The sun was warm and soft on Pockets’ fur. Pockets was a joyful kangaroo joey with bright eyes and a big, bouncy smile. He sat on the peaceful front porch with Mama Roo. The porch smelled like sweet flowers and fresh bread. Birds sang, “Chirp-chirp!” A gentle breeze tickled Pockets’ ears.

A little mailbox sat by the porch steps. It made a tiny clink. Pockets’ ears perked up. “Mama! Did the mail say hello?” he giggled.

Mama Roo nodded. “Let’s see what came today.”

Pockets hopped-hopped-hopped to the mailbox. He opened the door. Inside were colorful postcards. One had a tall, snowy mountain. Another showed shiny city lights. One had blue, blue ocean waves. Pockets’ tail swished with excitement.

“Wow,” he whispered. “So many places!”

Mama Roo smiled. “These are from friends all around our world.”

Pockets held a postcard to his nose. “This one smells like ocean air,” he said, sniffing and giggling. He pressed another to his cheek. “This one feels bumpy, like the mountain rocks!”

Mama Roo read aloud, “Hello from Maya by the big ocean. We eat coconut rice and sing to the moon.” She showed Pockets a picture of a bright boat.

Pockets tapped his chin. “Coconut rice… is it sweet? Is it crunchy? I want to try!”

Then Mama read another card. “Hello from Aki under the snowy mountain. We wear cozy hats and sip warm tea.”

Pockets wrapped his arms around himself. “I feel warm just thinking about it,” he said.

A tiny ant crawled across the porch rail. Pockets watched carefully. “Mama, our world is big,” he said slowly. “So many friends. So many foods. So many songs.” He bounced once, then paused. “But… how can I be kind to friends far away? I am small. I am here.”

Mama Roo squeezed his paw. “You can learn. You can listen. You can share. Even small paws can do big kindness.”

Pockets nodded. He put the postcards in a neat stack. “I want to learn. I want to listen. I want to share,” he said in a brave, clear voice. His heart felt full and bright.

Just then, Mr. Koala shuffled by the porch with a heavy basket. “Oof,” he puffed. “I made cookies, but I ran out of jam.”

Pockets stood tall. “I can help,” he said. “I have mango jam! It’s sweet and sunny.” He looked up at Mama. “Is it okay to share?”

Mama Roo smiled. “That is generous, Pockets. Ask Mr. Koala how much he needs.”

Pockets hopped to Mr. Koala. “How much jam is good for you?” he asked.

Mr. Koala chuckled. “Just a little scoop, please.”

Pockets brought a small jar. Together they spread jam on warm cookies. Crunch, crunch. Yum! The porch smelled like mango and sugar. Pockets’ belly hummed happily.

As they munched, a breeze flipped one postcard open. A picture of a market with bright fruits fluttered. Pockets stared. “Mama, can we make a porch market? We can share tastes from around our world!”

Mama’s eyes sparkled. “Let’s try.”

They set up a small table. Pockets arranged little bowls: mango jam from home, coconut flakes for Maya’s ocean place, a pinch of tea leaves like Aki’s mountain tea, and a tiny jar of honey from nearby flowers. He made hand-drawn signs with big, friendly letters: Taste, Listen, Learn.

Neighbors came by. Wally Wombat sniffed. “Mmm, sweet coconut!”

Pockets offered a tiny spoon. “This tastes like Maya’s moon songs,” he said. “We can tap our feet while we taste.” He tapped gently. Tap-tap.

Kira Kookaburra tilted her head. “What about this?” she pointed to the tea.

Pockets poured a warm, tiny sip. Steam rose in curly clouds. “This tea is like Aki’s snowy mountain. Cozy and slow,” he said. Everyone took a little taste, then hugged themselves like they wore soft hats.

A small echidna peeked from behind a post, shy and quiet. Pockets crouched so his eyes were kind and level. “Hi,” he said softly. “Would you like a taste? Or would you like to just watch?”

The echidna whispered, “Just watch.”

Pockets nodded. “Watching is okay,” he said. “When you’re ready, I will save some for you.” He placed a tiny bowl to the side with care. The echidna’s eyes twinkled.

“Pockets,” Mama whispered, “you are being generous and gentle.”

Pockets took a breath. He looked at each neighbor. “I want to learn about your homes, too,” he said. “What foods do you love? What songs do you sing?”

Wally hummed a bouncy tune from his grandma. Kira taught a clap-clap pattern. Mr. Koala told how he plants trees for shade. The porch filled with sounds and smiles. Pockets listened with wide eyes, ears high and happy. He asked questions when he was curious. “Can you show me?” “What does it smell like?” “How do you say hello?”

They taught him words from far places. Pockets practiced slowly, kindly. He made mistakes. He giggled. He tried again.

As the sun painted the sky peach and gold, Pockets gathered the empty bowls. His paws were sticky with jam. His heart felt big, like the whole wide world was sitting right on the peaceful front porch.

Mama Roo wrapped him in a warm hug. “What did you discover today?” she asked.

Pockets smiled. “Our world is full of friends,” he said. “When I listen and share, I understand more. I can be small and still make big kindness.”

He looked at the shy echidna and added, “And I can say what I need, and I can ask others what they need, too.” The echidna scooted closer and took a tiny taste, brave and proud.

Pockets bounced once, gently. “Next time,” he said, “let’s send a postcard back.”

Mama nodded. “Yes. We will say, ‘Hello, friends. We are listening. We are sharing. We are thankful.’”

Pockets looked up at the glowing sky. “Hello, big world,” he whispered. “I’m happy to know you.”

The End.
