# Joey Roo and the Sparkly Sink

In a sunny house with soft blue tiles, a joyful kangaroo joey named Joey Roo hopped into the bathroom. The mirror was tall and shiny, the towels were fluffy, and the soap smelled like sweet oranges. “Time to brush my teeth!” Joey Roo sang, his ears wiggling happily.

He squeezed the toothpaste. Splat! A little too much squirted out and plopped onto the sink. “Oops,” Joey Roo giggled. He brushed in tiny circles. “Swish, swish,” went the toothbrush. Bubbles tickled his lips. He spit into the sink. “Puh-too!” The water made a gurgle sound. Joey Roo smiled at his clean, sparkly teeth.

But when he looked down, his smile faded. The sink was messy. Toothpaste dots dotted the white bowl like little stars. The soap was slippery on the counter, and a towel was on the floor.

“Oh no,” Joey Roo whispered. His tummy felt twisty. He liked to be helpful. He liked being big and responsible. But he also wanted to hop away and play.

Joey Roo’s older cousin, Ellie Roo, peeked in. She wore a bright yellow headband and smelled like fresh soap. “Hi, Joey,” said Ellie Roo. “I need the bathroom next.”

Joey Roo hugged his tail. “Uh… I made a mess,” he said softly. “I don’t know what to do.”

Ellie Roo knelt down so her eyes were kind and level with his. “You told me the truth. That’s very responsible,” she said. “What do you think the bathroom needs?”

Joey Roo looked and listened. He heard the drip-drop of the faucet. He saw the toothpaste spots and the towel on the floor. He touched the counter. It felt sticky. “It needs… cleaning,” he decided. His voice was tiny, but brave.

Ellie Roo nodded. “That sounds right. Do you want help, or do you want to try first?” she asked.

Joey Roo took a little breath. He wanted to be big. “I can try first,” he said. “Then if I need help, I will ask.” His words felt strong and warm in his chest. That was Joey Roo being assertive—using his voice to say what he needed.

“Great plan,” Ellie Roo said. She handed him a small cloth. “Wipe, rinse, and wring. I’ll be right here.”

Joey Roo turned the water on. “Whoosh,” it went. He rinsed the cloth. It felt cool and squishy in his paws. He wiped the counter in gentle circles. “Round and round,” he sang. The orange soap smell puffed up again. He picked up the towel from the floor and hung it back on the hook. The towel felt soft as a cloud.

He looked at the toothpaste dots. “You are little stars, but the sky is the sink, and the sink likes to be clean,” he said kindly. He wiped them one by one. “Bye-bye, stars!”

“Check the faucet,” Ellie Roo reminded with a smile.

Joey Roo turned the handle until the drip-drop stopped. “No more drips,” he said. He made sure the toothbrush was standing up, the toothpaste cap was on tight, and the soap sat in its dish. He stepped back and looked.

The sink was sparkly again. The mirror showed a proud Joey Roo with bright eyes and a big grin. He felt light and bouncy, like a happy bubble.

Ellie Roo clapped softly. “You did it! How does your body feel?”

“Warm and proud,” Joey Roo said, patting his chest. “I took care of the bathroom. I was responsible.”

Ellie Roo nodded. “Yes. Taking care of our space is a healthy habit. It keeps us safe and happy. And you spoke up when you needed help. That is good, peaceful communication.”

Just then, little Wally Wombat waddled to the door, rubbing his eyes. “I need to wash my hands,” he said. “But I can’t reach the soap.”

Joey Roo hopped to the step stool and pushed it near the sink. “You can use this,” he said. “And I can show you.”

Wally climbed up. “Thanks, Joey,” he said. The water ran warm over Wally’s paws. Joey Roo sang, “Rub, rub, rub—front and back, between the fingers, nice and clean.” Wally copied the movements. “Squeaky!” he giggled, wiggling his clean paws.

When Wally was done, a little water splashed onto the counter. He looked worried. “I made a splash.”

Joey Roo smiled. “It’s okay. We can clean as we go.” He handed Wally a small cloth. Together they patted the counter dry. Pat, pat, pat. “We take care of our things,” Joey Roo said. “That’s being responsible.”

Wally beamed. “I can do it!”

Ellie Roo gave them both a thumbs-up. “Teamwork feels good,” she said.

Joey Roo put the cloth back where it belonged. He looked around one more time. Everything was tidy and calm. The bathroom smelled fresh. The quiet felt peaceful.

“I like this,” Joey Roo said. “When I make a mess, I clean it up. When I need help, I ask with kind words. My body feels safe and strong.”

Ellie Roo hugged him. “That is the lesson,” she said. “Responsibility helps everyone.”

Joey Roo hopped out of the bathroom, humming his cleaning song. His tail swished happily. He felt ready for the next big, little job.

The End.
