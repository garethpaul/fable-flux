# Tortoise Teaches Turn-Taking

In a soft, sunny nursery, the air smelled like clean blankets and warm applesauce. Colorful blocks made little towers. Crayons waited in tidy cups. A rocking chair creaked gently near the window.

On a cozy rug sat a wise tortoise named Tiko. Tiko’s shell was smooth and speckled, like tiny dabs of paint. His eyes were kind and calm. He moved slowly, but he thought carefully. Tiko liked to help children learn.

Two friends, Mia and Ben, wanted to play with the shiny red dump truck. It had big wheels that went click-clack on the floor. The truck was the favorite toy today.

“I had it first!” Mia said, hugging the truck close. Her cheeks were pink.

“But I saw it first!” Ben said, reaching out. His eyebrows scrunched.

The nursery got quiet. The crinkly fish mobile above the crib swished, swished. Tiko lifted his head and blinked.

“I hear upset voices,” Tiko said in a warm, low voice. “What is the problem?”

Mia pointed. “We both want the truck.”

Ben nodded. “I want to zoom it fast!”

Tiko looked from Mia to Ben. He took a slow breath. “We have a problem,” he said. “How can we make it fair?”

Mia looked down at the truck. Ben shuffled his feet. Fair was a big word.

“What does fair mean?” asked Ben.

Tiko smiled. “Fair means everyone gets a turn, or a chance. Fair means we think about feelings. Fair means we share the fun.”

Mia hugged the truck tighter. “But I really, really want it now.”

Ben’s lip wobbled. “Me too.”

Tiko tapped his shell softly. Tap, tap. “Let us use our thinking. We can ask questions. We can look. We can listen. That is called critical thinking,” he said slowly. “We think with care to find a good answer.”

He pointed to the big, friendly clock with a yellow face. “This clock can help. We can take turns. We can practice waiting.”

“How?” Mia asked.

“We make a plan,” Tiko said. “A fair plan.”

Tiko moved to the clock. “Mia, you may have the truck for two songs. Ben, you may have the truck for two songs after Mia. We will sing together. When the songs are done, we switch.”

Ben looked at Mia. Mia looked at Ben.

“That seems fair,” Mia said in a small voice.

“I can try,” Ben said.

Tiko nodded. “Trying is practice. Practice helps us learn.”

Mia placed the truck on the rug. She pushed it gently. Click-clack, click-clack. “Vroom,” she whispered. Her face softened into a smile.

Tiko began to sing a simple song. “Row, row, row your boat, gently down the stream…” His voice was slow and soothing. Ben swayed and clapped softly. The nursery smelled like crayons and orange slices.

When two songs were done, Tiko said, “Time to switch. Is it fair to switch now?”

Mia looked at Ben. She held the truck out with both hands. “Here, Ben. Your turn.”

Ben took the truck. His eyes lit up. “Thank you, Mia!” He zoomed the truck. Click-clack, click-clack. “Vroom! Beep-beep!”

Mia watched. She hugged a soft teddy bear and waited. She felt a little wiggle in her tummy. Waiting was hard. She took a deep breath like Tiko showed her. In and out. Calm came back.

Tiko smiled. “You are practicing waiting, Mia. That is brave.”

Ben moved the truck in a circle. He made a tiny ramp with a book. “Look! It goes up and down.”

Mia clapped. “That is fun!”

Tiko sang again. “Twinkle, twinkle, little star…” The room glowed with gentle afternoon sun. The baby in the crib giggled at the dangling fish. The rugs felt soft under knees. The truck wheels ticked like a small clock.

When the two songs were done, Tiko said, “Time to switch back. Is it still fair?”

Ben stopped. He held the truck and looked at Mia. He took a breath. “Here, Mia. Your turn.”

Mia smiled big. “Thanks, Ben.” She zoomed the truck, and then she paused. She looked at Ben. “Do you want to build a road with blocks while I drive?” she asked.

Ben grinned. “Yes! I can make a bridge.”

Together, they built a road and a bridge. The truck rolled over the bridge. Click-clack, click. Their laughter filled the nursery like little bells.

Tiko watched with happy eyes. “You used your thinking. You made a plan. You practiced waiting. You shared. That is fairness.”

Mia nodded. “Fair means we both get a chance.”

Ben added, “And we can make a plan and take turns.”

Tiko touched his shell again, tap, tap. “When we have a problem, we stop, we think, we ask, and we plan. That is critical thinking. When we try again and again, that is practice. And when we share and take turns, that is fairness.”

The clock ticked softly. The sun made warm patches on the floor. The red truck was not just a toy now. It was a lesson on wheels.

Mia rolled the truck to Ben. “Your turn to drive over the bridge!”

Ben rolled it back. “Your turn!”

Tiko chuckled. “Now the plan is even better: play together.”

Mia and Ben smiled. Their hearts felt light. The nursery was peaceful again.

Tiko closed his eyes and rocked slowly. He was proud of his friends.

That day, Mia and Ben learned that fairness feels good, thinking helps, and practice makes it easier next time. And the shiny red truck? It still went click-clack, click-clack, happily sharing the road.

The End.
