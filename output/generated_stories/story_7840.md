# The Owl Who Asked Good Questions

Hootley was a wise old owl with soft gray feathers and bright, gentle eyes. One sunny afternoon, Hootley was invited to stay in a cozy guest room. The guest room smelled like clean sheets and lemon soap. The curtains were blue and wiggled when a small breeze came in. A little nightlight glowed like a tiny moon.

“Hooot hoot,” said Hootley, placing a small notebook on the bedside table. “This will be a good place to rest.”

Just then, two children peeked through the door. Mia wore red socks, and Ben had a dinosaur shirt. They smiled shyly.

“Hello, Mr. Owl,” Mia whispered. “Mama said we can bring you a snack.”

Ben held a plate of apple slices. “Do you like apples?”

“I do,” Hootley said kindly. “Thank you. But let’s be careful. Carry the plate with two hands.”

Ben nodded and used both hands. The plate felt cool and smooth. He set it on the dresser with a clink.

Mia looked around. “Can we play in the guest room?”

“We can,” said Hootley, “if we use our thinking eyes and our safety rules. Let’s use our best questions.”

“Questions?” Ben asked.

“Yes,” Hootley hooted softly. “When we ask good questions, we make smart choices. That is called critical thinking.”

Hootley pointed a wing at a tall lamp. “Question one: Is it safe to play tag around the lamp?”

Mia looked at the thin lamp. It wobbled a tiny bit. “Hmm,” she said. “The lamp could fall.”

Ben added, “We might bump it.”

Hootley nodded. “Good thinking. What could we do to improve our play?”

“Choose a calm game,” Mia said. “Maybe a puzzle.”

“Great idea,” said Hootley. “Let’s look for a puzzle.”

They opened the closet slowly. It smelled like cedar wood. On the shelf was a box with big, colorful pieces. The picture showed a farm with a red barn.

“Question two,” Hootley said. “Where should we build the puzzle?”

Ben touched the soft rug. “Here on the floor. It’s flat.”

Mia checked the space. “We need room. Let’s move the footstool back a little.”

Hootley smiled. “You are using your eyes, your hands, and your heads. That is smart and safe.”

They spread the pieces. The cardboard felt smooth and a little bumpy on the edges. Mia found a corner piece. “This one!” she cheered.

Ben held a piece with a cow. “Moo,” he giggled.

“Question three,” Hootley asked. “How can we work together?”

“We can share,” Mia said. “I will find the corners.”

“I will find the animal pieces,” Ben said proudly.

“And I,” said Hootley, “will look for the red barn.”

They worked side by side. Sometimes a piece did not fit. Mia tried to push it, but Hootley gently said, “Stop. Let’s think. If it does not fit, we do not force it. We try again.”

Mia took a deep breath. “Try again,” she repeated. She turned the piece. Click! It fit.

Ben smiled wide. “We did it!”

Hootley hooted happily. “Asking, looking, and trying again helps us improve.”

A soft thump came from the window. The breeze made the curtains flap like wings. The puzzle pieces fluttered.

“Uh-oh,” Ben said. “The pieces might blow away.”

“Question four,” Hootley said calmly. “What is the safe way to fix this?”

Mia walked to the window. “We can close it a little,” she said. “But we should ask a grown-up.”

Hootley nodded. “Yes. Safety first.”

Mia called, “Mama?” Mama came in with a smile. “What do you need?”

“May we close the window a little? The breeze is too strong,” Mia asked.

Mama helped slide the window down. The room felt still and warm again. “Good asking,” Mama said. “That was safe thinking.”

They returned to the puzzle. Soon only one spot was empty.

Ben held the last piece. “Where does it go?”

“Let’s look,” Hootley said. “What colors do we see? What shape is the space?”

Ben compared. “Red and triangle-ish,” he said, squinting. He turned the piece slowly. Click! It fit.

“Hoo-hoo! You solved it,” Hootley cheered. “You used your eyes, your clues, and your questions.”

Mia clapped. “We did it together!”

Hootley pecked at an apple slice. It was crisp and sweet. “One more question,” he said. “How can we clean up safely?”

Ben answered, “Pick up pieces gently. Put them back in the box.”

Mia added, “Push the box onto the shelf with two hands.”

They worked carefully. The box slid onto the shelf with a soft shush. The lamp stood tall. The rug was smooth. The guest room looked neat again.

Hootley fluffed his feathers. “Today, you learned to ask good questions, to be safe, and to keep trying. That is how we grow.”

Mia smiled. “We used our thinking eyes.”

Ben nodded. “And our careful hands.”

Hootley winked. “And your brave hearts. When we think before we act, we stay safe, we improve, and we help each other.”

Mia and Ben waved goodnight. The nightlight glowed. The curtains were quiet. Hootley tucked his head under his wing, feeling proud and peaceful.

“Tomorrow,” he whispered, “we will ask more good questions.”

The End.
