# Benny Bear Cub’s Tidy-Time Trade

Benny was a friendly bear cub with soft, fuzzy ears and a warm honey smile. He lived in a cozy den with a playful living room. The living room had squishy pillows, a rainbow rug, and a basket full of blocks that clicked and clacked. The air smelled like cinnamon cookies, and sunlight made little squares on the floor.

One bright morning, Benny stacked his blocks into a tall, wobbly tower. “Look, Mama!” he said. “It’s a castle! It’s so tall!”

Mama Bear peeked in with gentle eyes. She wore a blue apron and held a soft dusting cloth. “It is a grand castle, Benny,” she said. “But it is Tidy Time. We need to clean the living room. The floor is full of toys.”

Benny’s ears drooped a little. He hugged his block. “I want to keep playing,” he said. “Cleaning is not fun. Cleaning is slow.”

Mama Bear sat beside him on the rainbow rug. She patted his paw. “I know you want to play,” she said. “I want a clean room so we can walk safely and feel calm. We both want good things. Maybe we can find a way to help each other.”

Benny blinked. “How?”

Mama Bear smiled. “Let’s make a compromise. A compromise means we both give a little, and we both get a little.”

Benny thought. He touched his nose. “I like that word. Com-pro-mise.”

“What do you think would be fair?” Mama asked.

Benny listened to the clock tick-tock. He looked at his castle. He felt the soft rug under his paws. “I can clean,” he said slowly, “but can I keep one short play time for my castle?”

Mama nodded. “Yes. How about this: we clean for five songs, and then you get one song to play with your castle. We will trade back and forth until we are done. That is a compromise.”

Benny’s eyes lit up. “A tidy-time trade!”

“Exactly,” Mama said. “We can also learn new skills. You can learn to sort, stack, and sweep. That helps your brain and your paws get strong.”

Benny puffed out his chest. “I want strong paws!”

Mama tapped her music player. A happy song started. The room filled with bouncy beats. Benny felt the rhythm in his feet.

“First skill,” Mama said. “Sorting! Can you sort blocks by color?”

Benny nodded. He picked up a red block. “Red in this box.” He picked up a blue block. “Blue in this box.” He looked proud. “I can do it!”

“Great work,” Mama cheered. “You are growing your sorting skill.”

Benny found green and yellow and orange blocks. The blocks made clack-clack sounds as they dropped into the boxes. He hummed with the song. “Clack, clack, tidy stack!”

When the song ended, Mama said, “That was one song. Four more to clean, and then one to play.”

Benny felt a tiny grumble in his tummy. “Four more?” he asked.

“You can do hard things,” Mama said softly. “Growth takes time. When something feels hard, we can say, ‘I can try.’”

Benny put his paw on his heart. “I can try.”

The next song began. “Second skill,” Mama said. “Rolling the rug smooth.”

Benny pushed the bumpy rug edge. It felt rough and tickly. “Push, push,” he giggled. “It’s like a sleepy snake.” The rug lay flat, and the colors looked bright.

The third song started. “Third skill: Dusting!” Mama showed Benny a tiny duster. It was fluffy and soft, like a cloud. “Gentle swish.”

Benny swished the shelf. “Swish, swish!” He watched dust sparkle in the sun, like tiny stars. “I’m a dust star-catcher!”

The fourth song played. “Fourth skill: Pillow pat,” Mama said.

Benny patted the pillows into neat, puffed squares. “Pat, pat. Poof!” He laughed when a little puff of scent—cinnamon and soap—filled the room.

The fifth song began. Benny felt tired. He looked at Mama. “I can keep trying,” he said, “but can I have my play song soon?”

Mama gave him a thumbs-up. “Yes, after this song. Last cleaning skill: Carry and count. Put the stuffed animals in their basket and count them.”

Benny picked up his soft fox. “One.” He hugged his fuzzy bunny. “Two.” He tucked in the sleepy turtle. “Three.” He placed his tiny bear friend. “Four!” The basket looked snug and happy.

The song ended. Mama tapped the player. “Now, your play song.”

Benny clapped. He knelt by his castle. The music twinkled. He added a bridge from the red box. “Thank you for the compromise, Mama,” he said. “I like our tidy-time trade.”

“I like it too,” Mama said. “We are a good team.”

When the play song ended, Benny felt calm and strong. He looked around. The living room looked shiny and open. The rainbow rug was flat. The pillows were puffy. The air smelled sweet and clean.

Benny smiled. “I learned sorting, rolling, dusting, patting, and counting,” he said proudly. “I grew my skills!”

“And you practiced compromise,” Mama added. “You gave a little, and I gave a little, and we both got what we needed.”

Benny nodded. “I can do hard things if I try and take turns.”

Mama hugged him warm. “That’s a wonderful lesson.”

Benny picked up his tiny bear friend and whispered, “Next time, we can tidy and play again.”

He and Mama did a happy paw-bump. “Team Tidy!” they cheered.

The End.
