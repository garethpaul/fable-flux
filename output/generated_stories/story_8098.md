# Ellie Paints the Balcony Garden

Ellie was a gentle elephant calf with soft gray ears and curious eyes. She lived in a cozy home with a little balcony garden. The balcony had bright green leaves, tiny orange tomatoes, curly kale, and pots of sweet basil and mint. In the morning, the air smelled fresh, like rain on leaves. Birds chirped, “Cheep-cheep!” and the sun warmed Ellie’s cheeks.

Ellie loved the balcony garden. She loved to touch the velvety leaves with her trunk and listen to the tiny bees hum. She loved to taste a cherry tomato and say, “Pop!” when it burst sweet and juicy in her mouth.

But today Ellie had a problem.

“I want to make something special,” she said, looking at the plants. “I want to show our garden’s colors and shapes! But how do I do it?”

Mama Elephant came outside with a bowl. “Good morning, Ellie,” she said gently. “What do you want to make?”

“I want to tell a garden story,” Ellie said. “I want to show the red, the green, the round, the curly. But I don’t know how.”

Mama smiled. “There are many ways to tell a story. We can use words, food, or art. Which way calls to your heart?”

Ellie thought. She listened to the breeze. Rustle, rustle. She sniffed the basil. Mmm, spicy and sweet. She looked at the tomato plant’s bright red dots. Then she looked at her trunk. It was strong, careful, and kind.

“I want to paint!” Ellie said. “I want to paint our balcony garden.”

Mama clapped softly. “What a creative idea! Let’s make a plan. First, we gather knowledge. We look closely. We learn the shapes and colors. Then we choose healthy paints and safe tools.”

Ellie nodded. “Learn first. Then make.”

Mama spread a big cloth on the floor. She brought out thick paper, chunky brushes, and finger paints that were safe for little ones. The paints were red, yellow, blue, and white.

“Can I use my trunk?” Ellie asked.

“Yes,” Mama said. “Your trunk is gentle. It can dip and dot. But we also have brushes and sponges. You can choose.”

Ellie’s ears flapped with joy. She tiptoed to the tomato plant. She looked closely. “Round,” she said. “Red like a warm hug.” She touched a leaf. “Green and smooth.” She smelled the basil. “Peppery and sweet.”

She dipped her trunk in red paint. “Ooh! Cold and squishy,” she giggled. She made a round dot on the paper. “Pop! Tomato!”

She mixed a tiny bit of blue with yellow. “What color is this?” she asked.

Mama smiled. “What do you think?”

Ellie watched the colors swirl. “Green!” she squeaked. “Green for leaves!” She painted long green swishes like the kale curls. Swish, swish.

A butterfly floated by the balcony, flutter flutter. Ellie watched it land on the mint. “Hello, friend,” she whispered. “Do you like the mint smell?” She sniffed too. “Ahh, cool and bright.”

She dabbed a little white with blue. “Sky color,” she said. She painted tiny blue-white dots for the bee hums. “Bzzz, bzzz,” she hummed, smiling.

But then the problem grew bigger. The paper was filling up, and Ellie felt stuck. “Mama,” she said, her trunk drooping, “I don’t know how to show the sun and the crunchy kale and the juicy tomato all together. It feels too big.”

Mama sat beside her. “When a big idea feels too big, we break it into little steps,” she said gently. “Let’s use what we know. What does the sun feel like?”

“Warm on my cheeks,” Ellie said.

“What color makes you think warm?” asked Mama.

“Yellow,” Ellie said. She dipped a sponge into yellow and patted the corner of the paper. Pat, pat, pat. A soft, sunny glow appeared.

“And how does kale sound when we eat it?” asked Mama.

“Crunch!” Ellie said, laughing.

“Let’s make crunchy shapes,” Mama said. “Short, bumpy lines.”

Ellie used a brush to make little bumpy green lines. Crunch, crunch, crunch, the brush whispered.

“What about juicy?” Mama asked.

Ellie made red circles with tiny white dots for shine. “Juicy!” she said. “Pop!” She added a tiny brown stem. “Hello, tomato.”

Ellie stepped back. She looked. She listened to her painting with her eyes. She could see warm, crunchy, juicy, and green. She could hear bees humming in the tiny dots. She could smell basil in the swishy lines. She could feel the sun in the yellow glow.

“I did it!” Ellie cheered. “I told our garden story with colors and shapes!”

Mama hugged her. “You used your senses. You used your knowledge. You made a plan. You solved your problem with creative expression.”

Ellie’s tummy grumbled softly. Rumble-rum. She giggled. “Now I want a garden snack.”

Mama nodded. Together, they picked a few cherry tomatoes—plop, plop, plop—and washed them in a bowl. They tore a little kale and mixed it with mint leaves. They added a squeeze of lemon. The salad smelled bright and fresh.

Ellie took a bite. Crunch. “Mmm,” she said. “Green makes me strong. Tomatoes give me energy. Mint helps me feel fresh.”

Mama smiled. “That’s good nutrition, Ellie. Food helps our bodies grow. Art helps our hearts glow. Learning helps our minds know.”

Ellie looked at her painting again. “I learned by looking and listening,” she said. “I used my trunk and my ideas. I can solve big problems with small steps.”

As the sun slid lower, the balcony felt warm and happy. The birds sang a soft song. Ellie taped her painting on the balcony wall. It fluttered a little in the breeze—flap, flap—and shone like a garden smile.

Ellie whispered, “Thank you, garden, for colors and crunch.” She patted her trunk with pride.

Mama said, “Your voice is special, Ellie. Keep creating.”

Ellie nodded. “I will. I can paint, cook, and learn. I can tell stories in many ways.”

She took another crunchy bite, and her eyes sparkled. “I solved my problem,” she said. “I found my way to show our garden.”

The balcony glowed, the painting glowed, and Ellie’s heart glowed too.

The End.
