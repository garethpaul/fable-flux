# Tully the Turtle Helps in the Pantry

Tully was a very shy turtle with a smooth green shell and curious eyes. One sunny morning, Tully peeked into the pantry. The pantry was cool and cozy. It smelled like apples and cinnamon. Jars lined the shelves like neat little soldiers. Bags of rice made soft crinkly sounds. A basket of oranges glowed like tiny suns.

Tully tucked his head a little. “Hello?” he whispered. “Is anyone here?”

“Good morning, Tully!” chirped Pip the mouse from behind a flour bag. Pip’s whiskers twitched. “Today is Pantry Helper Day. We tidy, we sort, and we share food with friends. Would you like to help?”

Tully’s heart thumped. He was shy. Helping felt big. But Tully took a slow breath. The cool pantry air smelled sweet. He wanted to be brave and responsible. “I can try,” he said softly.

Pip clapped. “We take turns and use gentle hands. That is our pantry promise.”

Just then, Bea the bunny hopped in with a soft thump-thump. “I brought my basket,” she said. “We can pack food for the community wagon.”

From the doorway came Sori the squirrel, tail fluffy like a feather duster. “I will count the cans,” Sori said. “One, two, three!”

Tully looked at the shelves. The jars of jam were jumbled. The cereal boxes leaned like wobbly towers. A bag of beans had tipped and made a tiny hill.

“I can fix the beans,” Tully whispered. He moved slowly and carefully. Scoop, scoop. Plop, plop. He put the beans back in their bag. The tiny beans felt smooth and cool on his feet. “All done,” he said, a little louder.

“Great job, Tully,” said Bea. “That is responsibility!”

Tully smiled a tiny smile. He felt warm and proud inside.

Pip pointed to a row of jars. “We should sort these by color and kind. Strawberry jam goes here. Blueberry jam goes there.”

Sori counted again. “We also need to count for the wagon. We share with friends who need food.”

Tully blinked. “Share our food?” he asked.

Pip nodded. “Yes. That is community service. We help our neighbors.”

Tully thought about this. He liked the idea. It felt kind and big. “I want to help,” he said.

They made a plan and took turns.

Pip said, “I will read the labels.”

Sori said, “I will count and stack.”

Bea said, “I will pack the basket.”

Tully tucked his head, then peeked out. “What can I do?”

“You can be the Shelf Helper,” said Bea. “You can keep each shelf neat and safe.”

Tully stood tall on his short legs. “Shelf Helper,” he repeated. It sounded important.

He started with the cereal boxes. He moved them gently so each box stood straight. Crunch, rustle, rustle went the boxes. He checked the lids on the jars. Twist, click. He wiped a tiny spill with a clean cloth. Swipe, swipe.

“Look!” said Sori. “Tully made the cereal stand like a line of trees!”

Pip sniffed the air. “And no more sticky spots. Good responsibility, Tully!”

Tully’s cheeks felt warm. He liked being careful. He liked doing his job.

Then they heard a soft whimper. In the corner, a little raccoon named Rini tugged at a big oat bag. “I want to carry the heavy bag,” Rini grumbled.

Bea shook her head kindly. “It is Tully’s turn to organize, and we lift heavy things together.”

Tully looked at Rini’s sad eyes. He took another slow breath. “We can take turns,” Tully said in his gentle voice. “And we can use two hands. Or two friends.”

Rini blinked. “Two friends?”

Tully nodded. “We can lift the bag together. That is safe and kind.”

Rini smiled. “Okay.”

Tully and Rini bent their knees. They lifted the bag together. Heave, ho! The bag slid onto a lower shelf. Thump. Safe.

“Teamwork!” cheered Pip. “And a smart, responsible choice.”

Sori bounced. “Now the wagon!” They packed the basket with rice, beans, jam, and oranges. The oranges smelled bright and sweet. Bea tucked in a note that said, “From your pantry friends.”

Tully checked each shelf one more time. Straight boxes. Clean spots. Lids tight. He felt calm and strong.

“Thank you, Tully,” said Bea. “You were careful. You took turns. You helped others.”

Tully’s shy voice was steady now. “I can be brave and responsible,” he said. “I can help my community.”

Pip squeaked, “And you learned new skills! Sorting, counting, lifting safely, and being a leader.”

Tully touched his shell. It felt warm under his hand. He looked into the tidy pantry. It looked cozy and kind, like a hug made of shelves.

The community wagon bell jingled outside. Ding-a-ling! Everyone waved as the basket went out to neighbors.

Tully smiled big. “I will come back next Pantry Helper Day,” he said. “I will do my job, and I will share.”

Bea patted his shell. “That’s responsibility.”

Tully took one last deep breath of apple-and-cinnamon air. He wasn’t so shy anymore. He was careful, open to new ideas, and ready to help.

And the pantry stood bright and neat, full of food and full of love.

The End.
