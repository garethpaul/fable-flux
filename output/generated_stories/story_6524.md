# Poppy the Parrot and the Busy Basement

Poppy was a creative parrot with bright green feathers and a rainbow tail. She loved to sing, “Squawk-a-doodle-doo!” and paint with her little beak. One rainy day, Poppy fluttered down to the busy basement. The basement hummed and buzzed with sounds. Washing machines whirred. A dryer thumped, thump, thump. Tools clinked. The floor felt cool and smooth under Poppy’s feet, and the air smelled a little soapy and warm.

“This is the perfect place to make art,” Poppy chirped. She saw boxes, buttons, paper, and paint. She saw a big cardboard box that looked like a ship. “Aha! I will make a Boat of Colors!” Poppy flapped her wings in excitement.

Her friend, Benny the bunny, hopped down the stairs. His ears swished. “Hi, Poppy! It’s busy down here,” Benny said, twitching his nose. “Be careful.”

“I will,” Poppy said. “Do you want to help?”

Benny smiled. “Yes!”

They got to work. Poppy dipped her beak in blue paint and dotted little stars. Benny glued shiny buttons that clicked and clacked. The washing machine swished water, like waves. Poppy sang, “Sail, sail, sail!”

Then their friend Tilda the turtle waddled in, slow and steady. “Hello,” Tilda said softly. “What are you making?”

“A Boat of Colors!” Poppy said. “Come aboard!”

Tilda climbed into the big box boat. The box wobbled. The dryer thumped louder. Poppy felt a happy flutter in her chest. She flapped her wings and sprinkled glitter. The glitter sparkled like tiny suns.

Just then, a tall shelf rattled as a train toy went around on a track. Benny hopped to watch. Tilda turned to see too. The box tipped. The Boat of Colors slid across the floor, bump, bump, bump. It bumped into a stack of paper cups. The cups toppled and rolled. One cup knocked over a little jar of red paint.

Splat!

Red paint splashed everywhere—on the boat, on the floor, and onto Benny’s soft fur. “Oh no!” Benny cried, eyes wide. “My fur! It’s sticky!”

Tilda pulled her head into her shell. “That was loud,” she whispered.

Poppy’s heart sank. Her wings drooped. “I’m sorry,” she said. “I didn’t mean to spill. I feel so bad.”

Benny frowned. “I don’t like sticky,” he said. His nose wrinkled. He looked at the red streaks and then at Poppy.

Poppy took a deep breath. The basement still hummed and thumped. She felt a lump in her throat. “I will fix it,” she said. “I will make it right.” Her voice was small, but brave.

That was Poppy’s decision. She would not fly away. She would not hide. She would try again.

First, she got a towel. Swoosh, swoosh, she wiped the floor. Tilda peeked out. “I can help,” Tilda said. She nudged paper towels with her shell.

Benny stood very still. “I am mad,” he said honestly. “I am sticky and mad.”

Poppy nodded. “It’s okay to feel mad,” she said. “I understand. I feel sad, and I am sorry.”

Benny watched Poppy scrub the floor. He saw her careful eyes. He heard her gentle voice. The sticky paint on his fur felt cold. He looked at the sink. Water ran with a soft hiss.

“Can you help me wash?” Benny asked.

“Yes,” Poppy said. She dipped a soft cloth into warm, soapy water. She dabbed Benny’s fur. Dab, dab, dab. The soap smelled like lemons. Benny’s nose twitched. He took a deep breath. “It smells nice,” he said, a tiny smile peeking out.

Tilda made a path with towels so no one would slip. “Safety first,” she said proudly.

Soon the floor was clean, and Benny’s fur was not sticky. The Boat of Colors was red in some places now. Poppy tilted her head. “Maybe the red can be our sunset,” she suggested. “We can add gold dots for sunshine.”

Benny looked at the boat. He thought. He felt the last bit of mad float away, like a bubble popping. “I forgive you, Poppy,” he said. “Accidents happen.”

Poppy’s eyes shone. “Thank you,” she said softly. “That helps my heart.”

Tilda nodded. “Forgiveness feels warm,” she said, patting her shell.

They got back to work. Poppy added gold dots. Benny glued yellow paper rays. Tilda stuck blue waves along the bottom. The dryer’s thump sounded like a drum, keeping time with their happy work.

Poppy discovered something new. “When I listen and fix mistakes,” she said, “we can make something even better.”

Benny discovered something too. “When I forgive,” he said, “my tummy doesn’t feel tight.”

Tilda smiled. “And when we are careful and patient, we stay safe.”

At last, the Boat of Colors was done. It had blue stars, golden sunshine, red sunset, and sparkly glitter that shone when the basement light clicked on. They climbed inside and pretended to sail. The washing machine swished like the sea. The warm air felt like a hug. Poppy sang, “Sail, sail, sail!” and Benny joined, “Hop, hop, hop!” Tilda tapped the side, tap, tap, tap, like a gentle drum.

Poppy fluffed her feathers. “Today we learned to forgive,” she said. “We learned to be kind when things go wrong.”

Benny nodded. “We made a mess, and then we made it better,” he said. “That is resilience—keep trying!”

“And we listened to each other,” Tilda added. “That is tolerance—making space for feelings.”

Poppy smiled wide. “Next time, we will move slowly in the busy basement,” she said. “We will make a safe plan.”

They all cheered. “Teamwork!”

The Boat of Colors gleamed. Their hearts felt light. The busy basement hummed softly, like a happy song.

The End.
