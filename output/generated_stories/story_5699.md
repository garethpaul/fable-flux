# Ellie’s Sparkly Soap Story

Ellie was a gentle elephant calf with soft gray ears and a tiny, wiggly tail. She loved her comfortable nursery. The nursery had sunny windows, cozy pillows, and a round blue table for drawing. On a low shelf, there were fat crayons, smooth paper, and a shiny cup filled with clean brushes. The air smelled like oranges and soap.

One morning, Ellie wiggled into the room and said, “Good morning!” Her voice was soft and warm. Her friends, Pip the panda and Nia the bunny, waved. Ms. Star, their kind teacher, smiled. “Today we will write our names and draw a story,” she said. “We will take turns with the crayons, and we will keep our hands clean.”

Ellie’s eyes grew wide. “Write our names?” she asked. Her heart felt bouncy and bright. She loved to try new things.

Ms. Star pointed to the sink in the corner. It had a friendly frog sticker and a bar of sparkly soap. “First,” Ms. Star said, “we wash our hands. Clean hands help us stay healthy. Clean hands keep our paper bright.”

Ellie looked at her hands. She had been playing with blocks, and there was a line of dusty gray on her palm. She sniffed. Her hands smelled like the floor. “Oh!” she said. “I will make them shiny.”

Pip hurried to the sink too. Nia hopped behind him. Ellie walked softly so her feet made little thump-thump sounds on the rug. She could hear the water go whoosh, and she could smell the soap—fresh and sweet, like oranges in sunshine.

“Let’s take turns,” Ms. Star said. “Pip first, then Nia, then Ellie.”

Ellie wanted to go first, but she took a deep breath and nodded. “I can wait,” she said. She watched Pip rub his paws. He sang, “Rub, rub, scrub, scrub,” and counted to ten. Nia did the same. Ellie felt proud of waiting. Waiting was hard, but she could do it.

Now it was Ellie’s turn. She pressed the tap, and cool water splashed on her fingertips. She giggled. The water felt tickly. She picked up the sparkly soap. It was smooth and slippery. “Rub, rub, scrub, scrub,” she sang. She rubbed the tops and bottoms of her hands. She rubbed between her fingers. She rubbed her thumbs. Bubbles grew and grew—white and puffy like little clouds. The bubbles smelled bright and happy.

Ms. Star nodded. “Great scrubbing, Ellie! Now rinse.”

Ellie rinsed. The bubbles slid away with a swoosh. She shook her hands and felt tiny drops like raindrops. She patted them dry with a soft towel. Her skin felt clean and cool. She smiled. “My hands are shiny!”

At the round blue table, Ms. Star gave everyone a paper with a big friendly line. “This is where you can write your name,” she said. “And then draw a little story.”

Ellie picked up a purple crayon. She pressed gently. The crayon felt waxy and warm. “E,” Ms. Star whispered. “Down, across, across, across.” Ellie tried. The line wobbled like a baby worm, but it was her E. She felt brave. “L,” said Ms. Star. “Down and little across.” Ellie drew. “L,” Ellie said proudly. “L.” She smiled bigger. “I,” she whispered, and drew one more line. “E.” She tapped the last letter. “Ellie!”

Pip clapped. Nia clapped too. “Hooray for Ellie!” they said.

Ellie began to draw her story with soft, slow strokes. She drew a sink with a frog sticker. She drew bubbles that floated like tiny moons. She drew her hands, clean and bright. “This is my Sparkly Soap Story,” she said. Her ears wiggled with joy.

Pip reached for a blue crayon at the same time Ellie did. Their fingers touched. Uh-oh. They both stopped. They looked at each other.

“Let’s take turns,” Ellie said. She remembered waiting at the sink. “You use blue first. I will use green.”

Pip smiled. “Thank you, Ellie,” he said. He colored a sky, and then he passed the blue crayon to her. “Your turn.”

Ellie took the blue crayon gently. “Thank you,” she said. It felt good to share.

Nia looked at Ellie’s paper. “Your bubbles look so shiny,” Nia said. “How did you make them?”

Ellie showed her. “I drew soft circles,” she said. “And I left a little white spot. That is the sparkle.” Nia tried it too, and her bubbles looked bright. They all giggled.

Soon Ms. Star rang a tiny bell. “Time to show our work,” she said.

Ellie held up her paper. “This is my Sparkly Soap Story,” she told the class. “First I washed my hands. Clean hands keep us healthy. Then I wrote my name. Then I drew. I took turns with the crayons. I felt happy inside.”

Ms. Star beamed. “Beautiful story, Ellie. You discovered how clean hands help you write and draw. You remembered to take turns. You learned, and you helped your friends learn too.”

Ellie’s heart felt warm, like a cozy blanket. She smelled the last sweet hint of soap on her hands. She touched her letters lightly. “I can do it,” she whispered. “Wash, write, and share.”

On the way out, Ellie saw the sink again. She waved at the frog sticker. “See you soon,” she said. “Rub, rub, scrub, scrub.” Pip and Nia sang with her, and their voices made a happy song.

That day, the nursery felt extra sunny. The papers were clean. The crayons were shared. The bubbles were bright. And Ellie knew a clear lesson: Clean hands help us stay healthy and ready to learn. When we take turns and help each other, we can write our own happy stories.

The End.
