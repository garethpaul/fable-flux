# Momo Makes It Right

Momo was a courageous monkey with bright, curious eyes and soft, fuzzy fur. One rainy morning, Momo tiptoed into the pantry. The pantry smelled like sweet bananas, warm oats, and a little bit like cinnamon. It was quiet, except for the gentle drip-drop of rain outside.

“Ohhh,” Momo whispered, peeking at the shelves. “Bananas! Crunchy crackers! Honey!” His tummy made a small rumble. “Grumble, rumble,” giggled Momo.

Momo’s best friend, Nana the parrot, had painted a sign on the door. It said, “Please ask before taking.” The letters were big and colorful. Momo knew the rule. He felt brave and strong, but he also felt impatient. He wanted a snack now.

“I will just take one banana,” Momo said to himself. He reached up, grabbed a banana, and peeled it. The peel was soft and stringy. The banana was sweet and creamy. “Mmm,” he hummed, cheeks full.

Then he saw the honey jar. It sparkled like gold. “Maybe a tiny taste,” Momo whispered. He dipped a finger. The honey was sticky and warm. But the jar wobbled. “Oh!” Momo tried to catch it.

Clink! Tip! Plop! The honey jar fell onto a bag of oats. Puffs of oats flew like snowflakes. Sticky honey dripped down the shelf and onto the floor. “Oh no,” gasped Momo. His tail drooped. The pantry was a sticky, oaty mess.

Nana flapped in from the kitchen. “Momo?” she called. “What happened?”

Momo’s cheeks felt hot. He wanted to hide. He wanted to pretend it was not him. But Momo was a courageous monkey. He took a deep breath. In and out. Slow and gentle, like the rain.

“I made this mess,” Momo said. His voice was small, but brave. “I did not ask. I was impatient. I am sorry.”

Nana looked at the honey and oats. She looked at Momo’s sticky fingers. “Thank you for telling the truth,” she said kindly. “That is strong and brave.”

Momo’s eyes were shiny. “I want to make it right,” he said. “How can I fix it?”

Nana nodded. “We can make amends,” she said. “Making amends means saying sorry and also fixing the problem. Let’s work together.”

Momo stood tall. “I can do it,” he said. “I will be patient.”

First, they made a plan. Momo liked plans. Plans helped him feel calm and well.

- Step one: Wipe up the honey.
- Step two: Sweep the oats.
- Step three: Wash the floor.
- Step four: Replace what was used.

Momo took a warm, soapy cloth. It was soft and squishy. He wiped slowly, up and down, up and down. “Slow is okay,” Nana said. “Breathe in… and out.” Momo breathed in the smell of soap and cinnamon.

Next, Momo held a small broom. Swish, swish. The oats made gentle crinkle sounds. “Swish, swish,” sang Nana. “You are doing it.”

Momo smiled. His heart felt lighter. He was fixing the mess. He was making amends.

They filled a bucket with warm water. Momo dipped the mop. Drip, drip. The floor became shiny and clean. “Look,” Momo said. “It is better!”

Nana nodded. “You are helping your home and your heart,” she said. “This is wellness. We care for our space, and we care for our feelings.”

Momo’s tummy felt calm now. He was still a little sad about the jar. “We still need a honey jar,” he said.

Nana showed him a little savings jar. “We can use some coins to replace the honey,” she said.

“I have two shiny buttons,” Momo said. “And I can do extra gentle jobs to earn more.” He imagined himself watering plants, humming softly. He imagined folding tiny towels, pat-pat, nice and neat.

“That is a kind plan,” Nana replied. “You are using your imagination to help.”

Momo also made a sign. He drew a big banana and a speech bubble that said, “Please ask. Please wait.” He taped it under Nana’s sign. “This will help me be patient,” he said.

Later, Momo and Nana sat in the pantry with cups of warm banana-oat tea. It smelled sweet and cozy. “I feel peaceful,” Momo said, sipping carefully. “I was scared to tell the truth, but it feels good to fix things.”

Nana smiled. “You were brave. You made amends. You were patient. You took care of your feelings and our pantry. That is wellness.”

Momo nodded. “Next time, I will ask,” he said. “And I will wait.”

Just then, there was a knock. Tap, tap. Their friend Piko the mouse peeked in. “May I have a cracker?” Piko asked.

Momo’s eyes lit up. “Yes! Thank you for asking,” he said. He gave Piko a cracker and a tiny napkin. “Let’s eat at the table.”

They all crunched together. Crunch, crunch. The pantry felt bright and clean. It smelled like cinnamon and smiles.

Momo patted the clean floor. “I made a mistake,” he said softly, “and I made it right.”

Nana nodded. “That is what courageous friends do.”

The End.
