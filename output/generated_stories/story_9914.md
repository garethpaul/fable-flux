# Rocco’s Closet Concert

Rocco was a musical raccoon with soft gray fur and bright, curious eyes. He loved to make music. He tapped spoons. He shook buttons in a jar. He hummed and sang. His favorite place to play was the closet hideaway. It was warm and cozy inside. Coats felt like big, soft hugs. The floor smelled like clean soap. A tiny light glowed like a firefly.

One rainy afternoon, Rocco ducked into the closet hideaway. “Time for a closet concert!” he said. He set up a tiny drum from an old box. He had a twinkly bell, a rubber band guitar, and two wooden spoons.

Rocco whispered, “One, two, three,” and tapped the drum. Tap-tap. He shook the bell. Jingle-jingle. He strummed the rubber band. Boing-boing!

Just then, his little sister, Rosie, peeked in. “I want to play too!” she said, wiggling her whiskers.

Rocco frowned. “But this is my concert,” he said. His tail twitched. He had planned a quiet song. He had planned a big finish. He did not plan for Rosie.

Rosie scooted in anyway. She grabbed the two wooden spoons. Clack! Clack! Clack! The sound was loud. The sound was fast. Rocco’s ears drooped. “No! Not like that,” he said. “You’re messing up my song!”

Rosie blinked. “I just want to play.”

Rocco felt his chest get tight. His face felt hot. He wanted to shout. He wanted to slam the closet door. He wanted to hide under a coat.

He remembered Mama’s calm voice: “When you feel big mad, stop and breathe.”

Rocco closed his eyes. He placed one paw on his tummy. In, two, three. Out, two, three. He listened to the soft swish of coats. He smelled the clean, soapy air. He felt the soft carpet under his toes. His tummy slowed down. His shoulders dropped.

He opened his eyes. “I feel frustrated,” he said. “I wanted my concert my way.”

Rosie nodded. “I feel sad,” she said softly. “I want to make music too.”

Rocco tapped his drum gently. Tap. “Maybe we can fix this,” he said. “Let’s use our words. Let’s make a plan.”

Rosie hugged the spoons. “Okay.”

Rocco thought. “First, we can share. We take turns. I play one song. Then you play one song.”

Rosie tilted her head. “Can I keep the spoons?”

Rocco looked at the spoons. He took another slow breath. “Yes,” he said, “but please play soft for my song.”

Rosie smiled. “Soft spoons. Like rain.”

Rocco smiled back. “And after our songs, we do closet chores together,” he added. “We put things away.”

Rosie wrinkled her nose. “Chores?”

Rocco nodded. “If we keep it tidy, we can find our instruments next time. It makes music time easy.”

Rosie thought. “Okay. After songs, chores. Then a high-five.”

“Deal,” said Rocco. They touched paws. “Deal.”

They set the rules:
- Soft spoons for Rocco’s song.
- Rosie’s turn next with loud spoons.
- Then tidy time together.

Rocco started again. Tap-tap. Jingle. Boing. Rosie clicked the spoons softly. Clack… clack… like tiny raindrops. The coats rustled like applause. Rocco’s tail swished with joy. “That was beautiful,” he said.

“My turn!” Rosie grinned. She played a bouncy beat. Clack-clack! Clack-clack! Rocco shook the bell and hummed along. The little light glowed warm. The closet felt like a tiny stage.

After the music, Rocco pointed to the drum box on the floor. “Chores time,” he said in a sing-song voice.

Rosie sighed. “I want more music.”

Rocco felt a little tug inside. He wanted more music too. He took a breath. “We can make tidy time part of the music,” he said. “Let’s sing a tidy song.”

Rosie’s eyes sparkled. “Yes!”

They sang together:
“Pick it up, put it back,
Drum in box and spoons in sack.
Ring goes bell on the little hook,
Guitar by the coats—take a look!”

They scooped up buttons. They folded a scarf. They tucked the drum into its box. They slid the spoons into a pocket. The closet smelled like oranges from a clean wipe. The floor felt smooth.

“High-five,” said Rocco.

“High-five!” said Rosie.

Rocco looked around the neat, cozy space. He felt proud. His body felt calm and light. “Rosie, thank you for playing soft when I asked.”

Rosie nodded. “Thank you for sharing and making a tidy song.”

Rocco sat on a small cushion. “When I got frustrated, breathing helped,” he said. “Using words helped too.”

Rosie wiggled. “Next time, we can plan before we start.”

Rocco grinned. “Yes. We can say, ‘What do you need? What do I need?’ Then we make a plan.”

Rosie tapped the closet door gently. “Can we have one more tiny song?”

Rocco listened to his body. It felt calm. He checked the time. “One more tiny song,” he said. “Soft and sweet.”

They played a quiet lullaby. Tap. Jingle. Boing. The coats swished like soft waves. The little light hummed. The rain outside whispered on the window. The closet hideaway was full of music, sharing, and smiles.

The End.
