# Toby Turtle Finds His Calm

The basement was busy-busy-busy. Fans hummed. A washing machine swish-sloshed. Boxes made cardboard mountains. The air smelled like warm laundry and dusty books. Lights buzzed like soft bees.

In the corner, behind a stack of paint cans, lived Toby Turtle. Toby was shy. He liked quiet. He liked the soft thunk of his shell on the rug. He liked to draw tiny pictures with a stubby blue crayon. He wrote his name, “Toby,” in big round letters.

Today, the basement was extra busy. Neighbors were cleaning for a community sale. Feet thumped on the stairs. A radio played bouncy music. Children laughed and rolled toy cars. A broom scraped the floor—scritch, scritch, scritch.

“Hi, Toby!” called Nina, a kind girl with bright green shoes. “We’re setting up tables. Want to help?” Her voice was friendly, but it bounced around the room and bumped into Toby’s ears.

Toby tucked his head halfway into his shell. “Um… maybe later,” he whispered. He felt a tight, hot bubble in his chest. He wanted quiet. He wanted space. He wanted the radio to turn down. He wanted his crayon.

The radio got louder. Thump-thump. Someone bumped Toby’s drawing paper, and it slid under a box. “Oh!” Toby’s face grew warm. His cheeks felt prickly. His heart went thud, thud, thud.

Inside his shell, Toby felt the hot bubble get bigger. His hands curled into little turtle fists. He wanted to shout, “Stop it! Too loud!” He wanted to push the box away hard. He wanted to hide.

Toby remembered something Mama Turtle had taught him. “When your feelings get big, stop and make them small. Breathe in like you smell a flower. Breathe out like you blow a candle.” Toby looked at the small blue crayon. He held it gently. He closed his eyes.

“Smell a flower,” he whispered. He breathed in through his nose. “Blow a candle,” he whispered. He breathed out through his mouth. In. Out. In. Out. The hot bubble began to shrink. His shoulders felt softer. His fists opened.

Toby peeked out of his shell. He still felt upset, but now it was a little upset. “I can use my words,” he told himself. “I can say what I need.” That was self-advocacy. Mama called it speaking up with respect.

He took a slow step. Then another. He walked to Nina and gently tugged her sleeve. “Nina?” His voice was small, but it was steady. “It’s very loud. I feel… mad and squished.” He pressed a hand to his chest. “Can we make a quiet spot?”

Nina knelt so her eyes met his. “Thank you for telling me, Toby. I hear you.” She turned to the others. “Friends! The basement is busy. Let’s make a quiet corner for Toby and anyone who needs it.”

Some children nodded. “That’s kind,” said a boy with a red hat. A grown-up turned the radio down. The fans still hummed, but softer now. The broom slowed—scritch… pause… scritch.

Toby felt a little braver. “I can help,” he said. Helping was civic responsibility—working together for everyone. “We can use a table as a wall.” He pointed to a folding table. “And a sign.”

Nina smiled. “Great idea!”

They moved a small table near the paint cans. They placed a pillow on the rug. Nina found a box of markers and paper. “Let’s make the sign,” she said.

Toby picked his stubby blue crayon. He wrote big round letters: QUIET CORNER. He drew a little turtle with headphones and a peaceful smile. He added a star. He wrote, “Please use inside voices. Thank you!”

He showed the sign. “Is this okay?” His voice trembled just a bit.

“It’s perfect,” said Nina. “You’re a thoughtful turtle.”

Toby taped the sign to the table. Tape made a sticky rip-rip sound. He placed his drawings on the pillow. He put a small basket of crayons beside them. He set a paper that said, “Take a calm breath.”

Children came to look. “Can I come here when I need a break?” asked the boy with the red hat.

“Yes,” said Toby. “One at a time. Soft voices.” He pointed to the sign. The boy nodded and whispered, “Thank you.”

A girl in sparkly socks held up a loud toy. “Is this too noisy?” she asked.

Toby took a breath. “In the quiet corner, we use quiet toys,” he said. “You can play with the loud toy over there.” He pointed to the far side of the basement. The girl nodded and skipped away.

As the day went on, people worked together. The radio stayed soft. The broom took gentle turns. When someone forgot and shouted, Toby touched the sign and said, “Inside voices, please.” He used his words. He felt proud and calm.

At the end of the day, the basement looked neat and friendly. Boxes were labeled. Tables were ready. Everyone clapped. Clap, clap, clap.

Nina gave Toby a small notebook. “This is for your writing,” she said. “You used your words. You made a plan. You helped the whole basement.”

Toby opened the notebook. The pages smelled like fresh paper. He wrote, “I can be calm. I can speak up. I can help.” He drew a tiny flower and a candle to remember his breaths.

Toby felt warm in a good way now. Not hot-mad. Warm-proud. He smiled. “Thank you,” he said.

That night, the fans hummed softly. The washing machine was quiet. Toby curled on the pillow in the quiet corner. He took one more flower breath in, one more candle breath out.

He whispered, “When my feelings get big, I can make them small. I can use my words. I can help my community.” The sign rustled gently, like it was nodding.

The End.
