# Tilly Turtle’s Guest Room Band

Tilly was a shy turtle. She liked quiet corners and small, soft sounds. One day, Tilly visited Grandma’s house. “You can play in the guest room,” said Grandma with a smile. “It’s a special place.”

The guest room felt cozy. The bed had a bouncy quilt. The curtains were soft and swishy like clouds. The window let in warm, golden light. It smelled like fresh soap and clean blankets. Tilly tucked her head in and peeked out again. “I can do this,” she whispered.

Tilly heard a tiny tap-tap. It was her heart. “I feel nervous,” she said to herself. “My tummy feels fluttery.” She took a deep breath in, and a slow breath out. “I can be brave in small steps.”

On the shelf, there were baskets with old things to recycle. Grandma liked to make new things from old things. Tilly saw empty tissue boxes, paper tubes, a metal tin, and bottle caps that clicked like little shells.

“I wish I could make music,” Tilly said softly. “Music helps me feel calm. Music helps me dance.”

But Tilly was shy. Her voice was small. “What if it sounds silly?” she wondered.

She looked at the baskets again. “I can try,” she decided. “I can make quiet music first.”

Tilly picked up a tissue box. She stretched rubber bands around it—twang, twang. She plucked one band. “Boing!” It sounded funny. She giggled. Her shell felt a little lighter.

She put dry rice into the metal tin and closed the lid tight. “Shake-shake,” she tried. It made a soft rain sound. “I like that,” she said. “It sounds like tiny feet dancing.”

She stacked paper cups and tapped them with a wooden spoon. “Tip, tap, tip, tap.” The sounds were gentle, like raindrops on the window.

Tilly lined up bottle caps on the floor and slid them together. “Chirr-chirr.” The caps shivered like a little snake dance. Tilly smiled. “I am making a band!” she cheered quietly.

Just then, the door creaked. Grandma peeked in. “What’s that lovely sound?” she asked.

Tilly tucked her head again. “It’s only a little music,” she whispered. “I made it from recycling. Is it okay?”

Grandma’s eyes twinkled. “It’s more than okay. It’s wonderful. Music can come from many things. Would you like me to listen?”

Tilly’s tummy fluttered again. She took a slow breath. “Yes,” she said. “But I feel shy.”

Grandma nodded. “Let’s make a plan. We can start with one small sound. I will close my eyes and listen.”

Tilly strummed the rubber bands. “Boing, boing, boing.” Grandma smiled and swayed.

“Now the rain tin,” said Grandma.

Tilly shook the tin softly. “Shhhh, shhhh.” It sounded like a gentle storm. Tilly’s feet began to wiggle. Her shell felt warm.

“Now the cups,” said Grandma.

“Tip, tap, tip, tap,” went the cups. Tilly’s body wanted to move. Her head peeked out all the way.

Grandma opened her eyes. “I hear a beat,” she said. “Do you feel it?”

Tilly nodded. “I feel the music in my chest. It says, ‘Be brave, little step. Be brave, little step.’”

Grandma clapped a steady clap. Tilly clicked the bottle caps. “Chirr-chirr, clap, clap.” The guest room filled up with happy sounds. The curtains swished like dancers. Dust sparkles in the sunlight twirled like tiny stars.

Tilly stood up taller. She took another breath. “I can dance slow,” she told herself. She swayed left. She swayed right. Her feet went slide-slide on the soft rug. “I am dancing!” she laughed.

Grandma cheered, “Go, Tilly, go!”

Tilly added the rain tin. “Shhhh.” She added the rubber bands. “Boing!” She added the cups. “Tip, tap!” The sounds layered like a sandwich. “I made a song!” she said.

Then Tilly heard a new sound—her own giggle, bright and bubbly. “I feel proud,” she said. “My shy feelings are still here, but they are smaller now.”

Grandma nodded. “Feelings change when we name them and breathe. You were resourceful, too. You used old things in a new way. That helps the Earth.”

Tilly looked at her band. “Recycling makes music,” she said. “Recycling makes dance!”

She put labels on each instrument: Rain Tin, Boing Box, Cup Drums, Cap Shakers. She drew little pictures so friends could play, too. “I will share,” Tilly said. “We can make a guest room band for everyone.”

When Mom came to pick her up, Tilly said, “Listen to my song!” She played soft at first. Then a little louder. Mom swayed. Grandma clapped. Tilly danced with small, brave steps. The guest room felt like a warm hug.

Tilly took a bow. “Music helps me when I feel shy,” she said. “I can breathe, name my feeling, and play my song. I can dance my feelings out.”

Grandma smiled. “That is a beautiful lesson.”

Tilly packed her instruments in a basket for next time. “I will teach my friends how to make music from recycling,” she said. “We will listen, share, and be brave together.”

The guest room was quiet again, but it felt full of happy music. Tilly’s heart went tap-tap, calm and steady.

The End.
