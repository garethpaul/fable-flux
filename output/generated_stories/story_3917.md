# Tully the Turtle Tries New Tastes

Tully was a shy turtle who liked quiet and cozy places. One sunny morning, Tully sat in a warm kitchen. The oven hummed softly. The air smelled like butter and cinnamon. Sunlight made little gold squares on the floor.

Tully pulled his head into his shell. “It is safe in here,” he whispered. He peeked out when he heard a gentle clink. His friends were visiting today.

Maya walked in with a bright scarf. “Hello, Tully! I brought mangoes,” she said, holding up a yellow fruit. It smelled sweet like sunshine.

Omar came next with a little jar. “Hello! I brought hummus,” he said. It smelled like garlic and lemon.

Lily tiptoed in with a bowl. “Hi, Tully! I brought rice and seaweed,” she said. It smelled like the sea and sesame.

Grandma Rosa stirred a pot on the stove. “Hola, everyone,” she said. “I am making warm tortillas. They smell like home.”

Tully tucked his head back in. So many smells! So many things! His tummy made a small flip. He liked his plain lettuce. He liked his quiet kitchen.

Grandma Rosa smiled kindly. “Today is Taste Day,” she said. “We each share a food we like. We can learn about each other.”

Maya sliced the mango. “It is soft and juicy,” she said. “Would you like a tiny bite, Tully?”

Tully peeked out. The mango looked like orange sunshine. He felt shy. “What if I do not like it?” he asked in a small voice.

“That is okay,” said Maya. “Trying is brave.”

Omar opened the hummus. “We can spread it on warm bread,” he said. He held out a spoon. “Just a little taste?”

Tully sniffed. The smell was new and strong. “What if it is too different?” he whispered.

“Different can be interesting,” said Omar. “We can use our thinking. We can try and decide.”

Lily set the rice on the table. “You can roll it or just taste a grain,” she said. “We can learn new skills. I can show you how.”

Tully’s heart beat thump-thump. He pulled his head back, then peeked again. He did not want to be rude. He wanted to be brave. He wanted to learn.

He looked at the warm kitchen. He looked at his friends’ smiling faces. He wiggled his toes on the soft rug. He took a slow breath. In… and out. He felt the sweet mango smell and the toasty tortilla smell. He felt the gentle heat from the stove on his shell.

“I will try one small thing,” Tully said. “I can be open. I can try and think.”

Grandma Rosa clapped softly. “Buenísimo,” she said. “Let us make a plan. One small taste at a time.”

Maya gave Tully a tiny mango cube. “You can touch it first,” she said.

Tully touched it. It was smooth and slippery. He put it on his tongue. The juice burst like a little sun. “Oh!” he said. “It tastes bright and sweet.” He smiled a small smile.

“Good job,” said Maya. “You tried.”

Omar spread a dab of hummus on a cracker. “Just a dot,” he said.

Tully sniffed. He took a nibble. It was creamy and lemony. “Hmm,” he said. He thought. “It is new. I think… I like it a little.” He nodded. “I can try more later.”

Lily showed Tully how to roll rice and seaweed. “Press, roll, pat,” she said. “Like a little blanket.”

Tully pressed, rolled, and patted with his small turtle hands. The seaweed felt smooth and a little sticky. “I am learning a new skill,” he said. He took a tiny bite. The rice was soft. The seaweed tasted like salty wind. “It is different,” he said, “and I like the soft part best.”

Grandma Rosa flipped a tortilla. It puffed like a small balloon. “Listen,” she said. The tortilla made a little hiss. She placed one on Tully’s plate with a sprinkle of cinnamon sugar.

Tully tore a warm piece. The steam tickled his nose. He tasted. It was warm and gentle. “This feels like a hug,” he said.

Everyone cheered. “You did it!” said Maya.

Tully’s shell felt lighter. He looked at the table. It was a rainbow of foods and feelings. He had tried new tastes from different places. He had listened to his body. He had used his thinking. He had learned with his hands.

“I was shy,” Tully said, “but I was open. I tried one small thing at a time. I learned new skills. I found some things I like.”

Grandma Rosa nodded. “That is independence,” she said softly. “You made choices. You were kind and curious.”

Tully smiled big. “Next time,” he said, “I can share my lettuce with a twist! Maybe with mango. Maybe with hummus.” He giggled. “I can mix and think.”

The warm kitchen hummed. Friends laughed. The sun made gold squares on the floor again. Tully felt brave, kind, and full.

He whispered to himself, “When I am open-minded, my world gets bigger and brighter.”

The End.
