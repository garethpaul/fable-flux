# Cub Benny and the Balcony Garden Helpers

Cub Benny was a friendly bear cub with soft, fuzzy fur and bright, curious eyes. He lived in a cozy den with Mama Bear and Papa Bear. Outside their window was a small balcony garden with pots of mint, cherry tomatoes, sunflowers, and tiny wildflowers. In the morning, the sun warmed the leaves. The air smelled like mint and sweet tomato vines. Bees hummed, “Bzz-bzz,” and a gentle breeze tickled Benny’s nose.

One sunny day, Mama Bear said, “Benny, today we will help our neighbors on the balcony garden. We will be volunteers.”

“Volun-teers?” Benny tasted the big word. “What does that mean?”

“It means we help because we care,” Mama Bear said with a smile. “We listen. We are kind. We try new things with an open mind.”

Benny’s ears perked up. “I can try new things,” he said. “I can listen.”

On the balcony, the neighbors waved. Ms. Robin the bird fluffed her feathers. “Welcome, helpers! We have three jobs,” she chirped. “Water the plants, tidy the path, and read a story to the sprouts.”

“Read a story?” Benny blinked. “Plants like stories?”

Mr. Snail peeked from a pot. “We do,” he said slowly. “Stories help us feel calm. They help us grow.”

Benny was not sure. He had never read to plants. His paws felt wiggly. The mint leaves smelled cool and strong. The sun felt warm on his fur. He took a slow breath in and out, just like Mama Bear taught. The air felt soft in his chest.

Mama Bear whispered, “Let’s be mindful. Let’s notice what we see, hear, smell, and feel.”

Benny looked and saw yellow sunflowers turning their faces to the light. He heard bees and faraway city sounds. He smelled mint and damp earth. He felt the smooth watering can handle. He nodded. “I am ready to help.”

Ms. Robin gave directions. “First, water. Two small cups for each pot. Not too much.”

Benny listened carefully. “Two small cups,” he repeated.

He lifted the can. “Slooosh.” The water sparkled. He counted, “One… two,” for the tomatoes. He counted, “One… two,” for the mint. He moved slowly, and some tiny drops splashed his paw. It felt cool and funny. He giggled.

“Good following directions,” Ms. Robin chirped. “Thank you for being careful.”

Next, it was time to tidy. Mr. Snail pointed with his antenna. “Dry leaves here. Please put them in the compost.”

Benny picked up the dry leaves. They made a soft “crunch-crunch.” He put them in a big brown bin. He found a lost little seed packet under the bench. “I found it!” he said. He felt proud. Helping felt good in his tummy, like warm oatmeal.

Then Ms. Robin said, “Now the story.”

Benny’s ears twitched. “Um… I don’t know if plants like stories,” he said. “Maybe they like music. Or maybe they like jokes.”

Papa Bear knelt beside him. “Having an open mind means we are ready to learn,” he said gently. “We can try. If it feels new, we can be curious.”

Benny squeezed the storybook. The cover showed a tiny seed becoming a tall tree. He took another slow breath. In… and out. The minty air made his nose tingle. “Okay,” he said. “I will try.”

He sat close to the pots. He could see tiny drops of water on the tomato leaves. “Hello, plants,” he whispered. “This story is for you.”

He began to read. His voice was soft and cozy. “Once there was a small seed who was brave and patient. The seed listened to the rain and the sun and grew, little by little.”

As Benny read, the balcony felt calm. The bees hummed like a lullaby. The breeze brushed his ears. The leaves seemed to whisper, “Shhh.” Benny smiled. “I think they like it,” he said.

When the story ended, Ms. Robin clapped her wings. Mr. Snail nodded slowly. “Lovely,” he said. “I feel taller already.”

Benny giggled. “Plants can’t clap,” he said. “But maybe they can listen.”

Mama Bear gave Benny a gentle hug. “You tried something new,” she said. “You kept an open mind. How do you feel?”

“I feel brave,” Benny said. “And calm. And helpful.”

Ms. Robin brought a small basket. “Thank you, volunteers,” she chirped. “Please take two tomatoes and a sprig of mint.”

Benny held a plump red tomato in one paw and a cool mint sprig in the other. He smelled the mint. “Mmm,” he said. “Fresh!” He tasted the tomato. It was juicy and sweet. The juice ran down his chin. “Slurp!”

On the way home, Benny told a story of his own. “Once there was a bear cub who read to plants,” he said. “He listened, he helped, and he learned new things.”

Papa Bear smiled. “That is good storytelling.”

That night, Benny put the mint by his bed. He closed his eyes and breathed in the calm, cool smell. He thought about the plants, the bees, and the story.

“Today I was open-minded,” he whispered. “I listened. I followed directions. I helped my neighbors.” He felt warm and happy. “Tomorrow, I can try new things again.”

The End.
