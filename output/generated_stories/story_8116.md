# Milo the Brave Monkey Speaks Up

Milo was a small, courageous monkey with bright eyes and quick hands. He lived near a cozy garden. At the edge of the garden stood a little shed with a creaky door and a round window like a cookie. The shed smelled like warm wood, sweet soil, and fresh mint from Grandma Lila’s herb pots outside.

Every morning, Milo helped Grandma Lila in the garden. He liked to water the tomatoes, wipe the muddy trowels, and put the gloves on the shelf. Today, when the sun was soft and golden, Grandma Lila called, “Milo, could you please bring the watering can from the shed?”

“I can do that!” Milo said. He puffed up his chest. “I am brave and helpful!”

Milo pushed the creaky door. “Creeeak.” Inside, the shed was full of sounds and smells. He heard the quiet tick of a clock. He smelled straw and lemon soap. Dust danced in the sunbeams like tiny floating stars. But the shed was a big jumble. Rakes leaned sideways. Pots piled up like towers. A ball of twine rolled under a bench.

Milo stepped inside and bumped his tail on a broom. “Thump!” He rubbed his tail. “Ow.” He looked around. “Where is the watering can?”

He climbed on a low stool to see the shelves. “Hello? Watering can?” he whispered like it could answer. He peeked behind a bucket. He peeked under a blanket. He found a red ribbon, a noisy bell, and three shiny buttons. But no watering can.

Milo’s tummy felt fluttery. “I want to help, but I need help too,” he said softly. He remembered Grandma Lila’s words: “Use your brave voice, Milo. Tell me what you need.”

He took a deep breath. In and out. He felt the cool air in his nose. He stood tall. “I will use my brave voice,” he said.

Milo trotted back outside. Sun warmed his fur, and the mint leaves tickled his toes. “Grandma Lila?” he called.

“Yes, dear?” Grandma Lila turned with a smile. She wore a big hat with a blue ribbon.

Milo said, “I want to get the watering can. But the shed is messy. I can’t find it. I feel a little worried. Can you help me? Can we make a plan?”

Grandma Lila’s eyes sparkled. “Milo, thank you for telling me how you feel and what you need. That is very brave. Yes, let’s make a plan.”

They walked to the shed together. “Creeeak,” went the door. Grandma Lila sniffed the air. “Smells like lemon soap today.”

Milo nodded. “And dusty stars,” he said, pointing to the sunbeams.

Grandma Lila said, “First, let’s take a big breath. Then we’ll tidy together. We will organize by groups: water tools, digging tools, and plant things.”

Milo smiled. “I like groups.”

“Me too,” said Grandma Lila. “And we will take kind breaks. That’s a healthy habit for our bodies.”

Milo clapped his hands. “Breaks and water,” he said. He took a sip from his little bottle. “Cold and yummy!”

They started. Milo gathered all the watering things: the sprinkler heads, two small cans, and a long green hose. He put them in a blue bin. “Water tools live here,” he said.

Grandma Lila stacked pots from big to small. “Plant things in this basket,” she said.

Milo wiped the shelf with a damp cloth. The cloth felt cool and squishy. “Squish, swish,” he sang. He lined up the gloves like ducks in a row. He hung the broom on a hook so it would not bump his tail again. “There,” he said. “Safe and tidy.”

After a while, Milo’s arms felt tired. “I need a pause,” he said.

“Good self-check,” said Grandma Lila. “Let’s stretch.” They reached up high. “Hello, clouds!” They touched their toes. “Hello, shoes!” They took sips of water. Milo crunched a sweet apple slice. “Crunch, crunch,” went the apple. “Yum,” said Milo.

Back to work they went. Milo spotted a shiny spout peeking from behind a basket. “Aha!” he cried. He pulled out a big green watering can. It sloshed with a little water. “I found you!”

“You did it,” Grandma Lila said. “How do you feel?”

Milo’s eyes shone. “Proud. I used my brave voice. I asked for help. We made a plan. We took care of our bodies. And we organized the shed!”

Grandma Lila tapped her nose. “That’s self-advocacy, Milo. You said what you needed in a calm, clear way. That helps you, and it helps me too.”

Milo hugged the watering can. It felt cool and smooth. “Thank you, Grandma. Now the plants can have a drink.”

They went to the garden. Milo poured the water gently. “Sip, sip, little plants,” he said. Drops sparkled like tiny jewels on the leaves. The tomatoes looked happy. The mint smelled extra bright.

When they finished, they put the watering can back in the blue bin. Milo made a small sign with a crayon: “Water Tools.” He stuck it on the bin. He made two more signs: “Digging Tools” and “Plant Things.” He drew little pictures next to the words. A droplet, a shovel, and a flower.

Grandma Lila smiled wide. “This will help us next time.”

Milo nodded. “And if I need help, I will say so. I can be brave and kind to myself.”

The shed door closed with a soft “click,” not a creak. The garden felt calm and bright. Milo’s heart felt calm and bright too.

The End.
