# Ellie on the Rooftop Garden

Ellie was a gentle elephant calf with soft gray ears and a curious trunk. One sunny morning, the wind smelled like wet leaves and sweet flowers. Ellie climbed the wide, safe steps to a rooftop garden. Her hooves made a soft tap-tap on the steps. The rooftop felt warm under her feet, and a soft breeze tickled her ears.

On the rooftop, there were bright green plants, tiny trees, and a blue rain barrel glistening in the sun. Bees buzzed. Birds chirped. The city below hummed quietly. Ellie loved this rooftop garden. It was a little home for plants and animals. It was a place to help nature, even in the big city.

“Good morning, little leaves,” Ellie whispered. She blew a gentle puff of air from her trunk. The leaves shook like they were giggling.

Just then, a small sparrow hopped from a bush. “Cheep! Cheep! Cheep!” the sparrow cried. The bird’s wings trembled.

“What’s wrong?” asked Ellie. She lowered her head to be close and calm. “I’m listening.”

The sparrow hopped in a circle. “Cheep! Cheep! My nest is gone!”

Ellie looked around. She saw twigs scattered by the rain barrel. Some straw, some tiny feathers, and a shiny button were on the ground.

“Oh,” Ellie said softly. “You are feeling worried. Your body is shaking. Your voice is fast. It is okay to feel sad. I am here.”

The sparrow took a wobbly breath. “Cheep. Thank you.”

Ellie blinked her kind eyes. “Let’s talk,” she said. “Talking helps us share feelings. When we share, we can find a plan.”

The sparrow nodded. “Cheep. I feel scared. I feel lonely.”

Ellie listened. She did not speak over the sparrow. She waited. She watched the bird’s tiny chest rise and fall.

Ellie looked at the garden. The wind had been strong last night. She could still smell rain in the air. “Maybe the wind blew the nest down,” Ellie said. “We can fix it together. We can keep it safe.”

“Together?” the sparrow asked, tilting its head.

“Yes,” said Ellie. “I will use gentle words. I will use careful steps. You can tell me what you need.”

The sparrow hopped closer. “Cheep. I need a dry place. I need soft things. I need a strong spot that does not shake.”

Ellie smiled. “Good telling. That is helpful. Let’s find a spot.”

They looked at the garden. By the corner was a small tree in a pot, with sturdy branches. Near the center was a tall planter with a bush that smelled like lemons. By the rain barrel, there were wide leaves that waved like fans.

Ellie touched each with her trunk. The tree was strong but wobbly in the wind. The lemon bush was cozy and did not move much. The wide leaves were too floppy.

“The lemon bush,” Ellie said, sniffing. “It smells fresh. It is steady.”

The sparrow chirped softly. “Cheep. Yes.”

Ellie placed the scattered twigs in a little pile. “We will reuse. We will not waste,” she said. “We take care of nature.” She felt proud of helping the garden. She felt the cool twigs under her trunk. They made a little scratchy sound.

“Cheep-cheep,” said the sparrow. “I can find soft fluff.” The bird flew up and came back with bits of dry grass and a tiny piece of clean string. The string shone in the sun like a thin rainbow.

Ellie lowered her body so the sparrow could reach the lemon bush. “I will be still,” Ellie said. “Tell me if I am too close.”

“You are okay,” the sparrow said. “Thank you for asking.”

They worked slowly. Ellie tucked twigs gently. The sparrow pressed the soft grass. Ellie hummed a low, calm tune. The garden seemed to hum with her. Bees buzzed along. A butterfly with bright yellow wings floated by, like a soft, dancing petal. The air smelled like lemons and wet earth.

Soon, the nest looked round and snug. Ellie tested it by blowing a tiny puff. It did not wobble.

“Cheep! It is perfect,” sang the sparrow. “But I am still scared the wind will come again.”

Ellie nodded. “Thank you for telling me your feeling. Let us make a plan. Plans help us feel safe.”

They looked around. Ellie saw the blue rain barrel. “If we save rainwater,” Ellie said, “we can water the plants gently. Strong roots make strong branches. Strong branches hold nests.” She filled a small watering can with her trunk and dribbled water like a thin ribbon. The sparrow watched, eyes bright.

“Cheep,” said the sparrow. “I can tell other birds not to pull leaves for fun.”

“And I can ask children who visit to talk softly and walk slowly,” said Ellie. “We will use kind voices. We will listen to the garden.”

The wind picked up, and the leaves whispered. Ellie felt a little flutter in her tummy. She put a big ear over her heart and took a slow breath in and out. “I feel a bit nervous too,” she said. “Breathing helps me. Would you like to breathe with me?”

The sparrow puffed its tiny chest and breathed in and out. “Cheep.” The bird’s wings relaxed.

Together, they watched the nest sway just a little and then stay still. The lemon smell hugged the air. The sun warmed their backs.

“Thank you for listening,” said the sparrow. “Thank you for helping me talk about my feelings and make a plan.”

“Thank you for telling me what you needed,” said Ellie. “When we talk and listen, we can help each other and help nature too.”

They sat quietly, feeling proud and peaceful. The city hummed below, and the rooftop garden felt like a safe, green hug.

Ellie smiled. “We used our words. We used our hearts. We cared for the earth.”

The sparrow chirped a happy song. “Cheep-cheep-cheep!”

And the nest stayed safe.

The End.
