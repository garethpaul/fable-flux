# Bounce, Joey, Bounce!

In a busy basement full of boxes and buzzing noises, a joyful kangaroo joey named Kiki bounced on her soft tail. “Boing! Boing!” she sang. The air smelled like clean soap and warm dust. A washing machine hummed and spun. A small heater clicked and ticked. Light strings twinkled like tiny stars.

Kiki’s mom called from the stairs, “Kiki, can you help me tidy, please?”

“I can try!” Kiki said, her ears perking up. She loved to help. She loved to be kind. She loved to bounce, too.

Kiki looked around. Toys were scattered on the floor. Socks lay in little piles. A big box of buttons sat open on a low shelf. The buttons were shiny and colorful, like candies. “Ooo,” Kiki whispered. “So many circles!”

Then—oops! Kiki bumped the shelf with her tail. The box tipped. Buttons spilled like rain. Tinka, tinka, tink! They rolled under the table. They rolled near the humming washer. They rolled like tiny beetles across the smooth floor.

Kiki’s eyes went wide. “Oh no,” she said softly. Her belly felt wiggly. Her ears drooped. “I made a big mess.”

Kiki stood very still. She could hide. She could call for Mom. She could cry. But she took a deep breath. She listened to the hum-hum-hum and the tick-tick-tick. The sounds felt steady and calm.

“I can fix it,” Kiki said. “I can be brave. I can be kind.”

Kiki bounced once. “First, I will make a plan.” She looked around for help. She saw a little dustpan. She saw a soft paintbrush. She saw a bright red bucket. The bucket had a big smile sticker. It looked friendly.

“I will use you,” Kiki told the bucket. “We can work together.” She set the bucket on the floor. Tap.

Kiki got down low. Her fur brushed the cool ground. She could smell the lemon soap. She could feel the smooth floor under her paws. She gently swept buttons with the paintbrush. Swish, swish. “Come here, little friends,” she said. “I will keep you safe.”

A tiny mouse peeked from behind a box. “Squeak?” it asked.

“Hello,” said Kiki. “I made a mess. I am cleaning up. Do you want to help?”

The mouse nodded and nudged a button closer. Plink. Kiki smiled. “Thank you! That is kind.”

A spider lowered on a silky string. It waved a leg. “Hello,” said Kiki. “You can watch. I will be careful.”

Some buttons were under the table, where it was dark like a cave. Kiki felt a little scared. She took another deep breath. “I am brave,” she whispered. “I can do hard things.” She scooted under the table. Her whiskers tickled the wood. Her tail made a soft shoop sound. She found three blue buttons and a tiny heart-shaped button. “I found you,” she said. “You are not lost now.”

Kiki brushed and picked and plopped the buttons into the bucket. Plink, plink, plink. Each sound made her feel better. The bucket grew heavy. Kiki’s legs felt tired, but she kept going. “I can rest, then try again,” she told herself. She took a small sip of water from her cup. Sip. “Okay. I can do more.”

One last button rolled near the humming washer. It was a shiny gold one. Kiki reached carefully. The washer hummed warm air on her paw. “Almost,” she said. The button slipped. Kiki did not give up. She used the paintbrush to guide it. Swish. The button slid into her paw. “Got it!” she cheered.

Mom walked down the stairs. Step, step. She looked at the bucket. She looked at the tidy floor. “Kiki,” Mom said, “what happened here?”

Kiki’s ears wiggled. “I bumped the box,” she said. “The buttons fell. I felt scared. But I made a plan. I cleaned up. The mouse helped. The spider watched.” She giggled.

Mom knelt and hugged Kiki. The hug felt warm and safe. “I am proud of you,” Mom said. “You told the truth. You showed kindness to our little helper. You did not give up. That is called resilience. And you started by yourself. That is called initiative.”

Kiki’s eyes shone. “Resi…li…ence,” she tried. “And ini…sh—”

“Initiative,” Mom said, smiling. “It means you saw a problem and began to fix it.”

Kiki nodded. “I like those words. They feel strong.”

Together, Kiki and Mom poured the buttons back into the box. Tinkle, tinkle. Mom put the box higher on the shelf. Kiki placed the brush and dustpan back where they belonged. The basement looked neat. It smelled like lemon and clean laundry. The light strings still twinkled like tiny stars.

Kiki patted the red bucket. “Thank you,” she said. She waved to the mouse. “Thank you.” The mouse squeaked and zipped behind a box.

Mom took Kiki’s paws. “What did we learn today?”

Kiki thought. “Be kind,” she said. “Tell the truth. Make a plan. Try, rest, try again. Ask for help. Help others.”

Mom smiled. “Yes. Kindness makes hard things softer. Resilience keeps us going. Initiative gets us started.”

Kiki bounced once, gently. “Boing! Boing! I can do it.”

“Yes,” said Mom. “You can.”

The End.
