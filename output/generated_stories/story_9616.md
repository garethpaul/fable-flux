# Buttons the Rabbit Waits His Turn

Buttons the rabbit woke up in his cozy bedroom. His room smelled like fresh hay and warm carrot muffins from the kitchen. Soft sunlight made stripey patterns on the quilt. Buttons wiggled his whiskers and whispered, “Today, I will build a pillow fort. I can do it!”

He was a determined rabbit. When Buttons had a plan, he tried and tried. He pulled the big blue pillow. He stacked the small green cushion. He set a blanket roof and patted it smooth. “Plip, plop, perfect,” he said, feeling proud.

Just then, his little sister, Dot, bounced in. Her ears flopped, and her paws were muddy from hopping outside. “Fort! Fort!” she squeaked. She loved forts too.

Buttons took a deep breath. He remembered that calm breaths felt like warm tea in his tummy. In and out. Slowly. “Hi, Dot,” he said kindly. “I am building. Do you want to help?”

Dot clapped. “Yes! I put blanket!” She grabbed the blanket and tugged. The roof slid. The fort wobbled. The green cushion fell with a soft thump.

Buttons felt his cheeks get hot. His ears twitched. He wanted to shout, “No! Not like that!” But he paused. He remembered a gentle rule: Tolerance means we accept others, even when they do things differently. He whispered to himself, “Calm and kind. We can take turns.”

“Dot,” Buttons said, keeping his voice soft like a pillow, “let’s take turns. I will show, and then you can try. We can do it together.”

Dot nodded. “Take turns,” she repeated, smiling.

Buttons showed Dot how to hold the blanket by the corners. “Like holding butterfly wings,” he said. The blanket felt cool and smooth on his paws. “Now it’s your turn.”

Dot copied him. She held the corners carefully. “Light and soft,” she said. They lifted together. Swoosh! The blanket floated down, and they made a perfect roof.

“Great job!” Buttons cheered. He felt proud of Dot and proud of himself for staying calm.

Soon, their friend, Tico the turtle, tapped at the door. Tap, tap. “May I come in?” he asked. Tico moved slowly and smiled shyly. He carried a shiny blue button he found in the yard.

“Come play,” Buttons said. “We are building a fort.”

Tico looked at the fort and said, “My shell is big. I might bump it.” He frowned a little.

Buttons blinked. He remembered to be tolerant and kind. “That’s okay,” Buttons said. “We can make the doorway bigger for you.” He pointed to the front. “I will move this pillow. Then you can go inside safely.”

Dot nodded. “We help,” she said.

Together, they scooted the big pillow. It made a squeaky sound on the floor. Eeeep! Tico giggled. “That sound tickles my ears,” he said.

“Let’s take turns going through the doorway,” Buttons suggested. “First me, then Dot, then Tico.” He held up three soft paws. “One, two, three.”

They took turns, calm and patient. Buttons went first—shuff, shuff—his feet whispering on the rug. Dot followed—pitter-patter. Tico went last—tap, tap—slow and careful. No bumps!

Inside the fort, it was cozy and dim. The blanket roof smelled like clean laundry. A little patch of sun made a golden square on the floor. Buttons set out toy carrots and tiny books. “Story time,” he said.

“I want to read first!” Dot shouted, bouncing. The fort shook.

Buttons touched his chest and breathed in. Warm tea tummy. He smiled at Dot. “Let’s listen and share,” he said. “We can take turns reading. I go first this time, then you, then Tico. Is that fair?”

Dot’s ears drooped, then perked back up. “Fair,” she said.

Buttons read a short story about a brave leaf floating on a pond. He used a gentle voice. Tico listened with bright eyes. Dot waited, hugging a pillow. When Buttons finished, he handed the book to Dot. “Your turn.”

Dot read in her own way, pointing at pictures. “Leaf splish-splash,” she said. She made little water sounds with her tongue. Splish, splash! They all laughed.

Then Tico showed his shiny blue button. “I want to make a doorbell,” he said. “May I add it to the fort?”

Buttons looked at the fort and thought. He liked his plan, but he also liked Tico’s idea. Tolerance means new ideas can join our plans. “Yes,” Buttons said. “We can put it by the doorway.”

Tico pressed the button into the blanket corner. It made a soft click. “Ding,” he said. Dot giggled. “Ding-dong!”

They played ding-dong-doorbell. They took turns ringing and answering, using soft voices and kind words.

After a while, Mama Rabbit called from the kitchen. “Snack time!”

They cleaned up together. Buttons lifted the blanket carefully. Dot carried the little books. Tico pushed the green cushion back with his steady shell. They worked slowly and talked calmly. “My turn,” “Your turn,” “Thank you,” they said.

At the door, Buttons felt happy and light. He had built his fort. He had shared it. He had listened. He had helped his friends feel welcome.

He patted his heart. “Today I learned something,” he said. “Tolerance means I can stay calm, take turns, and accept different ways. When I do, we all have fun.”

Dot hugged him. “Calm and kind,” she said.

Tico smiled. “Together is best,” he added.

Buttons’ cozy bedroom felt extra warm now, like sunshine on soft fur. He wiggled his whiskers and grinned. “Tomorrow, we can build again,” he said. “And we will share, listen, and take turns.”

They all nodded. Ding-dong! The pretend doorbell rang one more time, and they laughed.

The End.
