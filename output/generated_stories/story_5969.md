# Pippa Penguin's Feelings Blanket

In a cozy bedroom with soft blue walls, a cheerful penguin named Pippa wiggled her toes under a puffy blanket. The blanket felt like warm clouds. The night-light made gentle stars on the ceiling. The air smelled like clean soap and a little bit like cocoa.

Pippa hugged her favorite pillow. It was shaped like a tiny globe, with green lands and blue water. “Hello, World Pillow,” she whispered. “Today was big.”

Her tummy felt twisty. Her cheeks felt hot. Her wings felt wiggly. Pippa didn’t like those feelings. “I want to feel calm,” she said softly. “But I don’t know how.”

Mama Penguin peeked in. “Good night, Pippa.” Mama’s voice was like a lullaby.

Pippa hesitated. “Mama, my tummy is fluttery. I feel… I don’t know. I feel lots.”

Mama waddled in and sat on the bed, making the blanket rustle. “Lots of feelings?” she asked kindly.

Pippa nodded. “Yes. Today at preschool, we painted flags from many places. Mine smeared. I felt sad. Then I got loud. I said, ‘Stop!’ when my friend splashed my water cup. My words came out too big. Then I felt… oops.”

Mama nodded. “Thank you for telling me. Your feelings are important. Your words are important too.”

Pippa took a deep breath. In. Out. The air felt cool in her beak. “But how do I know which feeling is which?”

Mama smiled. “We can use our Feelings Blanket.” She patted the puffy blanket. “Each corner is a feeling. This corner is Happy—soft like a feather. This one is Sad—cool like the sheets. This one is Mad—warm like cocoa. This one is Scared—shivery like the night air. Touch and tell me what you feel.”

Pippa slid her flipper to the cool corner. “Sad,” she said. Her eyes felt watery.

She pressed the warm corner. “And a little Mad,” she added. Her flippers squeezed her pillow.

“Good noticing,” said Mama. “That is emotional-awareness—naming feelings. When we name them, we can help them.”

Pippa blinked. “How do we help them?”

Mama held up her flippers. “Step one: Stop and breathe. Step two: Name the feeling. Step three: Choose what to do.”

Pippa whispered, “That is using logic. Step, step, step.” She wiggled her toes.

They breathed together. In for three, out for three. The room sounded quiet and safe. The night-light stars seemed to twinkle slower.

“Now,” Mama said gently, “what can you do?”

Pippa thought. “At school, when my flag smeared, I felt sad. Next time, I can say, ‘I feel sad. I need a dry brush, please.’ That is self-advocacy—asking for what I need.”

Mama smiled big. “Yes.”

“And when my friend splashed, I felt mad,” Pippa went on. “I can say, ‘Please keep the water in your cup,’ in a small, strong voice. Not too loud. Just right.”

Mama nodded. “That is also kind. We use our words to help, not hurt.”

Pippa hugged her globe pillow. She traced a little island with her flipper. “We painted many flags. Some I didn’t know. My friend wears a scarf from far away. She taught me hello in her language. I can say hello back. That is global citizenship—learning about our big world and being respectful.”

Mama’s eyes shone. “Our world is full of friends. We listen. We learn. We care.”

Pippa’s tummy felt less twisty. The blanket felt extra soft. “Can we practice?” she asked.

“Let’s try,” Mama said. She picked up Pippa’s cup from the bedside table and pretended to splash. “Splash!”

Pippa took a breath. “I feel mad,” she said in a small, strong voice. “Please keep the water in your cup.”

Mama put the cup down. “Thank you for telling me how you feel and what you need.”

Pippa smiled. “Now the flag.” She dipped her finger in the air like paint. “Oops, smear!” she said.

Pippa breathed. “I feel sad. I need a dry brush, please.”

Mama pretended to hand her a brush. “Here you go.”

Pippa giggled. The giggle felt like tiny bells. “This is working!”

They made a tiny plan. Pippa drew three dots on a sticky note with her crayon: Stop. Name. Choose. She stuck it by her night-light. “My logic steps,” she said proudly.

Mama tucked the blanket around Pippa’s shoulders. “You listened to your body, used your words, and made a plan. That is brave and smart.”

Pippa yawned. The room smelled warm and sleepy. The globe pillow felt cool and smooth. “Tomorrow,” she murmured, “I will say hello in my friend’s language. I will use my small, strong voice. I will remember my dots.”

Mama kissed her head. “The whole world is in your heart, little penguin.”

Pippa closed her eyes. She whispered to her pillow, “Feelings can be big. I can be calm. I can be kind.” The stars on the ceiling seemed to nod yes.

In the cozy bedroom, Pippa drifted to sleep, feeling safe, understood, and ready for tomorrow.

The clear lesson: When feelings get big, stop and breathe, name the feeling, and choose kind, helpful words. We can care for ourselves and for our big, beautiful world.

The End.
