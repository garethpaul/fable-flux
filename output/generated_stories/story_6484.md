# Roary’s Calm Paw Plan

The home office hummed like a soft bee. The computer screen glowed. Papers made neat little mountains. A tiny fan whirred, sending a cool breeze that smelled like fresh paper and lemon spray.

In the doorway stood Roary, a brave little lion cub. His fur was golden and fluffy, and his whiskers twitched with excitement. “I’m ready to help!” he said. His tail flicked like a small flag.

Mama Lion sat at the desk. “Hi, Roary,” she said warmly. “I’m sending emails and talking to neighbors about our park clean-up. That’s civic responsibility—helping our community. Would you like an office helper job?”

“Yes!” Roary bounced on his paws. “I can stamp letters!”

Mama slid a stamp pad toward him. “Press gently,” she said. “Practice makes strong skills.”

Roary pressed the first stamp too hard. SPLAT! Red ink smeared across the envelope. The next one slipped from his paw. FLOP! It landed on the floor. His cheeks felt hot. His ears pressed flat.

“I can’t do it,” he growled. His chest felt tight, like a drum. “I’m mad!”

Mama Lion looked up. “I see your angry face,” she said calmly. “Anger is a big feeling. Let’s use our Calm Paw Plan.”

Roary huffed. He puffed. “What is the Calm Paw Plan again?”

Mama held up one gentle paw. “Step one: Stop and notice. Say, ‘I feel mad.’ Step two: Breathe in like you smell a flower. Breathe out like you blow a bubble. Step three: Use your words to ask for help. Step four: Practice slowly.”

Roary stomped his paw once. “I feel mad,” he said, his voice shaky. He sniffed in: in… in… like sniffing a sweet flower. He blew out: out… out… like making a big, round bubble. He felt his shoulders drop and his paws relax.

Mama smiled. “Good breathing.”

Roary took another breath. Then he said, “Mama, I need help, please.”

“Thank you for telling me,” Mama said. “Let’s practice together. Gentle paws. Light touch.”

Roary pressed the stamp with Mama’s paw guiding his. Thip! A perfect little paw print. He giggled. “It worked!”

They tried another. Thip! Still good. Roary’s tail swished. “I want to try one by myself.”

“Go ahead,” Mama said.

Roary lined up the stamp. He squeezed his eyes in focus. Thip! Perfect again. He beamed. “I did it!”

Just then, the computer chimed: beep-beep! A message popped up. “Oh,” said Mama, reading. “The city team will come pick up trash at the park if we finish our letter stamps today.”

Roary pricked his ears. “That helps our neighbors!” He puffed his chest. “I am brave. I can keep going.”

He stamped carefully. Thip. Thip. Thip. But then his paw slipped. SQUISH. Ink smeared on his fur. It felt cold and sticky. Roary’s nose wrinkled. His tummy flipped.

“I’m mad again!” he shouted. His tail lashed. His eyes filled with hot tears.

Mama nodded and pointed to the small poster on the wall. It showed simple pictures: a stop sign, a flower, a bubble, a talking face, a paw practicing. “What can you do?” she asked softly.

Roary looked at the poster. He whispered, “Stop.” He placed both paws flat on the desk. “I feel mad.” He breathed in, smelling lemon and paper. He breathed out, making a quiet bubble sound. He wiped his tears with a soft tissue. “Mama, can I have a wet wipe, please?”

“Yes,” Mama said, handing him one. The wipe felt cool and smooth. Roary cleaned his paw. “Thank you for asking with your words.”

Roary tried again, slower this time. Thip. Thip. Thip. The stamps lined up like tiny paw soldiers. His heart felt light and warm.

After the last envelope, Roary helped carry them to the front door. The envelopes rustled, and the stamps looked neat and bright. He listened to the fan hum and the birds chirp outside. His anger was gone, like a cloud blown away.

Mama rubbed his mane. “Roary, you practiced. You used your Calm Paw Plan. You asked for help. That’s great anger management and great communication.”

Roary purred softly. “And we helped the park. That’s civic responsibility!”

Mama nodded. “Helping our community starts at home, with small jobs and kind choices. When we keep calm and talk, we can do big things together.”

Roary smiled a big lion smile. “I’m a helper. I’m brave. When I feel mad, I can stop, breathe, and speak.”

They placed a gold star sticker on a chart labeled Practice. The sticker crinkled and shone like a tiny sun.

“Ready to send the letters?” Mama asked.

Roary stood tall. “Ready!” He took a deep breath, just because it felt good. The office still smelled like lemon and paper, but now it also smelled like pride.

The End.
