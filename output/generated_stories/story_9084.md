# Bella Bird Sings for a Snack

Bella was a small singing bird who lived by the window of a bright home office. The office smelled like warm paper and pencil shavings. Sunlight made soft squares on the floor. The keyboard clicked like tiny raindrops. Tick-tock went the clock. Bella loved to sing along.

“Tweet-tee-tweeet!” sang Bella. “Good morning, desk! Hello, chair! Hello, big books with big words!”

Bella especially loved the books. She liked the feel of their smooth pages under her little feet. She would hop from one book to another and sing new sounds. “La-la, lo-lo, letter L!” she trilled. Words felt like music.

One morning, Bella felt tired and grumpy. Her tummy made a small rumble. “Grum-grum,” it said. Bella tried to sing, but her song came out wobbly. “Tweeee… oh,” she sighed. Her wings drooped.

In the chair sat Sam, who worked in the home office. Sam wore soft blue socks and bright glasses. Sam listened to Bella’s wobbly song.

“Hmm,” said Sam. “Bella, your music sounds sleepy. Are you okay?”

Bella puffed her tiny chest. “I want a sweet cookie,” she chirped. “A cookie will make my song shiny!”

Sam smiled kindly. “Cookies are yummy, but not the best snack for singing strength,” Sam said. “Let’s find good fuel for your notes.”

“Fuel?” asked Bella. She tilted her head. “Like a car?”

“Kind of,” said Sam. “Food is fuel for bodies and voices. Good food helps you sing clear and strong.”

Bella’s tummy agreed with a louder rumble. “Grum-grum-GRUM!”

Sam opened a little lunch box. Inside were colorful foods: crunchy carrot sticks, round blueberries, a slice of whole-grain bread, a little spoon of nut butter, and a cup of cool water with lemon. The colors were bright—orange, blue, golden brown, sunny yellow. The air smelled fresh and lemony.

Bella hopped closer. “Oh! They look like a rainbow,” she said softly. “But I don’t know what to pick. I still want a cookie.”

Sam nodded. “It’s okay to want treats. But let’s understand what each food does. Understanding helps us choose.”

Bella blinked. “Teach me with a song?”

Sam clapped gently. “Yes! A food song.” Sam tapped the desk like a drum. Tappa-tap.

“Crunch-crunch carrots help eyes see bright.
Blue-berry bubbles make mornings light.
Grainy bread gives wings a steady beat.
Nut butter makes strong notes—warm and sweet.
Sip-sip water keeps the song clear.
Good food helps music that all can hear!”

Bella’s eyes sparkled. “I like that!” She tried the carrots first. Crunch! They snapped like tiny sticks. “Crunch-crunch!” She chewed and felt awake. Then she pecked a blueberry. Pop! Sweet juice tickled her tongue. “Mmm,” she hummed. She nibbled the bread with a dab of nut butter. It was soft, nutty, and cozy, like a warm nest. She sipped the cool lemon water. “Ahh!”

Sam listened. “How does your body feel?”

Bella fluttered her wings. “Light and strong. My tummy is happy.” She took a deep breath. The office smelled like lemon and paper and sunshine.

“Try your song now,” said Sam.

Bella stood tall on the window ledge. She opened her beak. The notes floated out, clear and bright. “Tweet-tee-tweeet! La-la-lo! Hello, letters, words, and books! My music is back!”

Sam clapped. “Beautiful! Your good food helped your beautiful voice.”

Bella tilted her head. “I learned new word music too,” she chirped. “Fuel, crunchy, steady. Words can be tasty for the mind!”

Sam chuckled. “Yes. We can taste words with our ears. That is language. We can understand what foods do by listening to the words.”

Bella nodded. “Understanding helps me choose. I can say, ‘Carrots for eyes, berries for bright, grains for steady, nuts for strong, water for clear.’”

Sam gave a thumbs-up. “That’s a great plan. And sometimes, a small cookie can be a treat. But good food comes first.”

Bella thought about the cookie. She still liked cookies. But she liked her strong song more. She puffed her chest and sang a soft thank-you. “Tweee-thank you, Sam.”

The clock went tick-tock, tick-tock. The keyboard went click-click. The lemon water went sip-sip. Bella sang along with the office sounds. She made a new little language song for herself, tapping the window with her tiny feet.

“Listen and learn,
taste and see,
good food helps
the best of me!”

Later, a friend, a tiny moth, fluttered by the window. “Your song is bright!” buzzed the moth. “What changed?”

Bella smiled. “I ate rainbow foods. I learned new words. I used understanding to choose. Now my song feels clear.”

The moth twirled. “I want a bright song too!”

Bella shared the food song, one gentle drum tap at a time. The moth hummed along. They laughed, they sang, and they both sipped water.

That night, as the sun went soft and the room turned golden, Bella tucked her head under her wing. Her tummy felt calm. Her heart felt proud. She whispered, “Good food first. Treats sometimes. Words help me choose.”

And in the quiet home office, the little singing bird drifted to sleep, ready for more music tomorrow.

The End.
