# Toby Turtle Shares the Window

Toby was a shy turtle with a smooth, green shell. He liked to tuck his head in when things felt big and loud. Today, Toby was riding in the back seat of the family car with Mama, Papa, and his big sister, Lila. The car hummed and purred. The seat was soft. The air smelled like orange snacks and a little like fresh rain.

“We’re going to Grandma’s house,” Mama said. “Click, click—seatbelts on!”

Toby clicked his belt and peeped out the window. He loved the window. Outside, trees waved like they were saying hello. Birds swooped and sang, peep-peep. Sunlight made tiny sparkles on the glass. Toby pressed his little turtle nose up and felt the cool window on his skin.

“I want the window seat,” Lila said. “I want to see everything!”

Toby’s heart did a slow thump. He liked the window too. He wanted to say, “I want it,” but his words hid inside his shell.

Mama looked in the mirror. “We can share,” she said. “Let’s be kind.”

Toby pulled into his shell a little. Share? But what if he couldn’t see the sparkly trees? What if he lost his favorite spot?

The car rolled along. Swish, swish went the wipers as a light rain began. The drops made tiny tap-tap sounds. Toby watched little rivers run down the glass. He felt calm. Then he heard Lila sigh.

“I can’t see the rain,” Lila said softly.

Toby peeked at his sister. Lila’s hands were folded. Her smile was upside down. Toby felt a warm squeeze in his heart. He was curious. What if sharing made two smiles?

He took a small breath. “L-Lila?” he whispered.

Lila turned. “Yes, Toby?”

“You can—um—have a turn,” Toby said, his voice like a tiny bell. “We can take turns. I can scoot. You can sit by the window for a little bit.”

Lila’s eyes got bright. “Really?” she asked.

Toby nodded. “Really.”

Papa smiled. “That is very generous, Toby.”

Toby scooted carefully toward the middle. The seat made a soft squeak. Lila moved to the window. The cool air from the little vent brushed Toby’s cheeks like a feather. He missed the sparkles, but then—oh! From the middle, he could see both windows. He could see raindrops race on one side and a big red truck on the other. Vroom!

Lila pressed her nose to the glass and giggled. “The raindrops are tickling my eyes!”

Toby giggled too. The car smelled like wet pavement now, and the tires made a soft shhh sound. He felt the seat’s gentle bounce as they drove over tiny bumps. His shell felt safe and warm.

After a while, Papa asked, “Who would like a snack break?”

“Me!” said Lila.

“Me too,” Toby said.

Mama passed a little bag of orange crackers. “We have one bag. How can we make sure everyone gets some?” she asked.

Toby looked at the bag. He remembered how it felt when Lila shared the window smile with him. He wanted to make that feeling again.

“We can share,” he said. He opened the bag slowly. The crackers smelled cheesy and yummy. “One for Lila, one for me,” he said, placing them carefully into their little hands. Crunch, crunch!

“Thank you, Toby,” Lila said.

“You, too,” Toby said, and he meant it. His shy heart felt big and brave.

Soon the rain stopped. Sunlight peeked out. Lila looked at Toby and scooted back from the window. “Your turn again,” she said. “You were generous to me. I will be generous to you.”

Toby smiled. He moved back to the window. The glass was warm now, and he saw a rainbow curve over the road, red to violet, like a bright bridge in the sky. “Wow,” he breathed.

“Beautiful,” Mama said. “Thank you both for being respectful and generous. You listened to each other. You took turns. That shows kindness.”

Toby felt proud. He thought about how his curious idea to share made more fun for everyone. He thought about the crackers and how good it felt to hand one to Lila first. He thought about the rainbow and how sharing gave him a rainbow smile inside.

When they reached Grandma’s house, the car stopped with a gentle bump. Toby unclicked his belt. “Grandma!” he called as he climbed out slowly. The driveway smelled like wet grass and cookies. Grandma opened the door, and warm cinnamon air floated out.

“My little turtles!” she said, hugging them. “I made cookies. One plate. How shall we eat them?”

Toby looked at Lila and grinned. “We can share,” they said together.

Grandma clapped. “That is generous and respectful.”

They sat at the kitchen table. Toby broke a cookie in half and gave the bigger piece to Lila. Lila broke hers and gave the bigger piece to Toby. They laughed. Crumbs tickled their fingers. Outside, a tiny drip-drop from the roof made a gentle song.

Toby’s shy felt smaller now. His generous felt bigger. He learned that sharing the window made more to see, sharing snacks made them taste better, and sharing smiles made hearts warm.

On the way home, Toby said, “I like taking turns.”

“We do too,” Mama and Papa said.

Toby pressed his nose to the window once more. “Thank you for my turn,” he said to Lila.

“Thank you for yours,” Lila said back.

And the car hummed happily down the road.

The End.
