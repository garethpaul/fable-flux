# Polly's Fair Share Adventure

Polly the parrot loved car rides with her family. Her bright green feathers sparkled in the sunlight streaming through the windows, and she could see the whole wide world rushing by.

"Look, look!" squawked Polly excitedly, pressing her orange beak against the cool glass. "I see a playground!"

The Johnson family was driving to visit Grandma, and they had packed a special snack bag for the long trip. Polly's tummy rumbled as she smelled the sweet crackers and juicy apple slices.

"Can I have a snack, please?" asked Polly politely.

Mom reached into the bag and gave Polly one small cracker. But then she gave Tommy, their 5-year-old son, three crackers and two apple slices.

Polly's colorful feathers drooped. "That's not fair!" she chirped sadly. "Tommy got more than me. Why don't I get the same amount?"

Dad looked in the rearview mirror with kind eyes. "Polly, I understand you feel upset. Let's talk about this."

"But I'm hungry too!" Polly flapped her wings. "We should all get the same!"

Tommy looked at his snacks, then at Polly's one small cracker. His face scrunched up as he thought hard. "You know what, Polly? You're right. That doesn't seem fair."

Mom smiled warmly. "You're both being very thoughtful. Polly, fairness is important, but it can be tricky. Let me explain."

She turned around to face them both. "Tommy is bigger than you, Polly, so he needs more food to grow strong. But you're absolutely right that everyone should be treated with kindness and respect."

Polly tilted her bright red head. "So fair doesn't always mean exactly the same?"

"That's right!" said Dad. "Fair means everyone gets what they need. Like how you get special perches in your cage that fit your feet, and Tommy gets a big bed that fits his body."

Polly thought about this as she nibbled her cracker. The salty, crunchy taste helped her think. "Ohhh, I see! Like how in different countries, people eat different foods that help them stay healthy!"

"Exactly!" beamed Mom. "You're so smart, Polly. In Japan, children might eat rice and fish. In Mexico, they might eat beans and corn. Different, but all healthy and fair."

Tommy nodded eagerly. "And some kids need glasses to see, and some don't. That's fair too!"

Polly's feathers brightened up again. "I'm learning so much! But I still feel a little hungry."

Tommy smiled and broke one of his crackers in half. "Here, Polly. I want to share because sharing is part of being fair too."

"Thank you, Tommy!" Polly chirped happily. "You're the best brother ever!"

As they continued their journey, Polly watched the world outside with new understanding. She saw tall buildings and small houses, different kinds of cars, and people of all sizes walking around.

"Everyone is different, but everyone deserves to be treated fairly," she said thoughtfully.

"That's the most important lesson of all," said Grandma's voice from the phone speaker. They had called to say they were almost there. "I can't wait to hear about your learning adventure, Polly!"

Polly preened her beautiful feathers proudly. She had learned that fairness wasn't about getting exactly the same things, but about everyone being treated with kindness and getting what they needed to be happy and healthy.

"I love learning new things!" she squawked joyfully as they pulled into Grandma's driveway.

The End.
