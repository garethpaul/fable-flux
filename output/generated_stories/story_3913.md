# Tully Turtle Makes Toast

Tully Turtle was shy. He liked quiet corners and soft sounds. His shell felt extra cozy in the warm kitchen. The morning sun made the butter dish shine. The toaster hummed a tiny tune. The air smelled like cinnamon and warm bread.

Tully peeked over the counter. “I want to make my own toast,” he whispered. His voice was small, like a little bubble. He had always asked for help. Today, he wanted to try by himself.

He took a deep breath. In…and out. “I can do small steps,” Tully said. “Small steps are safe steps.”

He looked around. The kitchen was friendly. The wooden spoon felt smooth. The towel was fluffy. The toaster sat on the counter, quiet and cool.

“First, I get the bread,” Tully told himself. “Safe hands, slow hands.” He opened the bread bag with careful fingers. “Crunch, crinkle,” the bag whispered. Two soft slices landed on a plate. They felt squishy and light.

Tully looked at the toaster. He remembered the kitchen rules on the fridge. He read them slowly.
“Number one: Ask, ‘Is it safe?’
Number two: Keep shells and hands dry.
Number three: Use tools, not paws, for hot things.
Number four: Go slow. Think first.”

Tully nodded. “Is it safe?” He touched the counter. It was cool. He looked at the cord. It was not tangled. He felt his paws. They were dry. “Okay,” he said. “I’m ready.”

He slid the bread into the toaster slots. “Plop, plop.” He checked the dial. “Not too high,” he thought. “I like golden, not dark.” He chose a middle click. He pressed the lever. “Click.” The slices sank down.

Tully waited. Waiting felt wiggly. His tummy did a tiny dance. “What if it pops too loud?” he worried. He tucked into his shell a little. Then he peeked out. “I can be brave and careful,” he told himself.

To help the waiting, Tully planned. “What else do I need?” He looked at the butter, the strawberry jam, and the child-safe butter spreader. He laid a napkin on the table. He set a small plate in the middle. He put the spreader on the edge. “This is my toast station,” he said proudly.

He sniffed the air. Toast smells floated up—warm, cozy, a little sweet. Pop! The toast jumped. Tully startled, then giggled. “Hello, toast.”

“Hot things need tools, not paws,” he reminded himself. He used wooden tongs to lift each slice. “Tap, tap,” the tongs touched the toast. He placed the pieces gently on the plate. The toast felt toasty-warm and crisp at the edges.

“Butter first,” he said. He scooped a small bit with the spreader. The butter felt cool and creamy. It melted into shiny little puddles. “Swish, swish,” went the spreader. “Now jam!” He opened the jar slowly—“pop!” The sweet smell made his nose wiggle. He spread a pink heart on one slice. “For courage,” he smiled.

He took a tiny bite. Crunch. Warmth spread in his mouth. He felt proud. His heart felt big and light, like a bright balloon.

Then Tully noticed crumbs on the counter. “Independence means cleaning up, too,” he said. He shook out the towel and wiped gently. “Swipe, swipe.” He closed the jam jar. He folded the bread bag and clipped it. He unplugged the toaster with a careful hand. “Off and safe,” he said.

A little voice in his head asked, “Did I forget anything?” Tully looked around. He checked the stove—off. He checked the floor—dry. He checked the cord—neatly resting. “All safe,” he decided. “I used my thinking steps!”

He carried his plate to his favorite chair by the window. The sun warmed his shell. The toast warmed his tummy. He felt brave and calm.

Just then, his neighbor, Pip the Parakeet, fluttered onto the windowsill. “Cheep-cheep! Smells yummy! Who made that?” Pip chirped.

“I did,” Tully said, still shy but smiling. “I made my own toast.”

“By yourself?” Pip asked, eyes wide.

“Yes. I used small steps,” Tully said. “I checked for safety. I used tools for hot things. I went slow. I cleaned up, too.”

Pip bobbed his head. “Cheep! That’s smart. That’s safe. That’s independent!”

Tully blushed under his shell. “It felt hard at first,” he said. “But I breathed. I thought. I tried. And now I know how.”

Pip pecked a crumb. “Next time, can I learn with you?”

Tully nodded. “We can practice together. We can make banana slices, too. We can use the child knife. It’s not sharp.”

Pip fluffed his feathers. “Cheep! A toast team!”

Tully laughed. “Yes. A safe and smart toast team.”

The warm kitchen hummed again. The butter dish shone. The toaster rested. Tully took another happy bite and whispered, “I can do many things, one small step at a time.”

Lesson: With careful thinking, safe tools, and slow, small steps, you can be independent and proud.

The End.
