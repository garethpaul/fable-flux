# Olive Owl Says "I'm Sorry"

In a bright creative art room, the tables were covered with paper, paints, and little cups of water. Sunlight made rainbows on the floor through colored plastic shapes on the window. The room smelled like fresh crayons and clean soap.

A wise old owl named Olive watched over the children. Olive had soft speckled feathers and big round eyes. She wore a tiny apron with paint splatters. “Hoo-hoo,” she said kindly. “Today we make art and we keep things clean.”

Milo and Maya stood at a table with thick paper. They dipped fat brushes into paint. Swish, swish! The brushes made gentle sounds. Pop! went the paint lids. “I’m making a blue ocean,” said Milo, smiling. “I’m making a sunny sky,” said Maya, humming.

Olive Owl nodded. “Lovely. When we paint, we also wash hands and wipe spills. We share, we ask, and we say sorry if we make a mistake.”

Milo bounced on his toes. He wanted to use the shiny gold paint. He reached across the table fast. His sleeve knocked a cup of water. Splash! The water spilled over Maya’s picture. The blue ocean ran into the sunny sky. The paper felt cold and soggy. Maya’s eyes grew wide and wet.

“Oh no,” said Maya. Her lower lip wobbled. “My sky!”

Milo pulled his hand back. His tummy felt twisty. He wanted to hide. The room felt too quiet. Drip, drip, the water slid off the table onto the floor.

Olive Owl fluttered closer. “Hoo-hoo,” she said softly. “I see a spill. I see sad feelings. Let’s take a slow breath.” Olive breathed in and out. Milo and Maya copied her. In… out… Their shoulders eased a little.

Olive tilted her head at Milo. “What happened, dear one?”

Milo peeked at the floor. “I…I wanted the gold paint. I reached too fast. I made a big mess.”

Olive nodded. “When we make a mess, what can we do?”

Milo thought. His cheeks felt hot. He remembered Olive’s words. “Wash, wipe, and… and say sorry,” he whispered.

Milo looked at Maya. “I’m sorry, Maya. I spilled water on your picture. I didn’t ask for the paint. I made you feel sad.”

Maya sniffled. She touched her damp paper. “I am sad,” she said honestly. “But… thank you for saying sorry.”

Olive smiled with her bright eyes. “Good words help hearts feel better. Now, let’s use our helpful hands.”

Olive hooted a gentle rhythm. “First, hygiene,” she said. “Clean hands help clean art!” She showed them the sink. The water was warm on their fingers. Squish-squish went the soap bubbles. “Scrub the tops, scrub the thumbs,” sang Olive. Milo and Maya giggled and scrubbed. They rinsed. Drip-drop. They dried their hands with soft towels. Pat, pat.

“Next, we wipe the spill,” said Olive. They got paper towels. Swish, swish. The floor got dry. The table got tidy. Olive lifted the soggy paper carefully. “Sometimes art changes,” she said. “Sometimes we start new.”

Maya held the edge of the paper. “It looks like rain now,” she said, surprised. The blue and yellow had swirled into pretty green puddles. “It looks like a rainy park!”

Milo’s eyes lit up. “A rainy park needs shiny puddles,” he said. “Maybe the gold paint can be sunshine peeking out.”

Olive’s feathers ruffled with pride. “What a creative idea. Let’s plan together. Ask before you use.”

Milo turned to Maya. “May I use the gold paint with you?”

Maya nodded. “Yes. Let’s share.”

They dipped their brushes slowly this time. Tap, tap. No more spills. Milo painted tiny golden dots around the edges. Maya added little umbrellas with careful strokes. They both hummed a happy tune. The room smelled like oranges now, because Olive had set a small bowl of peels on the shelf to keep the air fresh.

Other children peered over. “Wow,” said one. “That’s a rainy park with sunshine!”

Milo smiled, but he still felt a little sorry. He looked at Maya again. “I will be more careful,” he said. “I will ask before I reach.”

Maya smiled back. “Thank you. I like painting with you.”

Olive Owl clapped her wings softly. “Hoo-hoo! I see apology. I see hygiene. I see creative expression and helpfulness. You turned a problem into a new idea.”

When they finished, Milo helped Maya carry the picture to the drying rack. They moved slowly, step by careful step. Shhh went the paper as it slid onto the rack. Then they cleaned up the brushes. Swirl in the water cups, wipe on the cloth, lay them to dry. The sink sang its watery song again. Wash, rinse, pat. Their hands were clean, and the table shone.

At circle time, Olive held up the painting. “What did we learn?” she asked.

Milo raised his hand. “I learned to say, ‘I’m sorry,’ when I make a mistake.”

Maya added, “I learned to keep my hands clean and to share and ask.”

Olive nodded. “A good apology uses clear words, a kind tone, and helpful actions. Clean hands, clean tools, and a caring heart make beautiful art.”

The children clapped. Milo and Maya beamed. The art room felt warm and bright again. The gold dots sparkled like tiny suns.

The End.
