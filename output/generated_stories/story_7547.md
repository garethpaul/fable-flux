# Roo the Rabbit Hears the Attic

Roo was a small, determined rabbit with soft gray ears and bright, careful eyes. One rainy afternoon, Roo hopped up the creaky wooden steps to the quiet attic. Tap, tap, tap went Roo’s feet. The air smelled like old books and warm dust. A tiny window let in gentle light, and dust sparkles danced in the sunbeam.

“This is my practice place,” Roo whispered. “I will build the tallest block tower ever.” Roo’s whiskers twitched with excitement.

In the attic were soft rugs, a basket of colorful blocks, and a box of costumes. Roo nudged the basket closer and began to stack. Click, clack went the blocks. Up, up, up went the tower.

Just then, Roo heard a slow, kind voice. “Hello, Roo.” It was Tilda the turtle, peeking from behind a trunk. Her shell was shiny and green.

And from under a blanket popped Pip the mouse. “Hi!” squeaked Pip. “Can we play too?”

Roo’s ears stood up. Roo wanted to keep building fast. Roo wanted to win and be the tallest. Roo felt a little tight feeling in the tummy.

“I am busy,” Roo said, stacking another block. “I want to build the biggest tower.”

Pip’s whiskers drooped. Tilda blinked and said, calm and steady, “We can make it together.”

Roo huffed. “But I like my way.”

The attic stayed very quiet. The rain tapped the roof. Drip, drop, drip. Roo’s tower grew wobbly. Roo’s paws felt hot. One more block, and—oh no! The tower leaned and fell with a soft crash. Clatter-clatter went the blocks across the rug.

Roo’s eyes filled with tears. “No! I worked hard,” Roo cried. Roo’s ears drooped low.

Tilda moved slowly closer. “I see you are upset,” she said. “That was a lot of work.”

Pip nodded. “It made a big sound,” Pip said, covering tiny ears. “It scared me.”

Roo took a deep breath in. Sniff. The attic smelled like cedar and crayons. Roo let the breath out. “I feel frustrated,” Roo said in a wobbly voice.

Tilda said, “When I feel frustrated, I pause. I count. One… two… three.”

Roo tried it. “One… two… three.” The tight feeling got a little softer.

Pip picked up a red block and held it out. “We can build again. Can we take turns?”

Roo looked at Pip. Roo looked at Tilda’s kind eyes. Roo remembered something Mama Rabbit always said: “Respect means we listen and we care about others.” Roo rubbed a paw over a tear and nodded. “Okay. We will take turns. We will listen. We will use gentle paws.”

Tilda smiled. “That is fair.”

They made a plan. Roo would place the first block. Pip would place the second. Tilda would place the third. Then repeat. Roo said, “We can each share ideas.”

Roo put down a wide, sturdy block. “This will be the base,” Roo said.

Pip squeaked, “I like a blue block next. It looks like the sky!”

Tilda added a flat block with slow, careful hands. “Flat helps balance,” she said.

They took turns. Click, clack, click. Roo listened when Pip said, “Wait, I need help reaching.” Roo moved the basket closer. “Here you go,” said Roo.

When Roo wanted to hurry, Tilda said, “Slow is strong.” Roo’s ears twitched. Roo slowed down. The tower stayed steady.

Sometimes Roo’s paws itched to grab the next block out of turn. Roo felt that tight tummy again. Roo remembered respect. “It’s Pip’s turn,” Roo whispered. Roo folded paws and watched. Pip grinned and placed a small yellow block. “My favorite color,” Pip said.

The attic felt warm and friendly now. Their voices were soft. Their smiles were bright. The rain on the roof was a cozy drum.

Roo had an idea. “Let’s make windows.” Roo asked, “What do you think?” Pip and Tilda nodded. “Good idea,” said Tilda. Pip clapped. “Yes!”

They built and talked and listened. When Pip dropped a block, Roo said, “It’s okay. We can try again.” When Tilda needed more time, Roo waited and hummed a gentle tune.

At last, the tower stood taller than Tilda’s shell, taller than Roo’s ears, almost to the sunbeam. It did not wobble. It stood strong and proud.

“We did it!” Pip squeaked, hopping in a tiny circle.

Tilda tapped the tower lightly. “Teamwork,” she said.

Roo’s heart felt warm. “We did it together. Taking turns and listening made it better. I like building with you.”

They sat on the soft rug and looked up at their tower. The attic was quiet, but their happy breathing and little giggles filled the room. The dust sparkles floated like tiny stars.

Pip said, “Roo, you respected us. You let me have a turn.”

Roo nodded. “And you respected me. You waited and helped. That was fair.”

Tilda smiled, slow and bright. “We improved each time. We tried again when it fell. We shared. We were kind.”

Roo’s ears perked up. “Respect means we listen, wait our turn, and care for each other,” Roo said. “I learned that when I feel frustrated, I can pause, breathe, and count.”

They gave the tower a name. “The Friendship Tower,” they said together.

The rain stopped. A sunbeam warmed their faces. Roo hugged Pip. Tilda patted Roo’s shoulder with a gentle flipper.

“Tomorrow,” Roo said, “we can build again. We will take turns and listen.”

“Yes,” said Tilda.

“Yes!” squeaked Pip.

Roo looked at the tower one more time. The attic felt peaceful and proud. Roo felt proud too—proud of the tower, and proud of being respectful.

The End.
