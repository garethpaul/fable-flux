# Mango’s Pantry Plan

Mango was a small, courageous monkey with bright, curious eyes. One rainy afternoon, he tiptoed into the pantry. The pantry was cool and smelled like cinnamon and ripe bananas. Shelves stood tall like trees. Jars clicked softly. Crinkly bags rustled like leaves.

“My tummy is rumbly,” Mango whispered. “I will find a snack.”

He saw a shiny jar of peanut butter way up high. He saw a basket of bananas on a low shelf. He saw a box with a picture of crackers. “Yum!” he said. “But I must be careful.”

Mango reached for the crackers. The box slid. A jar bumped. “Uh-oh!” Clink, clink! A lid rolled to the floor. Mango’s eyes went wide. “I didn’t mean to make a mess!”

Suddenly, he heard tiny squeaks near the flour bin. Two little mice peeked out. One wore a blue ribbon. The other had a tiny acorn cap. They sniffed the air.

“Eee-eee?” squeaked the mouse in the ribbon.

Mango took a brave breath. “Hello,” he said gently. “I am Mango. I won’t hurt you.”

The mice squeaked and pointed at a bag of seeds on a very high shelf. The bag had slipped behind a tin. The mice looked worried.

Mango tilted his head. “You need… seeds?” He made a circle with his hands like a bag. He pointed up. He squeaked softly, copying them, “Eee-eee?”

The mice squeaked back and clapped their tiny paws. The pantry felt friendlier. Mango smiled. “We can talk,” he said. “We can listen.”

Just then, the door creaked. The wind whooshed. The high shelf wobbled. The peanut butter jar slid closer to the edge. Mango’s heart thumped. “We need a plan,” he said. “A safe plan.”

He looked around. He saw a wooden spoon. He saw a dish towel. He saw a sturdy step stool. He saw the basket of bananas. He touched the stool. It felt smooth and strong.

“First, we stop the wobble,” Mango said. “Then we get the seeds.”

The mice nodded fast. The ribbon mouse squeaked, “Eee!” and pushed a little box to help.

Mango spoke clearly. “I will hold the shelf. You push the box under the wobbly corner.” He showed with his hands, slow and calm.

The mice watched his hands and listened to his words. They squeaked back and pushed together. Mango counted, “One, two, three.” They moved as a team. The shelf settled. The jar stopped sliding.

“Phew,” Mango said, wiping his forehead. He could smell peanut butter now, sweet and nutty.

Next, he pointed to the step stool. “I will climb. You tell me if I am close to the seed bag.” He climbed one step. The stool creaked. “Is this okay?”

The acorn-cap mouse squeaked, “Eee-eek!” and pointed left.

Mango listened. He moved left. He climbed another step. The bag peeked out behind the tin. He stretched his little monkey arm. “Almost,” he said. “Please guide me.”

“Eee!” the ribbon mouse squeaked, pointing up, then circling a paw to show “turn.” Mango turned his wrist and hooked the bag with the wooden spoon. He pulled gently. The bag slid into his hands.

“We did it!” Mango cheered. The mice twirled, tiny tails swishing.

But then—thump! The step stool wobbled again. Mango’s tummy flipped. He held the bag tight. “I am brave,” he told himself. “I can ask for help.”

“Friends,” Mango called, “can you steady the stool?” He kept his voice calm and clear.

The mice pressed their little bodies against the legs of the stool. Mango took slow breaths. One step down. Tap. Another step. Tap. Safe!

They all giggled. The pantry sounded warm now, filled with happy squeaks and soft monkey laughs.

Mango opened the seed bag just a tiny bit. He poured some seeds into a small bowl and pushed it toward the mice. “For you,” he said. “Please share.”

The mice nibbled with happy squeaks. “Thank you,” their eyes seemed to say.

Mango picked up the rolling lid and screwed it back on the jar. Click! He straightened the cracker box. He shook the towel and wiped a little flour. The pantry looked neat again. It smelled like clean wood and cinnamon.

A soft voice called from the hallway. “Mango? Are you okay?”

“Yes!” Mango called back. “I am okay. I used brave words and careful listening. I cleaned up, too!”

He turned to the mice. “I learned something,” he said. “When we use kind words, when we listen and point and show, we can fix big problems.”

The mice squeaked, “Eee!” and touched their tiny paws to Mango’s hand, like a high five.

Mango peeled a banana and took a bite. It was sweet and soft. “This tastes like teamwork,” he giggled.

He waved to his new friends. “If you need help again, we can talk it through.”

The mice nodded and tapped their little hearts.

Mango left the pantry feeling proud and calm. He had been brave. He had solved a problem. He had used clear words, gentle squeaks, and careful hands. He had listened, too.

And that day, Mango learned a big lesson: brave hearts and kind voices make strong teams. When we talk, listen, and try again, we can make any wobbly shelf steady.

The End.
