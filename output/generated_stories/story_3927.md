# Timmy Turtle’s Thank-You Soup

Timmy was a shy turtle with a small, smooth shell. One sunny afternoon, he peeked into a warm kitchen. The kitchen smelled like cinnamon and toast. The air felt cozy, like a hug. Sunlight made golden squares on the floor.

Timmy whispered, “Hello?” His voice was soft, like a leaf.

“Hi, Timmy!” said Nana Goose, wearing a big blue apron. “I’m making soup. Would you like to help?”

Timmy’s cheeks felt hot. “I’m shy,” he said. “I don’t know how.”

“That’s okay,” Nana Goose said kindly. “We can learn together.”

Timmy took a deep breath. He listened to the bubble-bubble of the soup pot. He heard the tick-tock of the clock. He smelled carrots, onions, and warm bread. His tummy rumbled.

“First, we wash our hands,” said Nana Goose.

Timmy looked at the sink. The water looked bright and splashy. He had never used a sink before. “How do I do it?” he asked.

“Good question,” said Nana Goose. “Turn the handle. Make the water warm. Use soap. Scrub, scrub, scrub.”

Timmy thought hard. He turned the handle slowly. The water felt too cold. He tried again. Now it felt just right—warm like a bath. He used soap. Scrub, scrub, scrub. The bubbles smelled like oranges.

“You did it!” said Nana Goose.

Timmy smiled a little. His hands felt clean and tingly.

“Next, we chop veggies,” said Nana Goose. “But the knife is sharp. We must be careful.”

Timmy’s eyes grew wide. “I’m shy about sharp things.”

“We can use a safe turtle knife,” Nana Goose said, handing him a small plastic knife. “It is for learning.”

Timmy took a slow breath. “How do I hold the carrot?”

“Like a bridge,” said Nana Goose. “Tuck your fingers.”

Timmy made a bridge with his little turtle hand. Chop… chop… chop. The carrot made happy clacks on the board. He thought, I can learn new skills, one small step at a time.

“Now smell the carrots,” said Nana Goose.

Timmy sniffed. “Sweet!” he said, smiling more.

They chopped celery. Snip… snip… snip. They stirred the pot. Stir, stir, stir. The steam was warm and soft on Timmy’s face. He felt brave.

Then Nana Goose said, “Oh dear. We are out of salt.”

Timmy blinked. “Is the soup ruined?”

Nana Goose shook her head. “Let’s think.”

Timmy put a flipper to his chin. “We can’t go to the store. The soup is cooking now.”

“What else could help?” asked Nana Goose.

Timmy looked around. He saw a lemon, a basket of herbs, and a little shaker. He sniffed the lemon. It smelled bright. He sniffed the herbs. They smelled green and fresh.

“Maybe the lemon?” Timmy said. “A tiny squeeze?”

“Let’s test,” said Nana Goose. “Skill number one: tiny tastes.”

Nana Goose dipped a spoon. Timmy tasted. “It needs a little more happy,” he said.

“Try herbs,” said Nana Goose.

Timmy pinched tiny leaves. He rubbed them between his fingers. His fingers smelled like a garden after rain. Sprinkle, sprinkle. Stir, stir.

They tasted again. Timmy’s eyes lit up. “It tastes warm and bright!”

“Good thinking,” said Nana Goose. “You solved the problem.”

Timmy felt a shy giggle bubble up. He had used his brain. He had used his senses. He had built a new skill.

Then they set the table. The tablecloth was soft. The bowls were smooth and round. The bread was crusty on the outside and fluffy inside. Butter shone like sunshine.

Nana Goose poured the soup. Steam curled up like little clouds. “Timmy,” she said, “would you like to say something before we eat?”

Timmy felt a flutter in his shell. He was shy. But he remembered how Nana Goose helped him wash, chop, and taste. He remembered the warm kitchen and the kind voice.

Timmy stood a little taller. “Thank you, Nana Goose,” he said. “Thank you for teaching me. Thank you, veggies, for growing. Thank you, hands, for helping. Thank you, soup, for being warm.”

Nana Goose’s eyes twinkled. “That was beautiful.”

They clinked spoons. Sip. The soup was cozy and bright. Timmy’s tummy felt happy.

After they ate, Timmy helped wash the bowls. Swish, swish, swish. He dried them with a soft towel. Pat, pat.

“Today,” said Nana Goose, “you learned hand washing, safe chopping, and tasting to fix a problem.”

Timmy nodded. “And I learned to say thank you,” he said. “Thank you feels like a warm blanket.”

Nana Goose smiled. “Gratitude helps our hearts grow.”

Timmy looked around the warm kitchen. He smelled the last curl of soup steam. He heard the friendly tick-tock. He felt brave and kind.

“I was shy,” he said, “but I tried. I thought. I helped. And I said thank you.”

Nana Goose patted his shell. “Those are big turtle steps.”

Timmy tucked into his shell for a tiny happy moment, then popped back out. “Can we cook again tomorrow?” he asked.

“Of course,” said Nana Goose. “We will learn one new skill each day.”

Timmy grinned. “And we will say thank you each time.”

The End.
