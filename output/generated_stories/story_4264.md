# Polly’s Rainbow Poem

Polly was a creative parrot who lived in a cozy home with a sunny balcony garden. The garden was full of green leaves, tiny tomatoes, and bright flowers that smelled sweet, like honey. Wind chimes tinkled softly. Bees hummed. The sun felt warm on Polly’s feathers.

Polly loved to write. She didn’t use a pencil. She wrote with her voice. “Squawk! Words are colors. Words can paint!” she liked to say.

One morning, Polly puffed up her bright feathers and flapped to the garden rail. “I will write a poem,” she chirped. “A poem for everyone!”

Milo the snail peeked from a pot. “I am slow,” he whispered, leaving a shiny trail. “Can I help?”

“Yes,” said Polly. “Slow is good. Slow can see tiny things.”

Bella the butterfly fluttered by, wings like stained glass. “I am quiet,” she said softly. “Can I help too?”

“Yes,” said Polly. “Quiet is good. Quiet can hear soft sounds.”

Gus the grasshopper hopped high. “I am bouncy!” he shouted. “I want to help!”

“Yes,” said Polly. “Bouncy is good. Bouncy can see far places.”

Polly fluffed her feathers. “We will write a poem about our garden. Everyone can share a part.”

Just then, a pigeon named Pip landed with a clatter. His feathers were gray and a little messy. “Hi,” Pip said. “Can I help too? I’m… loud. And my words come out jumbly.”

Polly blinked. “We already have slow, quiet, and bouncy,” she said, thinking hard. “Loud and jumbly… um…”

Gus whispered, “Maybe loud is too much.”

Bella murmured, “Maybe jumbly won’t fit.”

Pip’s wings drooped. “Okay,” he said softly. “I’ll just watch.” He shuffled to the corner near the mint leaves. The mint smelled fresh, but Pip felt heavy and sad.

Polly started. “First line!” She closed her eyes and listened. “The sun is warm on my bright, bright wings.”

Bella smiled. “Second line,” she said. Her voice was like a breeze. “Rain drops dance like tiny bells.”

Gus bounced. “Third line! Tomatoes are little red moons!”

Milo took a deep breath. “Fourth line,” he said slowly. “Snail trails shine like silver roads.”

Polly nodded. “We need one more line. A big ending!”

But something was missing. The poem felt small. The wind chimes clinked, and a cloud covered the sun. The garden seemed to hold its breath.

Polly looked at Pip. He was staring at the sky. His eyes were kind. He tapped his claws, making a soft tip-tap on the rail. Tip. Tap. Tip-tap-tap.

“What do you hear?” Polly asked.

Pip blinked. “I hear the city and the garden together,” he said. “Cars zoom, bees hum, and the wind says whooooo. My head is full of all the sounds at once. It gets jumbly, but it feels like a drum. Like a heart.”

Polly listened. Zoom. Hum. Whooooo. Tip-tap-tap. Her feathers tingled. “That is important,” she said. “Your jumbly is music.”

Pip looked hopeful. “Really?”

“Really,” said Polly. She fluffed and stood tall. “Friends, we forgot to include Pip. Our poem is for everyone. We need his voice.”

Bella nodded. “Quiet needs loud to make a song.”

Gus grinned. “Bouncy needs steady.”

Milo smiled. “Slow needs a drum.”

Polly turned to Pip. “Would you share the last line? Use your loud. Use your jumbly. Use you.”

Pip took a deep breath. He puffed his chest. “Fifth line!” he boomed. “Our garden hearts beat—boom, boom—together!”

He added a soft rhythm with his claws: tip-tap, tip-tap. The wind chimes jingled along. The sun slid from behind the cloud and warmed the leaves. A bee buzzed like a tiny violin. Everything felt bright and big.

Polly’s eyes shone. “Let’s read our poem,” she said.

Together they said:

“The sun is warm on my bright, bright wings.
Rain drops dance like tiny bells.
Tomatoes are little red moons.
Snail trails shine like silver roads.
Our garden hearts beat—boom, boom—together!”

Polly flapped with joy. “That is our poem! It sounds like all of us.”

Pip laughed, a happy, loud laugh. “My loud can help,” he said.

“It does,” said Polly. “Every voice helps. Every difference makes the poem better.”

They shared the poem with the city birds, the bees, and the butterflies. A little child on the balcony next door clapped. “Again!” the child giggled. The mint leaves waved. The flowers swayed. The whole garden seemed to smile.

Polly looked at her friends and felt warm inside. “Today we learned something big,” she said. “Inclusion means everyone belongs. We listen. We make space. We celebrate what makes us different.”

Milo nodded. “Slow is welcome.”

Bella fluttered. “Quiet is welcome.”

Gus hopped. “Bouncy is welcome.”

Pip beamed. “Loud and jumbly is welcome!”

Polly tucked her wings and whispered, “And together, we write something beautiful.”

The End.
