# Fawn's Feelings in the Warm Kitchen

In a warm kitchen, a gentle deer fawn named Fern stood on a soft rug. The kitchen smelled like cinnamon and toast. The morning sun made orange squares on the floor. The kettle hummed. Pots sat quietly on the stove. Fern’s hooves clicked softly as she looked around.

Fern loved helping. She liked to stir, to sprinkle, and to taste. Today, Aunt Maple Deer was visiting. She wore a floury apron and a bright smile. “Let’s bake fruit muffins!” Aunt Maple said. “Blueberry, apple, and a pinch of cinnamon.”

Fern’s ears perked up. “Yes, please!” she said. Her tail gave a tiny wag. She liked blueberries best.

Aunt Maple set out bowls and shiny spoons. “We will go step by step,” she said. “First, we mix.”

Fern stood tall on a little stool. The bowl was big and smooth. The spoon was cool in her hoof. She began to stir. Swish, swish, swish. The batter smelled sweet and cozy. Fern breathed in. “Mmm,” she said.

Then Aunt Maple turned to the sink. The faucet whooshed. Water splashed like rain. The sound was loud. Fern’s ears twitched. Her chest felt tight, like a knot. Her knees felt wobbly. She did not like loud whooshing sounds.

Fern thought, Maybe I can stay quiet. Maybe it will stop soon.

But the faucet kept whooshing. The kettle began to whistle. A timer ticked. Tick, tick, tick. The kitchen felt too busy and too loud. Fern’s eyes felt hot and wet. Her tummy rumbled, not hungry, just fluttery.

Aunt Maple looked back and smiled. “You are doing so well, Fern.”

Fern tried to smile. Her lips shook. A little tear slipped down. She looked at the big bowl and the shiny spoons and the noisy faucet. She wanted to be brave. She wanted to help. But her body said, This is too much.

Fern took a small breath. It felt bumpy, like a hiccup. She remembered something Mama Doe had said: “Feelings are clues. You can listen to them. You can tell someone.”

Fern put the spoon down. “Aunt Maple?” Her voice was tiny and soft.

Aunt Maple turned off the faucet. The whoosh stopped. The kitchen got quieter. “Yes, little fern leaf?” Aunt Maple said gently.

Fern pressed a hoof to her chest. “My heart feels fast,” she said. “My ears are tired of loud. I feel… overwhelmed.”

Aunt Maple’s eyes grew kind. “Thank you for telling me,” she said. “That is brave. Your feelings matter.”

Fern peeked up. “They do?”

“They do,” Aunt Maple said. “Let’s help your body feel safe.” She pointed to the window. Sunlight made a warm square on the floor. “Would you like to stand in the warm sun and take slow breaths with me?”

Fern nodded. They stood in the sunny square. The light felt like a soft blanket on Fern’s back. Aunt Maple put a hoof on her own belly. “In through your nose like you smell a blueberry,” she said. “Out through your mouth like you blow a candle.”

Fern breathed in. Sniff. She imagined a sweet blueberry. She breathed out. Whooo. The air felt gentle. Again. Sniff. Whooo. Her chest softened. Her knees felt strong again.

“How do you feel now?” Aunt Maple asked.

“Calmer,” Fern said. “Still a little wiggly inside.”

“That’s okay,” said Aunt Maple. “Would it help if we make the kitchen quieter? We can turn off the kettle. We can count together when the timer ticks.”

Fern thought. She felt the warm sun. She listened. It was quieter. “Yes,” she said. “And can I wear the soft ear muffs?” She pointed to fuzzy green muffs hanging by the door.

“Great idea,” said Aunt Maple. “That is self-advocacy. It means asking for what you need.” She handed Fern the muffs.

Fern put them on. The world got soft and muffled, like a gentle cloud. She smiled. “Better.”

“Would you like to stir again, or would you like a different job?” Aunt Maple asked.

Fern stood tall. “I can stir,” she said. “But can we count five slow stirs and then rest?”

“Yes,” said Aunt Maple. “That is a good plan. You are taking care of you. That is self-reliance.”

Fern stirred. One… two… three… four… five. She stopped. She took a slow breath. Sniff. Whooo. She smiled. “I did it.”

“You did,” Aunt Maple said. “High hoof.” Their hooves tapped, soft and happy.

They added blueberries. Plop, plop, plop. The berries looked like tiny moons. The batter turned swirly purple. Fern giggled. “Purple clouds!”

Aunt Maple laughed. “Into the oven they go.” The oven clicked and hummed. Fern watched the muffins rise, round and puffy. The kitchen smelled like warm fruit and cinnamon hugs.

When the timer ticked, Aunt Maple said, “Let’s count the ticks together.” Fern nodded. “Tick one, tick two, tick three…” Counting felt calm. On tick ten, the timer dinged. Aunt Maple turned it off right away. No long ding. Just a quick hello.

They set the muffins on a rack. The air felt warm on Fern’s nose. She took off the muffs. “I feel proud,” she said. “I told you my feelings. I asked for help. I helped myself. And I still got to bake!”

Aunt Maple nuzzled her cheek. “You listened to your clues. You spoke up. You made a plan. That is strong and smart.”

Fern took a tiny bite. The muffin was soft and sweet. Blueberry juice made a purple smile on her mouth. “Mmm,” she said. “Feelings can be big. And I can be kind to them.”

Aunt Maple nodded. “When feelings feel big, you can breathe, ask, and plan.”

Fern looked at the sunny square, the quiet kettle, and the cozy oven. She put a hoof on her chest. Thump, thump. Calm. “I can do that,” she said.

The End.
