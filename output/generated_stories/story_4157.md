# Little Bear Says, "I Can Speak Up!"

Sunlight peeked through soft curtains in the comfortable nursery. The room smelled like clean cotton and pine blocks. A friendly bear cub named Bibi stretched on a fuzzy rug. Bibi’s ears were round and his fur was warm and golden-brown. He loved his cozy corner, his picture books, and his little watering can.

“Good morning, plants,” Bibi said softly. Three small pots sat on the window ledge. One had a sprout with two tiny leaves. One had a leafy vine. One had a seed not yet awake.

Bibi took a deep breath. “Today is a big day,” he said. “I have a goal.” He tapped his paw on a paper chart with three stars drawn in crayon. “Goal one: ask for help when I need it. Goal two: care for the plants. Goal three: use kind words to say what I feel.”

Mama Bear peeked in, carrying a basket of clean blankets. “Good morning, Bibi,” she sang, her voice soft like a lullaby. “What do you smell?”

“I smell fresh blankets,” Bibi giggled. “And I smell wet dirt from my plants.”

Mama smiled. “A busy nose! What is your plan today?”

Bibi stood tall. “I want to be brave and speak up. I will water only what the plants need. And I will ask for a turn when I want one.”

Mama nodded. “That is strong self-advocacy. Speaking up helps others know what you need.”

Bibi felt a warm, proud flutter in his tummy.

After breakfast, friends came to play in the nursery. There was Tilly the turtle, who loved puzzles. There was Peeper the chick, who loved blocks. And there was Mo the mole, who loved to dig in the indoor sand tray.

The room buzzed with happy sounds—block towers clacked, pages rustled, and little paws and claws tapped the floor. Bibi looked at the window. The sun made the plant leaves glow. He held his watering can. “I will water just a little,” he whispered. “Plants need sips, not floods.”

Peeper hopped over. “Can I help?” chirped Peeper, eyes bright.

Bibi took a small breath. He wanted to water by himself today. He wanted to reach his goal. “I want a turn first,” Bibi said gently. “Then I can share.”

Peeper smiled and nodded. “Okay!”

Bibi tipped the can. Drip, drip—tiny drops sparkled. The soil turned dark and smelled like rain. “There,” he said. “Just right. That helps the plants and saves water.” He felt calm and glad.

Next, Mo scurried to the sand tray. “Dig, dig!” Mo hummed, paws quick. Sand whispered and slipped. Bibi liked the sand too. He wanted to make a river for toy boats. But he felt shy.

He looked at his star chart. Goal one: ask for help when I need it. Bibi pressed his paw to his chest. “I can speak up,” he told himself.

He padded to the sand tray. “Mo,” he said, voice a little wobbly, “I would like a turn with the sand. Can we take turns?”

Mo looked up, nose twitching. “Yes,” Mo said kindly. “Let’s set a timer! Ding means switch.”

Tilly brought a tiny timer. Tick, tick, tick. Bibi smiled. “Thank you for listening,” he said. His tummy felt steady, like a rock.

While they waited, Tilly worked on a puzzle with animals. “I need a corner piece,” Tilly said. “Can anyone help me look?”

Bibi peered close. He saw a green triangle with a bit of leaf. “Here!” he said, holding it up. “It goes by the tree.”

Tilly beamed. “Thank you, Bibi. You used your eyes so well.”

Ding! The timer sang. Bibi took his turn at the sand. He made a curvy river and patted the banks smooth. “This is our clean water,” he said. “We take care of water, just like plants. That is conservation.”

Peeper floated a tiny leaf-boat. “Save the water!” Peeper chirped.

Bibi nodded. “We can use only what we need. And we can keep things tidy.” He scooped the extra sand back into the tray. Swish, swish.

Soon it was snack time. Crunchy apple slices smelled sweet. But one apple had a brown spot. Bibi frowned. He liked the red, crunchy parts best.

He remembered his third goal: use kind words to say what I feel. He went to Mama Bear. “Mama,” he said, looking up, “I feel a little worried about this brown spot. May I please have a different slice?”

Mama Bear checked the apple. “Thank you for telling me how you feel, Bibi. That is good self-advocacy.” She swapped the slice. “You spoke clearly and kindly.”

Bibi munched and smiled. The apple was crisp and juicy. “Yum!”

After snack, it was story time. The nursery grew quiet. The clock ticked softly. The plants’ leaves made tiny shadows on the floor. Mama Bear read about a little stream that stayed clean because animals took care of it.

Bibi hugged his tail. He thought about his day. He had asked for a turn. He had watered just enough. He had used kind words. He had followed his goals.

When the story ended, Bibi stood and said, “I did my goals today!”

Peeper clapped. “You did!”

Mo nodded. “You spoke up.”

Tilly smiled. “You cared for our things.”

Bibi felt brave and bright. He drew three big stars on his chart. Star, star, star. He looked at his friends. “We can all speak up kindly,” he said. “We can care for water and plants. We can make goals and try, little by little.”

Mama Bear hugged Bibi. Her fur smelled like warm honey. “Yes,” she said softly. “Small voices can make big, kind changes.”

Bibi curled on the fuzzy rug, listening to the gentle rustle of leaves and the soft hum of friends at play. His heart felt peaceful. He had learned that when he used his voice, others listened. When he took care of the things around him, the nursery felt happy.

“I can do it,” he whispered. “I can say what I need. I can help the world, one small sip of water at a time.”

The End.
