# Roo’s Big Band of Imagination

Roo was a joyful kangaroo joey. His paws were soft, his tail was springy, and his heart felt like a drum, tap-tap-tapping. Today, Roo hopped into the musical practice room. It smelled like fresh wood and lemon polish. Sunlight made warm squares on the floor. Shiny drums, tall stands, and sleepy violins rested in their cases.

Roo’s ears perked up. “I want to make music!” he said. “Big, kind music!”

He bounced to a drum and tapped with a paw. Thump. Then thump-thump! He smiled. But when he tried the sticks, they slipped. Clatter! The sound was too loud. “Oh no,” Roo whispered. “That was scary-loud.”

He tried a small flute. He puffed his cheeks. Pffff. No music came out, only a whisper of air and a tickle in his nose. “Ah-choo!” Roo giggled, then frowned. “I can’t play these. How can I make music?”

Roo’s tummy felt wobbly. He wanted to share a song, but the real instruments were hard. He closed his eyes. He took a little breath that smelled like wood shavings. “I can use my imagination,” he said softly. “I can make my own band.”

Roo looked around. In a corner was a craft box. It held paper cups, rubber bands, cardboard tubes, buttons, and colorful tape. “I can craft my own instruments,” Roo said. His eyes sparkled. “I can do it myself.”

He found an empty tissue box and some rubber bands. Twang! Twang! The bands sang when he stretched them across the hole. “Hello, tiny guitar,” Roo said. “You sound like the wind plucking grass.”

He taped two paper cups together and filled them with dry rice. Shake-shake-shake. “Maracas!” he laughed. “You sound like rain on the roof.”

Roo slid a comb into a strip of paper. Bzz-bzz. “A kazoo!” He hummed and felt the paper tickle his lips. It sounded silly and happy.

Roo tapped a cardboard tube with a spoon. Tok-tok. “A little drum,” he said. “You sound like footsteps in the hall.”

He looked at the shiny drums and violins again. He bowed to them. “Thank you for teaching me to be gentle,” he said. “I will try again another day.”

Roo set his crafted band in a circle. He sat in the warm square of sun. “Who will I play for?” he asked. The room was quiet. His tail curled around his feet.

Roo closed his eyes once more. “I can invite friends with my mind,” he whispered. He imagined friends from all around the world.

He imagined Luna from a snowy place. Her cheeks were pink like apples. “I like soft sounds,” Luna said in Roo’s mind. “They feel like snowflakes.”

He imagined Amir from a sandy place. His smile was bright. “I love drums,” Amir said. “They sound like camel steps.”

He imagined Mei from a rainy place. She wore a tiny yellow hat. “I like splashy beats,” Mei said. “Like drops on leaves.”

“Welcome, friends,” Roo said, even though he was alone in the room. His heart felt full. “Let’s make kind music together.”

Roo gave Luna the paper cup maracas. “Shake softly,” he said.

“Shake, shake,” Luna whispered. The sound was like snow. Roo smiled.

He handed Amir the cardboard drum. “Tap gently,” Roo said.

“Tok, tok,” Amir played. The sound was like hoofs in sand. Roo’s ears wiggled.

He gave Mei the kazoo. “Hum like a raindrop,” Roo said.

“Bzz-bzz,” Mei hummed. It was silly. Roo laughed and hummed too.

Roo picked up the tissue-box guitar. Twang, twang. He plucked slow and kind. The sounds bumped together, then started to dance. Roo felt brave. He felt calm. He felt kind.

Just then, the real door opened. Ms. Echo, the music teacher, peeked in. She heard the gentle shake, the soft tap, the happy buzz, and the twang. She saw Roo sitting in the sun, his crafted band all around him.

“Roo,” Ms. Echo said, smiling, “what a lovely band!”

Roo’s cheeks warmed. “I wanted to make music,” he said. “The big instruments were hard. So I imagined friends. I crafted these. We made a song together.”

Ms. Echo knelt down. Her necklace jingled like tiny bells. “That is wonderful,” she said. “You used imagination. You cared about how the sounds felt to your friends. You made instruments yourself. That is smart and kind.”

Roo looked at his band. “Luna likes soft,” he said. “Amir likes drums. Mei likes rain sounds. I listened to them in my mind.”

“That is empathy,” Ms. Echo said. “You listened with your heart.”

Roo nodded. He strummed once more. Twang. “Can you listen to our song?”

Ms. Echo closed her eyes and swayed. “It sounds like the whole world saying hello,” she said.

Roo’s tail thumped gently, tap-tap. His worry was gone. He felt big inside, not small.

When the song ended, Ms. Echo clapped softly. “Roo,” she said, “you solved a problem. You made your own instruments. You imagined friends from faraway places. You shared kind sounds. That is music.”

Roo hugged his tissue-box guitar. “I learned that my mind can help me,” he said. “When something is hard, I can imagine, be gentle, and try my own way.”

Ms. Echo nodded. “Yes. Your imagination is a strong friend.”

Roo packed his crafted band with care. “Tomorrow,” he said, “I will make a new song. Maybe ocean sounds. Maybe forest sounds. I can listen and be kind.”

He hopped to the door, then looked back at the warm, bright room. “Thank you,” he whispered, to the drums and to the sun and to his faraway friends.

The End.
