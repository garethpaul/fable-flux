# Lark’s Little Balcony Helpers

On a sunny morning, a singing bird named Lark woke up in a balcony garden. The balcony smelled like mint and warm soil. The wind felt soft on Lark’s feathers. Tiny tomatoes glowed red. Basil leaves were shiny and green. A blue watering can sat by the door.

“Good morning, garden!” Lark sang. Her song sounded like tinkly bells. “Tweet-a-dee, tweet-a-dum! Today will be kind and yum!”

Lark hopped along the rail and looked down at the busy street. People walked quickly. A child waved up at Lark. Lark waved back with her wings and giggled.

Lark loved her balcony home. She loved the crunchy seeds in her little bowl. She loved the juicy strawberries the kind neighbor, Mrs. Jun, grew in a pot. But today, something felt wrong.

She peered at the leaves. “Oh no,” Lark chirped. “Some plants look droopy.” The mint bent like a sleepy friend. The basil looked thirsty. Even the tomatoes sighed.

Lark felt a tickle of worry in her chest. “What can I do?” she asked. Her heart went thump-thump. She wanted to help. Helping felt good and bright, like sunshine.

She hopped to the blue watering can. It was heavy. “I am just a small bird,” she said. “But I can try.”

Lark pushed the handle with her beak. The can did not move. She tried again. “Push, push, push,” she sang, making a silly face. The can budged a little and sloshed. Water went splish-splash onto Lark’s toes. It felt cool.

“I can’t lift it,” Lark said. “But I can think.” She tapped her head with her wing. “I can make a plan.”

She looked around. On the shelf sat tiny paper cups, some string, and a little spoon. Lark smiled. “I will make a helper,” she decided.

She tied a string to a cup. Tug, tug, tug. The knot hugged the cup. “I did it by myself!” she cheered. She dipped the cup into the watering can. Scoop! Slowly, she carried the cup with the string in her beak and poured water at the roots. Drip-drip, drip-drip.

“Here you go, mint,” Lark sang. “Sip, sip, sip.” The mint lifted a little. The basil perked up too. Lark’s chest felt warm with pride. “I can do hard things,” she said. “I can keep trying.”

Just then, a small ant came by, carrying a crumb. “Hello, Lark,” the ant said. “You look busy.”

“I am watering,” Lark chirped. “The garden needs help.”

The ant nodded. “Community means we help each other,” he said. “I can carry tiny drops.” He dipped his legs, shook like a tiny dog, and sprinkled a leaf. Lark laughed. “Thank you!”

A ladybug landed on a tomato leaf. “I can eat the naughty bugs,” she said. “They make leaves sad.” She got to work, munch, munch, munch.

Mrs. Jun opened the balcony door. She smelled the mint and smiled. “What a happy garden,” she said. She saw Lark with the cup and string. “Oh, clever bird! You made a little water helper.”

Lark puffed with joy. “We are helping the garden together,” she said. “Me and Ant and Ladybug.”

Mrs. Jun nodded. “That is community-service,” she said. “When we see a need, we use our hands, our minds, and our hearts.” She set out a small tray of sliced cucumbers and strawberries. “Fresh snacks for my helpers,” she said. “Cucumbers are crunchy and full of water. Strawberries are sweet and good for our bodies.”

“Yum!” Lark pecked a tiny bit of strawberry. Juice popped on her tongue. “Sweet!” Ant nibbled a cucumber crumb. “Crunch!” Ladybug wiped her mouth. “Delish!”

Lark looked at the droopy mint again. It was standing taller. She looked at the basil. It smelled spicy and bright. The tomatoes seemed to smile. The garden felt alive. Bees buzzed softly. The breeze sang.

But then clouds rolled in. Drip, drop, drip. Rain fell! A gust of wind tipped the tiny cup. Lark’s feathers fluffed. “Oh no!” she squeaked. “The cup!”

Mrs. Jun closed the balcony door partway to stop the wind. “Storms come,” she said. “We can still help.”

Lark took a breath. In, out. “I won’t give up,” she said. “I can make a new plan.” She used the spoon to dig a little trench in the soil. “This will give the water a path,” she said. Ant patted the dirt flat with his tiny feet. Ladybug held a leaf steady.

The rain water followed the little path. It went to the roots where it was needed. The plants drank. Slurp, slurp. Lark smiled. “We did it!”

When the clouds moved on, the balcony garden sparkled. Drops of water shone like stars. The air smelled clean and green. Lark shook her feathers and sang a bright song. “Tweet-a-dee, we helped as a team! Tweet-a-dum, we tried and learned!”

Mrs. Jun clapped softly. “Today you made choices,” she said. “You saw a problem. You made a plan. You asked friends. You kept trying. That is independence and a growth-mindset.”

Lark felt strong and calm. “Helping makes me happy,” she said. She looked over the railing at the street. The child who had waved before looked up again. Lark called, “We can all help. We can share water, food, and care.”

The child smiled and held up a little bottle of water for a street tree. Lark’s heart sang.

That night, as the sky turned pink, Lark tucked her head under her wing. The mint smelled cozy. The basil whispered thank you. Lark whispered back, “We are a community. We take care of each other. Good food, kind hearts, smart choices.”

She fell asleep with a happy hum.

The End.
