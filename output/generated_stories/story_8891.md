# The Sharing Supper of Little Fawn

In a cozy dining room, a gentle deer fawn named Lila stood on soft hooves. The table was low and round. A warm light glowed like honey. The smell of fresh bread floated in the air. Bowls of soup steamed. Small plates waited like little moons.

Lila’s ears twitched with excitement. “Tonight is our sharing supper,” she whispered. Her voice was soft like a lullaby.

Friends from near and far came to the door. There was Timo the raccoon, with striped whiskers and a tiny hat. There was Asha the sparrow, with bright eyes and a scarf of golden thread. There was Bao the bunny, with floppy ears and a gentle smile. There was Zara the turtle, slow and steady, carrying a shell painted with bright patterns. Each friend brought a dish from home.

Timo set down a bowl. “This is sweet corn stew,” he said. It smelled buttery and warm.

Asha fluttered to the table. “I brought spice rice,” she chirped. The rice was bright yellow and smelled like sunshine.

Bao hopped carefully with a tray of carrot dumplings. “They are soft and cozy,” he said. A little steam puffed up like tiny clouds.

Zara placed a plate of mango slices. “They are juicy and cool,” she said. The mango shone like gold.

Lila looked at all the food. Her tummy rumbled. She loved dumplings most of all. The dumplings looked round and perfect. She wanted them very much.

Lila took a deep breath. “Welcome, friends. We each brought something special. We will eat together,” she said. Her tail flicked with joy.

They sat on cushions around the table. The room was full of happy sounds: clink, clink went the spoons. Slurp, slurp went the soup. Asha hummed a tiny tune. The light hummed softly too.

Then a small problem came. Lila saw the dumplings. There were only six. Lila counted, “One, two, three, four, five, six.” There were five friends and one extra. Lila’s eyes grew big. “I want two,” she thought. Her hoof inched closer.

Timo’s whiskers twitched. “I love dumplings,” he said.

Bao smiled. “I made them with Nana,” he said. “We folded them with care.”

Lila felt a tight pinch in her chest. She wanted more. But she also wanted to be kind and fair. She looked at her friends’ faces. She saw hope. She saw trust.

Lila pulled her hoof back. “We need a fair way,” she said. “We can share.” Her voice shook a little, but her heart felt brave.

“How can we make it fair?” asked Zara, slow and calm.

Lila thought. She listened to the soft simmer of the soup. She smelled the sweet mango. She watched steam swirl like little dancers. “First,” Lila said, “everyone gets one dumpling.” She placed one on each plate. “Now we have one dumpling left.”

Asha chirped, “Who should get the last one?”

Lila looked at Bao. “Bao made them. Bao can choose,” she said. “That is fair and kind.”

Bao blinked. “I want to be generous,” he said. He broke the last dumpling in half. The skin was tender. The filling smelled like ginger and love. “Lila, you take half,” he said. “And Timo, you take half.” Bao smiled. “I like to share.”

Timo grinned. “Thank you! That is so kind.”

Lila’s cheeks felt warm. “I wanted more,” she said softly. “But sharing feels better.” She passed the basket of bread to Asha. “Your rice looks so bright,” Lila added. “May I try a little?”

“Yes,” chirped Asha. She spooned a small heap on Lila’s plate. “And try my spice blend,” she said. “It is gentle, not hot.”

Zara nudged the mango plate toward Timo. “Taste a slice,” she said. “It is sweet like a summer nap.”

They all tasted each dish. Each bite had a new story. Timo said, “In my family, we eat corn stew on cool nights.” Asha said, “My auntie sings while she cooks rice.” Bao said, “My Nana folds dumplings and laughs.” Zara said, “My mangoes grow near a blue river.”

Lila listened. She felt her heart stretch wide. Each friend had a different home and a different way. Each way was special. The room felt full of care.

After the plates were empty, Lila stood up. She poured warm tea into tiny cups. The tea smelled like flowers. “Thank you for sharing your foods and your stories,” she said. “We made a fair plan. We were generous. We learned from one another.”

Timo tapped his cup. “Justice means we all get a fair chance,” he said.

Asha nodded. “Generosity means we give with happy hearts.”

Bao giggled. “Tolerance means we welcome each other’s ways.”

Zara smiled. “And together, we are a big family.”

Lila’s eyes sparkled. “I feel full in my tummy and full in my heart,” she said. She wrapped a small napkin around two mango slices. “These are for our neighbor Fox,” she added. “He could not come. Let’s share with him too.”

They all cheered, “Yes!”

Lila trotted to the door with the little bundle. The night air was cool and sweet. She knocked and left the mango gift. Then she returned to the warm glow of the dining room.

“We shared our food,” Lila said, curling up on her cushion. “And we shared kindness.”

The friends smiled, bellies warm, hearts brighter than the lamp.

The End.
