# Polly Paints with Words

Polly was a creative parrot with bright green feathers and a sunny yellow beak. She lived with Mia and her family. Today, Polly perched on the headrest in the family car. The car smelled like orange snacks and warm seats. The sun made little sparkles on the window. Vroom, went the engine, like a soft hum.

Mia buckled her seat belt and smiled. “Ready, Polly?” she asked.

Polly puffed up with pride. “Ready! Ready!” she chirped. Her voice sounded like tiny bells. She loved words. She loved sounds. She loved making feelings with words.

Dad started driving. Mom passed back a small bag of crunchy crackers. Crack, crunch. Polly listened. She liked to listen before she spoke. She felt brave and happy.

“Let’s play a word game,” said Dad. “Let’s use kind words.”

Mia clapped. “Yes! Kind words!”

Polly bobbed her head. “Kind words are colors!” she said. “Kind words paint pictures.”

“What color is ‘please’?” asked Mia.

“Please is soft blue,” Polly said. “Like the sky that says hello.”

Mia giggled. “What color is ‘thank you’?”

“Thank you is golden,” Polly said. “Like warm sunshine in your tummy.”

Mom smiled. “That is beautiful, Polly.”

The car rolled past tall trees. Whoosh went the wind. Polly watched the leaves dance. She wanted to share more. Her feathers felt fluffy with ideas.

Dad said, “Polly, can you make a poem?”

Polly’s smile got big. Then she heard a tiny voice inside say, Maybe your poem is not good. Maybe you are too silly. Polly’s feathers drooped. She felt small.

Mia noticed. “Polly, what’s wrong?” she asked softly.

Polly looked at her shiny beak in the window. “I… I’m not sure,” she said. “What if my words are not special?”

Dad glanced in the mirror. “Everyone’s words are special,” he said. “Your words are yours.”

Mom nodded. “We respect each other’s voices,” she added. “We listen.”

Mia held up a cracker. “Polly, I want to hear you. Your words help me feel brave.”

Polly took a breath. In. Out. The air smelled like orange and new crayons. She remembered she was creative. She remembered she was kind. She remembered that trying is brave.

“Okay,” Polly said. “I’ll try.”

She looked out the window and began, slow and gentle.

“Road is a ribbon,
Wind is a song,
Together in the car,
We all belong.”

Mia clapped again. “I love it!”

Polly’s heart felt warm. She sat taller. “I can do more,” she said. “Words can show respect.”

“What would that sound like?” asked Mom.

Polly sang it softly, like a lullaby. “Please and thank you, yes and no, kind and careful, take it slow.”

Dad grinned. “That helps us drive safely, too.”

They stopped at a red light. The car was quiet. Tick, tick went the turn signal, like a tiny woodpecker. A little boy in the next car looked over. He waved. He looked shy.

Polly waved a wing. “Hello,” she said kindly.

The boy whispered, “Hi.” He looked like he wanted to say more, but he held back.

Polly remembered feeling unsure just a minute ago. She wanted the boy to feel strong, too. “Your hello is bright,” Polly said. “Like a star. Thank you.”

The boy smiled. His shoulders relaxed. “Thank you,” he said louder. He showed a toy car. “Vroom!”

“Vroom!” Polly echoed. “Great car!”

The light turned green. The cars rolled forward. The boy waved again. Polly felt a happy glow. Respect feels warm, she thought. Kind words help us shine.

Mia leaned her head back. “Polly, can you teach me a new word?” she asked.

Polly nodded. She loved new words from many places. “How about ‘hola’?” she said. “It means hello in Spanish.”

Mia tried it. “O-la,” she said.

“Ho-la,” Polly corrected gently, smiling. “You did great!”

Dad tried too. “Hola!”

Mom added, “Bonjour!” She laughed. “That’s hello in French.”

Polly clapped her wings. “Languages are like songs,” she said. “Each one is a new color.”

Mia’s eyes were big. “I want to learn more colors!”

Polly’s feathers shone. She felt proud. Her voice was steady and sweet. “You can,” she said. “We all can. Our words can be kind in any language.”

They parked at the grocery store. Doors clicked. Sunlight warmed the seats. Mia unbuckled and hugged Polly gently. “Thank you for your words,” she whispered. “You helped me feel brave.”

Polly hugged back with her wings. “I felt scared,” she admitted. “But I tried. My words matter.”

Mom smiled. “That is self-esteem,” she said. “Believing in yourself.”

Dad nodded. “And respecting others.”

Polly bobbed. “And loving language!” she added.

They all laughed. The family stepped out together, hand in wing, hand in hand. Polly looked at the blue sky and the dancing leaves. She felt tall on the inside.

She took a breath and said, “Kind words are colors. Today, we painted a happy day.”

The End.
