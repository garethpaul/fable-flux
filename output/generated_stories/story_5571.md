# Tiko the Shy Turtle Takes Turns

Tiko the turtle was shy. He liked quiet sounds: the soft rustle of leaves, the tiny tap-tap of his toes on wood, and the whoosh of wind. Today, Tiko was climbing up, step by careful step, to a big treehouse with a round green door. The treehouse smelled like pine needles and sunshine.

Inside, friends were busy. Mia the mouse shook a little drum. Omar the owl stacked colorful cups. Priya the panda made a soft scarf from old t-shirts. Yumi the yak and Ben the bunny hummed a song. Everyone smiled.

“Welcome, Tiko!” said Mia. “We have a plan.”

Omar blinked his bright eyes. “We will make a Treehouse Sharing Fair!”

Tiko tucked his head, just a little. “A fair?” he whispered.

Priya nodded. “Yes! We will share games from our homes. We will take turns so everyone can play.”

Ben wiggled his nose. “And we will use boxes and bottles to make toys. Recycling is kind!”

Yumi’s hooves clacked softly. “We will plan together,” she said. “One step at a time.”

Tiko liked the word plan. It felt safe. It felt slow. He peeked out more and listened. The treehouse floor felt warm under his feet, and spiky pine cones made a pretty pattern on the windowsill.

“First,” said Omar, “we write the plan.” He held up a big paper. It smelled like crayons and sweet fruit.

Mia read aloud, pointing with her small paw:
- Make toys from old things.
- Share games from our families.
- Take turns with a timer.
- Be brave and speak up kindly.

Tiko nodded. He loved lists. “I can help,” he said softly.

They began. Ben and Tiko made a marble maze from a pizza box and paper rolls. “Roll, little marble, roll,” Ben sang. Tiko felt the smooth round marble in his hand. He taped the rolls with neat lines, making tiny tunnels.

Priya showed a ribbon dance from her family. “We dance to say hello,” she said. Bright ribbons swished and tickled the air. Mia taught a clap-clap, stomp-stomp game from her grandma. “Clap, clap, stomp, stomp!” It made the floor thunk in a happy way. Yumi hummed a mountain song, low and cozy. Omar drew a moon puzzle from a cereal box, and Tiko placed the pieces, click, click, click.

Soon, friends from the forest came up to the treehouse. The room filled with warm breath, soft fur, and excited whispers. A breeze brought the smell of apples. The Sharing Fair began!

Mia rang a tiny bell. “We take turns,” she said. “One, two, three friends, then switch.” She held a kitchen timer that went tick tick tick.

At first, it worked. Three friends tried the ribbon dance. Then three friends played the moon puzzle. The timer went ding! and everyone switched, smiling.

But then more friends came. A long line formed at the marble maze. “My turn! My turn!” a squirrel chattered. A raccoon grabbed the marble. The line wiggled and wobbled. Tiko’s tummy felt twisty. He saw a little hedgehog in the back, holding a tiny toy drum. The hedgehog’s eyes looked shiny and wet.

Tiko wanted to help, but he was shy. He pulled his head into his shell, and the world became soft and quiet. He breathed in. He breathed out. He remembered the plan: Be brave and speak up kindly.

Tiko peeked out. His heart thumped like Mia’s drum. “Excuse me,” he whispered. No one heard.

He tried again, a tiny bit louder. “Excuse me.” The raccoon’s ears flicked. The squirrel paused.

Tiko took one brave step forward. He felt the wooden floor under his feet and smelled the warm apple air. “We… we take turns,” he said. “The timer can help. Three friends, then switch. Please.” His voice trembled, but he stood tall for a turtle.

Mia smiled at him. “Go, Tiko!”

Omar lifted the timer. “Tick tick tick!” he said, and set it by the maze.

Tiko spoke again, a little stronger. “Let’s make a turn line. Big friends help small friends. If you had a turn, you can show someone new.”

The raccoon blinked. “Oh. Okay.” He handed the marble to the little hedgehog. The hedgehog’s smile grew like sunshine.

“Thank you,” whispered the hedgehog.

Ben hopped. “I can be a helper!” He gently guided the line. “When the timer dings, we switch!”

Ding! went the timer. Friends cheered and changed places. The room felt calm again. Swish went the ribbons. Click went the puzzle. Roll went the marble. Tiko’s tummy felt smooth, not twisty.

Priya clapped. “Tiko was brave. He helped us take turns.”

Yumi nodded. “That is good planning.”

Mia waved her drum. “And we shared games from our homes!” She showed Tiko a new move from her clap-stomp game. Omar taught a moon word from his family: “Qamar,” he said softly. Everyone tried the sound together. “Qa-mar.” It felt round and lovely in their mouths.

At the end, they cleaned up. They put ribbons in a basket. They folded the paper plan. They saved the boxes and bottles for more toys next time. The treehouse smelled like crayons, apples, and happy friends.

Tiko looked around. He felt proud. He had been shy. He had been brave. He had helped everyone take turns.

Mia asked, “Tiko, what did we learn today?”

Tiko smiled. “We learned to take turns. We learned to be brave and speak up kindly. We learned to share and enjoy games from each other’s homes. We planned, and we used old things to make new fun.”

Everyone cheered. The sun slipped through the leaves like golden confetti.

“Thank you, Tiko,” said the little hedgehog. “I had a turn because of you.”

Tiko’s heart felt warm. “We all had turns,” he said. “That is fair. That is kind.”

They all said together, “Taking turns makes fun for everyone!”

The End.
