# Tilda Turtle’s Pantry Plan

Every morning, Tilda the shy turtle woke up in her cozy shell-bed. She stretched her tiny legs and whispered, “Good morning, day.” Tilda liked her routine. It made her feel calm and brave. She whispered her steps like a little song.

“Wash face. Brush teeth. Breakfast. Check pantry. Help friends.”

Tilda lived near a big kitchen pantry. The pantry smelled like warm oats and sweet apples. The floor felt cool and smooth under her little turtle feet. The shelves were tall like gentle giants. Jars clicked when the door swung. Crackers rustled in their boxes. It was a soft, friendly place.

One sunny day, Tilda shuffled to the pantry to do her daily check. She wore her tiny list around her neck. It was a simple picture list: a bowl, a broom, a clock, and a heart. The bowl meant food for breakfast. The broom meant tidy time. The clock meant routine time. The heart meant kindness.

“Hello, pantry,” Tilda said, peeking in. She sniffed vanilla, cinnamon, and fresh bread. Her tummy rumbled. “Routine time,” she said, smiling.

“Good morning, Tilda!” chirped Pip the small, bouncy mouse. Pip was not shy. Pip was bright and quick. “I’m hungry! Let’s eat all the crackers!”

Tilda shook her head gently. “We follow our steps,” she said. “We share. We count. We make a plan.”

Pip bounced. “But I’m hungry now!”

Tilda pointed to her picture list. “First, we look. Then, we count. Then, we choose.” Her voice was soft, but steady.

They looked together. On the lowest shelf sat a big jar of oatmeal, a box of crackers, three shiny apples, and a small jar of honey. The honey gleamed like sunshine.

Tilda took a breath. “We count.”

“One, two, three apples,” she said, touching each one. “One cracker box. One honey jar. One oatmeal jar.”

Pip wiggled. “I see! I see! Let’s take all the crackers!”

Tilda smiled. “Fair is fair. We share with everyone. Justice means fair.” She held up a tiny chalk and drew three simple shapes on a little slate: an apple, a bowl, and a star. “Breakfast plan.”

Suddenly, there was a rustle near the door. Bean the bear cub peeked in, paws dusty with flour. “I smelled honey,” Bean said, licking his lips. “Can I have the whole jar?”

Tilda’s heart thumped. She was shy, but she felt brave in her routine. She took a slow breath. “We have a plan,” she said. “We can all eat. We can be fair. We can try new ideas.”

Bean frowned. “But I am big and hungry.”

Tilda nodded. “Yes. And we are all hungry. Let’s be open-minded. Let’s listen.”

She tapped her slate. “Idea one: oatmeal with apple slices and a tiny drop of honey for everyone.”

Pip’s whiskers twitched. “Tiny drop?”

Bean’s ears drooped. “Tiny?”

Tilda nodded again. “We can have more tomorrow. Routine helps us save today and have some later. Fair today. Fair tomorrow.”

Pip looked at the cracker box. “What about crackers?”

Tilda thought. “Crackers can be snack later. Routine says breakfast first, snack later. We can use the broom after to tidy crumbs. We can put things back in the same place. Then we will always find them.”

Bean sighed, then smiled a little. “I can try.”

“Thank you,” Tilda said. “Trying new things is brave.”

They worked together. Tilda measured oatmeal with a tiny scoop. Tap, tap, tap. She poured warm water—swish, steam, and a soft oatmeal smell filled the pantry like a hug. Pip washed the apples. Splash, splash. Bean carefully carried the bowls, steps slow like a big gentle drum. Tilda sliced the apples into little smiles. She drizzled one tiny drop of honey on each bowl. Drip. Drip. Drip. The honey ribbon looked like golden threads.

They sat on a small mat. “Breakfast time,” Tilda said.

Pip took a bite. “Mmm! Sweet and soft.”

Bean tasted. His eyes went wide. “Warm and cozy,” he said. “The tiny honey is enough.”

Tilda smiled inside her shell. “Routine helps us. Fair helps us. Trying new things helps us.”

After breakfast, Tilda pointed to the broom on her picture list. “Tidy time,” she said.

Bean picked up the broom. “I can sweep.”

Pip gathered apple peels. “I can toss these in the little bin.”

Tilda wiped the bowls. Swish, swish. The pantry looked neat again. The shelves were happy and quiet.

Just then, a rustle came from the top shelf. A small bag of oats had a rip. Oats trickled out. Pip gasped. “A spill!”

Bean looked worried. “Is it okay if I climb and fix it?”

Tilda felt her heart beat fast. The shelf was tall. She took a slow breath. “We can solve it. We can be resourceful.”

She looked around. “We can build a safe step.” She stacked two sturdy boxes side by side. She pressed them to make sure they were steady. “I will hold the boxes. Pip will hold the flashlight. Bean will climb slowly.”

They all nodded. Working together felt strong and kind.

Bean climbed. Step, step. Pip shined the light. Tilda held the boxes, feet firm and sure. Bean tucked the bag and clipped it with a clothespin. The oats stopped trickling. “Got it,” he said.

Back on the floor, they smiled.

Tilda put the oatmeal back in its place and made a small label with a picture of a bowl. “Labels help our routine,” she said. “We can all see and share.”

Bean patted the broom. “I like this plan.”

Pip did a tiny hop. “Me too!”

Tilda touched the heart on her picture list. “Kindness time,” she said softly. “Thank you for listening. Thank you for helping. Today we were fair. We tried new ideas. We followed our routine. We solved problems.”

Bean and Pip hugged Tilda gently. Her shell felt warm.

“Tomorrow, we will do it again,” Tilda said, smiling. “Wash face. Brush teeth. Breakfast. Check pantry. Help friends.”

And the pantry seemed to hum with happy smells and quiet shelves, ready for another fair and friendly day.

The End.
