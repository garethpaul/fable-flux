# Ruby Rabbit Speaks Up

Ruby was a small, determined rabbit with soft gray fur and bright, curious eyes. One sunny morning, she hopped into the laundry room. The floor felt cool under her paws. The room smelled like warm soap and clean cotton. The washing machine hummed, “Whirr, whirr,” and the dryer made a happy, “Thum, thum, thum.”

Ruby’s favorite red scarf was in the laundry basket. She loved that scarf. It kept her neck cozy, and it made her feel brave.

“Oh!” said Ruby, peeking into the basket. “My scarf is all tangled.”

She tugged gently. “Oof.” The scarf was stuck under a heavy towel. The towel smelled like lemons and sunshine. The scarf smelled like strawberries from yesterday’s picnic.

“I can do this,” Ruby whispered to herself. Her ears perked up. “I am determined.”

Ruby pulled the towel to the side. She took a deep breath, like a slow balloon filling up. In, out. In, out. “I can ask for help if I need it,” she said. “Asking is brave.”

Just then, the washer started to beep. “Beep-beep!” The sound was loud. Ruby’s whiskers twitched. She felt a little jumpy inside.

“Beep-beep!” The noise made her heart thump. “I don’t like that sound,” she said. “It is too loud for me.”

Ruby remembered something Mama Bunny taught her: “Use your calm voice. Use your kind words. Tell others what you need.”

Ruby hopped to the washer. She placed a gentle paw on the warm door. It felt smooth and a little steamy. “Washer,” she said softly, “the beeping is too loud for me. I need a quieter sound, please.”

The washer blinked its little lights. “Beep,” it said, softer now. Ruby smiled. “Thank you,” she said.

Ruby turned back to the basket. “Okay, scarf, I will help you,” she said. She lifted the lemon towel with both paws. She moved slow and steady so the scarf would not stretch. “One, two, three,” she counted in a whisper. “Almost free.”

Just then, her friend, Dot the Dotted Sock, peeked out from the dryer door. Dot was pink with blue spots and a tiny wrinkle at the toe. “Hi, Ruby!” Dot chirped. “Let’s spin! It’s so fun in here. Wheee!”

Ruby shook her head. “No, thank you, Dot,” she said. “Spinning makes my tummy wobbly today. I need quiet and gentle. I am helping my scarf.”

“Aw,” said Dot, bouncing. “But I like to spin and bounce and sing!”

Ruby felt a little worried. She wanted to be a good friend. She also wanted to feel calm. She remembered: “My feelings matter. My voice matters.”

Ruby took another slow breath. “Dot,” she said kindly, “I like you. I like your songs. But I need a calm space right now. Please keep the singing soft while I help my scarf.”

Dot wiggled and thought. “Soft singing? I can do soft,” Dot said. Dot sang a tiny hum, like a sleepy bee. “Hmmmmm.”

Ruby smiled. The room felt peaceful again. The dryer went “Thum, thum,” like a quiet drum. The soap smelled sweet and clean.

Ruby tugged the scarf once more. “I will move this towel to the side, and I will pull gently,” she said. She spoke her plan out loud. It helped her feel strong.

The scarf slid free! “I did it!” Ruby cheered. She wrapped the scarf around her neck. It felt warm and soft. Her heart felt warm and soft, too.

Just then, a big sheet tried to slip off the laundry pile like a white snow cloud. It flopped over Ruby’s head. “Whoosh!” Everything turned white and muffled. Ruby could hear only her own breath. “Mmph!”

Ruby’s nose wiggled. She felt a little scared. She wanted to shout. But she paused, and she remembered: “Use calm words. Ask for what you need.”

“Dot?” Ruby said, voice steady. “I’m under the sheet. I can’t see. I need help, please.”

Dot hopped over. “I can help!” Dot grabbed a corner with her tiny sock tail and pulled. “One, two, three—whoosh!” The sheet slipped off. Light filled the room again.

“Thank you, Dot,” said Ruby, blinking. “That was kind.”

“You used strong words,” Dot said proudly. “You said what you needed!”

Ruby nodded. “Yes. I asked for help. I used my calm voice. I took care of myself.” She patted her scarf. “That is self-care.”

Dot bounced twice. “Can we play a quiet game now?”

Ruby thought. “Yes,” she said. “Let’s play Fold and Count.” She picked up a small washcloth. It was fluffy and smelled like clouds. “Fold,” she said, making a square. “One.” Dot helped fold a tiny sock. “Two.” They giggled and counted together. “Three, four, five!”

When they were done, the laundry room looked neat and cozy. The washer hummed softly. The dryer was quiet. The basket was tidy. Ruby felt proud. Her body felt calm. Her scarf hugged her neck like a gentle friend.

Dot sang a tiny thank-you song. “Thank you, Ruby. Thank you, room. Thank you, quiet boom-boom drum.”

Ruby smiled. “Today I learned I can speak up for myself. I can say, ‘No, thank you,’ and I can say, ‘Please help.’ I can make a peaceful plan.”

Dot nodded. “Peaceful plans are the best.”

Ruby hopped to the door and looked back. “I am determined,” she said. “And I am kind.”

The End.
