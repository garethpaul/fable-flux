# The Waiting Spoon

Benny Bunny was a small, determined rabbit with soft gray fur and bright, curious eyes. He loved to hop, he loved to sniff, and most of all, he loved to help. Today, Benny was helping in the dining room. The long table was set with colorful plates and shiny spoons. Warm, cozy smells floated in the air. Cinnamon, ginger, and a sweet, bready smell made Benny’s nose wiggle-wiggle.

“Mmm,” said Benny. “What is that yummy smell?”

Mama Bunny smiled. “Friends are coming for dinner, Benny. We are having special foods from different places.”

“From different places?” Benny asked, ears standing tall.

“Yes,” said Mama. “Auntie Mei is bringing dumplings. Mr. and Mrs. García are bringing rice and beans. Nana Coco is bringing plantains. And we made carrot stew.”

Benny’s tummy gave a happy rumble. “Can we eat now?” he asked, hopping from paw to paw.

“Not yet,” Mama said gently. “We wait for our guests. Waiting is part of sharing.”

Benny looked at the shiny spoons. He saw the steam from the carrot stew curl up like tiny clouds. He heard little pops from the kitchen and a soft clink-clink as plates touched the table. Waiting felt hard.

“I am a determined rabbit,” Benny told himself. “I can do hard things.”

He picked up a napkin and folded it into a neat triangle. “Fold, fold,” he whispered. He set it down. Then he folded another. “Fold, fold.” The napkins made a happy little pile. The waiting felt a tiny bit easier.

Ding-dong! The doorbell rang. Benny hopped to the door.

Auntie Mei came in with a steamy basket. “Ni hao!” she sang.

“Ni hao!” Benny tried, smiling big.

Auntie Mei opened the basket, and a puff of warm, gingery air floated out. “Dumplings,” she said. “We eat with chopsticks.”

Benny peered into the basket. The dumplings looked like soft little moons. “Can we eat now?” he asked, ears twitching.

“Soon,” Mama said. “We wait for everyone.”

Benny took a deep breath. “I can wait,” he said. He touched the basket. It felt warm on his paws. The smell made his tummy rumble again, but he kept waiting.

Ding-dong! Mr. and Mrs. García came in with a big pot. “Hola!” they said.

“Hola!” Benny chirped. The pot smelled like tomatoes and garlic. He heard a soft bubble-bubble sound. “Rice and beans,” Mr. García said with a grin. “We like to add lime.”

Benny rolled a lime between his paws. The skin felt bumpy. He sniffed. “It smells bright!” he said.

“Sí,” Mrs. García laughed. “Bright and fresh.”

“Can we eat now?” Benny asked again, bouncing just a little.

“Almost,” Mama said. “We are still waiting.”

Benny’s whiskers drooped, but he nodded. “I am a determined rabbit. I can wait.” He set tiny spoons by each bowl. “Clink, clink,” went the spoons.

Ding-dong! Nana Coco arrived with a dish covered in foil. “Bonjour!” she sang. “Plantains for sweet smiles.”

Benny lifted the foil corner. The plantains were golden and soft. They smelled like sunshine and sugar. “They look like little boats,” Benny said.

“Little boats to sail to your tummy,” Nana Coco joked.

Benny giggled. His tummy answered with a big “grrrr-umble.”

Everyone laughed kindly. “You are doing so well, Benny,” Mama said. “Waiting shows patience. Patience is kindness in time.”

Benny liked that. Kindness in time. He sat in his chair and put his paws in his lap. He watched the steam curl and the candles glow. He listened to happy voices. He smelled warm dumplings, bright lime, sweet plantains, and cozy carrot stew. He felt the smooth wood of the table under his paws. He waited.

Then a tiny problem popped up. “Oh dear,” Auntie Mei said. “We only have four pairs of chopsticks.”

The table was full. There were eight friends!

Benny’s ears perked. “We can share,” he said. “And we can take turns.” He looked around. “And we can use spoons too!”

Auntie Mei clapped softly. “Good idea, Benny.”

Mr. García squeezed a lime and passed it on. “We all try everything,” he said. “We can learn from each other.”

Nana Coco cut the plantains into small pieces. “Little bites for little mouths.”

Mama ladled carrot stew. “We pass to the left,” she said. “And we wait our turn.”

Benny watched a bowl come to him. He wanted to scoop fast. But he remembered: patience is kindness in time. He waited for the person next to him to be ready. Then he scooped a little, and he smiled.

“This dumpling is soft,” Benny said, tasting carefully. “It tastes like a warm hug.”

“The rice and beans feel cozy,” he added. “And the lime is tickly!”

“The plantain is sweet and mushy. It tastes like a smile.”

“And the carrot stew is my favorite,” he said proudly. “It tastes like home.”

Everyone shared, everyone tried, everyone smiled. The dining room felt full of colors and sounds and happy hearts. Benny’s tummy felt full too, but most of all, his heart felt big.

After dinner, Benny helped clear the table. “I waited,” he told Mama quietly. “I was patient.”

Mama hugged him. “Yes, Benny. You were patient, you shared, and you learned from our friends. That is how we show love and acceptance.”

Benny beamed. “Patience is kindness in time,” he repeated.

“And it makes dinner taste even better,” Nana Coco said with a wink.

Benny giggled. “I can wait again next time.”

The End.
