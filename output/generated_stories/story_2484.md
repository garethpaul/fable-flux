# Rusty’s Brave Brush

Rusty was a helpful robot dog with shiny silver ears and a soft whirr in his belly. He lived in the creative art room, where the floor felt smooth like paper, and the air smelled like crayons and fresh paint. Sunlight danced on jars of colored buttons, yarn, and reused bottle caps. On the wall was a big sign that said, “We make art. We are kind. We try again.”

“Good morning!” Rusty beeped. His tail, a little paintbrush, wagged back and forth. Swish-swish.

Miss Ana, the art teacher, smiled. “Good morning, Rusty. Today we will make a big mural with re-used things. We will use cardboard, old magazines, and bottle caps. We care for our Earth.”

Rusty’s bright eyes blinked. “I can help!”

Children came in with happy feet and giggles. “Hi, Rusty!” they said. Rusty nuzzled their hands with a gentle click. The room buzzed with the soft rip of paper, the tap-tap of caps, and the sweet smell of glue.

Miss Ana showed a big blank paper wall. “This is our mural. We will make a garden with a rainbow. Everyone can add something.”

Rusty looked at the tall paper wall. It was big. Very big. His little robot heart—click-click—felt nervous. The wall seemed higher than his head and as wide as the room. Rusty whispered to himself, “I feel a tiny scared.”

Miss Ana knelt down. “It’s okay to feel scared. Courage means doing your best even when you feel a little scared.”

Rusty took a slow, brave beep. “I will try.”

He picked up a green crayon with his brush-tail and drew a little leaf. His paw motors hummed: “Whrrr-whrrr.” The leaf wiggled a bit. “Oops,” Rusty said.

A child named Lila clapped. “It’s a wiggly leaf! I like it.”

Rusty smiled. “Thank you. I will try again.” He drew another leaf, a little steadier this time. “Practice helps,” he said, feeling warm and brave inside.

Miss Ana placed a box on the table. “Look! Reused things. Bottle caps, cardboard shapes, and magazine pictures. We can make a rainbow garden.”

Rusty sniffed the box. His nose sensors beeped, “Ping!” He saw red caps, blue caps, and shiny silver lids. He also saw a big, bumpy cardboard piece.

A boy named Theo said, “Let’s use the silver lids for moonflowers!”

Rusty tilted his head. “Moonflowers? Flowers at night?”

Theo nodded. “Yes! Flowers can be any color.”

Rusty’s eyes glowed. “I like new ideas.” He felt open and curious. “I will try.”

Together they glued the silver lids into a circle. The glue smelled sweet and sticky. Rusty held the lid steady while Theo pressed it down. “Press, press,” said Theo. “Stick, stick,” said Rusty.

A loud thump made everyone jump. The tall ladder near the mural wobbled. “Oh!” Lila said. “We need the ladder to reach the top for the rainbow.”

Rusty looked up. The ladder seemed so high. His tummy whirred faster. “I am scared again,” he whispered. “What if I slip?”

Miss Ana touched Rusty’s head gently. “Courage doesn’t mean you are not scared. Courage means you take a safe, brave step.”

Rusty took a breath. “Safe and brave.” He looked at the ladder and then at his little magnet paws. “I have magnet paws! I can click to the steps.”

He tested one step. Click. He tested another. Click. “I can do this,” he said softly. His brush-tail wagged. Swish-swish.

“Go, Rusty!” the children cheered.

Up he climbed, slow and careful. His sensors blinked. “Left paw. Right paw. Click-click.” He reached the top! He could see the whole room. The mural looked like a big, happy hug.

“I made it!” Rusty called. His voice sounded bright.

“Would you like to paint the top of the rainbow?” Miss Ana asked.

Rusty nodded. “Yes, please.” He dipped his brush-tail into red paint. It felt cool and smooth. He painted a big red curve. Then orange. Then yellow. “I can try new colors,” Rusty said. “I can learn.”

The children added green, blue, and purple. They glued leaves and caps for flowers. They cut pictures from old magazines to make butterflies. Snip-snip. Tap-tap. Swish-swish.

Rusty climbed down, click-click. His paws tingled from being brave. Lila giggled. “Rusty, your nose has purple paint!”

Rusty laughed. “It smells like grapes!”

Miss Ana said, “Look at our mural! We used old things to make new art. That helps our Earth.”

The children clapped. Rusty’s chest felt warm and bright. He remembered the tall wall and the high ladder. He remembered feeling scared. He remembered trying, and trying again.

Rusty stood tall. “Today I was brave. I tried even when I felt scared. I learned that I can do hard things with safe steps and help from friends.”

Theo raised a hand. “And we used old stuff! We gave it a new job.”

Lila added, “And we liked new ideas. Moonflowers!”

Miss Ana smiled. “Yes. Today we used courage. We grew our skills by practicing. We kept open minds. And we cared for our Earth.”

Rusty wagged his brush-tail. “I will remember: Be brave. Try again. Listen to new ideas. Use what we have with care.”

The mural shone in the sunlight, bright and beautiful. Rusty looked at it and felt proud. “Tomorrow,” he said, “I will try something new again.”

The End.
