# Panda Pippa’s Story Time Plan

In a comfortable nursery with soft rugs and twinkly star stickers on the ceiling, lived a patient panda named Pippa. Pippa had a cozy corner with a blue beanbag, a basket of books, and a small lamp that glowed warm like honey. Every evening, when the lamp clicked on, it was time for story time.

“Click,” went the lamp. “Ahh,” sighed Pippa, rubbing her fluffy cheeks. “Story time is my favorite time.”

Pippa liked routine. She brushed her teeth, washed her paws, and picked a book. She liked to tell stories too, using her gentle voice and big panda paws to make characters come alive.

One evening, Pippa heard a soft sniffle. Her little friends, Tilly the turtle and Max the mouse, peeked around the beanbag.

“Pippa,” said Tilly, pulling her tiny blanket, “I can’t sleep. My tummy feels wiggly.”

“My thoughts are bouncing,” squeaked Max. “Boing, boing!”

Pippa took a slow breath in and out. “We can fix wiggly tummies and bouncy thoughts,” she said calmly. “Let’s have story time.”

But there was a problem. The basket was full of books. There were animal books, shape books, a moon book, and a book with shiny, scratchy scales. Tilly wanted the moon book. Max wanted the shape book. Pippa wanted to tell a new story she made up.

“Oh no,” said Max. “What do we do?”

Pippa patted the soft rug. “We can make a plan,” she said. “First, we choose. Then, we tell. Then, we cuddle.”

She set three smooth buttons on the rug. One had a moon sticker, one had a triangle sticker, and one had a little speech bubble to show telling a story.

“Let’s decide together,” Pippa said. “We listen, we think, and we choose. That’s how we do it.”

Tilly tapped the moon button. “I like the moon. It’s quiet and round.”

Max tapped the triangle button. “I like shapes. They make my brain go click!”

Pippa touched the speech bubble button. “I like to tell a new story.”

Three choices. Three friends. One routine to keep.

Pippa hummed a gentle tune like wind in leaves. “We can do one tonight,” she said, “and save two for later. We will take turns.”

“How do we pick?” asked Max.

“By using our thinking caps,” said Pippa, placing a soft washcloth on each friend’s head like a silly hat. “We think about what helps us feel calm tonight.”

Tilly closed her eyes. “The moon is sleepy and soft,” she whispered.

Max wiggled his whiskers. “Shapes are snappy and bright,” he squeaked, “but my thoughts are already bouncy.”

Pippa nodded. “A calm story might help bouncy thoughts settle.”

Max peeked at Tilly. “Maybe the moon book tonight?”

Tilly smiled, her eyes shiny as buttons. “Thank you, Max.”

Pippa smiled too. “We decided together. That is good decision-making.”

She clicked on the lamp a little brighter. “Click.” The warm light cuddled the room. The smell of clean blankets and a hint of lavender filled the air. Pippa opened the moon book. The pages were smooth and cool under her paws.

“In a high, quiet sky,” Pippa began, “a sleepy moon watched over a tiny town.”

Her voice was soft, like a blanket. As she read, she added small sounds.

“Shhh,” went the breeze.

“Tip-tap,” went the rain on leaves.

“Hoo, hoo,” sang a faraway owl.

Tilly snuggled closer. Max’s whiskers stopped twitching. The star stickers on the ceiling seemed to twinkle along.

When the moon story ended, Pippa closed the book with a gentle “whump.” She looked at her friends. “I have a tiny extra,” she whispered. “A mini make-believe.”

Max’s eyes brightened. “A little one?”

“Little as a ladybug,” Pippa said. “Here it comes.”

She told a tiny tale about a triangle who wanted to be a moon, and a moon who wanted to be a triangle. They tried each other’s jobs, then decided they liked being themselves. Pippa used her paws to make a triangle shadow, then a round moon. The soft lamp made the shadows dance on the wall. Tilly giggled. Max made a quiet, happy squeak.

Pippa ended the mini story with a slow, deep breath. “The end,” she said softly.

Tilly yawned. “My tummy feels calm now.”

Max nodded. “My thoughts went from boing to ahh.”

Pippa tucked a corner of the blanket around them. “We followed our routine,” she said. “We brushed, we washed, we chose, we read, we cuddled. And we told a tiny story. We used our listening and our thinking to decide.”

“What about shapes?” Max asked, sleepy.

“Tomorrow,” Pippa said, tapping the triangle button and placing it on top of the basket. “We saved our turn.”

“And your new story?” Tilly murmured.

“The next day,” Pippa said, putting the speech bubble button under the lamp. “We have a plan.”

The room was quiet. The lamp hummed. Outside, the night whispered. Inside, three friends felt safe and soft. Pippa smiled and patted their paws.

“Stories can help our feelings,” she said. “Routines can help our bodies. Good choices can help our minds.”

Max and Tilly nodded, eyes closing.

Pippa clicked the lamp to a gentle glow. “Click,” she whispered. “Goodnight.”

They drifted to sleep, hearts light, a plan for tomorrow waiting like a bookmark.

The lesson settled like a soft blanket: tell your story, follow your routine, and use your listening and thinking to make kind choices.

The End.
