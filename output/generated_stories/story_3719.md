# Whiskers and the Wiggly Wrench

In a sunny little house, there was a garage workshop that smelled like wood shavings and orange soap. The floor felt cool, the shelves were neat, and the tools made tiny clink-clink sounds when they touched. This was Whiskers’ favorite place.

Whiskers was a clever cat with soft gray fur and bright, curious eyes. He loved to help. He loved to watch. He loved to tinker. His best friend was Grandma Dot, who wore blue overalls and a smile that crinkled her eyes.

“Today,” Grandma Dot said, patting a wooden stool, “we will build a toy shelf for your blocks and balls. But first, we follow directions.”

She held up a paper with big pictures. “Step one, step two, step three,” she said, tapping each picture with her gentle finger. “We go slow. We go in order.”

Whiskers’ tail flick-flicked. He wanted to help now. He wanted to twist the shiny screws and make the wood go together. He wanted to hear the happy thump of a finished shelf.

“I can do it!” Whiskers purred. “I am clever!”

Grandma Dot nodded. “Yes, clever cat. Clever means caring, too. We follow directions to stay safe and to make it right.”

She set out the pieces on a soft blanket: four boards that smelled like fresh trees, a small bag of silver screws that jingled like tiny bells, a wiggly wrench, and a quiet drill that hummed like a bee when it was on.

“Step one,” Grandma Dot said. “Sort the boards by size.”

Whiskers looked at the boards. Big, big, medium, small. He started to line them up. But then—ooh!—the wiggly wrench gleamed in the light.

“I will do the screws first!” Whiskers said. He picked up the wrench. It felt cool and smooth in his paw.

“Wait,” Grandma Dot said kindly. “Directions first.”

But Whiskers’ paws were quick. He tried to twist a screw into a board. Twist-twist. The screw went in crooked. The wood made a tiny squeak. Whiskers’ whiskers drooped.

“Hmm,” he said, “that did not work.”

“Let’s go back,” Grandma Dot said. “Step one. Sort.”

Whiskers huffed a little. He felt a small storm inside his chest. It was the feeling called frustration. His ears went a little flat. He wanted to zoom ahead. He wanted to be done.

He took a deep breath. In. Out. He remembered what Grandma Dot always said: “When the feelings feel big, stop, breathe, and wiggle your toes.” Whiskers wiggled his soft paws. The storm got smaller.

“Okay,” he whispered. “Step one.”

Together, they sorted. Big, big, medium, small. The boards looked neat. The workshop felt calm.

“Step two,” Grandma Dot read. “Find the matching holes.”

Whiskers leaned close. He sniffed the wood—sweet, like a pencil. He listened—quiet, like a nap. He counted the holes. “One, two, three, four,” he said, tapping lightly. Tap, tap, tap, tap. He matched the holes. Click! The pieces sat together like friends.

“Step three,” Grandma Dot said. “Use the screws.”

Whiskers picked up the screw bag. Jingle-jingle. He smiled. “Now the wiggly wrench?”

“Yes,” said Grandma Dot. “But gentle. And straight. I will hold. You will turn.”

Whiskers set the screw carefully. The wrench went twist, twist, pause. Twist, twist, pause. The screw went in straight. The wood did not squeak. Whiskers’ tail made a happy swish.

“You did it,” Grandma Dot said.

Whiskers purred. His chest felt warm. “We did it.”

They followed the rest of the steps. They checked the picture. They counted again. They tightened each screw with slow, steady turns. The drill buzzed softly—bzzzzz—and then stopped. The shelf stood up, strong and smooth.

Whiskers placed a red ball on the top shelf. Plop. He put blue blocks on the middle. Clack. He set a yellow car on the bottom. Vroom—then still.

“It works!” he cheered. He hopped up and down, thump-thump, on the soft mat.

Then Whiskers looked at the crooked screw he had tried first. He touched it gently. “Grandma Dot,” he said, “I didn’t follow directions at the start. I was too fast. I made a squeak and a crook. I am sorry.”

Grandma Dot knelt and stroked his back. “Thank you for your apology, clever cat. That was brave. Do you want to fix it together?”

“Yes, please,” Whiskers said. The storm inside was gone. The sun inside was back.

They loosened the crooked screw. Twist, twist. They set it straight. Click. They tightened it again. Twist, twist, pause. Now the board was snug. No squeak. Just quiet pride.

Whiskers looked around the workshop. The tools were back in their places. The bag of screws was closed. The paper with the steps was on a clip. The air smelled like oranges again. Everything felt organized.

“I like when things have a home,” Whiskers said.

“Me too,” Grandma Dot smiled. “Following directions helps us finish. Organizing helps us find and fix. And taking a breath helps when we feel frustrated.”

Whiskers nodded. “When I feel the storm, I can stop, breathe, and wiggle my toes.”

“And if we make a mistake,” Grandma Dot added, “we can say sorry and try again.”

Whiskers climbed onto the new shelf and curled his tail around his paws. “I followed the steps,” he purred. “Step by step makes strong and safe.”

Grandma Dot tapped the paper. “Step by step makes smart and calm.”

They shared a soft laugh. The workshop felt cozy. The shelf stood tall. Whiskers’ heart felt big and peaceful.

The End.
