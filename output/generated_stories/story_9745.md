# Tully Turtle’s Tap-Tap Task

Morning light peeked through the blinds in the home office. The room smelled like paper and warm cocoa. Keys clicked softly on a keyboard. Tap-tap-tap. Printers hummed. Hummmmm. It was a busy, cozy place.

Tully Turtle lived there with Dad and a big, leafy plant named Ferny. Tully was a shy turtle. He liked quiet. He liked soft socks. He liked listening to the tiny clock go tick-tick-tick on the desk.

Today, Tully had a big job. He wanted to finish his “To-Do Shell List.” He had drawn little pictures on sticky notes. Three jobs waited:

1. Stretch and shell-ercise.
2. Sort the colored papers.
3. Water Ferny the plant.

Tully took a deep breath. “I can do it,” he whispered. His voice was small and sweet.

Dad peeked in with a smile. “Good morning, Tully. What’s your plan today?”

Tully held up his sticky notes. “I’m going to do all three,” he said. His cheeks felt warm. He was shy, but he was brave, too.

Dad nodded. “Step by step. Slow is okay. Slow is strong.”

Tully smiled. He liked that. Slow is strong.

First job: Stretch and shell-ercise. Tully rolled out a tiny mat beside the desk. The carpet felt fuzzy under his feet. He wiggled his toes. He rolled his shoulders. “Stretch high,” he said, reaching his little turtle arms up, up, up. His shell felt snug and safe. “Stretch low,” he said, touching his toes. He bent side to side. Sway, sway. He marched in place. March, march. His heart went thump-thump in a happy way.

“I’m warming up like toast,” said Tully with a shy giggle.

“You’re doing great,” said Dad from the chair. Tap-tap went the keyboard. Hummm went the printer.

Tully tried ten tiny shell push-ups. Down, up. Down, up. After five, his arms felt wobbly like jelly. He stopped and frowned. “This is hard,” he whispered.

Dad swiveled his chair. “Hard can be good,” he said. “We get stronger when we keep going. How about two more? Then rest. Then two more.”

Tully nodded. “Step by step.” He did two more. Rested. Then two more. “I did it!” he said softly, but with sparkly eyes.

Second job: Sort the colored papers. On the low shelf sat a stack of papers for Dad’s work. Red, blue, yellow, green. The colors were bright like candy. Tully loved colors, but the pile was messy. It leaned like a wobbly tower.

Tully made four little piles on the rug. “Red here,” he whispered, sliding the red papers over. Rrrrip-shufff went the rough edges. “Blue here.” Swish. “Yellow here.” Flip. “Green here.” Rustle.

The stack wiggled and slipped. Some papers slid under the chair. “Oh no,” said Tully. His tummy fluttered. He felt shy asking for help. He tucked his head a little.

Dad noticed. “Want a paper picker-upper?” he asked, showing a long ruler.

Tully nodded. “Thank you.”

He used the ruler to reach the papers. He took his time. He kept going. Red with red. Blue with blue. Yellow with yellow. Green with green. His piles grew neat and tall. Tully clapped once. Clap! “I’m not giving up,” he told himself. “Slow is strong.”

Dad gave a thumbs-up. “You are persistent, Tully. That means you keep trying.”

Tully rolled the word around in his mouth. “Per-sis-tent.” It felt big and brave.

Third job: Water Ferny the plant. Ferny stood by the window, tall and leafy, with a shiny pot. The dirt looked dry. Tully filled a small watering can. The water made a soft glug-glug sound. He carried it carefully. Step. Step. Step.

A little splash dripped on the floor. “Oops,” said Tully. He paused. He wiped it with a cloth. Then he tried again, slower. He tipped the can just a little. Drip… drip… drip. The dirt turned dark and smelled like fresh rain. Ferny’s leaves trembled in the light breeze from the window. “There you go,” Tully whispered. “Drink, Ferny.”

All three jobs were done! Tully looked at his sticky notes. He put a tiny star sticker on each one. Star. Star. Star. His heart felt warm and proud. He had stretched his body. He had sorted the papers. He had watered Ferny. He had finished his To-Do Shell List.

Dad pushed back his chair. “How do you feel, Tully?”

Tully thought. He listened to the soft tick-tick-tick of the clock. He touched his strong little arms. He looked at the neat paper piles and the happy plant. “I feel… brave,” he said. “I kept going. I didn’t give up. Slow is strong.”

Dad knelt and hugged him gently. “That’s persistence,” he said. “You moved your body, you finished your tasks, and you did it step by step.”

Tully’s shy smile grew big. “Tomorrow,” he said, “I will make a new To-Do Shell List. I will try again. I can do hard things.”

He rolled up his mat. He lined up the paper piles. He set the watering can back by the window. The home office felt tidy and calm. The air smelled like paper and rain and cocoa. Tap-tap went the keyboard. Hummm went the printer. Tick-tick-tick went the clock.

Tully tucked his sticky notes into a little box. He gave Ferny a gentle pat. “Thank you for cheering me on,” he whispered.

Ferny’s leaves brushed his shell. Swish.

Tully laughed. “Slow is strong,” he said again, and his brave heart thumped like a happy drum.

The End.
