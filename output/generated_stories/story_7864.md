# Kiki Koala and the Busy Pantry

In a cozy house with a creaky wooden floor, there was a pantry. It smelled like warm bread, sweet honey, and crunchy oats. Little glass jars lined the shelves. Labels made soft crinkly sounds when the door opened: swish, swish.

Kiki the koala lived nearby with her family. Kiki was calm and curious. She liked soft hugs, slow climbs, and quiet thinking. Today, Mama Koala said, “Kiki, you may help make snack time. We will make oat balls with fruit.”

Kiki’s ears perked. “I can help all by myself?” she asked.

“You can help with three steps,” Mama said, tapping a paper with her claw. “Step one: Wash paws. Step two: Ask before opening jars. Step three: Use the small scoop. We follow directions to be safe.”

Kiki nodded. “I can do it,” she whispered. She liked being independent. She liked doing things on her own.

They walked to the pantry. The cool air tickled Kiki’s nose. She smelled cinnamon—warm and spicy. She heard the tiny rattle of oats in a tin: pat-pat-pat.

Mama smiled. “I will stir. You can gather oats, dried mango, and honey. Remember our steps.”

Kiki washed her paws. The water was splishy and cold. “Step one!” she chirped. She dried them on a soft towel.

She reached for the oats. The tin lid made a pop. “Oats!” she said. She used the small scoop. Scoop, tap. Scoop, tap. The oats fell like quiet rain into a bowl.

Then Kiki saw a bright jar high up. The label showed a little bee. The honey looked golden, like sunrise. “I want to see,” she said. She reached up, up, up. Her paw slipped. The jar wobbled—wibble wobble!

“Stop,” Mama said gently. “Remember step two. Ask before opening jars, especially high ones.”

Kiki felt her chest get tight. Her face felt warm. “But I can do it!” she said. Her tummy buzzed like a bee. Her paws curled. Her breath got fast.

Mama noticed. She knelt to Kiki’s eye level. “I see you are feeling big feelings,” she said softly.

Kiki stomped one foot. “I want honey now!” Her voice got loud. Tears tickled her nose. She wanted to grab the jar. She wanted to shout.

Mama put a paw on her heart. “Let’s use our calm koala plan,” she said. “When anger shows up, we can stop, breathe, and choose.”

Kiki blinked. “Stop, breathe, choose,” she whispered.

“Let’s try together,” Mama said. “Smell the flower.” They breathed in slowly through their noses. “Blow the candle.” They breathed out gently through their mouths. In… out. In… out.

Kiki felt her tummy slow down. The buzz got quieter. The tight felt less tight. “I can feel calm coming back,” she said, surprised.

Mama smiled. “You did that yourself. Now, what can we choose?”

Kiki looked up at the honey jar. “I can ask for help,” she said. “Mama, can you help me with the high honey?”

“Yes,” Mama said. “Thank you for asking.” She reached safely and brought the jar down. “Honey is special. Bees work hard. We use only what we need. That helps the bees.” She showed Kiki a tiny picture in a book from the shelf. Bees on flowers. Flowers in a garden. “We care for nature. That is conservation.”

Kiki nodded. “We take only a little,” she said. “So bees and flowers stay happy.”

Mama opened another small jar. “These are dried mango pieces from Auntie Mei,” she said. “Auntie Mei taught us how to dry fruit in the sun. In her culture, they share sweet fruits at festivals. We can appreciate and say thank you.”

Kiki held a mango square. It was chewy and smelled like sunshine. “Thank you, Auntie Mei,” she said softly.

Now Kiki followed directions. She used the small scoop for honey: scoop, drip, plop. She asked before opening the mango jar. She counted three pieces. “One, two, three!” She mixed with a wooden spoon. Swirl, squish, squish. Oats hugged the mango and honey.

“Roll the balls,” Mama said. “Gentle circles.”

Kiki rolled one, two, three oat balls. They felt sticky and cool. Her paws smelled sweet. “I did it!” she giggled.

There was one more jar on the shelf. It had green leaves inside. “What’s that?” Kiki asked.

“That is tea from Grandpa,” Mama said. “We drink it warm and say a little thank you for the farmers who grow it with care. We follow their directions to brew it right. Too hot, and it gets bitter. Just right, and it is cozy.”

Kiki listened. The kettle hummed. She watched steam curl like a ribbon. She waited, even though waiting was hard. She practiced: smell the flower, blow the candle. Her body stayed calm.

Soon they sat with oat balls and warm tea for Mama. Kiki took a bite. Crunch, chew, yum! The honey tasted like flowers. The mango tasted like sun.

Mama touched Kiki’s paw. “You used your calm koala plan. You followed directions. You asked for help and did your part.”

Kiki smiled. “When I felt mad, I stopped, breathed, and chose,” she said. “I can do hard things. I can be gentle with me.”

They put the lids back on the jars. Twist, click. They wiped the shelf with a soft cloth. Swish, shine. “We save some mango for later,” Kiki said. “We use only what we need.”

Mama nodded. “That helps our home and the planet.”

Kiki hugged Mama. The pantry felt warm and safe. “Next time I feel mad,” Kiki said, “I will smell the flower, blow the candle, and ask for help.”

“That’s a wonderful plan,” Mama said.

Kiki took another bite and whispered, “I am a calm koala.”

The End.
