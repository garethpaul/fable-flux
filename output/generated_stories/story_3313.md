# Bubbles, Breaths, and Helping Hooves

The gentle deer fawn, named Faye, tiptoed into the big, shiny bathroom. The floor felt cool on her tiny hooves. The mirror looked like a calm lake. The air smelled like sweet soap and minty toothpaste. Drip, drip, drip went the faucet, like little raindrops singing.

Faye carried a little basket. Inside were soft towels, a tiny brush, and a fluffy washcloth. “I am ready for bath time,” she said in a small, brave voice. “I can do it.”

She turned the knob. Warm water whooshed into the tub. Bubbles grew like white clouds. Pop! Pop! Pop! They tickled her nose. Faye giggled. “Hello, bubbles!”

Then—thump! The soap slid down, splat! It landed on the floor. The soap was slippery. Faye tried to pick it up. Slip! Slide! The soap ran away like a silly fish. “Come back, soap!” she said.

She tried again. Slip! The soap bumped her hoof and plopped under a stool. Faye’s ears drooped. Her cheeks felt hot. Her heart went thump-thump fast. “I don’t like this,” she said. “I feel mad.”

She stomped one little hoof. The stomp made a loud clop in the bathroom. The bubbles trembled. The mirror showed a fawn with crumpled eyebrows.

Faye remembered what Mama Deer always said. “When you feel big mad, stop and take three slow breaths.”

Faye put a hoof on her belly. “Breathe in… one,” she whispered. She smelled minty air. “Breathe out… whooo.” Her belly went soft. “Breathe in… two.” The water’s whoosh sounded gentle now. “Breathe out… whooo.” “Breathe in… three.” She heard drip, drip, drip. “Breathe out… whooo.”

Her eyebrows relaxed. Her heart slowed. Faye looked at the soap hiding under the stool. “I can be calm,” she said. “I can try a new way.”

She looked around. She saw a long wooden spoon near the sink. She saw a small towel on a hook. She had an idea. “I can be resourceful,” Faye said with a smile. “I can use tools!”

She wrapped the towel around the spoon so it would not scratch the floor. She slid the spoon under the stool. Scoot, scoot. “Come here, soap,” she said kindly. “I will help you.” The spoon nudged the soap. Plop! The soap slid out and sat still.

Faye clapped her hooves. “I did it!” She felt proud and calm. “Thank you, breaths. Thank you, spoon.”

Just then, she heard a tiny voice from the hallway. “Squeak-squeak! Help!”

Faye peeked out. A small mouse named Mo was near the bathroom door. Mo held a broken cup. Water dribbled down the hall. “I was carrying water for my family,” Mo said, eyes big and shiny. “The cup cracked. Now there is a mess. I feel mad and sad.”

Faye nodded. She remembered her hot cheeks from before. “It’s okay to feel mad,” Faye said softly. “Let’s breathe together.”

Mo sniffled. “Okay.”

“Hoof on belly,” Faye said. Mo put a tiny paw on his tummy.

“Breathe in… one,” said Faye. “Out… whooo.”

“Breathe in… two,” said Mo. “Out… whooo.”

“Breathe in… three,” they both whispered. “Out… whooo.”

Mo’s whiskers stopped shaking. “I feel better,” he said. “But there is still a mess.”

Faye smiled. “We can help. We can do community service. We can clean together.”

They went back into the bathroom. Faye gave Mo a small towel. She took a big towel for herself. “We are helpers,” she said. “We can make things nice.”

Swoosh, swish went the towels. They soaked up the water. Faye used the long spoon to push a sponge into the little cracks. Squish, squish. Mo picked up tiny wet leaves with his quick paws. Plink, plunk into a bucket they went.

“Thank you, Faye,” said Mo. “You are very smart.”

Faye shook her head and giggled. “We are smart together. We used calm breaths. We used tools. We helped our home.”

They checked the tub. The bubbles still waited, shiny and white. “Would you like a tiny paw bath?” Faye asked.

Mo laughed. “Just a rinse!” Faye turned the knob a little. Warm water kissed Mo’s paws. “Ahh,” said Mo. “It feels nice.”

Faye washed her own hooves with the soap she had rescued. She hummed a little song. “Wash, wash, wash. Calm and clean. Wash, wash, wash. Kind and keen.”

The bathroom smelled extra fresh now. The mirror showed two smiling friends. The floor was dry and safe. The drip, drip, drip had stopped because Faye gently turned the knob tight.

Mo waved. “I will bring a new cup next time. I will carry it with both paws.”

Faye nodded. “And when we feel mad again, we will stop, breathe three times, and try a new plan.”

Mo squeaked, “Yes!”

Faye packed her basket. Soft towels, tiny brush, fluffy washcloth—everything in place. She felt light and happy. She wiggled her ears and said, “Today I learned that mad feelings can get small when I breathe. I can use tools and friends to solve problems. Helping makes our home bright.”

Mo clapped. “We are calm helpers!”

They bumped paw to hoof. “Team calm!” they cheered.

The bathroom was quiet. The bubbles went pop-pop-pop, like little goodbye kisses. Faye turned off the light and tiptoed out with Mo. Their steps sounded soft and sure.

The End.
