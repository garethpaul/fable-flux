# Panda Poppy Speaks Up

Poppy was a patient panda with soft black ears and a round, warm belly. She buckled into her seat in the family car. Click! The seat belt made a snug little sound. The car smelled like oranges from a snack bag, and the sun made warm stripes on the seats.

Mama Panda started the engine. Vroom. “Ready to go, Poppy?” Mama asked.

“Yes, Mama,” Poppy said. She folded her paws and looked out the window. The trees swished by like green brushes. The tires sang a low hum. The car was cozy, and Poppy felt calm.

After a while, Poppy’s legs felt tingly. “My feet are fizz,” she whispered to herself. Her toes wiggled, like little worms wanting to dance. Poppy took a deep breath. She remembered something from preschool: It’s okay to speak up.

“Mama?” Poppy said in a small voice.

“Yes, sweet pea?” Mama kept her eyes on the road.

“My legs feel jumpy,” Poppy said. “I need to move. Can we stop soon?”

Mama glanced in the mirror. “Thank you for telling me,” she said. “That was good self-advocacy.”

“Self-advo… what?” Poppy asked, blinking.

“Self-advocacy,” Mama said, smiling. “It means you speak up about what you need.”

“Oh,” Poppy said. She sat taller. “I did that!”

But the car kept going. The hum got louder in Poppy’s ears. Her belly felt fluttery now, like a little bird. She wiggled her paws. She didn’t want to fuss. She wanted to be patient. She was a patient panda. But she also wanted to move.

Poppy thought. She could ask again. She could try to make a plan. “Mama,” Poppy said, “I have an idea.”

“I’m listening,” Mama said.

“If we can’t stop now,” Poppy said slowly, “can I do seat wiggles? And can we stop at the next safe place?”

“That’s a good plan,” Mama said. “Let’s try seat wiggles. And we will stop at the next rest area.”

Poppy felt proud. She started seat wiggles. She pushed her heels down. She lifted her toes up. She wiggled her knees. She did tiny shoulder circles. Round and round, like the car’s wheels. She took a deep breath in—smell the oranges—and blew out slowly—like blowing a bubble.

“Wiggle, wiggle, wiggle!” she whispered. Her legs felt better, not too fizzy now.

“Great self-advocacy and great movement, Poppy,” Mama said. “Thank you for being patient while I find a safe place.”

Poppy nodded. The car passed a big green sign. “Rest Area—1 Mile.” Poppy read the number with Mama. “One,” she said. “Almost there.”

The car turned in and rolled to a stop. The air outside felt cool and fresh. The breeze smelled like pine trees and dust. Birds chirped, “Peep, peep!”

“Let’s do a stretch,” Mama said. “What moves do you want?”

Poppy thought again. “I want Panda Stomp and Tall Bamboo.” She loved those.

They stood next to the car. Poppy stomped her feet—thump, thump—like a gentle drum. She raised her paws high—Tall Bamboo—stretch, stretch. She bent to touch her toes—hello, toes! She wiggled like a jelly bean. She shook her paws—shake, shake, shake.

A little rabbit family nearby watched. “That looks fun,” said a small rabbit. “What are you doing?”

Poppy smiled. “I am moving my body to feel good. I told my Mama I needed to stop. That is self-advocacy.”

“Can we try?” the small rabbit asked.

Poppy looked at Mama. Mama nodded. Poppy felt brave. “Yes,” she said. “I can show you. I can be a helper.” Her heart felt warm, like a cup of cocoa. Helping felt like volunteering.

“First, Panda Stomp,” Poppy said. Everyone stomped—thump, thump—soft but strong.

“Next, Tall Bamboo,” Poppy said. Everyone reached up—stretch, stretch—like green stalks in the sun.

“Then, Jelly Bean Wiggle,” Poppy sang. Giggle, wiggle, giggle!

The rabbits laughed and wiggled. A bird hopped closer, chirping along. Mama clapped. “Great leading, Poppy.”

When it was time to go, Poppy took another deep breath in and out. Her legs felt happy now. Her belly bird had flown away.

Back in the car, she clicked her seat belt. Click. “Mama,” Poppy said, “thank you for stopping.”

“Thank you for asking in a kind way,” Mama said. “You negotiated a plan. You were persistent and patient.”

“Ne-go-sha-ted?” Poppy tried the new word.

“It means you shared your idea, I shared mine, and we found a way that worked for both of us,” Mama said.

Poppy grinned. “I can do that.”

They drove on. The road hummed softly. The trees waved hello. Poppy watched clouds that looked like pillows. She felt calm and proud.

Soon, Poppy felt a little thirsty. She remembered the lesson. She sat up. “Mama,” she said, “I need a sip of water. Can I have my bottle when you stop at a light?”

“Yes,” Mama said. “Thank you for speaking up, Poppy. That’s self-advocacy again.”

At the next red light, Mama handed back the bottle. “Sip, sip,” Poppy drank. Cool water slid down, and she sighed, “Ahhh.”

When they reached home, Poppy unbuckled. Click. She gave Mama a hug. Mama’s fur smelled like clean soap and sunshine.

“I learned something today,” Poppy said.

“What did you learn?” Mama asked.

“I can say what I need. I can move my body. I can make a plan with you. I can help others,” Poppy said. “That makes car rides happy.”

Mama smiled. “Yes, sweet pea. When we speak up kindly, we keep our bodies and hearts feeling good. That is self-advocacy.”

Poppy felt tall like bamboo. She whispered to herself, “I can speak up. I can wait. I can wiggle. I can help.” Her paws felt steady, and her smile felt big.

The End.
