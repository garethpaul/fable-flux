# Tiko the Turtle Finds His Voice

Tiko was a shy turtle. He liked to tuck his head in his shell when the world felt big. Today, Tiko was riding in a cozy family car with his parents and his big sister, Lila. The car hummed softly. The seats were warm, and the air smelled like oranges from the snack bag.

“Road trip songs?” Lila cheered, kicking her feet in little happy taps.

Papa Turtle started a jazzy song. Mama Turtle clapped. Lila sang loud and bright, “La-la-la!”

Tiko smiled, but he felt his shy bubble grow. The music was fun, but he wanted something else. He had brought a new book about seashells. The pages felt smooth and cool. He wanted quiet to discover the pictures and whisper the names.

He pulled his head a little into his shell. The car window buzzed as they rolled over a bumpy road. Tiko sniffed the orange air and took a slow breath.

“Are you okay, Tiko?” Mama asked gently. Her voice was soft, like a blanket.

Tiko peeked out. “I’m okay,” he said in a tiny voice. “I want to look at my book.”

Lila sang louder, “LA-LA-LA!” She did not hear him.

The music made Tiko’s tummy feel fizzy. He wanted quiet, but he loved his family’s happy singing too. He wondered, Can we find a way that works for all of us?

He remembered what his teacher said: “Use your words. Say what you need. Listen to others. Make a plan together.”

Tiko took another breath. He could try.

He tapped the back of Papa’s seat with one gentle claw. “Papa?”

Papa turned the music down a little. “Yes, Tiko?”

Tiko pressed his book to his chest. “I want to read my seashell book. The music is loud for me. Can we make a plan?”

Mama smiled in the rearview mirror. “A plan sounds smart.”

Lila paused, her mouth still in a singing shape. “But I love to sing,” she said. Her voice was bright, like sunshine.

Tiko nodded. “I know,” he said, looking at Lila with wide, kind eyes. “Singing makes you happy. Reading makes me calm. Can we take turns?”

Lila tipped her head. “How?”

Tiko looked at the car clock. The numbers glowed a soft green. “Maybe we sing one song,” he said, tapping a finger, “and then we have quiet for one page. Then we sing one song again. Then quiet for one page. We can switch.”

Papa whistled a little tune. “That sounds fair.”

Mama asked, “How will we know when to switch?”

Tiko thought. He felt the smooth shell under his chin. “I can raise my hand when my page is done,” he said. “And Lila can clap once when the song is done.”

Lila wiggled with excitement. “I can do that! One clap!” She clapped one soft clap to practice. It sounded like a tiny raindrop.

“Let’s try,” Mama said, turning the volume to very low. “We respect each other. We listen. We share time.”

They started. Lila sang one gentle song with the music low. Tiko listened and even hummed a little, his shell feeling less tight. When the song ended, Lila clapped once. Clap!

Now it was Tiko’s turn. He opened his book. The pages smelled like fresh paper. He traced a picture with his claw. “This is a scallop,” he whispered. “It looks like a fan.” The car was quiet. The hum of the road was like a lullaby. When he finished the page, he raised his hand.

Lila grinned. “My turn!” she sang softly.

They switched again and again. Song. Clap. Page. Hand. Song. Clap. Page. Hand.

Sometimes Lila forgot and started a second song. Tiko gently said, “Lila, can we switch now?” Lila nodded. “Oh! Yes. Thank you for reminding me.” She respected his words.

Sometimes Tiko took a long time on a page. Lila wiggled in her seat. Tiko saw her feet dance and said, “I can do a shorter page this time.” He respected her turn.

As they went, Tiko discovered new things. He learned the names: scallop, conch, and whelk. He felt brave showing the pictures. “Look,” he said, holding up a page during a quiet turn. “This one has stripes like your scarf, Lila.” Lila’s eyes sparkled. “It does!”

Lila discovered something too. She found she could sing softly and still feel joy. “La-la,” she sang like a whispering breeze during her turn. It felt kind and nice.

Mama smiled at the road. Papa tapped the steering wheel in a soft rhythm. The car felt peaceful and cozy. The plan worked.

After a while, Tiko said, “I have one more idea.” His voice was a little stronger now. “At the end, can we sing a song about seashells together? Then I can read a page to you.”

Lila clapped her raindrop clap. “Yes! Let’s make a seashell song.”

They made up words. “Scallop fan, conch so grand,” they sang, giggling. “Whelk with swirls in soft, soft sand.” Tiko laughed. His shell felt warm. He was not hiding now.

At last, Mama parked the car under a shady tree. The leaves made a soft sh-sh sound. Tiko read a page out loud to his family. They listened with kind faces. When he finished, everyone clapped one gentle clap.

Papa said, “I’m proud of how you made a plan.”

Mama added, “You used your words. You listened. You showed respect.”

Lila wrapped her arm around Tiko’s shell. “You helped us take turns. That was good negotiating, Tiko!”

Tiko’s cheeks felt toasty. “We made a plan together,” he said. “That’s what made it work.”

Lesson: When we say what we need, listen to others, and make a plan, we can all feel happy and respected.

The End.
