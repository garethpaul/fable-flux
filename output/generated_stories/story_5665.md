# Fern's Colorful Table Adventure

Every afternoon, Fern the gentle deer fawn tip-tapped into the dining room on quiet hooves. The room smelled like warm bread and cinnamon. Sunlight made golden squares on the wooden floor. The big table sat in the middle, wearing a plain white cloth. Today, Fern wiggled her little tail. Her heart felt jumpy with a new idea.

“I want to make the table smile,” Fern whispered. She pressed her soft nose to the smooth wood. “You are kind to hold our meals. You should wear colors!”

Fern looked around. On the sideboard sat crayons in a tin, a stack of napkins, and a small basket of safe, washable markers. There were paper placemats too. Little sparkles of curiosity fizzed in Fern’s tummy.

Mama Deer stepped in, humming. “Good afternoon, Fern,” she said. “Snack time soon.”

Fern lifted her head. “Mama, can I decorate the table? I want to make it friendly and bright.”

Mama Deer’s ears twitched. “That is a lovely idea, sweet fawn. But we must be fair to the tablecloth,” she said kindly. “It is clean and white. It cannot wear marker. That would not be just.” She smiled. “We can make art on paper placemats. Then the table stays safe, and your art can shine.”

Fern nodded. “That is fair,” she agreed. She felt proud. She was learning to make good choices.

Mama Deer set out four big paper placemats, smooth and blank like little snow fields. She put the markers and crayons in the middle. “You may design the placemats. One for you, one for me, one for Papa, and one for our friend, Squirrel, who is visiting.”

Fern’s ears perked. “I will make them tell a story!”

She picked up a blue crayon. Scritch-scritch. The sound was soft and friendly. She drew a river with tiny waves. Next, a green marker whooshed as she made tall grass. She sniffed the sweet crayon smell and smiled.

Fern hummed, “Swish, swish,” as she colored. “This one is the River Placemat,” she said. “It sings.”

She moved to the next placemat. With a yellow crayon, she drew a big warm sun. “This one is the Sunshine Placemat! It feels cozy like a hug.”

Mama Deer watched. “I love your ideas,” she said. “Your art shows your feelings.”

Fern’s chest felt warm. She liked that. She liked being understood. On the third placemat, she drew tiny acorns and a little brown tree. Dot, dot, dot, went the crayon. “This one is for Squirrel. It says, ‘Welcome, friend!’”

For the last placemat, Fern paused. Her hoof tapped the floor. “Hmm,” she said softly. “What should I do?”

She looked at the plain white tablecloth. She looked at the markers. Her curiosity wiggled again. “Maybe I could put colorful dots on the cloth. Just tiny ones. The table would look happy,” she thought.

Her hoof reached out. Then she pulled it back. “Wait,” she whispered. “Mama said no marker on the cloth. That would not be fair.”

Fern’s ears drooped a little. She wanted color so much. She felt a little stuck.

She took a deep breath. “I can solve this,” she told herself. “I can be creative.”

Fern looked around. A basket of napkins sat nearby: pale, soft, and plain. “Napkins!” she said. “What if I make napkin rings from paper? Then the color hugs the napkins.”

Mama Deer smiled. “What a clever idea.”

Fern cut gentle strips from extra paper with safe, child scissors Mama Deer handed her. Snip, snip. The sound was crisp. She colored each strip with happy patterns: polka dots, zigzags, and tiny hearts. She looped the strips and taped them into rings. Slip! Each ring slid onto a napkin.

Fern clapped her hooves. “Look! The napkins are wearing little shirts!”

When everything was ready, Papa Deer trotted in, and Squirrel scampered behind him. The air smelled like apples and peanut butter. Snacks were here.

Papa Deer blinked. “Oh my,” he said softly. “What a beautiful dining room.” He touched the River Placemat. “I can almost hear water.”

Squirrel twitched his whiskers. “Acorns! For me?” he squeaked, happy. “This feels kind and fair.”

Fern stood tall. “I wanted to share my ideas. I wanted each place to feel special.”

They all sat. The chairs made gentle creaks. Mama Deer poured milk. Plip-plop went the milk into glasses. Papa took a bite of apple. Crunch.

As they ate, they talked.

Papa Deer asked, “Fern, how did you think of napkin rings?”

Fern smiled. “I was curious. I wanted more color. But it would not be just to draw on the cloth. So I used paper instead. I solved the problem.”

Mama Deer nodded. “You listened to rules and still made something new. That builds strong self-belief. That is creative expression.”

Fern felt her heart puff like a soft pillow. “I can make art and make good choices,” she said. “I can try, and try again.”

Squirrel giggled. “I believe in you,” he chattered. “Also, I believe in snack time!”

They all laughed. The dining room felt bright and safe, like a sunny nest.

When snack was done, Fern gathered the placemats. “These are little stories we can use again,” she said. She patted the tablecloth. “Thank you for being clean.”

The sun slid across the floor. The golden squares moved like slow dancers. Fern’s eyes felt sleepy and glad.

“I learned something today,” Fern whispered. “When I use my imagination, and follow what is fair, I can make beauty. I can solve problems. I can feel proud.”

Mama Deer nuzzled her. “Yes, little artist,” she said. “Your ideas matter.”

Fern’s tail flicked in a happy rhythm. “Tomorrow,” she said, “I will make placemats with stars.”

“And we will be ready,” Papa Deer said.

The dining room was quiet and colorful. Fern’s heart glowed like her sunshine drawing.

The End.
