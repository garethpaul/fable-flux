# Tully Tortoise’s Treehouse Library Day

In a tall oak tree, there was a cozy treehouse with a little wooden ramp. The ramp was smooth and warm in the morning sun. A wise tortoise named Tully lived there. Tully wore tiny round glasses. He had a soft green shell and a gentle smile.

Inside the treehouse, the air smelled like pine and fresh paper. Shelves held colorful books and jars of crayons. A small bell chimed in the breeze. Tully loved knowledge. He said, “Knowing things helps us be brave and kind.”

One bright day, Tully woke up early. He stretched his legs and listened. Birds whistled. Leaves whispered shhh, shhh. Tully tapped his shell and said, “Today is Library Day. Friends will come. I must prepare.”

He made a list on a big yellow card.
“Books? Check.
Crayons? Check.
Snacks? Check.
Map? Check.”

Tully smiled. “Preparation is smart,” he said. “It helps us feel ready.”

Soon, tiny feet pattered on the ramp. A squirrel named Pip peeked in. “Hi, Tully!” chirped Pip. “I want to learn how to paint.”

A shy bluebird named Bree fluffed her feathers. “I want to learn new words,” Bree whispered.

A little raccoon named Ria wiggled her paws. “I want to be brave,” she said, eyes wide.

Tully nodded. “We can do all of that. Knowledge helps with art. Knowledge helps with bravery. Come in.”

The friends sat on a soft round rug. Sunbeams made warm squares on the floor. Tully opened a book. The pages sounded like swish-swish. “Today’s plan,” he said kindly. “First, we read. Then, we make art. Then, we try something new and brave. We can do it together.”

Tully read a story about colors. “Red is warm like a strawberry. Blue is calm like the sky. Yellow is bright like the sun.” He showed pictures. Pip’s tail flicked with joy. “I know colors now!” Pip said.

Bree leaned closer. “What is the word for green?” she asked.

“Green,” Tully said, smiling. “Green like my shell. Can you say it?”

“Green,” Bree chirped. Her voice was soft like a feather.

“Good learning,” Tully said. “Knowledge grows when we ask.”

Next, Tully brought out big paper and thick crayons. “Art time!” he said. The crayons smelled sweet and waxy.

Pip drew red apples and yellow stars. Bree drew tiny blue feathers and a big green leaf. Ria looked at the paper and bit her lip. “I don’t know what to draw,” she said. “I feel a little scared.”

Tully sat beside her. His shell felt cool and smooth against Ria’s paw. “It is okay to not know yet,” he said. “Let’s learn first. Let’s look.”

He pulled a book of shapes from the shelf. “A circle is like a cookie,” Tully read. “A square is like a window. A triangle is like a little mountain.”

Ria traced a circle with her finger. The paper felt bumpy. “Circle,” she said. Her eyes brightened. She drew a circle, then a square, then a triangle. “I did it!”

“You used knowledge,” Tully said. “You learned and then you drew. That is brave.”

The little bell on the window chimed again. A breeze carried a tiny sound—tap tap tap. Tully’s ears pricked. “What is that sound?”

Pip tiptoed to the door. “It’s the ladder,” he said. “One rung is loose.”

Bree hopped near the ramp. “Oh, dear,” she murmured. “Friends might fall.”

Ria stepped back. “I feel scared,” she whispered.

Tully took a deep breath. He put on a small tool belt. It had a tiny screwdriver, a soft cloth, and a little book. He opened the book. “How to Fix a Ladder,” he read. “Step one: check the rungs. Step two: hold the rail. Step three: turn the screw slowly.”

He looked at his friends. “Knowledge first. Then bravery. We will be safe because we prepare.”

Pip held the rail. Bree held the soft cloth. Ria held the little map of the treehouse and watched the steps. Tully turned the screw slow, slow, slow. The screwdriver made a gentle click, click. The wood smelled sweet and clean.

“All done,” Tully said. He pressed the rung. It felt strong and steady. Ria touched it too. “It is strong,” she said. “We fixed it together!”

“You were brave,” Tully told her. “You used the map. You helped.”

They went back inside. The room felt extra warm and happy. Tully clapped softly. “Now we make a Brave Art Poster.”

On a big sheet, they drew what they learned. Pip drew a ladder with a happy face. Bree wrote the word green in careful letters. Ria drew circles, squares, and triangles with smiling eyes. Tully drew a tiny book and a heart.

Bree read the words on the bottom. “Learn, prepare, be brave, be kind.”

Pip nodded. “Knowledge helped us fix the ladder.”

Ria grinned. “Learning made me brave.”

Tully placed the poster on the wall. He spoke in a calm, warm voice. “Today we learned that knowledge is a light. It helps us see what to do. We prepare. We make art. We are brave. And we are kind to each other.”

The sun began to set. The treehouse glowed golden. The friends hugged Tully. The wood felt smooth under their paws and wings. Outside, the leaves whispered good night.

“Thank you, Tully,” they said.

“Thank you for learning,” Tully replied.

The friends went down the steady ramp, one by one, smiling and safe. Tully closed his book and smiled at the poster.

“Knowledge first,” he whispered. “It makes every day a good day.”

The End.
