# The Bird Who Kept the Beat

Chirpy was a small singing bird with bright blue feathers and a tiny gold scarf. Every morning, Chirpy flew to the musical practice room. The room felt warm and safe. It smelled like polished wood. Soft sunlight made the drums shine and the piano keys gleam like teeth in a smile. The metronome on the shelf tick-tock, tick-tock, like a little wooden heart.

“Good morning!” Chirpy sang. “Today I will sing my best song.”

Chirpy wanted to sing a long song with many parts. The song had quiet parts, loud parts, fast parts, and slow parts. It was a big plan for a small bird.

Chirpy looked around the room and made a plan out loud. “First, I will warm up. Then, I will clap the beat. Next, I will sing my story. Last, I will bow.”

Chirpy stood on the piano and took a deep breath. “La, la, laaa!” The sound bounced off the walls. Chirpy’s feathers tingled. The notes felt smooth in the air, like floating bubbles.

Tick-tock, went the metronome.

Chirpy tried to sing with it. “La—oh! I went too fast,” Chirpy said, giggling. “I need to move with the beat.”

Chirpy tapped a tiny claw on the wood. Tap, tap, tap. “Tap with the tick,” Chirpy whispered. “Tap with the tock.” Tap, tap. Tick-tock.

A drum in the corner rumbled softly when a breeze touched it. “Hello, little bird,” the drum seemed to say in Chirpy’s imagination.

Chirpy smiled. “Hello, big drum. Will you help me keep the beat?”

Chirpy closed one eye and pretended the drum answered, “Boom means step. Boom means breath.”

“Okay,” Chirpy said bravely. “I can do this.” Chirpy felt a flutter in the chest. It was a little fear. The song was big. But Chirpy puffed up and stood tall. “Small bird, big plan!”

Chirpy began to plan again. “Step one: clap. Step two: hum. Step three: sing the story.”

Clap, clap. Clap, clap. Chirpy’s wings made a soft whoosh. The sound was like a gentle fan. “Clap with the tick-tock,” Chirpy said. The claps matched the metronome now, and Chirpy’s heart felt calm.

“Hum, hum, hummm,” Chirpy hummed and listened. The piano keys made a tiny sunny smell when warmed by light. Chirpy heard the echo and smiled. “I can tell my story now.”

“Once there was a little nest,” Chirpy sang softly. “The nest was high and safe.” Chirpy paused and took a breath on the tick. “One windy day, the nest shook.” Chirpy sang a wavy note, up and down. “But the nest held strong.”

Chirpy lined up each word with each beat. The metronome kept ticking. Chirpy moved tiny feet, one, two, one, two, to feel the beat in the body. The coordination felt good. Head nodded. Wings lifted. Voice followed. “I am moving together,” Chirpy said. “Body, breath, and beat.”

Suddenly, the metronome stopped. The room grew quiet. No tick. No tock. Only still air.

Chirpy froze. “Oh!” The plan was stuck. “What do I do?”

Chirpy took a brave breath. “I can be resourceful,” Chirpy said softly. “I can make my own beat.”

Chirpy looked around. There was a wooden block. There were two small mallets. Chirpy hopped to the block, grabbed a mallet with tiny claws, and tapped gently. Tok. Tok. Tok. The sound was soft but clear.

“I will count,” Chirpy said. “One, two, three, four.” Tap, tap, tap, tap.

Chirpy clapped wings with the taps. Clap, clap. The rhythm returned like a friend. “I’m back!” Chirpy laughed. “I can keep the beat myself.”

With the new beat, Chirpy sang the next part of the story. “A little branch bent low,” Chirpy sang. “But friends helped it grow.” Chirpy used a strong voice on “friends” and a soft voice on “grow.” The sounds felt like warm soup and cool water. Chirpy loved the balance.

Chirpy planned the ending. “Last verse, then bow.” Chirpy counted in again. “One, two, three, four.”

“Together we stay,” Chirpy sang. “Together we sway.” Chirpy swayed left and right to the taps, feet stepping in time, body, breath, and beat all coordinated. It felt like a dance. It felt like a story that moved.

Chirpy finished with a long, gentle note. “Laaaaa.” The note floated like a ribbon and then faded. Chirpy bowed low. The scarf fluttered like a tiny flag.

The room was quiet and happy. Chirpy’s chest felt warm. “I did it,” Chirpy whispered. “I kept my plan. I told my story. I stayed with the beat.”

Chirpy tapped the mallet once more and said, “Coordination helps me sing. I use my body, my breath, and my brain together.” Chirpy smiled. “When something stops, I can be brave and resourceful. I can make a new plan.”

Chirpy put the mallets back and gave the drum a gentle pat. “Thank you, drum,” Chirpy said. “Thank you, beat. See you tomorrow.”

As Chirpy flew to the window, the sun felt warm on tiny wings. The room smelled like wood and hope. Chirpy hummed, “One, two, three, four,” and felt steady and proud.

The clear lesson stayed in Chirpy’s mind: Move together, breathe together, and plan your steps. That is coordination, and it helps you do big things, even if you are small.

The End.
