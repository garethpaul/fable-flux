# Nutkin's Daily Practice

In a cozy, quiet attic filled with dusty boxes and golden sunbeams, lived a little gray squirrel named Nutkin. The attic was peaceful, with soft creaking sounds from the old wooden beams and the gentle patter of rain on the roof above.

Nutkin had a big dream. He wanted to organize all the scattered acorns he had collected for winter. But every time he tried to sort them by size, he would get tired and give up.

"This is too hard!" Nutkin would say, throwing his tiny paws in the air. The acorns lay everywhere - big ones, small ones, medium ones - all mixed up in a messy pile.

One morning, Nutkin's grandmother visited the attic. She was a wise old squirrel with silver whiskers and kind, twinkling eyes.

"Why do you look so sad, little one?" she asked, her voice warm like honey.

"I want to sort my acorns, but there are so many!" Nutkin explained. "I try and try, but I never finish. It's impossible!"

Grandmother Squirrel smiled and sat down beside the pile of acorns. "Let me tell you a secret, Nutkin. Big jobs become easy when you practice a little bit every day."

"Every day?" Nutkin's ears perked up.

"Yes! Instead of trying to sort all the acorns at once, what if you sorted just ten acorns each morning? That's called making a routine."

Nutkin thought about this. Ten acorns didn't sound scary at all!

The next morning, as the first rays of sunshine crept through the attic window, Nutkin carefully picked out ten acorns. He sorted them - three big ones in one pile, four medium ones in another, and three small ones in the third pile.

"That was easy!" he chirped happily.

The second day, he sorted ten more acorns. Then ten more the next day. Each morning became a special time for Nutkin's sorting practice.

"Good morning, acorns!" he would say cheerfully. "Time for our daily sorting!"

As days passed, something wonderful happened. The big messy pile grew smaller and smaller. Three neat piles grew bigger and bigger. Nutkin's tiny paws became faster and more skilled at picking out the different sizes.

"Look!" Nutkin called to a friendly spider who lived in the corner. "I'm getting really good at this!"

The spider, whose name was Webster, smiled from his web. "Practice makes everything easier, doesn't it?"

After two weeks of daily practice, Nutkin sorted his very last acorn. He stood back and looked at his three perfect piles - all the big acorns together, all the medium acorns together, and all the small acorns together.

"I did it! I really did it!" Nutkin danced around the attic, his fluffy tail swishing with joy.

Grandmother Squirrel returned that afternoon and gasped with delight. "Oh my! Look at these beautiful, organized piles!"

"I practiced every day, just like you said!" Nutkin beamed. "Ten acorns each morning. It wasn't hard at all when I did a little bit each day."

"You've learned something very important," Grandmother said, hugging him tight. "When we practice regularly and complete small tasks every day, we can accomplish amazing things. This skill will help you your whole life!"

From that day on, Nutkin used his daily practice routine for everything - cleaning his nest, gathering leaves, and even learning new climbing tricks. He discovered that being patient and working a little bit each day made even the biggest jobs feel possible.

The End.
