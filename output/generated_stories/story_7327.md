# Joey Jumps into Fix-It Fun

In a warm, sunny garage workshop, a joyful kangaroo joey named Kip hopped in place. The floor smelled like fresh wood. Sunlight made bright stripes on the dusty floor. Tools hung on the wall and clinked softly when the breeze came in. Kip’s tail swished. His paws felt tingly with excitement.

“Today I learn to build,” Kip said. “I can do it!”

Grandpa Roo smiled and patted a wooden workbench. “We will make a rolling toy,” he said. “We will learn new skills. Step by step.” His voice was calm and kind.

Kip’s eyes grew big. “A rolling toy? Like a little car?”

“Yes,” said Grandpa Roo. “A little wooden car.” He set out smooth blocks, tiny wheels, a small hammer, sandpaper, and paint. “We will be safe. We will go slow.”

Kip took a deep sniff. “I smell wood. I smell paint. I like this place!” He ran his paw along a block. It felt rough. “Ouch. Scratchy.”

“First skill,” said Grandpa Roo. “Sand the wood to make it smooth.” He showed Kip how to hold the sandpaper. “Round and round. Gentle circles.”

Kip rubbed the sandpaper on the wood. “Round and round,” he sang softly. Dust made little clouds that sparkled in the sun. The block felt smoother and smoother.

“Good job,” Grandpa Roo said. “You are building your skills.”

Kip grinned. “I am learning!”

Just then, Kip’s friend Willa Wombat peeked in the door. “Hi! What are you making?” she asked. Her nose twitched at the sweet wood smell.

“A rolling toy,” said Kip. “I am learning to sand.”

“I want to help,” said Willa.

Grandpa Roo nodded. “Sharing is caring. You can take turns. Kip sands, then Willa sands.” He handed Willa a little square of sandpaper. “Gentle circles.”

Kip watched Willa try. She pressed too hard. The paper squeaked. “Oops,” Willa said. “This is hard.”

“It’s okay,” said Kip. “We can try again. We can learn little by little.”

Willa smiled and tried softer. “Round and round,” she whispered. The block got smooth like a pebble.

“Skill two,” said Grandpa Roo. “Tap in the wheels.” He placed a wheel on a tiny axle. “Hold it steady. Tap, tap, tap.”

Kip lifted the small hammer. The hammer felt cool and heavy. “Tap, tap—oh!” The wheel rolled away under the bench.

Kip’s ears drooped. “I can’t do it,” he said. His nose scrunched. “It is too hard.”

Grandpa Roo knelt down. “You can learn. Skills grow with practice. Let’s take a breath.” They breathed in and out together. The air smelled like pine.

Willa looked under the bench. “I got the wheel!” she said, holding it up with a grin.

“Thank you for sharing your help,” Kip said. “I am grateful.”

“Try again,” said Grandpa Roo. “Hold the wheel with one paw. Tap softly. Tap like rain.”

Kip tried. Tap, tap, tap. The sound was soft like tiny raindrops on a leaf. The wheel slid onto the axle. “I did it!” he shouted, tail thumping.

“Great growth,” said Grandpa Roo. “You kept trying.”

They tapped in the other wheels, taking turns. Sometimes Kip tapped. Sometimes Willa tapped. They said, “Your turn!” and “My turn!” They cheered for each other. “You can do it!” “Tap like rain!” The little car sat on the bench, all four wheels on.

“Skill three,” said Grandpa Roo. “Test the roll.” He set the car down. Kip gave a gentle push. The car rolled across the smooth wood and made a soft whirring sound. Willa clapped. Kip giggled. The sound made his tummy feel warm.

“Skill four,” Grandpa Roo said. “Paint.” He opened bright paints. Red smelled sweet and tangy. Blue looked like the sky. Yellow was sunny. “Brush slow and smooth.”

Kip dipped his brush in blue. “Slow and smooth,” he said. He made a blue stripe. Willa made a yellow star. They took turns and shared the colors. They wiped drips with a rag. Grandpa Roo smiled. “Beautiful work.”

Kip looked at the toy car. It was shiny and bright. He felt proud. “I learned to sand. I learned to tap. I learned to share. I learned to try again.”

Willa nodded. “We learned together.”

Grandpa Roo handed them each a small wooden token shaped like a heart. “These are gratitude hearts,” he said. “We say thanks for help and learning.”

Kip held his heart. “Thank you, Willa, for finding the wheel and sharing the paints.”

“Thank you, Kip, for taking turns and cheering me on,” said Willa.

“Thank you, Grandpa Roo,” they both said. “Thank you for teaching us skills.”

Grandpa Roo hugged them. “You practiced. You were patient. You shared. That is how skills grow.”

Kip pushed the little car along the floor. Whirr, whirr, whirr. He beamed. “When something is hard, I can learn. I can try. I can ask for help. I can practice. My skills will grow.”

Willa rolled the car back to him. “We can build more next time!”

“Yes,” said Kip. “Next time we can make a boat. Or a plane!” He bounced with joy, paws patting the floor.

The garage felt cozy and bright. The tools were quiet. The paint was drying. The little car rested between them, a reminder of their work and their care.

Kip tucked the gratitude heart into his pouch. His heart felt full. He looked around the workshop and whispered, “I am proud of my skills.”

The End.
