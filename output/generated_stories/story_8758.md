# Gemma Giraffe’s Helpful Bath-Time Plan

Gemma was a thoughtful giraffe with long, speckled legs and a gentle smile. One morning, she stepped into the bright, clean bathroom. The tiles felt cool on her hooves. The mirror was foggy from warm steam. She sniffed the lemon soap. “Mmm, it smells like sunny lemons,” she whispered.

Gemma had a problem. Bath time took a very long time. Her neck was long, her legs were long, and her tail liked to swish. She always forgot steps. Sometimes she forgot to brush behind her ears. Sometimes she forgot to wash her knees. Sometimes the towel ended up in a wet pile on the floor.

“I want to do better,” Gemma said softly. “I want to learn the steps. I want our bathroom to be neat and happy.”

Gemma’s friend, Pip the little bird, chirped from the window. “Tweet! What is wrong, Gemma?”

Gemma sighed. “Bath time is messy and slow. I forget things. I make puddles. Mama gets tired. I want to help. I want to get good at this.”

Pip tilted his head. “Let’s build a plan! We can make a routine. A routine is a set of steps. Step, step, step! You can learn and improve.”

Gemma’s ears perked up. “A routine! Yes! What are the steps?”

They looked around. The faucet dripped. Plink. Plink. The soft towels smelled like clean cotton. The toothbrush stood like a tiny soldier by the sink.

Pip fluttered to a sticky note pad. “Let’s write the steps,” he chirped. He dipped a tiny quill in purple ink and wrote big, simple words:

1. Hang towel on hook.
2. Turn on warm water.
3. Wash from top to bottom.
4. Rinse, shake, and pat dry.
5. Brush teeth in little circles.
6. Put things away and wipe.

Gemma read the list slowly. “I can do that. Top to bottom. Little circles. Put things away.” She took a deep breath. “I will practice.”

She hung her towel on the hook. “Step one!” she said. The cotton felt fluffy. She turned the knob. Warm water hummed and made sweet steam. “Step two!”

Gemma poured a small drop of soap into her hoof. “Just a little,” she said. “I can be careful.” She washed her head first. “Top!” The bubbles were light like clouds. They tickled her ears. She giggled. She washed her long neck. Swish, swish. Then her front legs and back legs. “Bottom!” The bubbles smelled lemony and bright.

“Good job!” Pip cheered. “Keep going!”

Gemma rinsed. The water ran smooth like a soft ribbon. She shook a tiny shake—just a tiny one—so the water didn’t splash everywhere. “Shake, but not too much,” she whispered with a smile. She patted dry with the towel. Pat, pat, pat. “Step four!”

She stood by the sink. “Step five. Little circles.” She squeezed a pea-sized dot of toothpaste. Minty smell! She brushed gently. “Top teeth. Bottom teeth. Front. Back.” Her mouth felt cool and tingly.

“Step six,” Pip sang. “Put things away and wipe!”

Gemma put the soap back. She set the toothbrush in its cup. She folded her towel and hung it neatly. Then she used a small cloth to wipe the little drips on the counter. Wipe, wipe, wipe. The counter shone.

Gemma looked around. The bathroom was calm. No puddles. No mess. She felt proud and warm inside. “I did it,” she said. “I followed the routine.”

Mama Giraffe peeked in. “Oh my, Gemma! The bathroom looks lovely. You did your steps. You improved so much.”

Gemma’s cheeks felt happy-hot. “Thank you, Mama. Pip helped me make a plan.”

Pip puffed his chest. “Tweet! Teamwork!”

The next morning, Gemma tried again. She read the steps. She moved slowly and kindly. Hang towel. Warm water. Top to bottom. Rinse and pat. Little circles. Put things away and wipe. Each day, it got easier and faster. Her hands remembered. Her body remembered. This was skill-building. Practice made her stronger and calmer.

One afternoon, Gemma heard a tiny whimper. It was Baby Ellie, a small elephant from next door. She stood by the doorway with big eyes. “Bath time is hard,” Ellie sniffled. “I make big splashes. I forget what to do.”

Gemma bent her long neck down kindly. “I used to feel like that,” she said. “Would you like help? I can volunteer to show you my routine.”

Ellie nodded. “Yes, please.”

Gemma shared the purple note. She said each step softly. “We can do it together,” she promised.

They hung towels on hooks. They turned on warm water. Ellie washed from top to bottom with tiny swirls. Swish, swish. Gemma reminded, “Little shake, little pat.” Ellie giggled at the minty toothpaste. “Tingly!”

When they finished, Ellie’s eyes sparkled. “I did it!” she trumpeted. “I can learn!”

“Yes,” Gemma said. “We can learn new skills. We can improve with practice. A routine helps our bodies remember.”

Ellie smiled. “Thank you for helping.”

Gemma felt proud. Helping felt good. Learning felt good. The bathroom felt calm and bright.

That night, Gemma tucked the purple note under a magnet by the mirror. She touched it gently. “This is our plan,” she said. “Step by step, I can grow.”

The End.
