# The Fawn Who Listened to the World

In a bright musical practice room, a gentle deer fawn named Daya stood on soft carpet. The room smelled like pine wood and lemon polish. Sunlight made golden squares on the floor. Shiny drums, tiny bells, and a big piano filled the room. Colorful flags hung on the wall, each with a different pattern. Daya’s ears twitched with every sound.

“Tap, tap,” went the sticks on a drum. “Ding-ding,” sang the triangle. “La-la,” hummed the piano. Daya loved music. She loved learning. Today was Sharing Songs Day.

Friends came in. A panda named Pema carried a small gong. A fox named Farid held a little oud with many strings. A penguin named Pia waddled with a drum from her icy home. A rabbit named Ritu brought ankle bells. Daya’s heart thumped with happy hops.

“Welcome!” said Teacher Noor, a kind owl. “Today, we share songs from our homes. We listen. We learn. We show care.”

Daya nodded. She wanted to lead the first song. She had practiced a bouncy tune on the piano all week.

First, Pema tapped her gong. “Bong… bong…” It sounded like a deep hello. The room hummed. Daya felt the sound in her chest.

“Where is your home, Pema?” Daya asked.

“My home is in the mountains,” Pema said softly. “We play the gong to begin and to give thanks.”

“Thank you,” Daya said. She smiled and clapped.

Next, Farid plucked the oud. “Plink-plunk, pliiiing.” The notes curved like warm wind. “This is a song my grandma hums,” he said. “We sing it at sunset.”

“Thank you,” said Daya, swishing her tiny tail.

Pia tapped her drum. “Tup-tup-ti-tup.” It sounded like soft snow footsteps. “We drum to dance and to keep warm,” Pia said. Her eyes shone.

Ritu shook her ankle bells. “Chim-chim-chim!” She did a little step. “My family dances for festivals. We share sweets.”

Daya’s hooves tingled. The sounds were new and bright. But she wanted to play her piano song now. She hurried to the bench.

“My turn!” Daya said. Her voice was loud and quick. Her nose twitched. She lifted her hooves to the keys.

“Wait,” said Teacher Noor gently. “We will listen to everyone. Listen first. Then we share.”

Daya’s ears drooped. She did not want to wait. She pressed the keys anyway. “La-la-la!” The piano rang.

Pema blinked. Farid stopped plucking. Pia hugged her drum. Ritu held her ankle bells still. The room felt tight and small.

Teacher Noor raised a soft wing. “Pause, Daya,” she said. “We need fair turns. That is justice. Justice means fair and kind.”

Daya’s cheeks felt hot. Her heart beat fast. “I’m sorry,” she whispered. “I didn’t wait. I didn’t listen.” She slid off the bench. Her tail lowered.

Pema nodded kindly. “We can try again,” Pema said. “We can start with my gong.”

Daya took a deep breath. She tried again to listen. She watched Pema lift the mallet, so careful and slow. “Bong… bong…” The sound felt like a hug. Then Farid’s oud flowed, Pia’s drum tapped, and Ritu’s bells chimed.

Daya listened to the music and the stories too. She learned that some songs say thank you. Some songs say hello to the sun. Some songs help you feel warm or dance together. Daya thought, The world is big. People do things in many ways. My way is one way. Their ways are good too.

When it was Daya’s turn, her hooves shook. “Um,” she said. “My song is bouncy. I play it when I feel brave.”

Daya looked at her friends. “May I begin with a soft note, like Pema? Then I will do a curvy part, like Farid. A steady beat, like Pia. And a tiny dance, like Ritu.” She smiled shyly. “I want to share and also include you. Is that okay?”

Pema, Farid, Pia, and Ritu smiled. “Yes,” they said together.

Teacher Noor’s eyes twinkled. “Daya, that is leadership,” she said. “Leadership listens. It brings ideas together.”

Daya touched one key. “Laaa…” It was soft like a first step. Then she played a curvy “pliiiing” part. Pia nodded and tapped a quiet beat. “Tup-tup.” Ritu shook her bells. “Chim-chim!”

The room filled with sounds from many homes. It smelled like lemon polish and joy. The golden sun squares seemed to dance.

When the song ended, everyone clapped. Daya’s ears perked up. Her heart was light.

“I am sorry I did not wait before,” Daya said. “I will make amends.” She turned to Pema. “Would you like to start the next song?” She turned to the others. “And we can make a plan so it is fair.”

They made a plan together. They drew a circle chart with names. Each friend would have a turn to start a song. Each friend would say a little story from home. Daya wrote with a blue crayon. The paper felt smooth, and the crayon made a soft “scritch.”

At the end, Teacher Noor asked, “What did we learn today?”

Daya lifted her chin. “We learned to listen to friends from all over the world,” she said. “The world is big, and we can share. Justice means fair turns. Making amends means saying sorry and fixing our plan. And leadership means helping everyone’s song be heard.”

Everyone smiled. The flags on the wall seemed to smile too.

Daya pressed one last note. “La.” It sounded like a promise.

The End.
