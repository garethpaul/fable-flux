# Ollie the Otter and the Wiggly Wrench

Ollie the otter loved to play. He had soft whiskers, shiny brown fur, and a giggle like bubbling water. Today, Ollie visited Grandpa Gus’s garage workshop. The workshop smelled like pine wood and orange soap. Sunlight made bright squares on the floor. Tools hung on the wall like shiny fish: hammers, wrenches, and big blue clamps.

Grandpa Gus wore a cozy cap and a kind smile. “Hello, Ollie,” he said in a warm, low voice. “Want to help me build a little cart?”

Ollie clapped his paws. “Yes! I want to zoom!”

Grandpa Gus nodded. “We will zoom later. First, we follow directions.” He pointed to a paper on the table. The paper showed pictures with numbers. “Step one. Step two. Step three,” Grandpa said, tapping each picture.

Ollie wiggled. He liked to go fast. “Can I just do it quick?” he asked.

Grandpa Gus chuckled. “We will go quick by going slow. Slow and steady keeps us ready.”

They opened a box. Inside were smooth wood pieces, small wheels that smelled like new rubber, and a wiggly silver wrench. The wrench felt cool in Ollie’s paw. “It’s like a shiny fish!” Ollie giggled.

“Step one,” Grandpa read. “Sand the wood. Smooth, smooth, smooth.”

Ollie pressed the sandpaper on the wood. Shhhh, shhhh, shhhh. Dust danced in the sun. The wood felt rough, then softer. “It tickles my paws,” Ollie said.

“Nice work,” Grandpa said. “Step two: attach the front wheel bar.”

Ollie wanted to skip to the wheels. He grabbed a wheel first. It slipped and rolled away. “Come back, wheel!” he cried, chasing it under the workbench. He bumped his nose. “Ow.”

Grandpa knelt beside him. “Are you okay, Ollie?”

Ollie rubbed his nose. His eyes felt hot. His chest felt tight like a knot. “I want it done now,” he said. “This is hard.”

Grandpa nodded slowly. “I hear you. It feels hard. Let’s take a slow breath.” He put a gentle hand on his chest. “In like smelling a flower.” He sniffed in. “Out like blowing a bubble.” He blew out. “Want to try?”

Ollie copied. In…flower. Out…bubble. His chest felt softer. The knot got smaller.

“Good,” said Grandpa. “Now, let’s look at the directions again. We can make a plan. Would you like to hold the wrench, or read the steps with me?”

Ollie thought. “I will hold the wrench,” he said.

“Great choice,” Grandpa said. “We are a team.”

They followed step two together. Grandpa held the bar. Ollie turned the wrench. Clink, clink. The metal made little music. The bar sat snug. “It fits!” Ollie cheered.

“Step three,” Grandpa read. “Add the wheels.”

Ollie’s whiskers twitched with joy. He picked up a wheel, then paused. “First the little bolt, right?” he asked, looking at the picture.

Grandpa’s eyes twinkled. “Yes. You are following directions.”

Ollie slid on the bolt. He turned the wrench. The wheel spun and clicked. “It goes round and round,” he laughed.

They worked step by step. Sometimes a bolt rolled away. Sometimes a nut felt stuck. When it was hard, Ollie felt the hot feeling again. Grandpa would whisper, “Flower…bubble,” and they would breathe. The hot feeling cooled like water.

At last, the cart stood ready. It was small and smooth, with a bright red rope in front. Ollie stroked the wood. It felt silky. He sniffed. It smelled like sunshine and trees. “We did it!” he squeaked.

Grandpa wiped his hands. “We did it by following directions,” he said. “And by using calm breaths.”

Ollie grinned. “And by being a team.”

Grandpa nodded. “Now let’s test it. Safety first.” He pointed to a helmet. “Helmet on, paws on, slow start.”

Ollie wanted to run and jump in. He looked at the helmet and murmured, “Slow and steady keeps us ready.” He put the helmet on. Snap.

They rolled the cart to the driveway. The wheels whispered on the ground. Shooo. Ollie climbed in. Grandpa held the rope. “Ready?”

“Ready!” Ollie chirped.

“Down the gentle bump we go,” Grandpa said, tugging slowly. The cart rolled. Wind kissed Ollie’s whiskers. His giggle bubbled up. “Wheee!”

At the bottom, Grandpa stopped the cart with a soft pull. “How was the ride?”

“Super!” Ollie said. He patted the cart. “I’m glad we did all the steps. The steps kept me safe. The steps made it work.”

Grandpa smiled. “When we follow directions, we save time. When we get upset, we breathe. When we want something different, we can talk and make a plan.”

Ollie nodded. “Next time, can I choose the paint color?” he asked.

“That’s a good idea,” Grandpa said. “We can make a list and decide together.”

Ollie bounced. “I choose blue.” He paused. “Maybe stripes too? What do you think?”

Grandpa rubbed his chin. “How about blue with two red stripes? We both win.”

Ollie clapped. “Deal!”

They shook paws. The garage smelled like orange soap again as they cleaned up. Ollie put the wrench back on its hook. The metal felt cool and happy. He whispered, “Thank you, wiggly wrench.”

On the ride home, Ollie thought about the day. “Follow the steps. Breathe when it’s hard. Talk and make a plan,” he said softly, tapping his paw in time.

Grandpa smiled. “That’s the way, little otter.”

The End.
