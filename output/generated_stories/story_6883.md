# Ellie Helps In the Guest Room

Ellie was a gentle elephant calf with soft gray ears and big kind eyes. Her feet made quiet thumps on the rug as she walked. Today, the house smelled like lemon soap and warm towels. Auntie Mina was coming to visit, and Mama said, “Ellie, Auntie will sleep in the guest room.”

Ellie peeked into the guest room. The curtains were sunny yellow, and the bed had a fluffy blue blanket. A small lamp glowed like a cozy star. Ellie loved the soft pillows. She pressed her trunk into one. “Squish!” she giggled.

Mama smiled and said, “We have a job to do. It is our home. We care for it together.”

“What does that mean?” Ellie asked, her trunk curling in a question.

“It means civic responsibility,” Mama said gently. “We keep our space clean and safe. We think about other people. We help our community. Our home is part of our little community.”

Ellie nodded. “I can help!”

Mama gave Ellie a small feather duster. It was tickly and light. “Dust the table, please,” Mama said.

Ellie swished the duster. Swish, swish! Dust sparkled in the sun like tiny stars. “I like helping,” Ellie sang.

Mama folded towels. “Thank you, helper,” she said. “Helping shows respect.”

Ellie puffed with pride. She set a glass of water on the bedside table. The glass felt cool on her trunk. She arranged a little bowl of fruit. The oranges smelled sweet and bright.

Then Ellie saw a shiny button on the lamp. “Click?” she whispered. She touched it with her trunk. The light flicked off. The room got soft and dim. “Click!” She turned it on again. On, off, on, off—flicker, flicker!

“Ellie,” Mama said, “please be gentle with the lamp. Guests need light at night.”

“Sorry,” Ellie said. She stroked the lamp. “I will be careful.”

She wanted to do more. Ellie reached for the blue blanket to smooth it. But her big foot stepped on the corner. Rip! A tiny stitch popped. Ellie froze. The sound was small but sharp, like a little tear in paper.

“Oh no,” she whispered. Her ears drooped. She hugged her trunk close. “Mama,” Ellie said, “I made a mistake.”

Mama came close and looked. “It is a small tear,” she said calmly. “Thank you for telling me.”

Ellie’s eyes felt wet. “I wanted to help,” she said. “Now I made it worse.”

Mama patted Ellie’s shoulder. Her touch was warm. “Helping means we try our best. Sometimes we make mistakes. Civic responsibility also means we fix what we can and say sorry.”

Ellie sniffled. “Can I fix it?”

“Yes,” Mama said. She brought out a tiny sewing kit. The thread was soft and blue. “We will fix it together.”

They sat by the window. Birds chirped outside. Ellie held the blanket steady. Mama sewed with small, careful pokes. Poke, pull. Poke, pull. Ellie watched the neat stitches. “It’s like little steps,” she said.

“Exactly,” Mama smiled. “Small steps make big help.”

When they finished, Ellie touched the mended spot. It felt smooth and safe. “Thank you, Mama,” she said.

“You helped,” Mama replied. “That is responsibility.”

Ellie looked around. She saw a basket of toys by the door. “These are my blocks,” she said. “They do not live in the guest room.”

“You noticed,” Mama said. “What should we do?”

Ellie nodded. “I will put them back.” She picked up each block. Tap, tap, tap, they clicked into the box. Her steps made soft thumps as she carried them away. The room looked calm again.

Later, Auntie Mina arrived. Her coat was fuzzy, and she smelled like fresh rain. “Hello, Ellie!” she said. “What a lovely room.”

Ellie’s heart felt warm. “I helped,” she said. “I dusted. I put water. I fixed a mistake. I said sorry.”

Auntie Mina knelt and hugged her. The hug was snug and kind. “Thank you, Ellie. I feel welcome. You showed respect to me and to your home.”

Ellie felt proud. “We care for our space,” she said. “That is… civic… res-pon-si-bil-i-ty!” She said the big word slowly. It felt like a big, strong word.

That night, after dinner, Ellie tiptoed to the guest room. Auntie Mina was reading. The lamp made a golden circle of light. The blue blanket looked smooth. The fruit bowl smelled sweet. Everything was peaceful.

“Good night, Auntie,” Ellie whispered.

“Good night, Ellie,” Auntie said. “I am grateful for you.”

Ellie smiled. “I am grateful for you too.”

She turned to go, then looked back. “Auntie,” she said, “if you need anything, please tell me. I can help.”

Auntie Mina nodded. “That is very responsible.”

Ellie walked to her room with happy thumps. Her heart felt full, like a warm cookie. She knew helping was not just for today. Helping was for every day—at home, at school, and in the neighborhood. Helping meant noticing, caring, fixing, and saying sorry when needed. Helping made people feel safe and loved.

Ellie curled up on her pillow. “I can help,” she whispered to the dark. “I can be responsible.” She closed her eyes, smiling.

The End.
