# Miko Mouse and the Quiet Click

Miko was a kind little mouse who lived in a cozy home office. The room smelled like warm paper and lemony cleaner. The desk hummed softly, and the computer made gentle clicks. Sunlight painted golden squares on the floor.

Every morning, Miko tidied the pencils in a tiny cup and dusted the keyboard with a fluffy feather. “Good morning, desk,” Miko said. “Good morning, chair.” The chair creaked back, as if it said hello too.

Today was a special day. Miko wanted to share a family tradition with his friends from faraway places. He had a small basket of crunchy rice cakes made from Grandma Mouse’s recipe. He also had colorful stamps shaped like tiny flags.

“I will invite my friends to a little office party,” Miko said. “We can share stories from our homes.”

Miko sent messages on sticky notes. He wrote simple words in big, friendly letters. “Party at the desk. Snacks. Stories. Please come.” He left one note by the bookshelf, one under the lamp, and one near the window.

Soon, there was a tap-tap at the door. It was Bibi Beetle with shiny green wings. “Hello, Miko!” Bibi buzzed. “I brought a sand rattle from my desert home.” She shook it gently. Shhh-shhh. It sounded like a tiny wave.

Next came Pasha Pigeon, soft and gray. “Coo! I brought a picture of my city. So many rooftops!” He set the picture on the mouse pad.

Finally, Zuzu Squirrel hopped in with a fluffy tail. “Hi! I brought a blue ribbon from my grandma. We tie it when we sing.”

Miko clapped. “Welcome, friends! Let’s share our traditions. We will listen to each other. We will use calm words and kind ears.”

They all sat in a circle on a soft, round mousepad. Miko passed the rice cakes. Crunch, crunch. The sound was happy.

Bibi spoke first. “In my family, we shake the sand rattle and tell a thank-you story. We say what we are thankful for.” She shook the rattle. Shhh-shhh. “I am thankful for friends.”

Pasha nodded. “In my city, we watch the moon on the roofs and coo a lullaby,” he said. He hummed, “Coo-coo, coo-coo,” soft as a cloud.

Zuzu tied the blue ribbon in a bow. “We tie a wish. We whisper it so it grows calm and strong,” she said. She whispered, “I wish to learn.”

Miko held up a tiny family photo. “We share rice cakes and say, ‘We listen, we learn, we love.’” He smiled. “Now, let’s take turns and talk.”

But just then, the computer went click-click-click, faster and louder. The screen flashed. Beep! Beep! The sound made Bibi’s wings shiver. Pasha fluffed his feathers. Zuzu’s ribbon slipped.

“Oh no,” said Miko, ears twitching. “The office is noisy. I can’t hear my friends.”

Bibi buzzed, “I don’t like the beeps.”

Pasha cooed, “I feel worried.”

Zuzu whispered, “I feel too wiggly.”

Miko put a tiny paw on his heart. “Let’s be calm. Let’s say how we feel. Then we can find a way.” He took a slow, deep breath in and out. “I feel surprised by the loud beeps,” he said.

“I feel shaky,” said Bibi.

“I feel tight,” said Pasha.

“I feel fussy,” said Zuzu.

“Thank you for telling me,” said Miko. “Now I understand.” He looked around the home office. He thought of a plan. “We can use calm voices. We can make a quiet sign for the computer. We can take turns.”

Miko drew a picture of a little ear with a smile. Under it he wrote, “Listening Time.” He taped the sign near the screen. Then he clicked one small button. The computer lights dimmed. The hum turned soft again.

“Better?” Miko asked.

Bibi listened. “Shhh,” she said. “Yes.”

Pasha smoothed his feathers. “Much better.”

Zuzu tied the ribbon tight. “Ready.”

“Thank you for telling me and for waiting,” said Miko. “Now we can share again. Bibi, would you like to go first?”

Bibi shook the sand rattle. Shhh-shhh. “In my home, we say, ‘Slow words are strong words.’” She smiled. “I am thankful for quiet ears.”

Pasha showed his city picture. “In my home, we coo in turns. One coos, one listens. Then we trade.”

Zuzu held the blue ribbon. “In my home, we tie a bow and take a breath before talking.”

Miko passed the rice cakes again. Crunch, crunch. “In my home, we repeat back what we hear,” he said. “I heard Bibi say slow words help. I heard Pasha say take turns. I heard Zuzu say breathe first.”

Everyone nodded. They felt warm and safe.

At the end, Miko wrapped tiny rice cakes for each friend. He put a small stamp on each pack: a desert star for Bibi, a city moon for Pasha, and a forest leaf for Zuzu.

“Thank you for sharing your traditions,” Miko said. “We learned about places far away and feelings close by.”

Bibi buzzed happily. “Today I learned to say how I feel.”

Pasha cooed, “I learned to listen and repeat.”

Zuzu grinned, “I learned to breathe and wait.”

Miko’s whiskers twitched with joy. “We used calm words. We took turns. We listened with our whole selves. That is good communication.”

The friends hugged. The computer hummed softly again. The home office smelled like warm paper and sweet rice cakes.

The lesson was clear: When we share our feelings and listen to others, we can solve problems, learn traditions, and feel close—no matter where we come from.

The End.
