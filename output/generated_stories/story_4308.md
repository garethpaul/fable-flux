# Roo's Big Musical Fix

Roo was a joyful kangaroo joey with bright eyes and springy feet. One sunny afternoon, Roo hopped into the musical practice room. The room smelled like fresh wood and lemon clean. There were shiny drums, a sleepy piano, soft tambourines, and a big, brave tuba that gleamed like a golden sun.

Roo wiggled his ears. “Hello, music friends!” he said. His voice echoed softly. Echo, echo, echo. Roo giggled. He loved the gentle whoosh of the air conditioner and the warm, fuzzy rug under his toes.

Today was band practice. But when Roo looked around, he gasped. The drumsticks were sticky. The piano keys were dusty. The music sheets were crumpled like sleepy leaves. The big tuba had a little dent and a tiny tear in its case.

Roo’s heart made a bump-bump of worry. “Oh no,” Roo whispered. “We can’t play like this.”

His friend, Tia the turtle, peeked from behind the cymbals. “I feel shy,” Tia said. “I don’t want to touch the sticky sticks.”

Milo the mouse squeaked, “I found a broken string on the guitar.” He wrinkled his nose. “It smells like old juice.”

Roo took a deep breath. He put a paw on his chest. “We can fix it,” he said. “Let’s use our imaginations. We can help. We can be kind to the room. We can make it clean and happy.”

Tia blinked. “How?”

Roo smiled big. “Let’s pretend we are Gentle Sound Doctors!”

Milo squeaked, “Beep boop! Doctor hats on!” They all pretended to put on hats. Roo’s hat had blue stars. Tia’s had tiny bubbles. Milo’s hat had a little bell that went ding!

Roo found a soft cloth and a spray bottle with a green leaf on it. “This spray is Earth-friendly,” Roo said. “It helps the room and helps our planet.” He sniffed. It smelled like oranges. Sssspritz. “We can clean with care.”

Tia wheeled over a small bin. “This is for recycling,” she said. “Paper here. Plastic here.” She tapped gently: tap-tap.

Milo brought a basket. “This is for trash,” he said. “Banana peels go here!” Plop.

Roo put on gloves. “Gloves keep our paws clean,” he said. “That is good hygiene. Clean hands keep us healthy.” He showed Tia and Milo how to rub, rub, rub the sticky drumsticks with the soft cloth. The sticks went from tacky to smooth. “Feel,” Roo said.

Tia touched them. “Not sticky!” She smiled. “Thank you.”

“Teamwork!” Roo cheered. He hummed a happy tune. Hummm-hummm.

They gently wiped the piano keys. White, black, white, black. The cloth whisked: swish, swish. Roo listened. “The keys say thank you,” he whispered. He pressed one key. Ting! It sounded bright, like a tiny star.

Milo held the crumpled paper. “Recycling or trash?” he asked.

Roo looked. “This paper is clean. Recycling,” he said. “We can make new paper later. That helps the Earth.” Milo nodded and slid it into the bin. Shhh.

They looked at the tuba. It stood tall and quiet. The little dent made it look sad.

Roo patted it gently. “We see you, tuba,” he said. “We care.” He turned to his friends. “We can’t fix the dent today, but we can help the tuba feel ready.”

They cleaned the mouthpiece with warm, soapy water. Bubbles tickled Roo’s paws. Pop, pop, pop. “We use soap and water,” Roo said. “We rinse, then dry with a soft towel.” He showed them how. The mouthpiece sparkled.

Tia frowned at the torn case. “Poor case,” she said softly.

Roo nodded. “We can be kind to it, too.” He found a needle and thread in the art box. “We will sew a small patch.” He guided small stitches: in-out, in-out. Tia handed him thread, and Milo clipped the end with child-safe scissors. Snip. Roo whispered, “There you go, case.” The case looked stronger.

Milo squeaked, “What about the broken guitar string?”

Roo thought. He closed his eyes and used his imagination. He pictured the guitar smiling. He pictured a string like a silver river. “We can’t fix it all by ourselves,” Roo said. “But we can ask for help.” He felt brave. “Asking is helpful, too.”

They went to Ms. Lark, the music teacher. She had gentle eyes and a soft, flowery smell. “Ms. Lark,” Roo said, “we cleaned the room with Earth-friendly spray. We sorted recycling. We washed the mouthpiece. We sewed the case. But the guitar string needs you.”

Ms. Lark smiled. “You used your imaginations to solve problems. You were helpful and careful. I will fix the string.”

They all went back to the room. Ms. Lark tuned the guitar. Twang, twang, twang. It sang again. Roo’s heart felt warm and light.

“Ready to play?” Ms. Lark asked.

Roo nodded. Tia tapped the clean drumsticks. Tick-a-tick. Milo shook a shiny tambourine. Chika-chika. Roo pressed a piano key. Ting! The tuba rumbled low and happy. Brrrrrm.

They made a gentle song. It sounded like sunshine and soap bubbles. It smelled like oranges and clean wood. It felt like soft rugs and smiling friends.

When the song ended, Roo said, “Today we learned to imagine ways to help. We cleaned to stay healthy. We cared about our Earth. We asked for help. We were kind.”

Ms. Lark clapped softly. “That is the music of empathy,” she said. “You listened to each other, and you listened to the room.”

Roo hugged his friends. “Imaginations can fix big feelings and small problems,” he said. “We can do this every day.”

Everyone smiled. The practice room shimmered with happy sound. Roo’s feet felt bouncy again.

The End.
