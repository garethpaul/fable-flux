# Penny Penguin’s Bubbly Bath Share

Penny was a cheerful penguin with bright eyes and a happy waddle. She loved to sing, “Waddle, waddle, wiggle!” Today, Penny waddled into the bathroom with a big smile. It was bath time!

The bathroom was warm and steamy. The mirror was foggy, the floor was soft and squishy under a bath mat, and the air smelled like sweet oranges from her soap. “Mmm,” Penny said, taking a deep sniff. “Orange bubbles!”

Penny’s bath toys were waiting: a yellow duck, a blue boat, and a tiny silver cup for pouring. “Hello, friends!” she chirped. “Time for a clean, happy bath.”

She turned the water knob with her flippers—slow and careful. “Warm, not hot,” she said to herself. “I can do this.” The water rushed out, whoosh, and soon there were bubbly bubbles, fizzy and soft. Penny giggled as bubbles popped on her beak. Pop! Pop! Pop!

Just then, her little brother, Pip, peeked in. He was smaller, with a squeaky voice. “Penny,” Pip whispered, “may I please have some bubbles? I want a tiny wash.”

Penny looked at the tub. It was full, and it was her bath time. She felt a tug in her tummy. “But I want my bubbles,” she thought. She held her favorite soap. It was orange and shiny. Penny loved it. She hugged it close.

Pip’s eyes looked big and hopeful. He wiggled his little feet on the mat. “I want to be clean like you,” he said. “I want to do it by myself.”

Penny took a slow breath. She remembered Mama’s words: “We take care of ourselves, and we care for others too. Sharing is generous. It makes hearts feel big.”

Penny opened her flippers and smiled. “Okay, Pip,” she said gently. “Let’s share the bubbles. You can have a little cup of warm water and some soap. I will help you get started, and then you can try by yourself.”

Pip squeaked, “Thank you, Penny!” His smile was as bright as the bathroom light.

Penny turned the knob a little for Pip’s basin. She checked with one flipper. “Warm, not hot,” she said. “Safety first.” She squeezed a tiny bit of soap into the silver cup. Plip! Plop! The soap smelled like sweet oranges. The bubbles grew, soft and shiny.

Pip dipped his flippers in. “Oooh,” he said. “It’s warm and tickly.”

Penny showed him, step by step. “First, wet your flippers. Then rub, rub, rub with soap. Sing with me!” Penny sang, “Rub-a-dub-dub, scrub-scrub-scrub!” Pip sang too in his squeaky voice, and they both giggled.

“Now rinse,” Penny said, pouring a gentle cup of water. “All clean! You can try on your tummy and toes.” Pip nodded, taking the silver cup himself. He poured slowly and carefully. Drip, drip, drip. He smiled. “I can do it!”

Penny felt warm and proud inside. “You are doing great, Pip,” she cheered. She climbed into her bath and scooped some bubbles, making a bubble hat. “Ta-da!” She wiggled her head. Pip laughed. “Bubble hat! Bubble penguin!”

Pip made a little bubble beard. “Hello,” he said in a deep, pretend voice. “I am Mr. Bubble.”

They laughed and played, but they also kept cleaning. Penny washed her wings and her neck. “I take care of my body,” she said. “I am learning to do it by myself.” She used a washcloth to scrub, scrub, scrub. The washcloth felt soft and squishy. The water made a gentle splish-splash sound.

Pip watched and copied. He cleaned his flippers, his tummy, and his toes. “I am independent,” he said slowly. “In-de-pen-dent.” Penny clapped softly. “Yes, Pip! Independent means you try to do things by yourself. And generous means you share and care.”

Pip nodded. “You shared your bubbles. That is generous.”

Penny smiled. “And you tried by yourself. That is independent. We also practiced self-care by getting clean.”

When they were done, Penny turned the knob off, twist, twist. The water was quiet. They listened to the tiny drip, drip. The mirror was foggy like a cloud. Penny drew a little heart on the fog. Pip drew a small heart next to it. Two hearts, side by side.

“Time to dry,” Penny said. She gave Pip a fluffy towel. It felt like a soft hug. “Pat, pat, pat,” she said. “Not rub hard.” Pip patted his feathers and puffed up proudly. “I did it!”

Penny hung the towel on the hook. She put the soap back in its dish and lined up the toys: duck, boat, cup. “Clean bathroom, clean bodies,” she said. It felt nice to care for their space.

Pip hugged Penny. “Thank you for sharing.”

Penny hugged back. Her heart felt big and light. “Sharing made both of us happy. I can be generous and still take care of me.”

They waddled out, warm and cozy, with orange-bubble smiles.

Lesson: Being generous means sharing and helping others. Self-care means keeping our bodies clean and safe. Independence means trying to do things by ourselves. When we share kindly, we all feel good.

The End.
