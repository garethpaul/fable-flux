# Polly Paints an "I'm Sorry"

Polly was a creative parrot with bright green feathers and a sunny yellow beak. She loved colors, songs, and making little art. Her favorite place was the closet hideaway. It was a cozy space behind a curtain, soft and quiet, with a little lamp that glowed like a firefly. Inside, there were pillows, a basket of crayons, and a box of stickers that smelled like strawberries.

One rainy day, Polly ruffled her feathers. Drip, drip, drip went the rain. Tap, tap, tap went her beak on the floor. Polly wanted to make a new drawing. She wanted it to be perfect. She wanted it to be big and beautiful. She wanted it right now.

She picked up a blue crayon. “Squawk! Blue sky!” she said.

She picked up a yellow crayon. “Squawk! Sun!”

She picked up a red crayon. “Squawk! Big red heart!”

But the paper was small. The crayons kept rolling. The heart looked wiggly, not smooth. Polly felt a hot, tight feeling in her tummy. Her wings felt stiff. Her eyes felt watery.

“I can’t do it!” Polly squawked. “It’s all wrong!”

In a frustrated flutter, Polly flapped her wings and the crayons flew. One crayon bumped a box. The sticker box popped open. Stickers sprinkled everywhere like shiny confetti. A glittery star stuck to Polly’s beak. “Mmph!” she squeaked.

Just then, Mia, Polly’s friend, peeked into the closet. “Polly? Are you okay?” Mia asked. She wrinkled her nose. “Oh no, the stickers!”

Polly looked around. The cozy closet now looked messy. Crayons on the floor. Stickers on the curtain. A rainbow owl sticker stuck to Mia’s sock.

Polly’s tummy got tighter. She did not mean to make a mess. She felt the hot feeling again. Her wings sagged.

Mia sat on a pillow. “Sometimes I feel mad too,” she said softly. “When my blocks fall. When my shoe won’t go on.”

Polly took a deep breath. In, through her beak. Out, slow and long. She tried again. In. Out. She stroked her feathers to calm herself. The glow of the little lamp felt warm. The soft pillow felt squishy under her toes. The strawberry smell of the stickers was sweet.

“I was frustrated,” Polly said in a small voice. “I wanted my drawing to be perfect. I flapped too fast. I made a mess.”

Mia nodded. “Frustration is a big feeling,” she said. “We can help it. We can breathe. We can ask for help. We can try again.”

Polly blinked. “I forgot to use my calm,” she said. She placed her wing on her chest. “I can fix one thing first.”

Polly looked at Mia. Her heart felt wiggly again, but in a different way. A brave way. She knew the right thing to do.

“I’m sorry, Mia,” Polly said. “I made a mess in our closet hideaway. I scared you and dropped the stickers. I did not mean to, but I did. I will help clean.”

Mia smiled and patted Polly’s wing. “Thank you for your apology,” Mia said. “That helps my feelings. Let’s clean together.”

They picked up crayons, one by one. “Blue,” said Polly. “Yellow,” said Mia. “Red!” they said together, and giggled.

They peeled stickers gently off the curtain. “Sticky, sticky,” Polly chirped. “Peel, peel,” Mia sang. They put the stickers back in the box. They made a little sticker bed inside with a napkin so the stickers would stay cozy.

When the closet hideaway looked neat again, Polly felt light. Her tummy was soft. Her wings were calm. She looked at her small paper.

“I still want a big drawing,” Polly said. “But the paper is small.”

Mia tapped her chin. “What if we make many little hearts?” she asked. “Lots of small can make one big.”

Polly’s eyes brightened. “Yes!” she chirped. “Small hearts, big love!”

They drew tiny hearts in red, pink, and purple. The crayons felt smooth and warm in their hands. The paper made gentle scratch-scratch sounds. Polly hummed a soft tune. Mia swayed side to side. The closet smelled like strawberries and crayons.

Polly stuck a glitter star at the top. She wrote two big words at the bottom in careful letters: “I’m Sorry.”

She held up the picture. The little hearts made one giant heart. It was beautiful.

Polly looked at Mia. “I’m sorry for my flappy mess,” she said again. “Thank you for staying.”

Mia hugged Polly. “I accept your apology,” she said. “You said what you did, you said you were sorry, and you helped fix it. That’s a good apology.”

Polly puffed up with pride. “When I feel frustrated,” she said, “I can breathe, say how I feel, ask for help, and try again.”

Mia nodded. “And we can use our creativity to solve problems.”

Polly hung the “I’m Sorry” art on the closet wall. The lamp light made it glow like a warm sunset. The hideaway felt cozy again.

Polly chirped a happy chirp. “Squawk! Apology art!” she sang.

They snuggled on the pillows, sipping warm cocoa in tiny cups, listening to the rain go pitter-patter. The closet hideaway was tidy, kind, and full of ideas.

Polly smiled. “Saying sorry helps hearts feel better,” she whispered.

“Me too,” Mia whispered back.

The End.
