# The Fair Rules in the Garden Shed

Gema was a thoughtful giraffe with long, gentle eyelashes and a warm, soft voice. One sunny morning, she bent her tall neck and peeked into the garden shed. The shed smelled like fresh soil and lemon soap. Sunlight made dust sparkle like tiny stars. Inside, there was a wooden table, a squeaky stool, and a tin box full of crayons and paper.

“I love to write,” Gema whispered, smiling. She took out a smooth blue crayon and a big piece of paper. “I will write a Fair Rules Poster.”

Tap-tap went the rain later on the roof. Creak-creak went the door. Gema hummed as she wrote in big friendly letters: “We share. We ask first. We take turns. We listen. We tell the truth.”

Just then, her friends arrived. Pip the puppy bounced in with muddy paws. “Play time!” he barked. Bella the bunny hopped, nose twitching. “I want the blue crayon!” Tito the turtle slowly scooted, blinking. “May I sit by you?” he asked.

Gema smiled. “Yes, Tito. Please sit here.” She placed a soft cloth for Tito, kept the blue crayon in her hoof, and said to Bella, “I am using the blue crayon now. You can have it next.”

Bella frowned. “But I want it now.” She reached for it.

Gema gently pulled the crayon close. “Please stop,” she said calmly. “This is my writing space right now. I will share after I write this line.” She pointed to her paper. “It says, ‘We ask first.’”

Pip wagged and grabbed the tin box with his teeth. “Mine!” he yipped. Crayons spilled and rolled across the floor, click-clack. One fell into a puddle by the door.

Gema’s heart felt tight. “Friends,” she said softly, “something is not fair.”

“Not fair?” Pip asked, tilting his head.

“Justice means fair,” Gema explained in simple words. “Fair means we take turns. We ask. We listen. We do not grab. We keep hands, paws, and hooves to our own space. We use gentle words.”

Bella’s ears drooped. “I didn’t ask.”

Pip shuffled. “I grabbed.”

Tito nodded. “I tried to ask.”

Gema took a deep breath. She loved her friends. She loved writing. She loved fairness. “Let’s make a plan,” she said. “A fair plan.”

“What is the plan?” Tito asked.

Gema tapped the paper with the blue crayon. “We will write the plan together. We can all help. We will use our voices and wait our turn. Listen to the sounds.” She pointed to the falling rain. “Tap-tap, then stop. Tap-tap, then stop. We can take turns like the rain.”

Bella smiled a little. “Tap-tap, then stop. My turn after yours.”

“Good,” said Gema. “First, I will finish this sentence. Then I will pass the blue crayon to Bella. Pip, you may pick three crayons from the floor and put them back in the box. Tito, please set the timer.” She put a tiny egg timer on the table. It ticked softly: tick-tick-tick.

Pip nodded. “I can help clean.” He gently picked up a red, a green, and a yellow crayon. He shook off the water drop and wiped with a cloth. “All clean,” he said proudly.

Tito twisted the timer. “Two minutes,” he said. “Tick-tick-tick.”

Gema finished her sentence and handed the blue crayon to Bella. “Thank you for waiting.”

Bella held the crayon, but she paused. “Gema, may I write with the blue crayon now?”

“Yes,” Gema said with a smile. “Thank you for asking.”

Bella wrote carefully: “We ask first.” Her paw moved slowly. The crayon made a soft shh-shh sound. “It feels nice,” she whispered.

When the timer rang, ding, Bella passed the crayon to Tito. “Your turn.”

Tito drew a little clock next to the words. “This shows we take turns,” he said.

Pip sat beside Gema. “What can I do now?”

Gema looked at the table. “You can draw a fence on the edge of the paper. The fence means personal space. It says, ‘Please do not step into someone’s space without asking.’”

Pip drew little posts: tap, tap, tap. “These are friendly fences,” he said. “We can open a gate when we ask.”

Gema clapped her hooves softly. “Yes! Friendly fences.”

Soon, the poster was full of simple words and bright drawings: big ears for listening, a hand raised for asking, a heart for telling the truth, and a little gate for personal space. The shed felt warm and calm. The air smelled like crayons and rain.

“Now,” said Gema, “we must use our plan.”

Bella looked at Gema’s writing spot. “May I sit next to you?”

“Yes,” Gema said, sliding over. “Thank you for asking.”

Pip nudged the box toward Tito. “Would you like the green?”

“Yes, please,” said Tito.

They all smiled. It felt good to be fair. It felt safe. It felt kind.

Gema taped the poster to the shed wall. “This is our Justice Poster,” she said. “Justice means fairness for everyone. We listen. We ask. We take turns. We keep our own space. We tell the truth.”

Pip said, “We made it together.”

Bella said, “We waited and shared.”

Tito said, “We decided with our voices.”

Gema’s heart felt light. “We used justice,” she said. “Justice helps us all.”

They cleaned the table, put away the crayons, and opened the door. Fresh air and soft rain greeted them. The shed no longer felt crowded. It felt happy.

Gema looked at her friends. “When we choose fairness,” she said, “everyone can shine.”

They all nodded and giggled, and the rain sang tap-tap, then stop, like turns on a timer.

The End.
