# Joey Hops to Calm

Joey was a joyful kangaroo joey with bright eyes and a soft, bouncy tail. He loved helping in the laundry room. The room smelled like warm soap and sunshine. The washing machine hummed, “Whirr, whirr,” and the dryer sang, “Thump, thump.” Socks hung like little flags on a string.

“Today I help!” Joey said, clapping his small paws. “Helping keeps our home clean. That’s good for everyone!”

Mama Roo smiled. “Yes, Joey. We wash only a full load to save water and power. That’s called being kind to our Earth.”

Joey puffed up with pride. “Kind to Earth! I can do that.”

Mama Roo placed a basket of clean clothes on the floor. “Please sort the socks into pairs,” she said. “White with white, blue with blue.”

Joey hopped close. The basket smelled warm and fresh. He liked the soft cotton on his paws. “Easy peasy!” he chirped.

But when Joey peeked inside, his smile fell. The socks were all mixed up—tiny socks, long socks, stripey socks, polka-dot socks. Some were inside-out. One sock was wet. One sock had a little hole.

Joey’s nose wrinkled. “Oh no. This is hard,” he whispered.

He tried to match a blue sock with a green sock. It looked wrong. He tried to fit a big sock with a tiny sock. It flopped over.

“I can’t do it!” Joey huffed.

His cheeks felt hot. His tail thumped the floor. Thump, thump. His tummy felt tight. His ears drooped. The dryer’s “Thump, thump” now sounded too loud. The soft soap smell felt too big. A bubble of mad feelings rose in Joey’s chest.

“I want to throw the socks!” he shouted. His paws squeezed a stripey sock into a crumple.

Mama Roo knelt down. “I see a storm in your tummy,” she said gently. “Your face looks red. Your tail is thumping. You are feeling angry.”

“I am,” Joey said. A little tear slid down his cheek. “I wanted to help. But it’s too hard.”

Mama Roo nodded. “It is okay to feel angry. Angry is a big feeling. We can care for it. Let’s try our calm plan.”

Joey sniffled. “Our calm plan?”

“Yes,” said Mama Roo. “Pause, breathe, and pick a peaceful tool.”

Joey blinked. “What is my peaceful tool?”

Mama Roo smiled. “For you, it might be a soft hop and a slow breath.”

Joey placed the crumpled sock back in the basket. He stepped away. He put his paw on his chest. He whispered, “Pause.”

He breathed in through his nose, slow and quiet. In… one, two, three. He breathed out through his mouth. Out… one, two, three. He did it again. In… one, two, three. Out… one, two, three.

He felt his cheeks cool. His tummy loosened. The dryer’s “Thump, thump” turned friendly again.

“I feel a little better,” Joey said.

“Good,” said Mama Roo. “Now pick a peaceful tool.”

Joey looked around. On the wall was a picture chart with little drawings. It showed a magnifying glass for “look close,” a turtle for “go slow,” and a little heart for “ask for help.”

“I pick… go slow,” Joey said. “And ask for help, please.”

“Great choice,” Mama Roo said. “That is a peaceful solution.”

Mama Roo showed Joey a trick. “First, flip socks right-side-out. That saves time. We take care of things so they last. That’s called sustainability.”

Joey nodded. “Save things. Be kind to Earth.” He gently flipped a sock. The fabric felt smooth. “Soft,” he giggled.

“Next,” Mama Roo said, “sort by color. Whites in one pile, colors in another. Then look for friends—stripes with stripes, dots with dots.”

Joey went slow. He looked close. “Blue with blue,” he said, making a neat pile. “Dots with dots.” He matched a pair and pressed them together. “Found you, sock friend!”

He found the wet sock. “This one is soggy,” he said.

Mama Roo brought a small rack. “We air-dry soggy socks on the rack. Air drying saves energy.”

Joey hung the wet sock with a tiny clip. It made a little click. “Click!” He smiled. “I am helping our home and our Earth.”

He found the sock with the hole. He held it up. “This one is hurt.”

“We can mend it,” Mama Roo said. “Fixing is better than throwing away. That’s civic responsibility—taking care of our things and our place.”

Joey felt proud. “We fix and we care.”

Soon the basket was almost empty. Joey matched the last pair. “Ta-da!” he cheered, hopping in a happy circle.

Mama Roo clapped softly. “You did it. You felt angry. You paused. You breathed. You chose a peaceful tool. You solved the problem and cared for our Earth.”

Joey’s heart felt light. The laundry room smelled cozy again. The dryer beeped. “Beep!” Joey laughed. “I am ready for the next load.”

“Only when the basket is full,” Mama Roo said with a wink. “We save water and power.”

Joey nodded. “Full loads, slow breaths, kind choices,” he said. “When I feel mad, I can pause, breathe, and pick a peaceful tool.”

“Exactly,” said Mama Roo. “That is a big joey skill.”

Joey hugged Mama Roo. His tail swished, soft and happy. He looked at the neat rows of socks like tiny flags of calm.

“I am small,” Joey said, “but I can do big kind things.”

“Yes,” Mama Roo whispered. “And that helps our whole world.”

The End.
