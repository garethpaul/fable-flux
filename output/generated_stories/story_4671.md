# Buttons, Bats, and Beaver Traditions

Benny was a busy beaver. His paws liked to build, and his heart liked to share. One rainy day, Benny opened the little closet hideaway under the stairs. It was his quiet spot. It smelled like cedar chips and warm cookies. The light was soft and yellow. Shelves held boxes and baskets, all neat and tidy.

Benny wiggled his whiskers. “Today, I will make something new,” he said. He pulled out a big box labeled “Old Things.” It was filled with buttons, ribbons, paper rolls, and shiny lids. They clinked and clacked. “Ooh!” said Benny. “We can recycle these and make treasures.”

He heard a knock-knock. His friends had come to play. Mia the mouse peeked in. “Hi, Benny!” she squeaked. “What are you doing?”

“I’m in my closet hideaway,” Benny said, patting the rug. “Come see. Let’s make something and share a tradition.”

“What’s a tradition?” asked Pip the puppy, his nose wiggling at the cookie smell.

Benny smiled. “A tradition is a special thing we do again and again. We do it together. We share it with friends and family.”

Mia’s eyes sparkled. “Like my family’s song before supper!”

Pip wagged. “Like my Sunday run at the park!”

Benny nodded. “Yes! My family has a beaver tradition too. We make ‘Button Bats’ every spring and play a friendly game. We cheer, we learn, and we help each other.”

“Button Bats?” Pip tilted his head. “Do they flap?”

Benny giggled. “No, silly. They’re little bats made from safe cardboard rolls. We tape a button to the end, so they make a tap-tap sound. We play a soft ball game indoors. We share snacks and stories about our grandparents.”

Mia clapped. “Can we make them? Can we share your tradition?”

Benny felt a warm bubble of pride. “Yes! Let’s do it. We will recycle and use our brains. That’s education! And we will move our bodies. That’s sports!”

They set to work. Benny gave gentle jobs. “Mia, please sort buttons by color.” Mia squeaked, “Red, blue, yellow,” as the buttons clicked and chimed. “Pip, please roll the paper and hold the tape.” Pip held very still, his tail swish-swish-swish.

Benny wrapped the cardboard rolls and taped on a big safe button. Tap-tap! The sound made them giggle. He showed how to draw friendly faces on the bats. “Add a smile,” he said. “Our tradition is about joy.”

Soon they had three Button Bats and a soft, squishy ball made from old socks. “We recycled!” Mia cheered.

“Now we play,” said Benny, “but there is a problem.” He looked at the tiny closet. It felt cozy but small. “We need safe space for sports. We do not want to knock things down.”

Pip’s ears drooped. “Oh no.”

Benny took a deep breath. He used his best thinking. “We can solve it. Initiative means we try to fix a problem. Let’s make a plan.”

He pointed to the baskets. “First, we move the fragile things up high.” They worked together, slow and careful. The baskets rustled. The cedar smell grew sweet. Light bounced off the shiny lids.

“Next,” Benny said, “we set rules. Gentle taps. Slow turns. Big cheers for everyone.”

Mia nodded. “And we take turns. That’s fair.”

Pip wagged. “And we wear our soft socks so we don’t slip.”

Benny grinned. “Great ideas! We are smart and safe.”

They placed a blanket as a goal line. Benny showed them the beaver cheer. “We say: ‘Tap, clap, learn, and share! We care!’”

They all tried. “Tap, clap, learn, and share! We care!” Their voices sounded happy in the small, warm space.

The game began. Tap! Mia tapped the soft ball with her Button Bat. The button made a tiny click. Pip scooped the ball with his paws and rolled it back. Benny tapped next, slow and gentle. They counted numbers. “One, two, three!” They named colors. “Red button, blue button!” They asked kind questions. “How do you hold your bat?” “Can I try your way?” They listened and learned.

But then, the soft ball bumped a stack of boxes. The boxes wobbled. “Uh-oh,” said Pip, eyes wide.

Benny took action. “Pause game,” he said, holding up his paw. “Let’s make a safer wall.”

He pulled out a flat box lid and some folded towels from the “Old Things” box. “These are recycled too. We can build a cushion wall.”

Mia stacked the towels. Pip pressed the lid in front. The wall felt soft and steady. They tested with tiny taps. Thump. Safe.

“Great teamwork,” Benny said. “We solved the problem!”

They played again. They cheered for tries, not just points. They learned to breathe in and out when they felt wiggly. The taps made happy sounds. The closet hideaway felt like a tiny gym and a tiny school and a tiny heart.

When the game ended, they sat in a circle. Benny passed warm tea in little cups and crunchy apple slices. The tea smelled like cinnamon. He told a story. “My grand-beaver taught me this game. He said, ‘Share a tradition, and you share love.’”

Mia sighed. “I will share my supper song with you.”

Pip smiled. “I will share my Sunday run. We can jog slow and count clouds.”

Benny’s eyes shone. “And every rainy day, we can make new things from old things. We can play, learn, and share.”

They tapped their Button Bats one last time. Click, tap, smile.

“Tap, clap, learn, and share! We care!” they sang together.

The End.
