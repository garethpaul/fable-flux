# Penny Penguin’s Balcony Book

Penny was a cheerful penguin who loved to tap her little feet. Tap, tap, tap! She lived with Nana on the sixth floor. Nana’s balcony had a tiny garden with green mint, sweet strawberries, and a small lemon tree. The air smelled like mint tea and sunshine.

One bright morning, Penny waddled onto the balcony with her notebook. It was soft and blue. It had a shiny star on the cover. “Today I will write a story,” Penny said. “I want to write like Nana.”

Nana smiled. “Writing is a skill,” she said gently. “We learn by trying. We learn by practicing. We also share our stories. That is our family tradition.”

Nana showed a wooden box. Inside were old cards and little letters. Some had stickers. Some had tiny drawings. “Long ago, our family wrote small letters to share love and news,” Nana said. “We shared kindness with words.”

Penny’s eyes grew round. “I want to share kindness too!” she chirped. She sat at a low table by the mint. The table felt smooth. The chair felt cool under her feathers. Penny picked up a pencil. She sniffed the fresh paper. Scratch-scratch went the pencil as she drew her first line.

Her friend Mina, a little mouse with bright eyes, peeked through the railing. “Hi, Penny!” squeaked Mina. “What are you doing?”

“I am writing,” Penny said proudly. “I am making a balcony book. It will have our smells, our sounds, and our smiles.”

Mina twitched her whiskers. “I want to help!”

Penny nodded. “Yes, let’s write together. We will use our senses. We will be safe too.” Penny pointed to the balcony rule sign Nana had made.

- Sit on chairs, not rails.
- Ask for help with sharp things.
- Keep little paws and flippers inside.

Mina saluted. “Safety first!”

Penny wrote: “I smell mint.” She paused. “How do I write the sound of the leaves?” She listened. The mint leaves went shhhh in the breeze. The lemon tree leaves went flip-flip-flip. Far below, a bus hummed. Hmmmm.

Penny wrote slowly. “I hear shhhh. I hear flip. I hear hmmmm.” She smiled. “Writing is like catching sounds.”

Mina added, “And feelings too!” Mina touched a strawberry. “It is bumpy. It is cold from the morning air.” She giggled. “It smells sweet.”

Penny wrote, “The strawberry is bumpy and sweet.” She drew a tiny red dot. “I can write pictures with words.”

Then, a little problem came. A big gust of wind whooshed across the balcony. Whooooosh! Penny’s pages fluttered. One page flew up, up, up and out. “Oh no!” cried Penny. “My page!”

Mina reached for it, but Penny held her friend back. “Stop! Safety first,” Penny said. “We do not climb the rails.”

Penny took a deep breath. In, out. “We can solve this,” she told herself. “Writers solve problems. We think and try.”

She looked around. “We can make a cozy writing spot,” she said. “We can keep pages safe.” Penny placed a smooth stone on the stack. She clipped the top with a clothespin. Mina brought a tiny basket to hold pencils so they would not roll away.

Nana came with warm mint tea in a safe cup and two tiny lemon slices. “I saw the wind,” Nana said. “You stayed safe. You fixed the problem. That is good writing skill. Planning is part of writing.”

Penny beamed. “We are building skills! We are careful. We are brave. We keep trying.”

Mina squeaked, “And we share! Can we write a letter like your family?”

Penny nodded. “Yes! Let’s write a balcony letter.” She wrote big, friendly words:

“Dear Neighbor,
Today we smell mint.
We hear shhhh leaves.
We taste sweet strawberries.
We feel happy and safe.
Love, Penny and Mina.”

Mina added a tiny heart. They both signed their names.

Penny looked at the lemon tree. “Nana, what is our next tradition step?”

Nana’s eyes twinkled. “We read aloud,” she said. “We use clear voices. We listen with kind ears.”

Penny stood tall. Mina stood on a small cushion. Penny read slowly, “Dear Neighbor…” Her voice was soft and steady. Mina held the page so it would not flap. The words felt warm in the air, like the sun on their faces.

When she finished, the balcony felt extra bright. A bird chirped as if to clap. Chirp-chirp! Chirp!

Penny hugged her notebook. “My heart is full,” she said. “Writing helps me share. Writing helps me learn. Writing keeps our traditions alive.”

Nana nodded. “Yes, dear. Writing is a gift you give to yourself and to friends.”

Mina wiggled her whiskers. “Let’s write more tomorrow. We can write about rain sounds!”

Penny laughed. “Yes! We will bring our clips, our stone, and our cozy spot. We will listen, smell, and feel. We will be safe and brave. We will practice and grow.”

The sun slid lower. The mint still smelled fresh. The lemon leaves went flip-flip. Penny added one last line to her balcony book: “Today I learned to write with my senses, to plan for safety, and to share with friends.”

She drew a tiny penguin and a tiny mouse, paw and flipper together.

The End.
