# Penny Penguin Plans the Big Fix

Penny was a cheerful penguin with bright eyes and a bright orange beak. She wore a tiny tool belt and waddled into Grandpa Gus’s garage workshop. The workshop smelled like pine wood and warm cocoa. Sunlight made little dust sparkles dance in the air. Clink, clank, went the tools. Whirr, hummed a gentle fan.

“Good morning!” Penny chirped. “I’m ready to help!”

Grandpa Gus smiled. “Morning, Penny. The red wagon’s wheel is wobbly. Your friends need the wagon for the neighborhood cleanup. Can you fix it?”

Penny puffed up with courage. “I can try! I will prepare first.”

She looked around carefully. She made a plan in her head, step by step. She wrote it on a big sticky note in chunky letters:

1. Find tools.
2. Find parts.
3. Ask for help if needed.
4. Work safely.
5. Check and test.

Penny tapped the note. “Preparation helps me remember.”

She put on goggles. Click. She pulled on little gloves. Snug. She tied her tool belt. Tight. “Safety first,” she said.

She waddled to the tool bench. “I will need a wrench, a soft cloth, and oil.” She touched each tool and named it. “Wrench. Cloth. Oil.” She placed them neatly in a tray. She also found a tiny bolt and a shiny nut in a small box of parts. “Just in case,” she whispered.

Her friend Miko Mouse peeked in. “Hi, Penny! Can we use the wagon soon? We want to pick up litter at the park.”

“Yes,” Penny said kindly. “I am getting ready. Preparation takes time. It helps the work go well.”

Miko nodded. “I can help sort the nuts and bolts.”

“Thank you,” Penny said. “That is kind.”

Penny rolled the red wagon into the middle of the workshop. The wheel squeaked, squeeeak. She crouched low and wiggled the wheel. “Wobble wobble,” she said. “Hmm.”

She spoke to the wagon. “Hello, Wagon. I will be gentle.” She felt the rim with her flippers. It felt scratchy. She smelled a little bit of oil. She listened. The little bolt was loose. Click, click.

Penny took a deep breath. “I can do this.” That was her courage waking up.

Grandpa Gus came over. “Do you need help, Penny?”

Penny looked at her list. “Step three says ask for help if needed. I think I can try first. If it is too hard, I will ask.”

She turned the wrench slowly. “Lefty loosey,” she said. The loose bolt came off. She cleaned the dirt with the soft cloth. Swish, swish. “Clean parts work better,” she said.

But oh! The hole in the wheel was a little too big for the old bolt. It would not hold tight. Penny’s heart did a small flop. “Oh no.”

She placed a flipper on her chest. “It is okay. I will use my plan.” She looked at the box of parts. She saw a bigger bolt and a small metal washer. “A washer can fill the space,” she said.

Just then, Benny Beaver poked his head in. “I need wood glue from the shelf,” Benny said. “Can I take it now?”

Penny noticed the shelf was wobbly, too. She wanted the washer and the glue, but Benny did too. She chose kindness and negotiation.

“Benny,” Penny said gently, “the wagon’s wheel needs a washer and I need a tiny drop of glue to hold the washer in place. You need glue for your dam. How about we share? I will use two drops, and then you can take the bottle. I will give you this new glue cap from our extras.”

Benny thought. “That seems fair. Thank you for asking kindly.”

They smiled and did a little flipper-and-paw tap. Penny added a tiny drop of glue. Drip. She placed the washer. She added the bigger bolt. “Righty tighty,” she said, turning the wrench slowly. Tight, snug, safe.

Penny spun the wheel. It moved smooth and quiet. “No more wobble!” she cheered.

Miko clapped. “Can we test it?”

Penny pointed to her list. “Step five: check and test.” She set a small bag of toy blocks in the wagon to make a pretend load. She pushed it across the smooth floor. Roll, roll, roll. No squeak. No wobble.

Grandpa Gus nodded. “Great preparation, Penny. You gathered tools, planned your steps, worked safely, asked kindly, and stayed brave.”

Penny smiled big. “Preparation makes hard jobs easier. Kindness helps friends. Courage helps me keep trying.”

They brought the wagon outside. The air smelled fresh, like rain and leaves. Friends gathered with little grabbers and gloves. “Thank you, Penny!” they sang.

Penny bowed. “Let’s clean the park together.”

As they worked, Penny showed the younger kids how to prepare. “Make a plan. Get your tools. Be safe. Ask for help. Check your work.” The children nodded and copied her steps. The cleanup went fast and fun. Birds chirped. Bags rustled. Laughter bubbled.

When they finished, the park was neat and bright. Penny placed her list on the wagon for everyone to see.

“Today,” she said, “we learned that being ready helps us do good things. When we prepare, we can be kind helpers and brave problem solvers. Preparation, kindness, and courage make a great team!”

Everyone cheered, “Hooray for being prepared!”

Penny’s heart felt warm and light. She took off her goggles and gave Grandpa Gus a hug. “Thank you for the workshop.”

“Thank you for your plan,” he said.

Penny waddled home, humming a happy tune, ready to make her next list.

The End.
