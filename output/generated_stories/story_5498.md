# Bubbles Makes a Choice

In a cozy basement lab, the air was cool and smelled like clean soap. Lights hummed softly. Beep, beep, beep went small machines. On a big table stood jars of colorful water: red, blue, and green. There were soft sponges, shiny buttons, and a little bell that went ding!

Bubbles, a playful dolphin, lived in a round, clear tank in the center of the room. The tank had gentle waves and a tiny waterfall that whispered shhh. Bubbles loved to splash and make silly sounds. “Eee-eee!” he clicked, flipping his tail so the water glittered.

Dr. Dot, a kind scientist with curly hair and bright orange shoes, came down the creaky stairs. “Good morning, Bubbles,” she said in a warm voice. “Today we will learn something new.”

Bubbles wiggled his fins. “Eee! Learn?” he chirped. He liked to learn. He liked to play even more.

Dr. Dot held up three big picture cards. One had a star, one had a circle, and one had a triangle. “We will play a matching game,” she said. “You get to choose how we play.”

Bubbles blinked. Choose? His belly tickled. He loved choices, but choices felt big.

Dr. Dot smiled. “You can choose one thing at a time. Listen to your feelings. Use your eyes and ears.”

She pointed at the jars. “Do you want the red water or the blue water in your tank?” The red looked like a sunset. The blue looked like the ocean.

Bubbles looked left and right. He listened to the soft hum. He smelled the clean soap. He thought about the ocean. He clicked softly, “Blue, please.” He nodded his head and tapped the blue jar with his nose.

“Great choice,” said Dr. Dot. “You used your eyes to help you decide.” She poured the blue water, and the tank glowed like a calm sea. Bubbles felt proud. His heart went thump-thump in a happy way.

“Next choice,” said Dr. Dot. She held up the picture cards again. “Star, circle, or triangle?”

Bubbles loved the pointy triangle, but the circle looked like a bubble. He took a deep breath. The air was cool in his blowhole. “Circle,” he clicked. “Like me! Like a bubble.”

“Circle it is,” said Dr. Dot. “You used your feelings to help you decide.”

They played the matching game. When Dr. Dot rang the bell—ding!—Bubbles found a circle sticker and booped it with his nose. “Boop!” The sticker made a tiny crinkle sound. Bubbles giggled and splashed.

After a while, Bubbles got a little tired. His tail felt slow. He wanted a rest, but the bell kept going ding! Ding! Ding! He looked at Dr. Dot. She held up another card.

Bubbles felt a wobble in his tummy. This was a new kind of choice. He could keep playing, or he could say, “I need a break.” He remembered what Dr. Dot had said: “Listen to your feelings.”

Bubbles took another breath. The waterfall whispered shhh, and the cool water brushed his skin. He lifted his head. “Dr. Dot?” he chirped.

“Yes, Bubbles?” Her voice was gentle.

“I need a little rest,” said Bubbles. “Please.”

Dr. Dot’s eyes crinkled with a smile. “Thank you for telling me,” she said. “You made a strong choice. That is called self-advocacy. It means you speak up for what you need.”

She turned the bell off, and the room felt soft and quiet. Bubbles floated in the blue glow and closed his eyes. He counted three slow waves—one, two, three—and stretched his fins.

When he opened his eyes, he felt better. “Ready,” he chirped.

“Wonderful,” said Dr. Dot. “Would you like the soft sponge or the shiny button for our next game?”

Bubbles looked at the sponge. It looked like a cloud. He looked at the button. It sparkled like a star. He remembered how he chose before: look, listen, breathe, and think. “Sponge, please,” he said. “It looks cozy.”

They played a gentle game. Bubbles pushed the soft sponge to match the circle cards. Squish, squish went the sponge. Ding! went the bell now and then. Bubbles laughed. “This is fun!”

Then Dr. Dot made a tiny mistake. She picked up the triangle card instead of the circle. Bubbles noticed. His brain felt like a light turned on. He could say nothing, or he could speak up politely.

“Excuse me,” Bubbles said in his small, kind voice. “I chose circles today.”

Dr. Dot looked down. “You are right,” she said. “Thank you for telling me. That was a smart decision.”

Bubbles beamed. He had used his eyes, his feelings, and his words. He made choices that helped him learn and play.

At the end, Dr. Dot clapped softly. “Bubbles, what did you learn?”

Bubbles clicked, “I learned to look and listen. I learned to breathe and think. I learned to say what I need. That helps me make good choices.”

Dr. Dot nodded. “Yes! Decisions can be hard. But you have tools. You can pause, notice, and choose.”

Bubbles splashed a happy splash. The blue water sparkled. The lab hummed. The bell gave one last gentle ding.

Bubbles whispered, “I can make good choices,” and his heart felt brave and calm.

The End.
