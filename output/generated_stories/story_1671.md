# Tessa Turtle Listens

In a quiet attic at the top of a creaky old house lived Tessa Turtle. Tessa was shy. She liked soft places and soft sounds. The attic was filled with boxes, button jars, and old blankets that smelled like warm dust and pine. Sunlight came in through a tiny round window and made golden dust sparkle like little stars.

One afternoon, Tessa heard a small tap-tap-tap. She pulled her head into her shell. Tap-tap-tap. She peeked out. “Who is tapping?” she whispered.

Tessa listened. She kept very still. Her heart went bum-bum, bum-bum. The attic was so quiet she could hear the clock downstairs say tick-tock, tick-tock. She took a deep breath. “I can do this,” she told herself in a tiny voice.

“Hello?” Tessa called, barely louder than a breath.

Tap-tap-tap. It came from a tall box wrapped in shiny paper. The paper smelled like peppermint and felt crinkly under Tessa’s little feet.

Tessa wanted to hide, but she remembered what Grandma Turtle always said, “When you feel shy, stop and listen. Listening helps you know what to do.”

So Tessa listened again. This time she heard a faint squeak. “Help?” whispered a squeaky voice.

Tessa gasped. “Someone needs help!” Her shy tummy fluttered, but her brave heart stretched. “I will try,” she said. “I will listen and use my little turtle steps.”

She crawled closer. “I am Tessa. Are you okay?”

“I am Pip,” squeaked the tiny voice. “I am a toy mouse. I fell inside this box. It is too tight. I cannot get out.”

Tessa pressed her ear to the box. It felt cool and smooth. She could hear the tiniest rustle, like a leaf. “I hear you,” she said. “I will help.” She looked around. The attic smelled like old wood and sugar cookies from long ago. Dust bunnies sat like gray clouds under a table.

Tessa took initiative. She did not wait for someone else. She looked for a way. On the floor lay a ribbon, red and shiny. “Maybe this can pull the paper,” she said.

But first, Tessa listened again. She listened for the tap. Tap-tap. It was near the top of the box. “The sound is high,” Tessa said. “So I will try up high.”

She climbed a step stool. The wood went creak, creak. Tessa felt scared, but she kept going, one slow step at a time. “Listening helps me choose,” she whispered.

At the top, she found a little hole in the shiny paper. She slid the ribbon through the hole. “Pip, I will pull. When you hear me count, push,” she said.

“Okay,” squeaked Pip.

Tessa counted softly, “One, two, three.” She pulled the ribbon. The paper made a loud rrriip! Pip pushed. Pop! The top of the box lifted a little. Warm, dusty air puffed out and tickled Tessa’s nose. “Ah-choo!” she sneezed.

“I heard that!” Pip giggled.

Tessa smiled. “Again?” she asked.

“Yes,” said Pip.

Tessa listened to the box. She heard Pip scoot to the corner. She pulled the ribbon again, steady and slow. Rrrip, rrrip. Pip pushed. Pop! The top opened. Out tumbled a tiny gray toy mouse with a pink nose and soft felt ears.

“Thank you!” Pip squeaked, brushing off sparkly bits of paper. “You listened so well. You heard where I was. You heard when I was ready. You helped me.”

Tessa blushed and tucked her chin. “I was shy,” she said. “But I listened. Listening told me what to do.”

Pip sniffed the air. “What else is in here?” he asked. The attic seemed bigger now, like a small, safe world to discover. Sunlight warmed Tessa’s shell. She felt brave and curious.

“Let’s look,” Tessa said.

They moved gently around the attic. Tessa touched each box with her little turtle toes. She listened before she opened. She heard the soft clink of buttons in a tin. She heard the jingle of bells in a blue bag. She heard a faint hum and found a music box. When she turned the key, it sang a sweet lullaby, like raindrops. Tessa closed her eyes and smiled.

“We found so much,” Pip said. “How did you know where to start?”

“I listened,” Tessa said. “I listened to the sounds. I listened to my feelings. When I felt scared, I took a breath. When I heard a clue, I followed it.”

They came to a tiny wooden chest with a heart painted on it. Tessa pressed her ear to it. Silence. She waited. Then, a gentle thump. “Maybe it is stuck,” she said.

Tessa tried the latch. It wiggled but would not open. She looked around and spotted a small key hanging on a nail. She took the key carefully. “I will try,” she said.

Click. The chest opened. Inside were shiny marbles, a feather, and a note. Tessa read the big letters slowly, “Listen first. Then try your best.”

Tessa and Pip laughed. “That is our rule!” Pip said.

As the sun began to go down, the attic turned rosy and calm. Tessa felt proud. She had listened, discovered new things, and used her own small steps to help.

Pip hugged her. “You are a good listener, Tessa.”

Tessa hugged back. Her shell was warm. “Listening helps me learn,” she said. “Listening helps me help.”

And from that day, when Tessa felt shy, she would stop, breathe, and listen. Listening made the quiet attic a place of discovery, and it helped her take kind, brave steps all by herself.

The lesson: When we listen— to sounds, to friends, and to our own feelings— we can find answers, help others, and do our best.

The End.
