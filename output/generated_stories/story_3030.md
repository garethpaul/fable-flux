# Tashi the Tortoise’s Sparkly Bath Time

Tashi was a wise tortoise who moved slowly but thought deeply. He wore a tiny blue towel like a cape and liked to hum songs from his grandma. One sunny morning, Tashi peeked into the bathroom. The mirror was foggy. The sink was messy. Toothpaste dotted the counter like little white clouds.

“Oh my,” Tashi said, blinking his gentle eyes. “This bathroom needs some calm.”

He placed his small basket on the floor. Inside were soft cloths, a wooden brush, and a lavender soap his grandma sent from far away. It smelled sweet and flowery. Tashi smiled and whispered, “We can learn many things in a bathroom.”

Just then, Mia and Theo, two little neighbors, ran in. “We need to brush!” Mia said, holding a toothbrush sideways. Theo held a comb and a rubber duck. The duck squeaked, squeak!

Tashi nodded. “Yes, friends. Let’s try something special. We will have a Sparkly Bath Time. We will learn to care for ourselves and for each other’s ways. We will learn about my grandma’s way too.”

“What is that smell?” Theo asked, sniffing the air.

“This is lavender soap from my grandma’s village,” Tashi said. “Where she lives, people sing a short clean song before washing. It helps everyone remember the steps.”

Mia tilted her head. “A clean song?”

Tashi took a breath and sang softly, tapping his shell in rhythm:
“Water on hands,
Soap in a twist,
Bubbles and scrub,
Don’t let a spot be missed.
Rinse and drip, dry with a cloth,
Clean hands, clean hearts—off, off, off!”

Mia giggled. Theo clapped. The duck squeaked again.

“Now,” said Tashi, “we will try it together. But first, we must make this room feel calm.” He opened a drawer and found hair ties, toothpaste, and tiny cups all jumbled. He gently sorted them into small baskets: brushes in one, cups in another, toothpaste and floss together.

“Organization helps everyone,” Tashi explained. “In my grandma’s home, we place things where they belong. Then we say thank you.”

“Thank you, brushes,” Mia whispered, placing them in line.

“Thank you, cups,” Theo said, stacking them carefully.

Tashi’s eyes twinkled. “Good. Now we wash our hands with the clean song.”

They turned on the warm water. It sounded like rain on leaves. The soap felt slippery and soft. Bubbles popped like tiny balloons. “Water on hands,” Tashi sang. “Soap in a twist.” The bathroom smelled like a purple garden.

“Bubbles and scrub,” Mia sang, rubbing her palms. “Don’t let a spot be missed,” Theo sang, rubbing between his fingers.

“Rinse and drip,” Tashi finished, “dry with a cloth.” They patted their hands dry with a fluffy towel. The towel felt like a warm cloud.

“Your grandma’s song is nice,” Mia said.

“It helps me remember. It helps me respect her way,” Tashi said. “Different families have different clean songs or steps. We can learn from each other. That is cultural appreciation. It means we listen, we learn, and we say thank you.”

Theo looked at the mirror. A toothpaste streak ran down like a white river. “Oh,” he said. “I did that yesterday.”

Tashi nodded kindly. “We all make messes. Now we make amends. Making amends means we try to fix what we did.”

Theo’s eyebrows wiggled with worry. “Can I fix it?”

“Yes,” said Tashi. He handed Theo a damp cloth. “Wipe in small circles. Slow is fine.”

Theo wiped. The streak faded. The mirror shone. “I did it!”

“You improved it,” Tashi said. “That’s improvement—doing a little better each time.”

Mia picked up the toothpaste. “I squeezed it too hard.” She replaced the cap and set it in the basket. “I made amends too!”

The bathroom already looked calmer. Tashi pointed to the toothbrushes. “In my grandma’s village, we greet the brush. We say, ‘Hello, helper!’ Would you like to try?”

Mia and Theo held up their brushes. “Hello, helper!” they chorused. Tashi bowed to his wooden brush. “Hello, helper.”

They brushed in gentle circles. “Round and round,” Tashi said. “Count to ten.” They counted slowly. The minty taste felt cool like a tiny snowflake on their tongues.

Theo spit into the sink. “I taste peppermint!”

“In my grandma’s place, we rinse with a tiny cup and then smile at the mirror,” Tashi said. He poured a small cup of water for each child. Swish, swish. Swish, swish. They spit and then smiled big, bright smiles. The mirror showed three kind faces and one silly duck.

Tashi set the duck on the clean edge of the tub. “Duck watches over the soap,” he said.

“Can we hear the song again?” Mia asked.

“Of course,” Tashi said. They sang the clean song once more, swaying side to side. Their voices blended like warm tea with honey. The bathroom felt peaceful and proud.

“Today we learned your grandma’s way,” Theo said. “We kept the bathroom tidy. We made amends. We got cleaner!”

Tashi nodded. “And we appreciated another culture. We listened to a story from far away. We tried a new song with respect. When we honor others’ ways, we grow gentle hearts and strong habits.”

Mia hugged Tashi’s shell. “Thank you for sharing.”

“Thank you for caring,” Tashi said, eyes soft and wise. He looked around the neat bathroom—baskets lined up, mirror clear, towels folded. He breathed in the lavender air. “Now this room feels like a smile.”

Theo gave the duck a final squeak. “Tomorrow we can share our song with someone else.”

“Yes,” Tashi said. “We can learn their way too.”

They turned off the light. The bathroom glowed quietly, clean and calm, like a small moon at rest.

The End.
