# Kiki Koala’s Warm Kitchen Plan

The warm kitchen smelled like sweet oatmeal and fresh oranges. The sun peeked in the window and made little golden squares on the floor. Kiki the calm koala stretched her soft paws and took a slow, happy breath.

“Mmm,” said Kiki. “Good morning, warm kitchen.”

Kiki liked to help. She liked to feel safe, clean, and ready. But today, the kitchen was a little messy. A lid was on the floor. A cup sat near the edge of the table. A towel was bunched up by the sink. Kiki blinked her gentle eyes.

“Oh,” Kiki said softly. “This does not feel safe. This does not feel ready.”

Her tummy made a tiny rumble. “I want a healthy breakfast,” she said. “But first, I will make a plan.”

Kiki took a slow, steady breath in and out. “I can do this,” she whispered. “Step by step.”

She looked at the cup near the edge. It wobbled when the table moved. “That is not safe,” Kiki said. “Cups like to sit far from the edge.” She wrapped her soft paws around the cup and moved it to the middle of the table. “There,” she said. “Safe.”

She picked up the lid on the floor. “Lids live with their pots,” Kiki giggled. She put the lid on the pot and patted it. “All together now.”

She shook out the towel and hung it neatly on the hook. The towel felt warm and a little damp. “You can dry there,” Kiki said. “Thank you for helping.”

Kiki smiled. The kitchen already felt calmer. The sun squares looked brighter. “I am organized,” Kiki said. “That helps me feel good.”

Now Kiki washed her paws. She turned on the water. It was warm like a hug. “Scrub, scrub, scrub,” she sang. “Front and back, between, and thumbs.” She counted slowly. “One, two, three, four, five, six, seven, eight, nine, ten.” She rinsed and shook off the drops. Drip, drop, drip! The drops tickled her fur. Kiki giggled. “Clean paws are safe paws.”

Kiki opened the fruit bowl. The oranges smelled bright and happy. She picked a banana too. “Fruits help me grow,” she said. “They are my healthy friends.”

She checked the stove knobs. “Off,” she said. “Off, off, off.” She nodded. “I ask a grown-up to use hot things.” Kiki remembered Grandma Koala’s rule: “Look, ask, and wait.”

Just then, Grandma Koala came in, wearing a soft blue apron. “Good morning, Kiki,” Grandma said. “It smells sunny in here!”

Kiki pointed. “Grandma, I moved the cup. I put the lid away. I hung the towel. I washed my paws. I checked the stove. Can we make oatmeal together?”

Grandma’s eyes twinkled. “What a calm and careful helper you are. Yes, let’s make oatmeal. You can pour and stir. I will use the hot stove.”

“Look, ask, and wait,” Kiki said with a smile.

Grandma turned on the stove. The pot made a tiny hiss. The oatmeal bubbled like a soft song: blub, blub, blub. Steam rose up, warm and sweet. Kiki stood back a little. She held the wooden spoon when Grandma passed it to her.

“Stir slow, Kiki,” Grandma said.

“Stir, stir, stir,” Kiki sang. “I am calm. I am careful. I am safe.”

They added sliced bananas and orange pieces. The kitchen smelled like sunshine and cozy hugs. Kiki’s tummy felt happy and ready.

While the oatmeal cooled, Kiki set the table. She put spoons on napkins. She kept cups in the middle. She made a little line of fruit slices. “All in their own spots,” she said. “That is organized.”

Grandma brought the warm bowls. “Not too hot,” she said. Kiki waited and blew gently. “Whooo,” Kiki whispered, watching the steam dance away.

They tasted the oatmeal together. “Mmm,” Kiki said. “Sweet and soft.” Her paws felt proud. Her heart felt big.

“Kiki,” Grandma said, “you made a plan. You kept things safe. You washed your paws. You ate a healthy breakfast. How do you feel?”

Kiki touched her chest. “I feel strong. I feel calm. I feel proud.”

Grandma clapped softly. “That is self-esteem,” she said. “It grows when you take care of yourself and your space.”

Kiki looked around the warm kitchen. It was tidy and bright. The towel hung straight. The cup was safe. The lid was in place. The spoons were waiting to be washed.

Kiki nodded. “Healthy habits help me. When I clean up, wash up, and eat well, I can do big things.”

Grandma smiled. “Yes. Little steps make big changes.”

After breakfast, Kiki put the bowls in the sink. She rinsed them with warm water. Swish, swish! She folded the napkins and put them in the basket.

“All done,” Kiki said. She took a slow breath and hugged Grandma. The hug felt soft and warm, like the kitchen.

Kiki waved to the sun squares on the floor. “Thank you, warm kitchen,” she said. “We did it, step by step.”

The End.
