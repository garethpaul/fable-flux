# Kiki the Koala Writes It Right

Kiki was a calm koala who loved quiet mornings. She lived in a warm kitchen with sunny yellow walls, a humming fridge, and a little wooden table by the window. The air smelled like toast and sweet honey. The kettle made a soft hiss, like a friendly snake saying hello.

Kiki sat at the table with a short pencil and a stack of paper. The paper was smooth, like cool leaves. Kiki whispered, “Today I will write a story.” She took a deep breath and smiled. Writing made her heart feel cozy.

Her friend Pip, a small parrot with green feathers, hopped on the chair. “What are you doing, Kiki?” Pip chirped.

“I’m writing a story,” said Kiki. “I want it to be about being kind and true.”

Pip fluffed his feathers. “Ooh! Can I watch?”

“Yes,” said Kiki. “You can help me think.”

Kiki picked up the pencil. Scratch, scratch. The sound was soft on the paper. She drew a big sun. Then she wrote, slowly and carefully. “Once upon a time, a koala shared her last cookie.”

Pip peered at the page. “That’s sweet,” he said.

Kiki paused. She remembered something. Yesterday, she had taken Pip’s shiny blue button from the craft box. She did not ask. She put it in her pocket because it was very pretty. Now she felt a little twist in her tummy. It did not feel good.

Pip looked around. “Kiki, have you seen my blue button? I want to make a necklace.”

Kiki’s ears felt warm. She looked at the paper. She looked at her pocket. She took a slow breath. “I think,” she said softly, “I need to tell the truth.”

Pip tilted his head. “What truth?”

Kiki reached into her pocket and pulled out the shiny blue button. It glimmered in the warm kitchen light. “I took your button without asking. I am sorry,” she said. “I should have asked. I want to be honest.”

Pip blinked. Then he smiled a little. “Thank you for telling me,” he said. “That was brave. Please ask next time.”

Kiki nodded. “I will.” Her tummy felt calm again. The kitchen felt even warmer.

She picked up her pencil. “I will write that in my story,” she said. Scratch, scratch. “Once upon a time, a koala was honest. She said, ‘I am sorry,’ and made it right.”

Pip clapped his little wings. “That is a good lesson!”

Kiki looked at the stack of paper. Some pages were scribbled. Some had big drawings. Some had tiny words and little stars. “I want to make a little book,” Kiki said. “But look at all this paper! Some pages are messy.”

Pip nodded. “We can fix it. We can use both sides!”

Kiki smiled. “Yes. We can also recycle.”

They made two piles. Kiki touched each page and said, “Use,” or “Recycle.” The “Use” pile had clean pages. The “Recycle” pile had crumpled ones. The paper made soft rustle sounds, like wind in trees.

Pip found a small box by the sink. It said, “Recycling.” He tapped it with his beak. “Here!” he chirped.

Kiki put the crumpled pages into the box. “We are helping the Earth,” she said. “We can make new paper later.”

She took a clean page from the “Use” pile. “I will write my story here,” Kiki said. “I will write slowly. I will write true.”

Scratch, scratch. The pencil danced. Kiki wrote about the sun and the cookie. She wrote about saying sorry. She wrote about asking first. She wrote about using both sides of paper. She wrote about recycling.

Pip read each line. “This part makes me feel warm,” he said. “This part makes me smile.”

Kiki added sounds and smells to her story. “The kettle says hiss,” she wrote. “The honey smells sweet.” She added feelings too. “The tummy twist goes away when we tell the truth.”

When Kiki was done, she looked at the last page. “Oh no!” she said. “I made a big scribble line.”

Pip leaned in. “It’s okay,” he said. “We can solve this problem.”

“How?” asked Kiki.

Pip showed her a scrap of paper from the “Recycle” box. “We can cut a heart and glue it here,” he said. “We can write a kind word on the heart.”

Kiki’s eyes lit up. “Yes!” She cut a small heart. Snip, snip. She glued it over the scribble. Squish, squish. On the heart, she wrote, “Honest.”

They read the book together at the table. The fridge hummed a happy tune. The warm light made the pages glow.

Pip hugged Kiki with his little wings. “Your story is kind and true,” he said. “You wrote it right.”

Kiki hugged back. “Thank you for helping,” she said. “Writing helped me be brave. Telling the truth made my tummy calm. Recycling helped us fix mistakes. We solved our problems together.”

Kiki set the book on the windowsill. A breeze from the open window made the pages flutter, whispering, flip, flip.

Kiki smiled. “Today, I learned three things,” she said. “Write your feelings. Tell the truth. Use, reuse, and recycle.”

Pip nodded. “And always ask,” he added.

Kiki giggled. “Always ask.”

They shared warm toast with honey. The honey was sticky and sweet. The kitchen felt safe and bright. Kiki felt proud, calm, and happy.

The End.
