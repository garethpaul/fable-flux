# Tiko the Tortoise Makes a World of Friends

Tiko the wise tortoise woke up in the sunny backyard. Warm light tickled his shell. The grass smelled sweet, and tiny flowers peeked like little stars. A soft breeze said, “Whoosh,” and birds chirped, “Cheep-cheep!”

“Good morning, backyard,” Tiko said in his gentle voice. He stretched his short legs, slow and strong. “Time for my shell-wiggle exercise.” He took ten steps—one, two, three—feeling the cool dirt under his feet. Step by step, Tiko felt brave and ready.

Across the yard, a group of children played. They built a tower with colorful blocks. One child waved. “Hi, Tiko!”

“Hello,” said Tiko with a smile. “What are you building?”

“A super tower!” a girl giggled.

Tiko blinked. “I like your tower. But today, I want to make something different. I want to make a world map. I want to learn about the big world we all share.”

The children looked surprised. “A world map? Here?”

“Yes,” said Tiko. “We can craft it. We can use things we already have. We can make art. We can learn about friends far away.”

The boy with the blue hat frowned. “But I like towers.”

Tiko nodded kindly. “Towers are fun. I want to build with you. But first, I need your help. Our world is big and full of friends. I feel it’s important to learn. I will be brave and say what I need.”

Tiko took a deep breath. “I would like to make a map, please.”

The girl clapped. “Okay! Let’s try!”

They gathered craft supplies from a big basket: paper, paint, string, buttons, leaves, and safe glue. The paper felt smooth. The paint smelled like oranges and apples. The leaves were cool and crinkly.

Tiko tapped the paper with his toe. “We will make blue water for oceans. We will make green and brown land. We will add tiny pictures. We will show how people live and play in different places.”

They began. Splat, splat! Blue paint made big oceans. Swish, swish! Green and brown made land. The sun warmed their backs as they worked. Tiko hummed a happy tune. “Hmm-hmm-hmm.”

The boy with the blue hat asked, “Why do we need a map?”

Tiko smiled. “Because the world is more than our backyard. The world has many homes. Some are snowy. Some are sandy. Some are tall with mountains. Some are flat with fields. When we know more, we can be kind more.”

The girl added little paper houses. “Like this?”

“Yes,” said Tiko. “Perfect.”

They stuck cotton for snow. They added sand for a beach. They made a paper mountain with a tiny brown button at the top. They glued a small leaf boat on the ocean.

A butterfly fluttered by. Tiko laughed softly. “Even our tiny friend flies across the yard like a traveler.”

Soon, a problem arrived. The wind blew. “Whooooosh!” The map wiggled. The leaf boat fell off. The cotton snow floated away.

“Oh no!” the children cried.

Tiko’s heart thumped. He felt worried. He looked at the messy paper. Then he took a slow breath. “We can fix this. We will solve the problem.”

“How?” asked the boy.

“First, we keep the map safe,” said Tiko. “Let’s put stones on the corners.” They picked smooth stones and set them down, one, two, three, four. The paper stayed. The wind still hissed, but the map did not fly.

“Second,” said Tiko, “we use stronger glue.” They pressed the boat and the cotton and the button mountain. “Pat, pat, pat,” they said together.

“Third,” said Tiko, “we learn from the wind. Wind is part of our world, too.”

They finished the map. It was bright and bumpy and beautiful.

Tiko pointed with his toe. “Here is a place where people wear warm coats. Here is a place where people dance in the sun. Here is a place where people eat rice. Here is a place where people eat bread. Here are drums. Here are flutes. Here are kites. Here are boats.”

The girl whispered, “So many friends.”

“Yes,” said Tiko. “We may not see them, but they share our sky. We can be kind to the big world. We can save water. We can pick up trash. We can plant flowers. We can say hello in new ways.”

The boy raised his hand. “How do we say hello?”

Tiko smiled. “We can say ‘Hello,’ and also ‘Hola,’ and ‘Namaste,’ and ‘Jambo.’ Every hello is a door to a friend.”

They all practiced. “Hello! Hola! Namaste! Jambo!” Their voices sounded warm and bright in the sunny backyard.

The boy with the blue hat grinned. “Tiko, I like your map. I learned new things.”

Tiko felt proud but gentle. “Thank you. I am happy I asked for help. I used my strong voice. That is being brave and kind.”

He did ten more shell-wiggle steps around the yard. Step, step, step. “Exercise helps me feel strong for learning,” he said.

The children looked at the map again. “What should we build next?”

Tiko chuckled. “Maybe a tower—one that welcomes the whole world.”

They stacked blocks beside the paper map. They added tiny paper flags. They sang a soft song. The breeze was friendly now. The sun felt like a hug.

Tiko looked at the shiny map and the tall tower. His heart felt full. “Today we made art. We solved a problem. We learned about friends everywhere. Our world is big, and we all belong.”

The End.
