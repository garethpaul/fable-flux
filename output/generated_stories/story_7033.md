# Fox Kit's Turn in the Car

Little Finley Fox was a curious fox kit with bright eyes and soft orange fur. Today, Finley hopped into the family car with Mama Fox, Papa Fox, and big sister Faye. The car smelled like warm seats and berry snacks. The seat belts felt cool and smooth. The sun made little sparkles on the dashboard.

Click, click! Everyone buckled up.

“I want the window button!” Finley said, wiggling in his car seat. He loved to press the window button and feel the soft wind.

Faye shook her head. “It’s my turn,” she said. “I didn’t get a turn last time.”

Finley’s ears drooped. “But I want it now,” he said, his tail twitching.

Mama Fox turned the key. The car hummed. “We can take turns,” Mama said in a calm voice. “Taking turns is fair, and it keeps our car time happy.”

“What does fair feel like?” Finley asked, tilting his head.

“Fair feels like calm and smiles,” Papa Fox said. “Like warm toast in your tummy.”

Finley thought about warm toast. He liked that feeling.

The car rolled down the road. Trees swished by. Finley heard birds chirping. He smelled pine and something sweet from a bakery.

Faye pressed the window button. The window slid down with a soft zzz. A gentle breeze brushed Finley’s nose. “Ahh,” Faye said. “It feels nice.”

Finley reached toward the button. “My turn now?”

“Let’s make a plan,” Mama said. “A turn for Faye for one song. Then a turn for Finley for one song. We listen. We wait. We trade. That is called negotiation.”

“Ne-go-shay-shun,” Finley tried the big word. It sounded bouncy.

Papa turned on music. A happy song played. Finley bobbed his head, but he watched the button. He felt wiggly. Waiting was hard.

Finley took a deep breath. He rubbed the seat belt ribbon between his paws. It felt smooth and safe. He looked at Faye.

“You can have the first song,” Finley said. “I can wait.”

Faye smiled. “Thanks, Finley. I will give you the next song.”

The song ended with a ding. “Turn time,” Papa said.

Faye pressed the window button up. Zzz. Then she moved her paw back. “Your turn,” she said.

Finley’s face went bright. He pressed the button gently. Zzz. The window went down a little. Fresh air tickled his whiskers. He giggled. “It smells like bread!” he said. “And leaves!”

“Good sharing,” Mama said. “Windows are for grown-ups to watch, but we can take turns together when it’s safe. Hands stay away when the car is moving fast or near other cars.”

Finley nodded. “Safe turns.”

Soon, the car stopped at a light. The red light glowed like a cherry. “At red lights, we can switch turns,” Papa said. “Green means go. Red means stop. Safety first.”

The light turned green. The car hummed on. Finley let go of the button. “I can wait,” he said softly. He felt proud.

After a while, Finley wanted to play with the soft music knob. “My turn for the music?” he asked.

Faye looked at him. “I like this song.”

Finley’s ears went flat for a moment. He felt a grumpy bubble in his tummy. He wanted to poke the knob. He wanted it now.

Mama noticed. “Finley, let’s use our words. Tell Faye how you feel. Ask, and then listen.”

Finley took a breath. “Faye, I feel grumpy,” he said. “I want a turn with the music knob.”

Faye nodded. “Thank you for telling me. Let’s make a deal,” she said. “I keep this song. Then you pick the next one. We take turns.”

Finley thought. He pictured warm toast again. He nodded. “Deal.”

They bumped paws in a gentle paw-bump. That felt good.

The song ended. Faye smiled. “Your turn.”

Finley turned the knob a tiny bit. Soft. Louder. Soft again. He picked a bouncy song with drums that went boom-boom. He danced in his seat, but his seat belt stayed snug. Click, click.

Soon, the car turned into the bakery lot. Papa parked. The car grew quiet except for a little tick tick from the engine.

“Before we unbuckle, let’s share our turns,” Mama said. “Who had turns?”

“Faye had the window, then Finley had the window,” Faye said.

“I got the music, then Faye gets next music,” Finley added.

Papa smiled. “You both waited. You both asked. You both listened. That is taking turns. That is negotiation. That is tolerance—being patient and kind when you want something too.”

Finley felt the warm-toast feeling for real now, because the bakery was making toast! The air smelled yummy.

He looked at Faye. “You can pick the first pastry,” he said. “I will wait.”

Faye’s tail swished. “Thank you. You can pick the second.”

Mama opened the door. Cool air brushed their fur. “Safe steps,” she said. “Hands to ourselves. We walk, not run.”

Finley hopped down carefully. He held Mama’s paw and looked both ways. The parking lot was busy. Cars beeped softly, and people talked. Finley kept close. Safety felt like holding paws.

Inside, the baker fox waved. “One at a time,” the baker said. “Who is first?”

Faye stepped up. “I will take a berry bun.”

Finley waited. It was hard, but he did it. Then he said, “I will take a honey roll, please.”

The buns were warm and sticky-sweet. They took little bites. “Mmm,” Finley said. “Taking turns tastes sweet too.”

Mama laughed. “Taking turns keeps peace in our car and in our hearts.”

Finley nodded. “I like our plan. We listen. We wait. We trade. We stay safe.”

He licked a honey drip and smiled. He had learned something big in their small car.

The End.
