# Rooftop Rainbow Recycle Day

Pip was a small, determined rabbit with soft gray fur and bright, curious eyes. One sunny morning, Pip hopped up the stairs to the rooftop garden. The rooftop felt warm under his paws. A gentle breeze made the wind chimes tinkle, and pots of mint and tomatoes smelled fresh and sweet. Pip wiggled his whiskers. Today felt important.

“I will be a helper,” Pip said. “I will keep our rooftop clean.”

Neighbors came to the rooftop, too. Mrs. Sato brought a tiny blue watering can. Mr. Diego carried a drum. Amina wore a bright yellow scarf that fluttered in the wind. Theo had a book about birds. Everyone smiled at Pip.

“Hello!” Pip said. “I am cleaning today.”

Amina pointed to three big bins by the door. One bin was blue, one was green, and one was black. The labels were clear. The blue bin said RECYCLING. The green bin said COMPOST. The black bin said TRASH.

“We all use the bins,” Amina said kindly. “We listen to the labels.”

Pip’s ears stood tall. “I can listen,” he said.

Mr. Diego tapped his drum softly. Dum, dum. “We sort together,” he said. “It is our responsibility.”

Pip looked around. Oh no! There were crinkly bottles on the bench, paper cups by the plants, and orange peels near the chairs. A little wind rolled a plastic bottle across the floor. It went bump, bump, bump.

“I will fix this,” Pip said. He picked up a glass jar. It felt cool and smooth. “Blue bin?” he asked.

Theo opened his bird book to a page with a blue jay. “Blue for recycling,” he said. “Listen to the label, Pip.”

Pip listened. He read the word RECYCLING on the blue bin. He dropped the glass jar inside. Clink!

“That sounded happy,” Pip said.

Mrs. Sato held up a paper cup. “This one?” she asked.

Pip thought. He felt the cup. It was soft and bendy. He looked at the sign. Paper can be recycled. But this cup had a little food on it.

Amina nodded. “Paper that is clean goes in recycling. If it is dirty, we use trash or compost. We listen to the story of the item.”

Pip sniffed. The cup smelled like soup. “Not clean,” he said. “Black bin.”

Plop! Into the black bin it went.

Pip found orange peels. The peels smelled sweet and tangy. “Green bin?” he asked.

“Yes,” Mrs. Sato said. “Compost helps the garden. It becomes food for the soil.”

Pip smiled. He liked helping the dirt. He dropped the peels into the green bin. Thump.

Soon, Pip moved fast and sure. Tin can? Blue bin. Banana peel? Green bin. Broken straw? Black bin. His ears wiggled as he worked. His paws felt the shapes. His nose smelled the smells. He listened, read, and always checked.

But then Pip saw a shiny thing—the beautiful cover from a candy wrapper. It was sparkly red and gold. “Pretty!” Pip said. He slipped it into the blue bin.

“Wait,” Amina said softly. “Let’s look together.”

Pip paused. He felt a little hot inside his ears. “Did I make a mistake?”

Theo smiled. “We learn by listening,” he said.

They looked at the wrapper. It crinkled loudly. Amina showed Pip a small chart on the wall, with pictures and words. “Some wrappers are not recyclable,” she explained. “They go in the trash.”

Pip nodded. He took a deep breath. “Thank you for helping,” he said. He moved the wrapper to the black bin. “It’s okay to fix mistakes.”

Mr. Diego tapped his drum. Dum, da-dum. “Good listening, Pip.”

A gentle rain cloud passed by and sprinkled tiny drops. The rooftop smelled like wet mint. Pip giggled as cool speckles touched his nose. Plip, plip, plip. The wind chimes sang again. Tinkle, tinkle.

Neighbors from many places worked together. Mrs. Sato folded a cardboard box flat. Pip slid it into the blue bin. Amina clipped dry leaves and put them into the green bin. Theo read a page about a bird that makes nests with little bits of string.

“We all bring different ideas,” Amina said. “That is our diversity. We share, we listen, and we care for our home.”

Pip’s heart felt big. He looked at the clean rooftop. The benches were neat. The floor was clear. The garden looked bright.

Just then, a new neighbor climbed the stairs. It was Mei, holding a juice box. “Hi,” Mei said shyly. “Where do I put this?”

Pip looked at the juice box. It had a straw, and a tiny bit of juice inside. He thought about the labels. He listened to what he learned. “First, we take out the straw,” he said. He pulled the straw and put it in the black bin. “Then we check the box. Our chart says some juice boxes are not recyclable here. This one goes in the trash.”

Mei smiled. “Thank you for showing me.”

“We can all learn,” Pip said. “We help each other.”

They finished sorting. The blue bin had clean paper, metal, and glass. The green bin had peels and leaves. The black bin had only a little trash. Mr. Diego played a happy rhythm. Amina poured mint tea for the grown-ups and water for the little ones. Pip nibbled a crunchy carrot stick.

“We did it,” Pip said. His whiskers twitched with pride.

Theo pointed to a rainbow stretching over the city. “Look,” he said. “A rooftop rainbow!”

Everyone cheered. Pip’s heart glowed like the rainbow. “I learned to listen to the labels,” he said. “I learned to ask for help. We used our different ideas. We took responsibility.”

Amina nodded. “When we recycle, we show love for our home and our world.”

Pip stood tall on his strong bunny feet. “I am a determined rabbit,” he said. “I can listen. I can recycle. I can help.”

The rooftop felt warm and safe. The bins were in their places, ready for next time. The wind chimes sang a soft good-bye song. Tinkle, tinkle.

Pip looked at his friends. “Let’s keep caring, together,” he said.

“Yes,” everyone agreed.

The End.
