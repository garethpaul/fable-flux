# Benny Beaver’s Study Corner Surprise

Benny was a busy beaver with bright eyes and strong, helpful paws. He had a cozy study corner in his home. His desk smelled like fresh wood. His chair was soft and squeaky. Sunlight made warm squares on the floor.

Today, Benny had a big plan. “I will build a Story Box,” he said, patting his tail. “I will use it to share our family stories.” His grandma had told him stories about old river days, and Benny wanted to keep those stories safe.

Benny opened a little box of smooth wooden sticks. He read the directions card, which smelled like paper and pine glue. “Step 1: Sort the sticks by size. Step 2: Stack the short ones. Step 3: Add glue. Step 4: Let it dry.” The words were simple. The picture showed a neat box.

Benny nodded. “I can do that,” he whispered. He lined up the sticks. Tap, tap, tap went his paws on the desk. He hummed a small song. His whiskers twitched with joy.

Knock, knock! His friend Mia Mouse peeked in. “Hi! What are you doing, Benny?”

“I’m building a Story Box,” Benny said. “I want to share our family tradition. Grandma tells stories on Friday nights.”

“Oh! I love stories,” Mia squeaked. “Can I help?”

“Yes,” Benny said. “But we need to follow the directions.”

Mia washed her tiny paws. Benny tied on a small apron. Together they looked at the card again.

“Step 1,” Benny read slowly. “Sort the sticks by size.”

Mia squeaked, “Small, medium, big!” They sorted. The small sticks made a little click sound. The big sticks went clack.

“Step 2: Stack the short ones,” Benny read. He stacked carefully. One, two, three, four. Then—oops! The stack slid and fell. Clatter!

Benny’s ears drooped. “Oh no,” he said softly.

Mia put a gentle paw on his arm. “It’s okay, Benny. Let’s try again.”

Benny took a deep breath. In… and out. “We can be patient,” he said. “We can keep trying.” He wiped a tiny dab of glue from the desk. It smelled sweet and sticky.

They tried again. One, two, three, four. The stack stayed. “Step 3: Add glue,” Benny read. “Just a small dot.” He squeezed too hard. Squish! Glue blobbed out.

Mia giggled. “That’s a big dot!”

Benny smiled. “I will try again with a little squeeze.” He wiped the extra with a cloth. This time, he used a small dot. Plip! Perfect.

They added a frame of medium sticks. Tap, press, hold. Tap, press, hold. Their paws made soft thumps. The glue felt cool on their fingers.

“Step 4: Let it dry,” Benny read. He looked at the clock. “We wait.”

Waiting was hard. Benny wiggled in his squeaky chair. “I want to hurry,” he said.

Mia shook her head kindly. “Directions say wait. We can tell a story while we wait.”

So they took turns. Mia told a tiny tale about a lost blueberry and a friendly bird. Benny told a story about a brave fish who found his way home. Their voices were calm and warm. The room felt safe and happy.

Grandma Beaver knocked on the door. Knock, knock. “Hello, little builders,” she said. Her voice was slow and smooth. She wore her red scarf. It smelled like cinnamon tea.

“We are making a Story Box,” Benny said. “We are following directions. We are being patient.”

Grandma smiled. “That is our tradition. We share stories. We listen. We try again when it is hard.” She patted Benny’s head. “Persistence is when you keep going.”

Benny nodded. “I kept going when the sticks fell. I kept going when I squeezed too much glue.”

They checked the box. The glue was dry. The edges were neat. “It worked!” Benny cheered. He and Mia clapped. Clap, clap, clap! Their tails swished with joy.

“Now,” said Grandma, “let’s add a lid.” She showed them a simple hinge. “First, measure. Then, line it up. Then, press.” Benny copied each step. Mia held the sticks steady. They worked together. Cooperation felt good, like a warm blanket.

Soon the lid fit with a soft click. Benny opened the box. He placed a small, smooth pebble inside. “This pebble is from the river where you taught me to swim,” he told Grandma.

Grandma’s eyes shone. “Thank you, Benny. Tonight, we will tell that story.”

Benny put in a page with the family recipe for pine tea. He put in a tiny drawing of their dam. He put in a little note that said, “Keep trying.”

Mia smiled. “Your Story Box is full of love.”

Benny took a deep breath. The room smelled like wood and tea. The light felt warm on his fur. He felt proud and calm. “I learned something today,” he said. “When I follow directions, when I work with a friend, and when I keep trying, I can do big things.”

Grandma nodded. “That is the heart of our stories.”

That night, they all sat in the study corner. They opened the Story Box with a gentle click. Grandma began, “Once, by a bright river…” Benny and Mia listened, cozy and happy. They had built more than a box. They had built a memory.

The End.
