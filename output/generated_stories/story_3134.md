# Ella the Rooftop Helper

One sunny morning, a gentle elephant calf named Ella climbed the safe stairs to the flat rooftop garden. The rooftop smelled like warm bread from the bakery below. A little breeze tickled Ella’s ears, and she heard pigeons coo, coo, coo. The sky was bright and blue. The roof was soft with green planters and a tiny sandbox. Ella loved this place.

“I can do it,” Ella whispered, tapping her chest with her trunk. Today, she wanted to water the rooftop plants all by herself. She wanted to be independent.

Ella found a small blue watering can. It felt cool and smooth. She checked the spout. “Hello, spout,” she giggled. She tested the weight. It was a little heavy, but her trunk was strong. “I can try. I can learn.”

Just then, two friends arrived. Pip the pigeon flapped down, and Mia the mouse peeked from behind a flower pot.

“Hi, Ella!” Pip cooed. “Want help?”

“Thank you,” Ella said kindly. “I want to try first. I want to do this on my own.”

Mia nodded. “We’ll cheer you on. Go, Ella!”

Ella walked slowly to the water tap. Drip, drip. She turned the handle with the tip of her trunk. The water hissed and splashed into the can. Splish-splash! It felt cold and made tiny bubbles that tickled her toes. Ella smiled. “I did step one.”

She lifted the can. Oof! Heavy. She took small steps. One, two, three. She wobbled. The can tilted. A little water sloshed onto the tiles. Splash!

“It’s okay,” Ella told herself. “I can make a plan.”

She set the can down and took a breath. She thought of a strategy. Ella looked around. There was a little wagon by the sandbox. It had a long handle.

“I have an idea,” Ella said. “Strategy time! I can put the can in the wagon. Then I can pull it.”

Pip clapped his wings. “Smart plan!”

Mia squeaked, “Try it, Ella!”

Ella gently lifted the can into the wagon. Clink! It fit just right. She tested the handle with her trunk. She pulled. The wagon rolled with a quiet rrrroll. Easy and safe.

“I’m doing it,” Ella said. She smiled big. “I am independent.”

As she watered the first pot, a tiny ant marched up. “Excuse me,” the ant said. “Please pour slowly. Our ant home is close.”

Ella listened. “Thank you for telling me.” She tilted the can just a little. Drip, drip, drip. The soil drank the water happily, and the ant bowed. “Fair and kind,” Ella whispered.

Soon, a new problem came. Two kid pigeons, Tiko and Tika, swooped down. They started pecking at the biggest strawberry plant. Peck, peck! They grabbed berries that weren’t ready. The plant shook.

“That’s not fair,” Mia said. “The berries are for everyone when they are ripe.”

Ella felt a flutter in her belly. She wanted to shout, but she remembered: calm words first. She took a breath. She stood tall, but gentle. “Let’s solve this,” she said softly. “We can be fair.”

Ella walked over. “Hello, Tiko and Tika,” she said in a warm voice. “Please stop pecking the baby berries. It’s not fair to the plant or to others who are waiting.”

Tiko puffed. “But we are hungry now!”

Ella nodded. “I hear you. Hunger is real. Let’s make a plan that is just and kind.”

She looked around and made another strategy. “See those ripe berries with red cheeks? We can pick two ripe berries to share today. Then we wait for more to grow. And we can eat the seeds in the seed dish over there too. That way, everyone gets a turn.”

Tika blinked. “A turn?”

“Yes,” Ella said. “Fair means everyone has a turn. We take what is ready. We leave what is not. That is justice.”

Pip bobbed his head. “That’s fair.”

Mia added, “And we can make a chart!” She drew little marks in the sand: Tiko, Tika, Pip, Mia, Ella. “Two berries today, and seeds for snack.”

Tiko and Tika looked at the chart. They looked at the red berries. They looked at Ella’s kind eyes.

“Okay,” Tiko said. “We will try.”

“Thank you,” Ella said, smiling. “You chose fairness.”

They picked two ripe berries. The sweet smell filled the rooftop. Mmm! The juice was bright and sticky. Everyone had a small taste. It felt good to share.

Ella went back to watering. Drip, drip, drip. She pulled the wagon around each plant. She checked leaves. She patted soil. She hummed a little tune. The sun felt warm on her back. The breeze felt cool on her ears. She felt proud.

When the last pot was watered, Ella turned off the tap all by herself. Click! She put the can away. She swept up spilled soil with a tiny broom. Swish, swish. She looked around. The rooftop was neat and happy.

Mia cheered. “Ella, you did it!”

Pip cooed, “You were brave, fair, and smart.”

Ella’s heart felt big and light. “I tried. I made plans. I used calm words. I did my work.”

Tiko and Tika fluttered close. “Thank you for being kind,” they said. “We learned to wait and share.”

Ella nodded. “We all learned. Independence means I try to do things by myself. Justice means we are fair. When there is a problem, we use calm words and a plan. That is conflict-resolution.”

They all sat together and watched the sun paint the clouds gold. The rooftop smelled like leaves and sweet berries. Ella wrapped her trunk around the broom and smiled.

“I can do it,” she whispered again, “and I can help others do it too.”

The End.
