# Timmy Turtle Takes a Slow Breath

In a sunny backyard, behind some tall sunflowers, there was a small garden shed. The shed smelled like fresh soil and lemon soap. Inside lived Timmy, a shy turtle with a smooth green shell. Timmy liked quiet. He liked the soft scratch of straw on the floor and the tiny tick-tick of rain on the roof.

One morning, Timmy peeked out of his shell. “Hello, shed,” he whispered. He listened to the breeze hum and the birds chirp. He was excited for his snack. He had saved a crunchy carrot, a juicy slice of tomato, and a handful of leafy greens. “A rainbow lunch,” he said, smiling. “Good food helps me grow strong.”

Timmy placed his snack on a little leaf plate. But just then—squeak, squeak—Milo the mouse ran by, chasing a rolling acorn. The acorn bumped Timmy’s leaf plate. The carrot tumbled. The tomato slid. SPLAT!

“My snack!” Timmy squeaked. His face felt hot. His belly felt tight and twisty. He scrunched his nose. “I’m mad!”

Milo froze. “I’m sorry, Timmy! I didn’t see your plate,” Milo said, his whiskers drooping.

Timmy’s heart thumped thump-thump. He wanted to shout. He wanted to stamp his little feet. He pulled into his shell, and in the quiet space, he remembered what Grandma Turtle had taught him.

“When mad comes like a storm, take a slow breath,” Grandma had said. “Smell a flower. Blow a bubble. Use gentle words.”

Timmy peeked out. He looked at the mess. He looked at Milo’s worried eyes. He put one hand on his belly.

“Smell a flower,” Timmy whispered. He breathed in slowly through his nose. Sniff. The shed smelled like mint leaves and damp wood.

“Blow a bubble,” he said. He breathed out slowly through his mouth. Fffff. He pretended a shiny bubble floated up, up, up to the rafters.

He did it again. Sniff. Fffff. His belly felt softer. The hot feeling cooled. The storm in his chest got quiet.

Milo squeaked, “Are you still mad?”

Timmy nodded a little. “I was very mad,” he said softly. “But I can use gentle words. Please be careful next time.”

“I will,” said Milo. “Can I help clean up?”

“Yes,” said Timmy. “Let’s do it together.”

They picked up the carrot. It had rolled under a small shovel. They wiped the tomato with a clean leaf. The greens were still good. Timmy smiled. “This food helps our bodies,” he said. “Carrots help our eyes. Tomatoes help our hearts. Greens help us be strong.”

Milo’s eyes shone. “Can I have a nibble?” he asked.

“Yes,” Timmy said. “We can share.”

They sat on a soft patch of straw. Crunch, munch, slurp. The carrot was sweet and crisp. The tomato was juicy and cool. The greens tasted like sunshine and rain. Timmy’s tummy felt happy again.

After the snack, they saw a small plastic cup on the floor. It had rolled out of a box. “This doesn’t belong on the ground,” Timmy said. He picked it up. “We take care of our home.”

Milo nodded. “We can sort it,” he said. He pointed to three jars: one for recycling, one for compost, and one for trash. The jars had little drawings on them: arrows for recycling, a leaf for compost, and a black square for trash.

Timmy dropped the plastic cup into the recycling jar. Clink. He brushed tiny tomato seeds into the compost jar. Plop. They folded the leaf plate and put it in compost too. “Waste less, help more,” Timmy said. He liked how the words felt.

Milo looked shy. “Will you teach me the calm breath?” he asked.

Timmy nodded. “Let’s practice.”

They stood tall. “Smell a flower,” Timmy said. They both sniffed. “Blow a bubble,” he said. They both blew. Sniff. Fffff. Sniff. Fffff. The air felt soft and gentle. The angry storm was gone.

Suddenly, the wind whooshed the shed door open. Bang! The broom fell over. Thunk! Milo jumped. Timmy felt a little spark of mad again. He put his hand on his belly. Sniff. Fffff. His shoulders dropped.

“I can handle big feelings,” Timmy said. “I can use my breath. I can use my words.”

Milo smiled. “You’re good at that,” he said.

Timmy blushed inside his shell. “I am learning,” he said. “We can all learn.”

They set the broom back up. They straightened the jars. They made a new leaf plate so they could have a tiny dessert: a slice of apple they found in the fruit basket. They shared it, crunching happily.

Timmy looked around the tidy shed. It smelled fresh and sweet. The sun made a warm square on the floor. He felt proud. He had been mad, but he had calmed down. He had used gentle words. He had eaten good food. He had cared for the shed.

“Thank you, Milo,” Timmy said. “For helping.”

“Thank you, Timmy,” said Milo. “For teaching.”

Timmy tucked into his shell, cozy and calm. “When mad comes like a storm,” he whispered, “I can smell a flower, blow a bubble, and use kind words.”

Outside, the birds sang a soft song. Inside, the shed was peaceful and neat.

The End.
