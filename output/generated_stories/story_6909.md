# Otty and the Whispering Attic

Otty was a playful otter with soft whiskers and bright, curious eyes. One sunny morning, Otty climbed the creaky stairs to a quiet attic. The air felt cool and dusty. Sunbeams slipped through a small window and made golden stripes on the floor. Otty’s paws made little pat-pat sounds on the wood.

“Hello, attic,” Otty whispered. “I’m here to play.”

The attic was filled with boxes, books, and a tiny desk. A small fan hummed, mmmm, like a sleepy bee. Otty found a box of paper and colorful crayons. “Oh! I can draw,” Otty said with a giggle. “I can write a note, too.”

Just then, a soft meow came from behind a trunk. A shy gray cat peeked out with wide green eyes. Her tail curled like a question mark.

“Hi,” Otty said gently. “I’m Otty.”

The cat blinked. “I am Moona,” she whispered. “I like quiet. Loud hurts my ears.”

Otty nodded. “I like to play. Sometimes I get excited and make big sounds. I don’t want to hurt your ears.”

Moona’s whiskers twitched. “Thank you for saying that.”

Otty looked around. There was also a little toy drum and a tin bell. Otty’s paws itched to tap. But Otty remembered Moona’s words. Loud hurts my ears.

Otty took a deep breath in, and out. “Let’s make soft music,” Otty said. “We can be gentle.”

Moona smiled a tiny smile. “Soft is nice.”

Otty tapped the drum with just one finger. Tip… tip… tip. The sound was quiet, like rain on leaves. Moona’s ears relaxed. Otty shook the bell very slowly. Ding… ding… ding. The sounds floated in the sunbeams.

Moona purred. “I like that.”

Otty felt warm inside. “I’m glad,” Otty said. “When we listen to each other, we can both feel good.”

They played a little more. Then, Otty stepped on a loose board. It squeaked—eeek! Moona jumped and hid behind a box.

Otty stopped. “Oh no,” Otty said softly. “Moona, are you okay?”

A small voice answered, “I feel scared.”

Otty sat down and kept very still. “Thank you for telling me. I can help. I will walk slow. I will watch my steps.”

Moona peeked out again. Her eyes were softer now. “Thank you,” she said.

Otty had an idea. “Let’s make attic rules together,” Otty said. “Rules can help us remember to be kind.”

Moona tilted her head. “How?”

“We can write them down,” Otty said, tapping the box of paper. “I like writing. We can make a list.”

Otty wrote with a blue crayon. The crayon smelled a little like wax. The paper felt smooth. Otty said each word slowly so Moona could hear.

“Rule One: Use soft sounds.”

Moona nodded. “Yes.”

Otty wrote more. “Rule Two: Tell how you feel.”

Moona took the purple crayon in her paw. “Rule Three: Ask for help,” she said.

Otty smiled big. “That is a good one.”

They put the paper on the tiny desk. Otty drew a little heart at the top. “This is our kindness list,” Otty said.

After a while, Otty wanted to roll a ball. The ball was bright red and bouncy. “Moona,” Otty asked, “can we roll the ball gently?”

Moona took a breath. “I want to say something,” she said. “I feel worried about fast rolling.”

Otty listened. Listening felt like holding a gift. “Thank you for telling me,” Otty said. “We can roll it slow. You can say ‘stop’ anytime.”

Moona’s tail uncurled. “Okay. Let’s try.”

They rolled the ball back and forth. Tap… roll… tap. The ball made a soft shuff sound on the floor. Moona giggled. Otty giggled too. It felt good to play in a way that was safe for both.

Soon, a small breeze came through the window. It smelled like dry wood and sunshine. The fan went mmmm again. Otty felt peaceful.

Then, a new sound rustled: scratch, scratch, scratch. A little mouse peeked out from a crack. The mouse held a tiny piece of ribbon.

“Hello,” said the mouse. “I like ribbons. Can I play?”

Otty looked at Moona. Moona looked at Otty. Otty asked gently, “Moona, how do you feel?”

Moona took a moment. “I feel curious. I also feel a bit nervous.”

Otty nodded. “Thank you for telling us. Mouse, we have rules. Soft sounds. Tell how you feel. Ask for help.”

The mouse’s ears perked up. “I can do that,” he said. “I feel happy. I can play soft.”

The three new friends tied the ribbon to the ball and made a slow, swishy game. Swish… roll… swish. When the ribbon got stuck under a box, Otty said, “We have a problem.”

Moona said, “Let’s think.”

The mouse said, “We can pull gently together.”

They counted, “One, two, three.” Pull… whoosh! The ribbon slid free. They cheered in whispers, “Yay!”

Otty clapped softly. “We solved the problem with gentle hands and gentle voices.”

Moona purred again. “We did it together.”

The sun moved, and the golden stripes on the floor stretched long. It was time to go. Otty looked at the kindness list on the desk.

“Today I learned,” Otty said, “that listening is love. When we care about feelings, we can play in a way that feels good for everyone.”

Moona touched the paper with her paw. “I can say what I need.”

The mouse nodded. “I can ask for help.”

Otty smiled. “And I can be gentle and kind.”

They said goodbye with quiet pats and soft smiles. Otty padded down the creaky stairs, feeling proud, warm, and light.

The End.
