# Fair Play on the Rooftop

Tiko was a gentle elephant calf with soft gray ears and big kind eyes. One sunny morning, Tiko climbed the wide stairs to the building’s rooftop garden. The air felt warm and sweet. A soft breeze tickled Tiko’s ears. He could hear city pigeons cooing, “Coo, coo,” and smell basil, mint, and tiny tomatoes growing in boxes.

“Good morning, rooftop,” Tiko said, swishing his little tail. “I am here to water the plants.”

Gardener Bee, a cheerful teacher with a straw hat, waved. “Hello, Tiko. Today we share. Everyone gets a turn with the watering can. We water every box until they sparkle.”

Tiko nodded. “I can share.”

On the rooftop were Tiko’s friends: Pip the puppy, Lala the lamb, and Miko the monkey. A shiny red watering can sat in the middle. Sunlight made it glow. The water inside went swish-swish when the wind moved it.

“I saw it first,” said Miko, hopping close.

“I touched it first,” barked Pip, tapping it with his paw.

“I need it first,” bleated Lala, pointing at her dry lettuce.

Tiko’s ears drooped. The friends’ voices sounded sharp and prickly. He felt a tight squeeze in his tummy. “We can all have turns,” he said softly.

Miko grabbed the handle. Lala grabbed the spout. Pip tugged the side. The can wobbled. Water splashed on the warm tiles—splish, splash. A little stream ran toward the tiny tomato plants.

“Stop!” cried Gardener Bee. “The water is for the plants, not the floor. We will be fair. Fair means everyone gets the same chance. That is justice.”

“Justice?” Tiko asked. The word felt big and important on his tongue.

“Yes,” said Gardener Bee. “Justice is making sure we treat each other right. No one gets more just because they are fast or loud. We listen, we plan, and we take turns.”

Tiko took a deep breath. The wind smelled like wet leaves now. He tapped the tile with one small foot and spoke gently, “Friends, let us make a plan. We can line up.”

Pip’s ears perked. “A line?”

“First, we choose the order,” Tiko said. “We use our words. Not grabbing. Not yelling.”

Miko frowned. “But I want the first turn.”

Tiko nodded slowly. “I know you want it. Lala wants it too. Pip wants it too. I want it too. Let’s be fair.”

Gardener Bee smiled. “How will you choose, Tiko?”

Tiko thought. He listened to the pigeons—coo, coo—and watched a cloud float by like a soft white pillow. He felt the cool shade on his back. Then he said, “We can use a counting rhyme. We point to each friend. The last word chooses who starts. Then we go in a circle. Everyone waters one box. We keep going until all boxes shine.”

“That sounds fair,” Lala said, her voice like a gentle bell.

Pip wagged his tail. “I like rhymes!”

Miko’s tail drooped. “What if I am not first?”

Tiko smiled kindly. “If you are not first, you are still part of the plan. Justice means we wait and we still get a turn.”

Miko looked at the wet tiles. He took a breath. “Okay. Let’s try.”

Tiko made up a simple rhyme, tapping softly with his foot:
“Drip, drop, rain will fall,
We take turns, one and all.”

He pointed to Lala on “drip,” to Pip on “drop,” to Miko on “rain,” and to himself on “will,” and then back to Lala on “fall.” Lala cheered. “I start!”

“Good,” said Gardener Bee. “Tiko, you will be the helper and checker. Make sure we water all the boxes and keep the can full. That is the task today.”

Tiko stood tall. He felt a warm glow of pride. “I can do this,” he said.

Lala lifted the can carefully. “I will water lettuce.” The water made a soft shhhh sound, and the leaves wiggled.

“Great job,” Tiko said. “Next is Pip.”

Pip watered the basil. “It smells spicy!” he barked, nose twitching.

“Nice work,” Tiko said. “Next is Miko.”

Miko watered the tomato plants—glug, glug. He smiled a little. “The drops look like tiny jewels.”

Tiko took his turn last and watered the mint. The cool smell made his trunk tingle. He checked every box. “We still have the strawberry box,” he said. “We are not done yet.”

“Let’s finish our task,” Gardener Bee said.

They went around again—Lala, Pip, Miko, Tiko—until each box was wet and shiny. The sun warmed their backs. The breeze dried the floor. No more splashes were wasted.

“We did it,” Tiko said. “We were fair. We took turns. We finished the job.”

Miko looked at Tiko. “Thank you for the plan. I did not get first, but I still got a turn.”

Pip wagged. “Justice feels calm.”

Lala nuzzled Tiko’s shoulder. “Justice helps everyone.”

Gardener Bee clapped softly. “You all listened and used kind words. That is conflict-resolution. You made a plan, took turns, and completed the task.”

Tiko’s heart felt happy and light. He lifted his trunk and let out a tiny toot of joy.

On the rooftop, the plants stood tall. The leaves shone. The pigeons cooed, and the clouds drifted by.

Tiko looked at his friends. “When we are fair,” he said, “everyone grows.”

The End.
