# Jump, Joey, Jump!

The sun was warm and bright in the backyard. The grass felt tickly on tiny toes. A joyful kangaroo joey named Kiki bounced near a shiny blue ball. Birds chirped, “Tweet, tweet!” A soft breeze smelled like sweet flowers and fresh dirt.

“I love to jump!” Kiki laughed, her little paws clapping. “I can jump over the ball! I can jump the highest!” Her tail swished like a happy paintbrush.

Kiki’s friends came to play. There was Tia the turtle, slow and steady. There was Pip the puppy, wagging so hard his ears flopped. And there was Momo the mouse, small and quick.

“Let’s play ball,” Pip barked. “I want to roll it!”

“I want to dribble it,” squeaked Momo.

“I want to take it down the slide,” said Tia, blinking kindly.

Kiki hugged the ball. “I had it first,” she said. “Watch me jump over it!” She set the ball down and boing! jumped high. She giggled when her feet landed in the soft grass.

“Can I try?” Pip asked, tail thumping the ground. Thump-thump-thump.

“Me next?” Momo squeaked, whiskers twitching.

“And after that, I’d like a turn,” Tia said slowly.

Kiki shook her head. “No, no. I have so many jumps! I want to jump again! And again!” She bounced and bounced. The ball rolled a little. Pip’s tail drooped. Momo’s whiskers drooped too. Tia looked down at her shell.

Kiki didn’t notice. She was too busy boing, boing, boing!

Then, the ball rolled away faster than she thought. It bumped into a flower pot and wedged under the low garden bench. “Oh!” Kiki gasped. She tried to pull the ball. It wouldn’t budge. “I can get it. I am strong,” Kiki said, taking a deep breath. She tugged with her little paws. Tug-tug. The ball stayed stuck.

Pip stepped closer. “I can help,” he said, tilting his head.

Kiki hugged herself. “I can do it. I’m a big joey,” she said, trying again. She wiggled her tail, set her feet, and pulled. The ball rolled a tiny bit, then stuck again.

Tia peeked under the bench. “Hmm,” she hummed. “It is tight in there.”

Momo crawled low and pressed his nose against the ball. “If I push from this side, will it help?” he asked.

Kiki felt hot tears in her eyes. She wiped them away with her paw. The sun was still warm, but her tummy felt tight. “I wanted to jump,” she whispered. “Now no one can play.”

She sat on the grass and took a slow breath. She looked at her friends’ faces. Pip’s hopeful eyes. Momo’s tiny brave smile. Tia’s calm, kind look. Kiki remembered how it felt when she was waiting at snack time. Waiting was hard, but it made her happy when the snack came to her turn.

Kiki blinked. An idea popped like a bubble. “Maybe,” she said softly, “maybe we can take turns. And maybe we can all work together to get the ball out.”

Pip’s ears perked. “Yes!”

Momo nodded fast. “I like turns!”

Tia smiled. “Taking turns is fair. We can be a team.”

Kiki stood tall. “Okay. First, I will try to pull. Then Pip can pull. Then Tia can nudge. Then Momo can push. We will all try. We will take turns. Ready?”

“Ready!” everyone said.

Kiki wrapped her paws around the ball and pulled. It wiggled. “Your turn, Pip!”

Pip gripped the ball with his paws and tugged. “Rrrr-oll!” It moved more. “Your turn, Tia!”

Tia pressed her shell gently against the bench leg and nudged the ball with her steady head. Creak. “Your turn, Momo!”

Momo scooted under the bench and pushed with his whole tiny body. “Eeeep!” he squeaked, pushing and pushing.

Kiki watched and counted. “One turn, two turns, three turns, four!” She cheered for each friend. “We can do it!”

With a pop, the ball rolled free and bounced into the sunlight. Boing! It made a happy sound on the grass. Everyone laughed. Kiki’s heart felt light, like a bubble in the breeze.

“Thank you,” Kiki said. “I didn’t let you have a turn before. I was not thinking of everyone. I want to try again. Let’s make a turn line.” She drew a little line in the dirt with her paw. “I’ll go first this time because I said the plan. Then Pip, then Momo, then Tia. After that, we start again. Is that fair?”

Pip wagged. “Fair!”

Momo squeaked, “Fair!”

Tia nodded. “Very fair.”

Kiki jumped over the ball one time. Just one. She landed with a smile. “Your turn, Pip!”

Pip rolled the ball with his nose. “Your turn, Momo!”

Momo dribbled the ball with tiny taps. “Your turn, Tia!”

Tia guided the ball to the slide and gave it a little push. Down it zipped—whoosh!—and everyone clapped.

Round and round they went, taking turns. Kiki noticed something new. Waiting gave her time to think of fun ideas. When it wasn’t her turn, she planned a new jump. She felt proud that she could wait and help herself calm down with deep breaths. She felt happy to share.

As the sun began to yawn and stretch, Kiki said, “Today I learned something big.”

“What did you learn?” asked Tia.

Kiki smiled. “Taking turns makes play more fun. Listening helps me learn new ways. And I can calm my body and try again all by myself.”

Her friends nodded. The birds sang a soft evening song. The backyard smelled like warm grass and tiny flowers. Kiki held the ball and passed it to Pip with a happy heart.

“Your turn,” she said.

The End.
