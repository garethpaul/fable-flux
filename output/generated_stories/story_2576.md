# Kiki Koala and the Garden Shed Fix-It Day

Kiki was a calm koala with soft, fluffy ears and a gentle smile. One sunny morning, Kiki blinked at the bright light and took a deep breath. The air smelled like sweet grass and warm wood. “Today,” Kiki whispered, “I will fix my wobbly wagon all by myself.” Kiki liked help, but Kiki also wanted to try being independent.

Kiki’s red wagon went “rattle-rattle” when it rolled. The wheel wobbled and squeaked. “Squeak-squeak!” sang the wheel. Kiki patted it. “We can do this,” Kiki said, calm and brave.

Kiki padded to the garden shed. The shed was cozy and cool. It smelled like soil, leaves, and fresh rain. Sunlight made tiny dust sparkles in the air. Inside the shed were boxes, bins, and shelves. “Lots of useful things,” Kiki said. “I will look and choose what I need.” That was resourceful!

On a low shelf, Kiki found a small toolbox. It was light blue and clicked when it opened. “Click!” Inside were safe, kid-sized tools: a little wrench, a soft cloth, a wooden ruler, and a tiny screwdriver with a round handle. Kiki read the labels on each tool, slowly. “Wrench. Cloth. Ruler. Screwdriver.” Kiki felt proud. Kiki could do this step.

“First, I will check the wheel,” Kiki told the wagon. “I am calm. I can try.” Kiki touched the wheel. It felt dusty. Kiki used the cloth. “Wipe, wipe,” went the soft cloth. The wheel looked clean and shiny.

Kiki heard a rustle. “Need help?” asked Cricket, hopping in the doorway. “I can turn the wrench fast!”

“Thank you, Cricket,” said Kiki, smiling. “I want to try by myself first. If I need help, I will ask.” Kiki was kind and clear. That was self-advocacy.

“Okay! I will cheer,” said Cricket. “Go, Kiki, go!”

Kiki picked up the tiny screwdriver. “This feels just right in my paw,” Kiki said. The screw on the wheel was loose. “Twist, twist,” Kiki whispered. The screwdriver made a soft “tuk-tuk” sound as it turned. The wheel hugged the wagon tighter.

“Hmm,” Kiki hummed. “The wheel still wiggles a little.” Kiki did not give up. Kiki breathed slowly. In and out. Calm and steady.

Kiki looked around the shed again. On a tall shelf sat a box with a green sign: “Recycling.” Inside were useful old things: bottle caps, bits of ribbon, cardboard circles, and a small rubber ring from a jar lid. “These can have new jobs,” Kiki said. “I can use them again.” That was recycling!

Kiki studied the rubber ring. It felt stretchy and soft. “Maybe this ring can help the wobbly wheel,” Kiki said. Kiki measured the ring with the wooden ruler. “It fits!”

Cricket clapped. “Smart and safe!”

Kiki slid the rubber ring between the wheel and the wagon. “Snug,” Kiki said. Kiki tightened the screw again. “Twist, twist.” The wheel sat straight. Kiki rolled the wagon on the shed floor. “Roll… roll…” No wobble. No squeak. Just a happy “whirr.”

“It works!” Kiki laughed, eyes bright. The shed felt even warmer now, like sunshine inside Kiki’s chest.

Just then, Moth fluttered in, wings soft like tissue paper. “I can hold the light if you need it,” said Moth.

“Thank you,” said Kiki. “I finished the fix, and I did it myself. I am proud. I used tools. I reused a ring. And I stayed calm.” Kiki’s voice was gentle and sure.

Cricket hopped high. “Kiki is independent! Kiki is resourceful!” Cricket sang.

Kiki smiled. “Yes. I tried first. I asked for space to try. If I needed help, I would have asked with my words.” Kiki patted the wagon. “Now let’s give the old box a new job too.”

Kiki looked at the cardboard circles. “These can be labels.” Kiki drew a wheel picture on one circle and tied it to a small bin with the ribbon. “This bin is for spare parts,” Kiki said. “This bin is for tools.” Kiki made another label with a picture of a wrench. The shed looked neat and friendly.

Cricket peered at the bins. “Everything has a place!”

Kiki nodded. “When I put things back, I can find them next time. That helps me do things by myself.”

Kiki closed the toolbox with a soft “click.” Kiki placed the rubber ring box back in the recycling bin. “Reusing and putting away are kind to our world,” Kiki said. “They also help me be ready.”

Outside, the breeze smelled like mint from the garden. Kiki pulled the wagon. It rolled smooth and easy. “Whirr,” it went. Kiki giggled and did a tiny hop.

“Ride to the carrot patch?” asked Cricket.

“Yes,” said Kiki. “I will carry carrots in my wagon. I fixed it myself. I feel strong and calm.”

Cricket smiled. “And if you ever need help, I’m here.”

“Thank you,” Kiki said. “I can try first, ask for help when I need it, and keep trying with a calm heart.” Kiki’s voice was soft and brave.

Kiki and Cricket rolled toward the garden. The sun was warm. The wagon was steady. Kiki felt proud, independent, and kind. Kiki learned that trying, thinking, and reusing can solve problems. Being calm and speaking up helped, too.

The End.
