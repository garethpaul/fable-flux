# My Quiet Tail and the Crayon Clock

In a sunny dining room, a kind mouse named Miro peeked over the edge of a big wooden table. The table smelled like warm toast and a little like lemon soap. A blue crayon rolled near a shiny spoon. “Good morning, table,” Miro whispered. His whiskers twitched. “Today I will write my very first list!”

Miro wanted to write a thank-you note to his friend Bea the Bee. But first, he wanted to make a plan, like a little map for the day. “Lists help me think,” Miro said. He climbed up a chair leg and sat by a paper napkin. It was soft and white, with tiny blue dots. “Perfect paper.”

Miro took the blue crayon in both paws. The crayon felt waxy and warm from the sun. He smiled. “I feel excited,” he said. “My body wants to wiggle. I will use my quiet tail.”

Miro wrapped his tail gently around his own waist. It felt like a soft hug. “Quiet tail,” he whispered. “I can be calm.” He took a slow breath in through his nose. He let it out like a tiny breeze. “Ahh.”

He drew a little square on the napkin and said, “Step one: Think.” He tapped the crayon on the table—tap, tap. “What do I need for my note to Bea?” He looked around. Fork. Spoon. Napkin. Cup. “I need words,” Miro said. “I need truth,” he added, because Miro liked honesty. “I can tell Bea the true reasons I am thankful.”

He wrote some wobbly shapes. They were not perfect letters, but they were Miro’s marks. He said them out loud. “Dear Bea. Thank you for the sweet drops of honey.” He smiled, remembering the taste—gold and sticky, bright on his tongue.

Miro felt a wiggle build up again. His feet wanted to dance. His paws wanted to tap-tap-tap. He noticed the feeling. “Hello, wiggles,” he said kindly to himself. “I see you.” He put down the crayon and squeezed his paws together, gently, like holding a baby pea. He counted, “One… two… three.” The wiggles softened. “Thank you, body,” Miro said. “Back to my plan.”

He drew another little square. “Step two: Say it simple.” He tasted the words like strawberries. “Bea, you help me. You buzz and make flowers grow. You share honey.” He made small pictures next to the words: a flower, a honey drop, a tiny bee with a smile.

Something bumped the table leg—thunk! The crayon rolled and made a bright blue line across the napkin and onto the table. Miro froze. His ears grew warm. “Oh no,” he whispered. “I made a mark on the table.”

His heart thumped fast like a tiny drum. He wanted to hide. He wanted to say, “I didn’t do it!” But Miro remembered honesty. He put a paw on his chest and took a deep breath in, and a slow breath out. He looked at the blue line. It was thin and wiggly. “I feel worried,” he said. “I can choose what to do.”

He thought like a problem solver. “What is true?” he asked himself. “I rolled the crayon. The mark is small. The table likes to be clean. I can try to help.” He nodded. Logical thinking, step by step.

Miro called, “Grandma Mouse?” From the other room, soft steps came. Grandma’s fur smelled like cinnamon toast. She peeked into the dining room. “Yes, little seed?”

Miro stood tall. “I made a blue line,” he said. He pointed. “I am sorry. I want to fix it.”

Grandma Mouse looked at the line and then at Miro’s brave face. “Thank you for telling the truth,” she said gently. “That is honest and kind.” She gave him a small wet cloth. It smelled like lemons. “We can wipe together. Slow circles.”

Miro nodded. He felt the cloth, cool and damp. He took another slow breath. “Slow circles,” he echoed. He wiped—round and round. The blue line faded like a cloud after wind. “It’s going away!” he said.

“It is,” said Grandma. “You noticed your feelings. You used your calm breath. You told the truth. That is strong self-control.”

Miro’s whiskers lifted with pride. “Thank you, Grandma.” He climbed back to his napkin. “I will make a crayon clock, so I remember when to write and when to rest.” He drew a circle with twelve tiny crumbs as numbers. He put the spoon at the top and the fork in the middle, like clock hands. “When the spoon points up, I write. When the fork points to me, I rest.”

Grandma smiled. “A clever plan.”

Miro watched the spoon shadow slide as the sun moved. “Spoon up,” he said. He picked up the crayon. He held it gently, like a small bird. He checked his tail—it was still hugging him. He wrote more wobbly words: “You make me brave. Your buzz is music.” He listened. A fly hummed near the window. The hum felt like a soft song in his ears.

His tummy rumbled. “Fork to me,” he said, and he giggled. “Time to rest.” He set the crayon down. He stretched his paws to the sky and shook out tiny wiggles. He took a sip of cool water from a thimble cup. The water tasted like rain.

Then he finished his note with a heart. He signed, “Love, Miro.” He read it out loud. It sounded true and simple. His body felt calm and warm.

Grandma tied the napkin note with a piece of ribbon. “Ready to deliver?”

Miro nodded. “I am ready.” He looked at the clean table, the blue crayon, and his crayon clock. He felt proud. “Today I learned: I can notice my big feelings. I can breathe. I can think in steps. I can tell the truth. And I can write my kind thoughts.”

He and Grandma stepped toward the door, the sun touching their whiskers like gold.

The End.
