# Polly's Paint Problem

Polly the parrot loved bright colors more than anything in the whole world. Her feathers were green and blue and yellow, and she thought colors made everything more beautiful. Today, she was staying in Grandma's cozy guest room, and she had a wonderful idea.

"I want to paint a picture!" Polly squawked happily, flapping her colorful wings.

Grandma had left three paintbrushes and three cups of paint on the small wooden table by the window. There was red paint that looked like strawberries, blue paint that looked like the sky, and yellow paint that looked like sunshine.

Polly hopped over to the table, her tiny claws clicking on the wood. The warm sunlight streamed through the window, making the paint cups sparkle. But just as she reached for all three brushes at once, she heard a soft knock on the door.

"Hello!" called a gentle voice. "May we come in?"

Two little mice peeked around the door. Their names were Pip and Dot, and they had been watching Polly through the keyhole.

"We love to paint too!" squeaked Pip, his whiskers twitching with excitement.

"Can we paint with you?" asked Dot, her tiny paws clasped together hopefully.

Polly looked at the three brushes, then at the two mice, then back at the brushes. Her first thought was to say no. She wanted to use all the colors herself! But then she noticed how sad the mice looked, and she remembered something important.

"Wait," Polly said, tilting her bright head to one side. "Let me think about this."

She looked around the sunny guest room and used her logical thinking. There were three brushes and three friends. That meant each friend could have one brush! And there were three colors, so they could each try a different color first.

"I have an idea!" Polly announced proudly. "We can take turns! I'll use the red brush first, Pip can use the blue brush, and Dot can use the yellow brush. Then we can switch!"

The mice's eyes lit up like tiny stars. "That sounds perfect!" they squeaked together.

Polly felt warm and happy inside. She realized that sharing would make painting even more fun than doing it alone.

They each picked up their brushes. Polly dipped hers in the red paint and painted a beautiful flower. The paint felt smooth and cool on the paper. Pip painted blue waves that looked like a flowing river. Dot painted a bright yellow sun with cheerful rays.

"Now let's switch!" Polly called out cheerfully.

They passed their brushes around in a circle. Polly tried the blue paint and added a pretty sky to her flower. Pip used the yellow paint to add sunshine to his river. Dot used the red paint to paint lovely roses by the water.

After they switched one more time, they all stepped back to look at their paintings. Each picture had all three colors, and they were the most beautiful paintings any of them had ever seen!

"Taking turns made our pictures even better!" Polly realized, puffing out her chest with pride.

"And it was more fun to paint together," added Pip.

"Thank you for sharing, Polly," said Dot. "You're a wonderful friend."

Polly felt proud of herself for thinking of a solution that made everyone happy. She learned that taking turns didn't mean less fun – it meant more friends, more ideas, and more joy to share.

From that day on, whenever Polly had something special, she always remembered to think about how she could share it with others.

The End.
