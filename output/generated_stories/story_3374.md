# Ollie Otter’s Balcony Helpers

Ollie was a playful otter with bright eyes and soft, shiny fur. He lived with Nana on the seventh floor, where a small balcony garden grew. The garden smelled like mint and soil. It sounded busy with tiny buzz-buzz bees and soft wind shhh across leaves.

Each morning, Nana said, “Good morning, garden,” and Ollie wiggled his whiskers. Water drops sparkled like stars on the leaves. Basil, mint, cherry tomatoes, and little flowers lived there. Ollie loved to pat the smooth pots and watch ladybugs crawl.

One sunny day, Nana said, “Ollie, we have a routine. A routine is our daily plan. We do our chores to help the garden and each other.”

Ollie bounced. “I can help! I am a helper!”

Nana smiled. “We are helpers together. Our balcony garden is our small world. We share it with bees, birds, sun, and rain. When we help here, we also help our big world.”

Ollie’s tail swished. “How?”

Nana pointed. “Today’s chores are: sweep the balcony floor, water the plants, and share the ripe tomatoes fairly. Fair means everyone gets what they need.”

Ollie grabbed the tiny broom. “Sweep-sweep!” He giggled and made swoosh sounds. Dust and dry leaves slid into a neat pile. His paws felt the broom handle, smooth and warm from the sun.

“Good job,” said Nana. “Next, water the plants.”

Ollie lifted the little blue watering can. The water went trickle-trickle. The soil drank and turned dark and cool. “Ahh,” Ollie said, pretending to be the plants. He sniffed the wet earth smell. “It smells like rain!”

“Last chore,” Nana said. “The tomatoes are ready. Let’s pick together and share.”

Ollie saw the red, round tomatoes. They looked like shiny marbles. “Yum!” he said. He picked one, two, three, four. His tummy rumbled.

Just then, a small sparrow hopped to the railing. “Cheep? Cheep?” It tilted its head.

Ollie waved. “Hello, bird friend!”

Nana nodded. “We have neighbors all around—people, animals, and plants. We are global citizens. That means we care for our home and our shared world. We think about others near and far.”

Ollie blinked. “Even the sparrow?”

“Yes,” Nana said. “And the bees, and our neighbors in other homes, and people in other countries. We all share one big Earth.”

Ollie hugged the tomato bowl. He wanted them all. They were so red and juicy. He licked a drop of tomato smell from his whiskers. “But I am hungry now.”

Nana knelt beside him. “I hear you. Fairness is tricky. Fair does not always mean equal. It means right and kind. You get some, I get some, and we save some. We also leave a bit for our tiny friends, and share extra with neighbors.”

Ollie’s ears twitched. “How do we decide?”

“Let’s make a plan,” said Nana. “Problem: we have a small bowl and many friends. Solution: we sort and share.”

They sat on the warm floor. Ollie lined up tomatoes: big, medium, small. The sun felt cozy on his back. Bees hummed like a lullaby.

“Big ones for our salad,” Nana said. “Medium ones to share with Mrs. Kim next door. Small ones for a treat now. And we leave the ones on the vine for the sparrow to peek at and for seeds to grow. We also save some seeds for new plants.”

Ollie thought. He rubbed his chin. “I get some, you get some, neighbors get some, and nature gets some. That is fair!”

“Yes,” Nana said. “This is global citizenship. We think about today and tomorrow. We help our place and our planet.”

Ollie grinned. “Helper time!”

They carried a little plate to Mrs. Kim. Ding-dong! The hallway smelled like soap and warm bread. Mrs. Kim opened the door and smiled. “Oh my, thank you!”

“We grew them,” Ollie said proudly. “We shared them fairly.”

Back on the balcony, Ollie placed two tomatoes on the railing. “For you,” he whispered to the sparrow. The bird chirped and pecked gently. Ollie felt his heart feel big and warm.

Nana and Ollie washed their paws. Splash-splash! Then they ate their small tomato treats. Pop! The tomatoes burst sweet and tangy. Ollie giggled. “My tongue is dancing!”

Nana nodded. “Our routine helped us: sweep, water, share. Chores can be caring. Caring can be fun.”

Ollie stretched like a long noodle. “I like our routine. Can we do it every day?”

“Yes,” said Nana. “Every day, we can help our small world and our big world too.”

Ollie looked at the garden. The leaves waved in the breeze. The bees buzzed happy songs. The sparrow cheeped from the railing.

Ollie whispered, “We are all neighbors.”

He put the broom away, set the watering can by the door, and patted the tomato pot. “Thank you, garden,” he said.

Nana hugged him. “When we share fairly and do our chores, we practice being global citizens—kind, careful, and helpful.”

Ollie smiled, his whiskers wiggling. “I am a global citizen.”

“Yes,” said Nana. “And a very playful otter.”

They laughed together as the sun dipped warm and golden, and the balcony garden glowed with happy light.

The End.
