# Biscuit Bot Builds at the Breakfast Table

Biscuit Bot was a helpful robot dog with soft whirring paws and a light-up nose that blinked beep-beep. He lived with Mia and her family, and every morning he helped in the dining room. The dining room smelled like warm toast and sweet oranges. Sunlight made the spoons sparkle.

“Good morning, Biscuit Bot,” said Mia, rubbing sleepy eyes.

“Beep! Good morning, Mia,” Biscuit Bot chimed. His screen smiled. “I am ready to help.”

On the big dining table, Mom set a basket of round rolls and a bowl of fruit. Dad poured water into little cups. Mia brought a box of colorful building blocks.

“I want to build a tall bridge,” Mia said, patting the box. “But I also want to eat breakfast.”

Biscuit Bot wagged his metal tail. “We can do both! Build first? Eat later?”

Mom shook her head gently. “Healthy bodies need breakfast first. Brains build better after bites.”

Mia frowned. “I’m hungry, but I really want to build.”

Biscuit Bot’s nose blinked. “We have a problem,” he said softly. “We want two things at the same time.”

Mia nodded. “Yes. How do we fix it?”

“We can use negotiation,” Biscuit Bot said. “Negotiation means we talk kindly and make a plan that feels fair.”

Mia looked at Mom and Dad. “Can we make a plan?”

Dad smiled. “Let’s try. What do you want, Mia?”

“I want to build a bridge that goes across the table,” Mia said, tapping the wood. “It will be strong and tall.”

Mom said, “I want you to eat healthy food. Fruit, water, and a little roll.”

Biscuit Bot tilted his head. “I want to help both. Beep!”

Mia thought. “What if we build, then eat later?”

Mom shook her head again. “Your tummy is empty. Building works best with energy.”

Biscuit Bot held up a block. “What if we make a building plan first? Then eat? Then build?”

Mia blinked. “A plan?”

“Yes,” said Biscuit Bot. “Step one: Look at the space. Step two: Count blocks. Step three: Make a simple drawing. Step four: Eat healthy bites. Step five: Build the bridge.”

Mia smiled. “That sounds fair.”

Dad nodded. “I agree. That is good negotiation.”

Biscuit Bot and Mia looked at the table. The smooth wood felt cool. The blocks clicked as Mia opened the box. “Click, clack,” went the pieces.

“Step one,” Biscuit Bot said. “Space check.” He padded to one end of the table and beeped. “We need room for plates and cups and the basket.”

Mia slid the plates to the edges. “We can build in the middle, but leave paths for the spoons.”

“Good,” Biscuit Bot said. “Step two: Count blocks.”

Mia counted with big, careful numbers. “One, two, three… twenty!” She stacked them in fours. The colors were bright—red, blue, green, and yellow. They felt smooth and warm from the sun.

“Step three: Drawing,” Biscuit Bot said. He used his nose light to trace lines on a small paper. “Beep.” The drawing showed two short towers with a flat bridge across. “Simple and strong.”

Mia bounced a little. “I’m excited!”

Mom set a little plate by Mia. “And now, step four: healthy bites.” She put slices of banana, a few carrots, and a small roll on the plate. Dad filled a cup with cool water.

Mia looked at the food. “I want to build, but I will eat first. That is our plan. That is our negotiation.”

Biscuit Bot wagged. “We build bodies before we build bridges.”

Mia took a bite of banana. “Mmm. Sweet.” She crunched a carrot. “Crunch!” She sipped water. “Cold.”

As they ate, Dad said, “People all over the world have breakfast, too. Some eat rice. Some eat bread. Some drink warm tea.”

Mom added, “And people everywhere build many things. Bridges, homes, schools. They plan, they talk, they work together.”

Mia’s eyes went wide. “So our bridge is like bridges everywhere?”

Biscuit Bot beeped proudly. “Yes. We are small builders with big hearts.”

Mia finished her roll and wiped her hands. “Step five: Build!”

They began to stack. The blocks clicked and clacked. Biscuit Bot held pieces steady with gentle paws. Mia pressed them down. “We need the bridge to be safe,” Mia said. “No wobbles.”

“Try a wider base,” Biscuit Bot suggested. “Two blocks by two blocks.”

Mia tried it. The tower felt sturdy. “It works!”

They built the second tower the same way. Then Mia placed the long, flat block on top. She held her breath. Biscuit Bot hummed. The bridge rested across both towers. It did not shake.

“We did it!” Mia cheered. She lightly tapped the bridge. It stayed strong.

Mom clapped. Dad smiled. “Good planning, good talking, good eating,” Dad said.

Mia petted Biscuit Bot’s smooth head. “Thank you for helping me negotiate and build.”

Biscuit Bot’s nose blinked. “Beep-beep. Teamwork!”

Mia looked at the bridge, then at her empty plate and cup. She felt full and happy. “Today I learned we can make a plan, talk kindly, and take care of our bodies. Then we build even better.”

Biscuit Bot wagged. “And builders everywhere do the same.”

They left a small path for the spoons, just like they planned. The sun shone on the colorful bridge, and the dining room felt warm and bright.

The End.
