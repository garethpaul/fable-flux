# Nutmeg's Words in the Guest Room

Sunlight sprinkled across the guest room like tiny gold dots. The room smelled like fresh soap and lemon. A small blue towel hung on a chair. A little mirror sat on a low table. In the corner, a cardboard box was turned into a cozy bed with a soft scarf.

Inside the scarf nest, a resourceful squirrel named Nutmeg stretched her fuzzy tail. “Good morning, guest room,” she chirped. Her whiskers wiggled with excitement. Today was a story day! Nutmeg loved stories. She also loved words—tiny sounds that made big pictures in the mind.

Nutmeg peeked into the mirror. “Hello, Nutmeg,” she said. “Your fur is fluffy!” She brushed her paws with the soft towel and washed her whiskers with a tiny dab of water. “Clean paws, clear words,” she sang. The water felt cool and tickly. Drip, drip!

Knock, knock. The door opened a little. A boy named Theo peeked in. He wore socks with stars and held a shiny book. “Hi, Nutmeg,” said Theo softly. “Can we tell a story in here? It’s the quiet room.”

Nutmeg’s eyes sparkled. “Yes! The guest room is perfect. It’s calm and clean.” She sniffed the lemon air. “Mmm. Fresh like a bright morning word—hello.”

Theo giggled. “Hello is a friendly word.” He sat on the rug. “I’m learning new words today. But some words feel big and wobbly.”

Nutmeg hopped onto the bed and patted the scarf. “Words can be big. But we can make them small and kind.” She pointed to the shiny book. “Let’s make a word picnic.”

“What’s a word picnic?” Theo asked.

“It’s when we pick words like berries,” Nutmeg said. “We taste them with our ears. We share them. We keep them safe.”

Theo smiled. “Okay! But first, can we set the room? Mom says guests feel good when the room is neat.”

Nutmeg nodded. “Good idea. Neat space, neat words.” She lifted a stray sock and wiggled under it. “Heavy sock!” she huffed, tug-tug-tug.

Theo helped. “Teamwork,” he said. He folded the sock and put it in a basket. He smoothed the blanket. He wiped a tiny smudge from the mirror. “All clean,” he said proudly.

Nutmeg washed her paws again. “Clean before we touch the book,” she said. “Hygiene helps our hands be kind.” The soap smelled like tiny flowers.

They opened the shiny book. The pages whispered, shhh, as they turned. “Let’s tell a guest room story,” Theo said. “But who starts?”

Nutmeg tapped her chin. “We can take turns,” she suggested. “I tell one part, then you tell one part. We share the story like we share snacks.”

“That’s fair,” Theo agreed. “You start.”

Nutmeg took a deep breath. “Once, in a lemon-fresh room, a brave spoon went on a trip across a tall pillow mountain.”

Theo added, “The spoon met a friendly towel. The towel said, ‘Hello!’”

Nutmeg nodded. “The towel asked, ‘Would you like to rest?’”

Theo whispered the towel’s answer. “‘Yes, please,’ said the spoon.”

They giggled. Their words made pictures: a shiny spoon sliding down a pillow, a towel like a soft cloud.

Theo stumbled on the word friendly. “Fr-fren…” He frowned. His cheeks grew warm.

Nutmeg noticed. Her voice was gentle. “Let’s say it together. Friend-ly. Like a hug for your tongue.”

Theo tried again. “Friend-ly.” He grinned. “I did it!”

“Your word is brave,” Nutmeg said. “Say hello to new words. That’s language love.”

Theo clapped softly. “I like how words feel. Some are smooth, like hello. Some are bouncy, like pillow.”

“And some are quiet,” Nutmeg whispered. “Like guest.”

Theo looked toward the door. “Mom said I can play here until snack time. Can we make a plan for when we clean up?” he asked.

Nutmeg tilted her head. “We can make a deal,” she said. “We tell three more parts. Then we tidy together. Deal?”

“Deal,” said Theo. They shook—paw to hand.

Nutmeg continued, “The spoon learned a new word: rest.”

Theo chimed in, “The towel learned a new word: share.”

Nutmeg added, “And the pillow learned a new word: hello.”

They finished with a big word: together.

When the story ended, the room felt warm and safe. Nutmeg and Theo smiled. “Time to tidy,” Theo said. He placed the book on the table. Nutmeg folded the scarf just right. They smoothed the rug and lined up the small pillows.

“Thank you for the story,” Theo said. “I like sharing and taking turns.” He paused. “Nutmeg, sometimes I forget words. Then I feel shy.”

Nutmeg patted his sleeve. “It’s okay to feel shy,” she said. “Words are friends you practice. We can whisper them first. We can clap the sounds. We can ask for help. That’s independence with kindness.”

Theo tried a new word, slow and careful. “Ne-go-ti-a-tion.”

Nutmeg’s eyes grew round. “Big word!” she cheered. “It means talking and making a fair plan, like our deal.”

Theo beamed. “I did it!”

A soft voice called from the hall. “Snack time!”

Theo waved. “See you tomorrow, Nutmeg. We can have another word picnic.”

“Yes,” Nutmeg said. “Hello to new words, every day.”

As Theo left, Nutmeg curled into her scarf nest. The guest room was neat and quiet. She felt proud. She had used clean paws, shared turns, made a fair plan, and loved words out loud.

She whispered to the room, “Thank you, words. You make pictures. You make friends.”

The End.
