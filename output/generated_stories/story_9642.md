# Leo Plans His Roar

In a quiet study corner, soft and sunny, a brave little lion cub named Leo curled his tail around a small stool. The room smelled like warm paper and crayons. His study corner had a low table, a fluffy rug like golden grass, and a basket of books and pencils. Outside the window, birds chirped peep-peep, and a gentle breeze tickled the curtains.

Leo had a new goal. “I want to learn my shapes and make a big roar poster all by myself,” he whispered. His whiskers wiggled with excitement. He wanted to be self-reliant. He wanted to plan. He wanted to do it kindly, without making a mess.

On the table, Leo placed a big sheet of paper. He lined up crayons: red, blue, green, yellow. He put a cup of glue and a safe, small pair of scissors within reach. He even set a sand timer that went shhh as it fell. “Plan first,” Leo said to himself. “Then do.”

“Step one,” Leo said, patting the paper. “Draw shapes.” He drew a circle like the sun. He drew a square like his rug. He drew a triangle like a mountain. His paws felt warm from holding the crayon. Scritch-scratch went the tip. He smiled. “I can do this.”

Just then, his friends peeked into the study corner. Nia the zebra, with zigzag stripes, clopped softly. Benji the hippo hummed a low tune. Amina the parrot fluttered to the chair, her feathers bright like tiny rainbows.

“What are you doing, Leo?” asked Nia, her voice gentle.

“I am making a roar poster,” Leo said. “I want to learn and make it myself. I have a plan.”

“What is the plan?” Benji asked, blinking with kind eyes.

Leo took a breath. “First, I draw shapes. Next, I cut big, safe pieces to paste. Then, I write R-O-A-R with stickers. Last, I clean up. I want to try by myself. But I would love your cheering.”

Amina tilted her head. “We can cheer softly,” she chirped. “Cheer-cheer, you can do it!”

Leo felt proud. It was brave to say what he needed. It was kind to include his friends in a new way. “Thank you,” he said.

He set the sand timer. “I will draw for this much sand.” The grains whispered down. Leo’s paw moved slowly. He made a rectangle like a book, an oval like an egg, a star like a twinkle. He paused and checked his plan. “Step one: done!”

“Nice planning,” Nia said.

“Step two,” Leo said. “Cut and paste.” He picked up safety scissors. Snip-snip! The paper made a soft sound. He cut out a blue circle and a green triangle. He held the glue. Squish, squish. He pressed the shapes on the poster. Press, count to three. One, two, three. He smiled when they stayed.

A small problem popped up. The star shape wrinkled. It stuck to his whiskers! “Oh no,” Leo giggled, going cross-eyed. The glue felt cool and sticky. He wanted to ask for help. His tail twitched. He remembered his plan: try first, then ask if needed.

Leo took a deep breath. “I can solve this.” He found a little damp cloth in the basket. Dab, dab on his whiskers. The paper star came free with a tiny pop. He smoothed it flat with the cloth, then pressed it gently. “There,” he said. “I did it.”

“Self-reliant and calm,” Benji hummed. “Good work.”

Amina fluffed her feathers. “Cheer-cheer, Leo!”

“Step three,” Leo said. He opened a box of bright letter stickers. He placed R. He placed O. He placed A. He paused. The last R would not stick. It curled up. Leo frowned a little. He could feel his heartbeat thump-thump. “I will plan a new way,” he whispered.

He looked around. On the shelf was a roll of clear tape. Leo pointed. “Friends, I want to keep working by myself. Can someone pass me the tape, please?” His voice was kind and clear.

Nia nodded and nudged the tape over with her nose. “Here you go.”

“Thank you,” Leo said. He tore a small piece—rip!—and pressed it over the R. Smooth, smooth. It stayed. “Yes!” His eyes sparkled.

“Step four,” Leo said softly. “Clean up.” He put the crayons back in their box with a click. He closed the glue. He tossed tiny scraps into the bin. He wiped the table with the damp cloth. Swish, swish. The study corner smelled fresh again.

Leo held up his poster. It shone with shapes and the word ROAR. The colors looked bright. The glue felt bumpy. The paper made a crisp sound when it waved.

“How do you feel?” asked Benji.

“I feel proud,” Leo said. “I made a plan. I tried by myself. I solved problems. I was kind. I also asked for what I needed with good words.”

Amina sang a happy note. “We are all different,” she said. “We help in different ways. Cheering, passing tape, giving space. That is good.”

Leo nodded. “Yes. We can work together and still do our own part.”

They all sat on the fluffy rug. The sun felt warm on their backs. Leo placed the poster on the wall of the study corner. It looked strong and brave.

Leo took a deep breath and gave a gentle roar. “Roooar,” he said. It sounded like wind in tall grass—soft but sure.

He smiled and spoke to his friends. “Today I learned that self-reliance means making a plan, trying my best, and solving small problems. It also means being kind and using good words to ask for help when I really need it.”

Nia, Benji, and Amina clapped and tapped and chirped. The study corner felt cozy and bright, a little world of learning and love.

The End.
