# Rosie Rabbit's Big Kitchen Adventure

Rosie Rabbit hopped into the warm kitchen. The morning sun streamed through yellow curtains. She could smell fresh bread baking in the oven. Her tummy rumbled loudly.

"I want to help make breakfast!" Rosie announced to her mama.

Mama Rabbit smiled. "That's wonderful, dear. You can wash the berries."

Rosie bounced on her hind legs. She reached for the blue bowl on the counter. But it was too high! She stretched her little paws up, up, up. Nothing.

"I can't reach!" Rosie said, her ears drooping.

"Try standing on your tippy-toes," Mama suggested, stirring pancake batter.

Rosie tried again. She stood as tall as she could. Her whiskers twitched with effort. Still too high! Her face felt hot. Her paws started to shake.

"This isn't fair!" Rosie stomped her foot. "I'm too small for everything!"

Mama Rabbit stopped stirring. She knelt down to Rosie's level. "I can see you're feeling frustrated, little bunny. That's okay. Everyone feels frustrated sometimes."

"But I want to help NOW!" Rosie's voice got louder. Her ears flattened against her head.

"Let's take three deep breaths together," Mama said gently. "Breathe in through your nose... and out through your mouth."

Rosie breathed in. The kitchen smelled like cinnamon and vanilla. She breathed out slowly. Her shoulders relaxed a little.

"Good," Mama said. "Now, what could we do to solve this problem?"

Rosie thought hard. She looked around the sunny kitchen. Her eyes landed on a sturdy wooden stool by the window.

"The stool!" she exclaimed. "I could use the stool!"

"That's excellent problem-solving!" Mama praised.

Rosie hopped over to the stool. She pushed it across the smooth kitchen floor. It made a soft scraping sound. She positioned it carefully in front of the counter.

Up she climbed! Now she was tall enough to reach the bowl. She felt proud and strong.

"Mama, I did it!" Rosie called out. "I found a way!"

"You certainly did," Mama said. "You felt frustrated, but you didn't give up. You took deep breaths and used your thinking brain to find a solution."

Rosie carefully carried the bowl to the sink. The cool water splashed over the red strawberries and purple blueberries. She hummed a happy tune while she worked.

"You know what, Rosie?" Mama said, flipping golden pancakes. "Speaking up about problems is important. When you told me you couldn't reach, I could help you think of ideas."

"Really?" Rosie asked, her pink nose twitching.

"Really. It's called self-advocacy. It means asking for what you need."

Rosie felt a warm glow in her chest. She had learned something important today. When things feel hard, she could take deep breaths, think of solutions, and ask for help when needed.

Soon, the kitchen table was set with fluffy pancakes, clean berries, and sweet honey. Rosie sat on her special booster seat, feeling very grown-up.

"Thank you for helping with breakfast," Mama said, giving Rosie a gentle ear rub.

"Thank you for teaching me about frustration," Rosie replied, taking a big bite of pancake. "Next time I feel upset, I'll remember to breathe and think."

The warm kitchen filled with the sound of happy munching and contented sighs.

The End.
