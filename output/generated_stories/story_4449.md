# Owl Hoots and the Rainbow Plan

In a bright creative art room, paper crinkled and crayons clicked. The room smelled like fruity markers and fresh soap. Paints sparkled in little cups. Brushes stood like tiny trees. A wise old owl named Hoots perched on a tall easel, his feathers soft and speckled like snow and cocoa.

“Good morning, little artists,” Hoots hooted gently. “Who is ready to make art?”

“I am!” said Mia, shaking a blue crayon. “I want all the blue!”

“I want red! All the red!” said Ben, hugging the red paint.

“I only like glitter,” whispered Sam, holding a shiny jar. “Only glitter, always.”

Hoots blinked his big golden eyes. “We have many colors and many friends. We can share and make a plan.”

Mia frowned. “But I love blue.”

Ben hugged the paint tighter. “Red is the best.”

Sam wiggled. “Glitter, glitter, glitter!”

The room grew noisy. Brushes clattered. A paper ripped. Little voices got loud.

Hoots lifted one wing. “Hoo-hoo. Let’s take calm breaths. In… out.” He breathed slowly. The children copied, their shoulders relaxing. The room sounded soft again.

“We have a problem,” Hoots said kindly. “We want the same things at the same time. We can solve it with words. We can make a plan together. That is called negotiation.”

“Ne-go-sha-tion?” Mia asked, eyes wide.

“It means talking and listening,” said Hoots. “It means finding a way that works for everyone.”

Hoots pointed to a big poster with pictures. “First, we wash hands. Then we make a plan. Then we create. Then we clean up. That is our routine.”

They went to the sink. Water splashed warm and bubbly. “Scrub, scrub,” sang Hoots. “Front and back, wiggle-waggle, between fingers!” The soap smelled like oranges. The children giggled and rubbed their hands until they were clean and shiny.

Back at the table, Hoots placed the blue crayon, the red paint, and the glitter in the middle. “Now, we listen.”

Mia spoke first. “I want to draw the sky. The sky is blue.”

Ben nodded. “I want to paint a big red rocket. Zoom!”

Sam whispered, “I want glitter on everything.”

Hoots tilted his head. “Thank you for telling us. Now let’s share ideas. What if we take turns?”

Mia shook her head. “But the sky is big. I need blue now.”

Ben stomped a little foot. “Rocket time now.”

Sam’s eyes filled with tears. “Glitter now.”

Hoots fluffed his feathers. “We can try a trade. We can try a timer. We can try mixing ideas.”

“Mixing?” asked Ben.

“Listen to this,” Hoots said softly. “Mia uses blue first for two minutes to draw the sky. Ben uses red first for two minutes to paint the rocket. Sam sprinkles a tiny bit of glitter at the end, like stars. Then we switch. Ben can use blue to paint space. Mia can use red for a sunset. Sam can add glitter to the rocket trail. Everyone gets a turn.”

Mia thought. “I can wait two minutes.”

Ben nodded slowly. “I can wait two minutes.”

Sam sniffled. “I can sprinkle at the end. I will wait.”

Hoots set a little sand timer. The sand whispered down. “When the sand is gone, we switch,” he said.

Mia’s crayon made soft scritch-scratch sounds as she drew the sky. “Blue makes me feel calm,” she said.

Ben dipped his brush. Swoosh! “Red makes me feel brave,” he said.

Sam watched, hands folded. “I like the sparkles,” Sam said, smiling a little.

“Switch!” sang Hoots when the sand ran out.

Ben reached for the blue crayon. “I will make dark space,” he said. “The rocket needs space to zoom.”

Mia took the red paint. “I will make a sunset,” she said. “The sky can be many colors.”

Sam bounced. “Glitter time soon!”

They worked and worked. The paper filled with a red rocket, a sunset sky, and shiny stars. The room smelled like paint and oranges and a little like glue. The sounds were happy—tap, tap, swoosh, scritch.

At last, Hoots nodded. “Now, Sam, sprinkle just a little glitter, like salt on soup.”

Sam tipped the jar gently. Soft twinkle-twinkles landed on the paper. “Stars!” Sam whispered.

“It is beautiful,” Mia said. “I like your rocket, Ben.”

“I like your sky, Mia,” said Ben. “Sam’s glitter is perfect.”

Hoots smiled. “We used our words. We listened. We made a plan. That is negotiation. We also followed our routine and kept our hands clean. And look! We celebrated our differences. Blue, red, and glitter all together.”

They washed their hands again. Splash, bubble, rinse. “Glitter likes to stick,” Hoots chuckled. “Soap helps it say goodbye.”

Mia laughed. “Goodbye, glitter!”

Ben waved at the sink. “Goodbye, red on my hands!”

Sam wiggled clean fingers. “Hello, shiny art!”

Hoots fluffed his wings. “What did we learn today?”

Mia said, “To talk and listen.”

Ben added, “To make a plan and take turns.”

Sam smiled, “To let everyone’s ideas shine.”

Hoots hooted softly. “Yes. When we negotiate, everyone gets a little of what they want, and together we make something wonderful.”

They hung the picture on the wall. It sparkled in the light. The children felt proud, warm and bright inside.

“Team art!” they cheered.

“Team hearts,” Hoots replied, tapping the poster with a gentle wing. “We are different, and that makes our art—and our hearts—shine.”

The End.
