# Tiko the Turtle’s Little List

Tiko was a shy turtle with a smooth, green shell. He had a cozy study corner by the window. The study corner had a tiny desk, a soft blue chair, a cup of pencils, and a little lamp that made warm, golden light. Outside the window, the breeze whispered in the leaves, shhh, shhh, like a friendly lullaby.

One morning, Tiko woke up and stretched his little legs. “Today, I will finish my list,” he whispered. He was shy, so he talked softly, even to himself.

Tiko picked up a small paper. It was his Little List. He had drawn boxes to check. The boxes said:
- Brush, wash, and tidy
- Write a thank-you note
- Practice letters
- Put away toys
- Read about the world

Tiko smiled, but his tummy felt fluttery, like tiny butterflies. “Can I do it all?” he asked in a small voice.

He took a deep breath. In-breath smelled like soap and paper. Out-breath felt warm on his lips. “Yes. One thing at a time.”

First, Tiko brushed, washed, and tidied. Swish, swish went the brush. Splash, splash went the water. He folded a towel and put books in a neat pile. “Check!” he said, and he made a little check mark. His heart felt calm and proud.

Next, it was time to write a thank-you note. Tiko wanted to say thank you to Grandma Tula for the bright blue pencil she sent from far, far away, across the sea. He picked the pencil up. It smelled like fresh wood. He pressed it to the paper, but his hand paused.

“I feel shy,” Tiko whispered. “What if I don’t write it right?” His cheeks felt warm. His shell felt extra heavy.

His friend Mina the snail peeked in at the window. “Hi, Tiko,” Mina said softly. Her tiny eyes twinkled. “What are you doing?”

“I have my Little List,” Tiko said. He showed the paper. “I want to write a thank-you note. But I feel shy.”

Mina nodded. “It is okay to feel shy. Do you want help?”

Tiko took another deep breath. “Yes,” he said. “I need help, please.” His voice was still soft, but it was brave. He was speaking up for himself.

Mina slipped into the study corner. “Let’s say it out loud first,” she suggested. “What do you want to say to Grandma Tula?”

Tiko closed his eyes and imagined Grandma Tula’s warm smile. He could almost smell her kitchen—sweet tea and cinnamon. “Dear Grandma Tula,” he said. “Thank you for the bright blue pencil. I love to write with it. It makes the letters look like the sky. Love, Tiko.”

Mina clapped her little hands. “That is lovely!”

Tiko wrote slowly. Scratch, scratch went the pencil. He made short lines and big letters. He took his time. When he finished, he read it again. He smiled. “Check!” he said, and he checked the box.

For practice letters, Tiko made curvy Ts and KOs. “T for Tiko,” he hummed. “K-O for ‘ko’ sound.” He traced lines with his finger, feeling the bumpy paper. “Check!”

Then he put away his toy blocks. Clink, clack went the blocks into the basket. “Check!”

Last, it was time to read about the world. Tiko opened a picture book called “Hello, World!” The book had bright pages. One page had kids in hats waving. Another showed a big blue ocean with boats. Tiko and Mina looked and listened to the windy sound he made with his mouth, whooosh, like the sea. They learned that people say hello in many ways.

“Hola,” Tiko read. “Bonjour. Jambo. Ni hao.” He tried each word slowly. The words felt like little songs. “The world is big,” Tiko whispered, eyes wide. “So many friends to meet.”

Mina nodded. “We can be kind to friends all around the world.”

Tiko closed the book and felt warm inside. He looked at his Little List. All the boxes had checks. “I did it,” he said. His voice was small, but it sparkled. He felt tall, even in his turtle shell.

He placed the thank-you note in an envelope. He drew a tiny heart on it. He imagined Grandma Tula opening it far away and smiling. “I am grateful,” Tiko said. “Thank you, Grandma.” He felt the good feeling of gratitude, like a soft blanket.

Mina smiled. “You finished your tasks, Tiko. You asked for help when you needed it. You wrote your note. You learned new words. That is a big day!”

Tiko nodded. “One thing at a time,” he said. “I can do it.”

The golden lamp glowed. The window breeze went shhh, shhh. Tiko tucked his list into his desk. He felt calm and proud. He waved to Mina. “Thank you for helping me,” he said.

“You’re welcome,” Mina said. “See you tomorrow for a new little list!”

Tiko smiled. “Yes. Tomorrow.”

And in his cozy study corner, the shy turtle rested, happy and brave, knowing he could speak up, write kind words, and finish his tasks—one by one.

The End.
