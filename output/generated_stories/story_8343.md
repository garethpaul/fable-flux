# Whiskers and the Garden Shed Rules

Whiskers was a clever cat with soft gray fur and bright yellow eyes. He liked to think and plan before he pounced. One sunny morning, he padded to the garden shed. The shed smelled like pine wood and soil. A tiny window let in warm, golden light. Tools hung like shiny fish on the wall. A broom leaned in the corner, and a coil of hose sat like a green snake.

Whiskers loved the shed. It was peaceful. It was quiet. He had a little job to do. He wanted to line up the flower pots from small to big, and sweep the floor. He purred. “I can do this,” he said to himself. “I can do my task. I can be careful.”

As he started, he heard a tap-tap at the door. “Knock, knock!” said a squeaky voice.

Whiskers opened the door a little. It was Pip the mouse. Pip wore a tiny red leaf like a hat. “Hi, Whiskers! Can I come in and jump on the pots?” Pip asked, eyes bright.

Whiskers blinked. He liked Pip. But he also liked the pots to stay safe and still. He took a slow breath. He remembered something he learned: My space, my voice. I can say yes. I can say no.

Whiskers said kindly, “Hello, Pip. I am working now. I need the pots to be quiet and safe. Please do not jump on them.”

Pip wriggled. “But it looks fun!” he squeaked. “Just one jump?”

Whiskers shook his head. “No, thank you. My job is to line them up. I need space to finish my task.” His voice was soft, but clear.

Pip’s whiskers drooped. “Oh,” he said. “Okay.” He looked around. “Can I watch?”

Whiskers smiled. “Yes, you can watch if you sit on the mat and keep your paws to yourself.”

Pip nodded and sat on a little straw mat by the door. “I will try,” he said.

Whiskers turned back to the pots. He touched each one gently. “Small, small, medium, big, bigger,” he said. He liked to use words to help his brain. He felt proud. He was using knowledge. He was using care. He lined the pots in a neat row. Tap, tap, tap, went his paw on the clay.

Just then, the door swung wide. “Boom!” The broom wobbled. In came Tilly the terrier, tail wagging fast. Her nose sniffed the dusty air. “Hi, hi, hi!” barked Tilly. “Can I play chase? Let’s run around the shed!”

Dust swirled. The sun made little sparkles in the air. Whiskers’ ears twitched. He did not like loud bumps near the pots. He felt a flutter in his tummy. He put his paw on his chest. He remembered: I can use my words. I can set a boundary.

“Tilly,” Whiskers said, standing tall. “I am working now. Please slow down. Please use quiet paws in the shed.”

Tilly skidded to a stop. “Quiet paws?” she asked, tilting her head.

“Yes,” said Whiskers. “Soft steps. No chasing. The shed is my work space. I need it to be calm.”

Tilly took a deep breath. “Okay,” she said. “I can try quiet paws.” She tiptoed. Tip, tip, tip.

Whiskers nodded. “Thank you.” He felt strong inside. His voice had worked. He had been clear and kind.

He went to the corner and picked up the broom. It smelled like straw and sunshine. He swept, swish, swish, swish. Little piles of leaves made soft sounds, like whispers. Pip watched with big eyes. “You are good at this,” Pip said.

Whiskers purred. “I learned how,” he said. “I practiced. I can do it myself.” He smiled. He liked that he did not need someone else to tell him what to do. He knew his plan. He could rely on himself.

As he swept, a beetle crawled by. It clicked and clacked. Whiskers leaned down. “Hello, Beetle,” he said. “The floor is busy right now. Please walk along the wall.” He pointed with his tail. The beetle turned and clicked along the edge, safe and sound.

Soon the floor was clean, and the pots were lined up like a parade. The hose was coiled in a neat circle. The tools on the wall did not move. The shed felt calm. It smelled fresh, like clean wood and a little bit of rain.

Tilly wagged slowly and smiled. “Your shed looks nice,” she said. “I liked using quiet paws.”

Pip clapped his tiny paws. “Yay, Whiskers! You finished your task!”

Whiskers felt warm and happy. “Thank you,” he said. “I set my rules. I used my words. I kept my space safe. And I finished my job.”

Pip raised a paw. “Can I help put one pot on the shelf? I will use gentle paws,” he said.

Whiskers thought. He liked to share. He also liked safety. He said, “Yes, you may put one pot on the low shelf. Please use two paws and go slow.”

Pip nodded, went slow, and set the pot down with a soft clink. “I did it!” he squeaked.

Tilly peered at the broom. “Can I try one sweep?” she asked.

Whiskers smiled. “Yes. One sweep with quiet paws.”

Tilly held the broom and made one soft swish. She grinned. “I did it too!”

Whiskers purred loudly. “We all did it. We used gentle bodies and clear words. We respected the shed rules. We finished the task.”

Pip, Tilly, and Whiskers sat together in the warm light. They listened to the birds sing outside. Chirp, chirp, chirp. The garden smelled sweet. Their bodies felt calm and proud.

Whiskers looked at his friends. “When I say what I need, and when you listen, we all feel safe,” he said. “That is a good rule.”

Pip and Tilly nodded. “We will remember,” they said.

The End.
