# Leo’s Little Apology Den

Leo was a brave little lion cub with soft golden fur and bright, curious eyes. He loved to explore and roar tiny roars. One rainy afternoon, he padded into the hallway and found a cozy place he called his closet hideaway. Inside the closet were soft coats that smelled like pine soap, a basket of buttons that clicked like tiny pebbles, and a box of clean cardboard tubes that felt smooth under his paws.

“This is my quiet den,” Leo whispered. He wiggled his whiskers and smiled. “Roar! I am brave.”

Leo’s big sister, Lina, had made a colorful paper crown for her school show. The crown sparkled with shiny stickers and carefully cut stars. She set it on the chair near the closet.

Leo peeked out and saw the crown. It was so bright and cheerful. “I just want to look,” he said softly. He reached his paw out. The crown crinkled. “Oops!” He pulled back. The paper tore a little. “Oh no,” Leo gasped. His tummy felt twisty. His ears grew warm.

Lina walked down the hall humming a happy tune. Leo’s heart thumped. He hid inside his closet hideaway and shut the door gently. It was dim and comfy. He could hear the rain tap-tap-tapping outside and smell the wooden floor. He hugged a soft coat sleeve and whispered, “I made a mistake.”

His friend Pip the plush penguin sat on a high shelf. Leo lifted Pip down and cuddled him. “Pip,” Leo said, “I touched the crown, and now it’s torn. I feel bad.”

Pip did not talk, but Leo pretended Pip had a wise, gentle voice. In his head the voice said, “Brave cubs tell the truth. Brave cubs make amends.”

“Make amends?” Leo repeated. He tasted the words like warm tea. “Make-amends means fix what I can and say sorry.”

Leo took a slow breath in and a slow breath out. He looked around his cozy closet den. There were the cardboard tubes. There were paper scraps from old mail. There was a little box of safe glue sticks and tape that Mom kept for crafts. There were jars for sorting: one for paper, one for plastic. Mom had taught him about recycling. “We recycle to help the Earth,” she always said.

Leo nodded. “I will make a new star to help fix the crown. I will say sorry to Lina. I will use recycled pieces. That is good for the Earth. That shows integrity. Integrity means doing the right thing, even when it’s hard.”

He opened the jar marked Paper. It smelled like dry leaves and crinkled softly. He picked a clean, colorful flyer. He cut with little safe scissors, snip-snip, snip-snip, making a star shape. He felt proud. He used a cardboard tube to trace a circle, then made a tiny moon too. He tapped the glitter jar, but it was empty. “That’s okay,” he said. “Shiny is nice, but honest is better.”

Leo stepped out of the closet hideaway and padded to the chair. He looked at the torn spot on the crown. “I did this,” he whispered. He heard Lina’s footsteps. His paws trembled, but he stood tall.

“Lina?” he called. His voice was small but brave.

“Yes, Leo?” Lina smiled, then she saw the crown. Her smile faded. “Oh.”

Leo’s eyes got watery. “I touched your crown,” he said. “I did not ask. I tore it a little. I’m sorry.” He held out the paper star and moon. “I made these from recycling in my closet den. Can I help fix it?”

Lina looked at Leo’s big, honest eyes. She touched his shoulder. “Thank you for telling the truth,” she said. “That is integrity. I am sad it tore, but I see you tried to make amends.”

“May I help?” Leo asked. “We can improve it together.”

Lina took a breath. “Yes,” she said softly. “Let’s fix it.”

They sat on the floor. The tape felt cool and sticky on Leo’s paw. “Sticky!” he giggled. Lina laughed too. They smoothed the torn paper, soft like a leaf. Together, they added the star and the tiny moon. Leo sorted the scraps into the paper jar. “Recycle, recycle,” he sang. Lina added bottle-cap gems from the craft box. They were shiny and made a happy clink. The crown rustled and rustled, like wind in tall grass.

When they were done, the crown looked even brighter. It had a new star, a tiny moon, and cap gems that shimmered. Lina put it on her head and twirled. “It’s better than before,” she said. “Thank you, Leo.”

Leo’s tail swished. “I am glad we fixed it. I am sorry I touched it without asking.”

Lina hugged him. The hug was warm and gentle. “You made amends,” she said. “You told the truth. You helped fix what you broke. That is brave.”

Mom peeked in. She smelled like cookies. “What a team,” she said. “I see you used recycled things. That helps the Earth. I am proud of your integrity.”

Leo grinned. His tummy felt calm now, like quiet rain. He looked at his closet hideaway. “My den helped me think,” he said. “Next time, I will ask before I touch.”

“That’s a good improvement,” Lina said. “Let’s make a crown for you too.”

They used more clean scraps and caps. They sang a little song: “Fix and try, tell and mend, brave and kind, friend to friend.” The glue smelled sweet. The paper felt crisp. Their laughter sounded like tiny bells.

Leo put on his new crown and gave Pip a tiny bow. “Roar,” he said, “a brave cub makes amends.”

The End.
