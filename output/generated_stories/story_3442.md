# Rocco Raccoon Finds His Rhythm

Rocco was a musical raccoon who loved to play in the sunny backyard. The grass was warm on his paws. The sun felt like a hug on his fur. Birds chirped, and a soft breeze smelled like sweet flowers and fresh dirt. Rocco wore a tiny blue cap and a big smile.

He had pots, pans, and a shiny tin can drum. He had a wooden spoon and a little whistle. “Ting! Bong! Toot!” he sang as he tapped and tooted. The sounds bounced around the yard like happy bubbles.

But one bright morning, Rocco’s music did not feel right. His tummy made a gurgle. His whiskers felt droopy. His eyes felt sleepy. He yawned a long, squeaky yawn.

“I want to play a big concert!” Rocco said. “But I feel slow and fuzzy.”

He took a deep breath and tried to play. “Ting—” The spoon slipped. “Bong—” The pan wobbled. “Toot—” The whistle squeaked too loud.

Rocco stamped his little feet. “Why is this so hard?” He felt a tiny tear. He wanted to give up.

Just then, a butterfly landed on the fence. “Hello, Rocco,” she said in a soft fluttery voice. “I am Bree. You sound a little tired.”

Rocco sniffled. “I am tired. I want to make happy music, but my body says no.”

Bree tilted her wings. “Maybe your body is asking for care. Bodies need love, just like songs.”

Rocco blinked. “Self-care?” he asked.

“Yes,” Bree said. “First, take a sip of cool water.”

Rocco walked to his little water bowl. The water glug-glug-glugged. He took slow sips. It felt like little cold stars in his mouth. “Ahh,” he said. His tummy gurgle grew quiet.

“Next,” said Bree, “let’s move our bodies. Exercise can wake us up.”

Rocco nodded. They stretched their arms up, up, up to the sky. The sun warmed their paws. They touched their toes and wiggled their tails. They did little jumps on the soft grass. “Bounce, bounce, bounce!” Rocco laughed. His heart beat like a drum, steady and strong.

“Now,” Bree whispered, “take calm breaths. In for four, out for four.”

Rocco breathed in: 1, 2, 3, 4. He breathed out: 1, 2, 3, 4. The breeze tickled his nose. His shoulders went from tight to loose. The world felt clear, like a clean window.

Rocco smiled. “Thank you, Bree. I feel better.” He lifted his spoon. “I think I can play now.”

But he looked down and saw a messy pile near his drum: old bottles, a box, and crinkly paper. “Oh no,” Rocco said. “My yard is messy. My music needs space.”

Bree nodded. “We can fix that. Let’s recycle!”

Rocco clapped. “Recycle means we sort and use again.” He got three small bins. One blue, one green, one yellow. “Bottles in blue, paper in yellow, boxes in green,” he said.

He picked up a bottle. “Clink!” It went in the blue bin. He folded a box. “Rustle!” It went in the green bin. He smoothed paper. “Shhh!” It went in the yellow bin. Bree helped by pointing with her tiny wing and humming a gentle tune.

Rocco moved slow and careful. “I want to be done now,” he said.

“Patience,” Bree said kindly. “Little steps make big changes.”

Rocco nodded and kept sorting. “Clink, rustle, shhh.” Soon the yard looked tidy and bright. He wiped his paws. They smelled like grass and sunshine.

“Now I’m ready,” Rocco said. He turned to his pots and pans. He looked at Bree. “Can I tell you something?”

“Of course,” Bree said.

“When I felt bad, I felt mad,” Rocco shared. “Next time, I will use my words. I will say, ‘I need water,’ or ‘I need a break.’”

“That is good communication,” Bree said. “Your feelings matter.”

Rocco took one more sip of water. He stretched his arms and rolled his shoulders. Then he tapped the tin can drum. “Bong.” He tapped the pan. “Ting.” He blew the whistle soft. “Toot.”

The sounds were smooth now, like a happy river. Rocco made a little song:

“Drink, stretch, breathe and rest,
Care for me to play my best.
Sort and clean, take time to grow,
Talk it out so friends can know.”

Bree danced in the air. A squirrel peeked over the fence and clapped. The birds chirped along. Rocco’s smile grew wide and warm.

When the song ended, Rocco bowed. “Thank you, body,” he said. “Thank you, Bree. Self-care helped me find my music.”

Bree fluttered close. “You solved your problem with water, exercise, patience, and kind words. And you helped the Earth by recycling.”

Rocco put his spoon away and patted his drum. The backyard felt peaceful and bright. He felt proud and calm.

“That was a good concert,” he said. “Now it’s time to rest.”

He curled up in a shady spot and closed his eyes. He breathed in and out, slow and soft. The grass whispered. The sun smiled. Rocco felt safe, loved, and ready for more music tomorrow.

The End.
