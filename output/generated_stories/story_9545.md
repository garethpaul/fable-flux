# Bucky Beaver and the Big Bathroom Helper Day

Bucky the busy beaver woke up with a wiggle and a smile. He stretched his little paws and twitched his flat, paddle tail. “Today I will be a helper,” he said. “Helpers make the world better.”

Bucky’s bathroom was bright and splashy. The tiles were cool under his feet. The sink sang a soft drip-drip song. The mirror smelled clean, like lemon bubbles. Bucky liked the bathroom. It was where he brushed, washed, and learned to care.

“Good morning, Toothbrush,” Bucky said, picking up his soft blue brush. “Are you ready?”

“I am ready!” squeaked the toothbrush in Bucky’s pretend voice. “Let’s scrub, scrub, scrub.”

Bucky put a pea-sized dot of toothpaste on the brush. “Just a little,” he said. “We do not waste.” The minty smell tickled his nose. He brushed in tiny circles. “Top teeth, bottom teeth, back teeth,” he sang. “Brush, brush, brush!”

He spat into the sink. He turned off the faucet. “Click,” went the handle. “We save water for the river and the fish,” he said. He felt proud. Saving water was a big helper job.

Just then, Bucky heard chatter in the hallway. “We’re here!” sang his friends.

First came Aya the otter with shiny whiskers. She wore a bright scarf from her family’s market. “Salam!” Aya smiled. “Can I wash my hands?”

Then came Mateo the moose calf with a gentle voice. He wore a little woven bracelet from his abuela. “Hola,” he said. “My hooves got sticky from breakfast.”

Last came Kiki the hedgehog, who had tiny glasses and a giggle. She wore beads that clacked softly. “Jambo!” Kiki chimed. “Can I brush, too?”

Bucky’s heart felt warm. “Yes! The bathroom is for everyone. We all take turns. We all help keep it clean.”

Bucky put out a small rug. “One at a time,” he said. “Aya first.” Aya hopped in and washed her paws with soapy bubbles. The soap smelled like oranges. The bubbles tickled and popped. “Pop! Pop! Pop!”

“Thank you, Bucky,” Aya said. “Shukran.” She reached for a towel.

Bucky nodded. “We dry hands on one towel. Then we hang it back up.” He lifted the towel and hung it neatly. “When we share, we care.”

Now it was Mateo’s turn. He turned the faucet gently. “Only a little water,” he whispered. He scrubbed and scrubbed, singing a song his abuela taught him. “Agua, agua, muchas gracias.” He smiled at Bucky. “Thank you for sharing your bathroom.”

“You are welcome,” Bucky said. “I am glad you are here.”

Kiki hopped next. “Please,” she said sweetly. She used just one pump of soap. She dried her paws and cleaned a tiny water drop on the counter with the towel. “I like to leave things nice for the next friend,” she said.

Bucky’s eyes sparkled. “That is civic responsibility!” he said carefully. The words felt big. He said them again, slower. “Ci-vic re-spon-si-bi-li-ty means we help our place. We keep it nice for everyone.”

The friends nodded. “We can all help,” said Aya.

“I can sweep,” said Mateo.

“I can tidy,” said Kiki.

“I can guide,” said Bucky. He felt brave and calm, like a good leader.

Then—oh no! The toilet made a gurgly sound. Glub-glub-glub! Paper swirled. Water rose higher. “Uh-oh,” said Bucky. “Too much paper!”

Kiki squeaked. “What do we do?”

Bucky took a deep breath. He remembered a rule. “Only a little paper,” he said. “And no toys in the toilet.” He turned the handle to stop the water. He got his small plunger, the one with a red handle. “This is a tool,” he told his friends. “We use it with care.”

“Can we help?” asked Aya.

“You can cheer,” said Bucky.

“Go, Bucky, go!” they chanted.

Bucky pressed the plunger gently. Thoop. Thoop. The water went down, down, down. The toilet sang a happy swirl. “We did it!” Bucky grinned.

“Teamwork!” said Mateo.

“And good choices next time,” said Kiki.

Bucky made a little sign with a crayon. He drew a toilet, a roll of paper, and a small number 3. “This says: three squares is enough,” he explained. He taped the sign near the roll. “Signs help everyone remember. That is civic responsibility too.”

The friends cleaned the counter with a soft cloth. They wiped tiny splashes. They put the soap back in the middle. They folded the towel neatly.

“Look,” said Aya. “It shines!”

“It smells fresh,” said Mateo. “Like lemons and oranges.”

“It feels safe and kind,” said Kiki. “Like a hug for all.”

Bucky’s mom peeked in. She wore a bright apron and a warm smile. “Good helpers,” she said. “Thank you for caring for our bathroom. You made it nice for the next person.”

Bucky’s chest felt big with happy. He turned to his friends. “Thank you for waiting your turn. Thank you for using just a little. Thank you for cleaning up. Thank you for bringing your songs and words. Our different ways make our team strong.”

Aya bowed a little. “Shukran, Bucky.”

“Gracias,” said Mateo.

“Asante,” said Kiki.

Bucky nodded. “Thank you.”

They tapped paws, hooves, and tiny hedgehog feet. Tap, tap, tap. The bathroom was bright and calm. The sink was quiet. The towel was dry. The floor was clean.

Bucky looked in the mirror. He saw four smiling friends. He saw helpers. He saw care.

“I learned something,” he said. “Being a good helper is being a good neighbor. We share. We save. We clean. We leave things better than we found them.”

His friends cheered. “Yes!”

Bucky wiggled his paddle tail. “Let’s carry this care everywhere,” he said. “At the park, at school, at home.”

The day felt sunny, even inside. Their hearts felt full.

The End.
