# Cubby Builds a Beat

The garage workshop smelled like warm wood and orange soap. Sunlight made little squares on the floor. Hammers slept in a row on the wall. A broom stood tall like a quiet guard.

Cubby, a friendly bear cub with soft brown fur, bounced in. “I want to build something!” he said, his eyes shining. He clapped his paws. Clap, clap! It echoed in the big space.

Grandpa Bear smiled. “Good morning, Cubby. What will you build today?”

Cubby looked around. He saw a wooden box, some nails, and a rubber ball. He heard a bird sing outside. Tweet-tweet! His paws wiggled. “I want to make music,” he said. “And I want to make a game to move my body!”

Grandpa Bear chuckled. “Music and exercise? That sounds fun. But remember, we use tools safely. We go slow. That is how we build skills.”

Cubby nodded. “I will learn,” he said. “Step by step!”

Just then, Cubby’s friend, Bree the bunny, hopped into the garage. Her ears perked up. “Hi, Cubby! Can we play jump ball?” She held the rubber ball and wiggled with energy. “I want to bounce, bounce, bounce!”

Cubby looked at the tools. He looked at the ball. He felt pulled both ways. “I want to build a drum,” he said softly. “But I also want to play with you.” His tummy felt twisty.

Bree’s nose twitched. “I want to play now,” she said. “Music can be loud. I don’t like too much bang-bang.”

Cubby took a deep breath. He remembered what Grandpa said: go slow, build skills, use words. He placed a paw on his chest. “Let’s talk,” he said. “I feel torn. I want to build and play. How can we make it work for both of us?”

Bree blinked, then smiled. “We can compromise,” she said. “We can take turns.”

Grandpa Bear nodded. “Good plan. Peaceful communication helps. Use your words. Make a plan.”

Cubby’s ears perked up. “First, I build a quiet drum,” he said. “Then we use the drum to make a hopping game!”

Bree clapped her paws. “Yes! Soft beats for me. Then jump to the rhythm.”

Cubby found the wooden box. It felt smooth under his paws. “Grandpa, what is first?”

“First, measure,” Grandpa said. He handed Cubby a soft cloth tape. “Measure the box so you know where to tap.”

Cubby stretched the tape. “One, two, three,” he counted. The tape felt cool and tickly. He made a small mark with chalk. “Marking helps,” he said proudly.

“Next, sand the edges.” Grandpa gave him a small piece of sandpaper. “Move gently.”

Shh, shh, shh, went the sandpaper. Fine dust made a tiny cloud that smelled like pine. Cubby moved slow. His paws learned the feel: rough, then smooth. “I am building my skills,” he said. “Slow and safe.”

Bree watched. “Can I help?” she asked.

“Yes,” said Cubby. “Please hold the box steady.” Bree’s paws were light and careful. “Thank you,” Cubby said. “You are a good helper.”

Grandpa placed felt pads on the bottom. “These make soft sounds,” he explained. “So the drum will not be too loud.”

Cubby nodded. He chose two wooden spoons for drumsticks. Tap, tap. He tried a soft beat. “Boom-boom… boom.” The sound was warm, like rain on a roof. Bree relaxed. “I like that,” she said. “Not too noisy.”

Cubby’s face glowed. “I did it! I measured, marked, sanded, and tapped gently.”

Grandpa smiled. “You followed steps. That is skill-building.”

Now it was Bree’s turn. “My idea,” she said, “is a jump pattern. When you make one beat, I hop once. When you make two beats, I hop twice. We will exercise and make music!”

Cubby grinned. “Let’s try.”

He tapped once. Boom. Bree hopped once. Boing! He tapped twice. Boom-boom. Bree hopped twice. Boing, boing! They giggled.

They added more steps. “Three soft beats means a big stretch,” said Bree. “Four tiny taps means shake your paws!”

Cubby counted out loud. “One! Two-two! Three-three-three!” Bree stretched up high, then shook her paws like little maracas. The garage filled with gentle sounds: boom, boing, shh, and happy giggles.

Soon, other friends peeked in. A chipmunk swished his tail. “Can we join?”

Cubby looked at Bree. He remembered to use words and include others. “Yes,” he said. “We can teach you.” He explained the steps slowly. “Listen. Watch. Try. We can do it together.”

The friends practiced. Sometimes they missed a beat. Sometimes they bumped. “Oops!” said Cubby. He stopped, breathed in, and smiled. “Let’s try again.” He looked at Bree. “Does the sound feel okay?”

Bree nodded. “Thank you for asking. The felt pads help. It is peaceful.”

They kept going. Tap and hop. Tap-tap and hop-hop. Three taps, big stretch! Four tiny taps, shake-shake! Their paws pitter-pattered on the floor. The air smelled like pine and a little bit like bunny fur. The sunlight squares moved slowly, warm on their backs.

After the game, everyone felt calm and proud. Cubby patted the wooden box drum. “We built a drum. We learned steps. We made a plan. We took turns. We got exercise. And we made music!”

Grandpa Bear clapped gently. “You solved a problem with kind words,” he said. “You practiced new skills. You found a compromise. That is how builders—and friends—grow strong.”

Cubby hugged Bree. “Thank you for talking with me.”

Bree hugged back. “Thank you for listening.”

Cubby looked around the garage workshop. It felt like a happy place, full of soft beats and steady hearts. He smiled. “Tomorrow,” he said, “we can build a shaker and make a new game.”

Bree’s ears wiggled. “Yes! Step by step.”

The End.
