# Joey Roo and the Gentle Pause

Joey Roo was a joyful kangaroo joey with bright eyes and a bouncy tail. One sunny morning, Joey hopped into Grandpa Gus’s garage workshop. The air smelled like warm wood and orange soap. Sunbeams made sparkly dust dance over shiny tools. Clink, clink went the little nails in a jar. Whirr went a small fan by the window.

“Today is Make-a-Message Day,” said Grandpa Gus, smiling under his white mustache. “We will write kind signs for our neighbors and keep our town tidy and happy.” He pointed to a big wooden board on the workbench. “We’ll write: Please Close the Gate. Thank You!”

Joey Roo clapped his paws. “I love writing! I love helping!”

Grandpa Gus set out paper, thick markers, and a smooth pencil. He also placed safety goggles and soft ear covers on a hook. “First, we plan,” he said. “Then we write, then we paint, then we clean up.”

Joey bounced. “I can do it all now!” He grabbed a marker. The cap popped off—pop!—and the sweet smell of ink filled the air.

“Wait, little hopper,” Grandpa Gus said gently. “Let’s use our calm steps. We breathe, we think, we write.”

Joey’s ears twitched. He wanted to zoom. He wanted to scribble big letters fast. His tail wiggled like a whip. “But I’m excited!”

“Excited is okay,” said Grandpa Gus. “We can hold our excitement with our breath. Watch.” He put a paw on his chest. “In through the nose, hold, out through the mouth.” He did it slowly, like a soft breeze. “This helps our bodies listen.”

Joey tried. In through his nose—sniff! He held it. Out through his mouth—whooo. His shoulders relaxed. His tail stopped wiggling so fast. “I feel softer,” he said with a smile.

“Good,” said Grandpa Gus. “Now let’s imagine together.” He closed his eyes. “I see a gate in the park. Children play. A puppy runs. A kind sign helps people remember to close the gate, so everyone stays safe.”

Joey closed his eyes, too. He imagined the puppy’s happy barks, yip-yip! He saw the park. He saw the gate. He felt his heart grow warm. “The sign will help our friends,” he said.

“That is civic responsibility,” said Grandpa Gus. “We care for our town. We use our knowledge to help.”

“Civic,” Joey repeated, trying the new word. It felt big and important. He liked it.

Grandpa Gus slid a notebook to Joey. “Let’s practice the words. Use the pencil first.”

Joey held the pencil. He wrote slowly: Please Close the Gate. Thank You! The pencil scratched on the paper—scritch, scritch. He sounded out the words. “Puh-lease. Clo-se. Gate. Thank. You.” He looked up. “Did I do it?”

“You did,” Grandpa Gus said. “Neat and kind.”

Joey reached for the marker again. His paws buzzed with energy. He wanted big letters now. But he saw his breath again, and he remembered the gentle pause. He breathed: in, hold, out. “I can wait,” he told himself. “I can go step by step.”

They moved to the wooden board. Grandpa Gus put down painter’s tape to make a straight line. “You can trace the letters you practiced,” he said.

Joey nodded. He dipped a brush into dark blue paint. It was cool and thick, like blueberry yogurt. Drip! A tiny drop fell on his paw. “It’s cold!” Joey giggled.

He painted P, then l, then e. He went slow. He whispered, “Slow is smooth. Smooth is kind.” When his tail tried to bounce, he pressed his feet on the floor and breathed again.

Outside the window, a car door shut. Thunk. A neighbor waved. “Hello, Joey! Hello, Gus!”

“Hello!” Joey called. His voice echoed in the workshop. He wanted to show the sign now, now, now. But he saw the wet paint shine. He remembered the plan. “First we finish. Then we share,” he said softly to himself.

Grandpa Gus gave a proud nod. “You are using self-regulation,” he said. “You are the boss of your body and your feelings.”

Joey smiled and painted the last word. Thank You! He added a small heart and a friendly paw print at the bottom. The letters looked bright and welcoming.

They set the board to dry on two blocks. The paint smelled a little like fresh rain. Joey felt the buzz inside come back. Waiting was hard. “What if I hop too close?” he asked.

“Let’s make a waiting plan,” said Grandpa Gus. He set a little sand timer. “When the sand is gone, we check. While we wait, we clean, we count, and we write a note.”

“A note?” Joey’s ears perked.

Grandpa Gus handed him paper. “We will write a thank-you note to the neighbor who will hang the sign.”

Joey wrote with his pencil: Dear Neighbor, Thank you for helping our park. We care for the gate. Love, Joey Roo.

He added a sunny face. He counted screws into the jar—one, two, three—plunk, plunk, plunk. He wiped a tiny paint dot from the table. He breathed in, hold, out, like a quiet song.

“Time,” said Grandpa Gus as the last sand fell.

They touched the sign gently. Dry! Joey bounced once, then stopped himself and made his feet heavy again so he didn’t bump it. “Gentle feet,” he whispered.

They carried the sign to the gate in the park. The grass smelled sweet. Children laughed. The puppy wagged and woofed. Grandpa Gus screwed the sign on. Turn, turn, turn. The board sat straight and proud.

A child read aloud, “Please Close the Gate. Thank You!” She smiled and closed the gate with a click.

Joey felt warm all over. “We helped!” he said. “My calm steps helped me write and wait.”

Grandpa Gus hugged him. “You used your breath. You used your plan. You used your kind heart and your words. That is knowledge and imagination working together for our town.”

Joey placed the thank-you note by the neighbor’s door. He took one more deep breath. In, hold, out. “I can be the boss of me,” he said. “I can pause, plan, and do.”

Grandpa Gus winked. “And that is a powerful joey skill.”

Joey Roo hopped home, gentle and proud, ready to write more kind messages another day.

The End.
