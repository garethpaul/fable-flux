# Rosie Rabbit and the Treehouse Turn

Rosie was a small, determined rabbit with soft gray fur and bright, curious eyes. She lived near a big oak tree with a cozy treehouse tucked high in its branches. The ladder felt smooth under her paws, and the wood smelled like warm sunshine and pine.

“I can do it! I can climb and help!” Rosie said each morning. Her ears wiggled with excitement as she hopped to the ladder.

Inside the treehouse, Rosie’s friends were playing. Squeaky the squirrel stacked blocks. Milla the mouse drew stars with a blue crayon. Pip the puppy lined up toy cars and made gentle “vroom” sounds. The room was bright and cool, with a small round window that let in the whisper of leaves.

“Good morning!” Rosie called. “I want a turn with the big red block.”

Squeaky hugged the block close. “I’m using it,” he said.

Rosie’s whiskers twitched. She clapped her paws. “But I want it now,” she said, her voice a little loud.

Pip looked up. “We can share,” he said softly. “We can take turns.”

Rosie crossed her paws and stomped one foot. Thump. “I want it first,” she said. Her tummy felt tight, and her face felt hot. She reached out and grabbed the red block.

The block slipped. Clunk! The tower tumbled. Blocks rolled across the floor, tapping and bumping.

Squeaky’s eyes went wide. “My tower!” he squeaked.

Milla’s whiskers drooped. “That scared me,” she whispered.

Rosie’s ears fell flat. She saw Squeaky’s sad face and heard Milla’s tiny sniff. Her tummy felt even tighter. The treehouse went quiet except for the soft tick-tick of a little clock on the wall.

Rosie took a slow, deep breath. In. Out. She remembered what Mama Rabbit always said: “Respect means we care about others. We listen. We wait. We use gentle paws and kind words.”

Rosie looked at the red block in her paws. It felt heavy now. She held it to her chest and spoke in a small voice. “I’m sorry,” she said. “I did not respect you. I did not wait. I grabbed. That was not kind.”

Squeaky blinked. Pip’s tail wagged. Milla came closer.

Rosie tried again, with stronger words. “I will fix it. I will help rebuild. I will ask before I take. I will wait for my turn.”

Squeaky hugged his bushy tail and nodded. “Thank you for the apology,” he said. “Let’s build again.”

Pip smiled a big puppy smile. “We can make it taller,” he said.

Milla held out the blue crayon. “And I can draw a door!”

Rosie’s ears perked up. “Can we make a plan?” she asked. “I want to learn to wait. I want to practice respect.”

Squeaky tapped his chin. “We can use a turn card,” he said. He picked up a leaf shape from the craft box. It was smooth and green. “When you hold the leaf, it’s your turn. When you are done, pass it with a smile.”

Rosie nodded. “I like that.”

They began. Squeaky started with two long blocks. He held the leaf. Tap, tap. He placed them gently. Then he breathed and passed the leaf to Milla.

Milla took her turn and drew a little window on a flat block. Scritch-scratch went the crayon. She smiled and passed the leaf to Pip.

Pip placed a ramp for the toy cars. “Vroom,” he whispered, and slid the leaf to Rosie.

Rosie’s paws tingled. She held the leaf. It felt cool and smooth. She looked at the red block. She wanted to grab it fast. Her heart made a quick thump-thump.

Rosie took another deep breath. In. Out. She whispered to herself, “I can learn. I can grow. Respect is waiting. Respect is asking.”

She looked at her friends. “May I use the red block?” she asked.

Squeaky nodded. “Yes, Rosie.”

Rosie smiled and set the red block on top. Tap. It fit just right. The tower stood tall and steady.

“Good job,” Pip said.

“Nice waiting,” Milla added.

Rosie felt warm and proud. Her tummy felt calm now. The treehouse seemed to glow with happy light, and the leaves outside made a gentle shhh sound like a quiet cheer.

They kept building and sharing. When the leaf came to Rosie again and again, she waited, she asked, and she tried new ideas. Some blocks wobbled. One piece fell. Clatter! Rosie flinched, then stood up straight.

“It’s okay,” she said. “I can try again. I am learning.”

Her friends cheered. “Growth mindset!” Pip barked. “We keep trying!”

Soon, the treehouse tower was done. It had a blue door, a window, and a tiny leaf flag on top. They all clapped. Clap, clap, clap.

Rosie looked at her friends. “Thank you for being patient with me,” she said. “I learned that respect means we listen, we wait, and we share. And when we make a mistake, we can say sorry and try again.”

Squeaky grinned. “And we can build together!”

Milla wiggled her nose. “Together feels nice.”

Pip wagged. “Together is fun!”

Rosie hugged her friends. The treehouse smelled like crayons and wood and happy. The wind hummed, and the warm sun kissed the little round window.

Rosie tucked the green leaf card on a hook by the door. “We will use this every day,” she said. “Respect lives here.”

The End.
