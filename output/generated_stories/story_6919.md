# Ollie Otter and the Attic Adventure

Ollie was a playful otter with shiny whiskers and a curious nose. One quiet afternoon, he tip-patted up creaky wooden steps to a quiet attic. The air smelled like old books and cinnamon dust. Sunlight made golden stripes on the floor. It was so still that Ollie could hear his own little “hmm” and the soft tick-tick of a tiny clock.

“Hello, attic,” Ollie whispered. “Let’s have an adventure!”

The attic was full of boxes, buttons, and a big, round trunk. A soft blue blanket hung from a chair like a little waterfall. Ollie’s paws were warm, and the wood floor was smooth under his feet. He twitched his whiskers and listened. Silence. Then—plink!

“Plink?” Ollie said. He looked up. Near the window, a small drip fell into a little metal pan. Plink. Plink. The pan was almost full of water.

“Oh no,” said Ollie. “Drips do not belong in a quiet attic. They need fixing.”

Ollie wanted to help. He felt a brave bubble in his chest. But he also remembered a lesson from Mama Otter: “Plan first, little one. Think, then do. And always tell the truth.” Ollie took a breath. “Okay,” he said. “I will make a plan.”

He sat on the blue blanket and spoke his plan out loud, tapping his paw for each step.

“Step one: Find the drip’s path.
Step two: Move things to stay dry.
Step three: Ask for help if I need it.
Step four: Tell the truth about what I find.”

Ollie smiled. “That is a good plan.”

He padded to the window. The glass was cool to his nose. He saw a tiny crack near the top, like a little smile that got too big. Wind whispered, and the drip went plink again. Ollie followed the water with his eyes. It slid along the window frame and dropped into the pan.

“Step one done,” said Ollie. “Now step two!”

He looked around. A cardboard box of picture books sat right next to the pan. “These must stay dry,” said Ollie. He wiggled his shoulders, hugged the box, and scooted it away—scoot, scoot. His paws made soft squeaky sounds on the floor. He moved a stuffed bear too. “Come on, Bear. You will be safe over here.”

Ollie checked the pan. It was very full now. “Uh-oh,” he said. “If it spills, the floor will be sad.” He looked for another pan. He found a small bucket with a smiley face. “Perfect!”

He took a deep breath and carefully slid the full pan out, set the empty bucket under the drip—plink!—and tiptoed the full pan to the stairs. The water sloshed. “Easy, easy,” Ollie whispered. He felt proud of his careful paws.

Just then, the attic door opened a crack. “Ollie?” called Grandma. Her voice was soft and warm like a cozy scarf. “Are you up there, dear?”

“Yes, Grandma!” Ollie called. “I found a problem, and I am fixing it. I made a plan!”

Grandma climbed up with gentle steps. She saw the bucket, the safe books, and the moved bear. “Goodness,” she said with a smile. “You are a thoughtful otter.”

“There is a crack,” said Ollie, pointing. “I wanted to fix it right away, but I’m not sure how. I can keep the drip in the bucket. And I can tell the truth about what I found.”

Grandma nodded. “That is good planning and good honesty. Grown-ups need to fix cracks. But you did the right steps.”

Ollie felt his chest grow warm with pride. “What is next?” he asked.

“Next,” said Grandma, “we make a quick, safe fix. Then we tell Grandpa, too.” She took a soft cloth and a small strip of tape from a drawer. “This will help for now.”

Ollie watched closely. “May I help, please?”

“Yes,” said Grandma. “You can hold the cloth.” The cloth felt cool and smooth in Ollie’s paws. Grandma gently pressed it against the tiny crack and added the tape. The drip slowed. Plink… pause… plink.

“It is calmer now,” said Ollie.

“Very much,” Grandma agreed. “Let’s write a note for Grandpa.” She gave Ollie a big pencil. It smelled like wood and lemon. Together they said the words, and Grandma wrote: “We found a tiny crack in the attic window. We moved the books and caught the drips. Please fix when you can. Love, Ollie and Grandma.”

Ollie read the note with his finger. “We told the truth,” he said.

“We did,” said Grandma, giving him a hug. Her sweater felt soft and fuzzy. “You planned, you solved the problem you could, and you were honest.”

Ollie’s whiskers twitched with happy wiggles. “I like planning,” he said. “It helps my brain feel neat.”

They carried the full pan down together and poured the water into the sink. Splash! It sounded like tiny rain on a drum. Back in the attic, the bucket went plink only now and then.

Grandma smiled. “Thank you, helper.”

Ollie smiled back. “Today’s adventure was in a quiet attic. I used my plan. I told the truth. And I kept the books dry!”

Grandma patted his head. “That is integrity, dear. That means doing the right thing, even when no one is looking.”

Ollie nodded. “I will remember: Think, plan, do, and tell the truth.”

The attic felt peaceful again. The golden stripes of sunlight were warm. Ollie took a deep, happy breath. His adventure was gentle, and his heart felt big and bright.

The End.
