# Melody Finds Her Feelings

Melody was a small singing bird with bright green feathers and a smooth golden beak. She lived in a cozy treehouse high in a friendly oak tree. The treehouse smelled like pine and sunshine. The wooden floor felt warm on her tiny feet. Little baskets, soft leaves, and shiny buttons lined the shelves.

Every morning, Melody sang, “Tweet-a-dee, tweet-a-doh!” Her song danced through the leaves. The wind hummed along.

One busy morning, Melody wanted to make a new song. “I will make a happy song, a calm song!” she chirped. She fluttered to her shelf for her favorite shiny button drum. But the shelf was messy. Buttons rolled. Ribbons tangled. Papers crinkled.

“Oh no,” whispered Melody. “Where is my drum? Where is my blue ribbon?” She looked in a basket. She looked under a cushion. She looked behind the tiny water cup. The treehouse felt loud and crowded. Her heart thumped fast. Her tummy felt twisty.

“I feel… funny,” said Melody. “I do not like this feeling.”

Peep, a little breeze, swished in through the round window and tickled her feathers. The window smelled like fresh leaves. Melody took a quick breath, but her eyes felt watery. She stomped one tiny foot. A button bounced and went plink-plink-plink across the floor.

“I can’t find anything!” she cried. “I can’t sing!”

Just then, wise Grandma Wren hopped onto the windowsill. She had soft brown feathers and gentle eyes. “Good morning, Melody,” she said in a warm, sweet voice. “I heard a loud plink-plink-plink. Are you okay?”

Melody sniffled. “Grandma Wren, I can’t find my drum. My room is a mess. My heart is going boom-boom too fast. My tummy is tight. I feel… I feel… mad and sad.”

Grandma Wren nodded. “Thank you for telling me. That feeling has a name. It is called frustration.”

“Fru… stra… tion?” Melody tried the big word slowly.

“Yes,” said Grandma Wren. “Frustration is when things are hard, and we want them to be easy. It makes our bodies feel busy. But we can help our bodies. We can be calm. Would you like to learn how?”

Melody wiped her eyes with a soft leaf. “Yes, please.”

Grandma Wren smiled. “Let’s do a belly breath. Put your wing on your belly.”

Melody placed her wing on her soft feathers. Her belly felt fluttery.

“In through your beak,” said Grandma Wren, “one… two… three.” They breathed in. The air smelled like sweet sap.

“And out slowly,” she said, “one… two… three.” They blew out soft. A ribbon moved in the gentle breath.

Melody’s heart slowed. The treehouse felt a little bigger. “Again?” she asked.

“Again,” said Grandma Wren.

They did three belly breaths. In… one, two, three. Out… one, two, three. Melody’s tummy untied. Her shoulders dropped.

“How do you feel now?” asked Grandma Wren.

“Calmer,” Melody said. “My body is softer.”

“Good,” said Grandma Wren. “Now let’s use our smart bird brains. We can organize. When we put things in places, we can find them. That helps our feelings too.”

Melody blinked. “Organize means putting things where they go?”

“Yes,” said Grandma Wren. “We will make a plan. First, we sing a tidy-up song. Then, we sort. Buttons in the button basket. Ribbons in the ribbon jar. Papers in the paper box. Ready?”

Melody’s beak curved into a smile. “Ready.”

They sang together, soft and steady:
“Tidy, tidy, one by one,
Sort and sing till we are done.
Buttons here, ribbons there,
Paper, pencils, everywhere they share!”

Melody picked up a shiny blue button. “Button in the basket!” Plop!

She folded a crinkly paper. “Paper in the box!” Rustle!

She rolled a ribbon like a tiny snake. “Ribbon in the jar!” Swish!

The room began to look neat. The floor felt smooth under her feet. The air smelled fresh again. Melody’s chest felt light.

“Look, Grandma Wren!” Melody chirped. “I found my button drum!” It sparkled in the sun.

Grandma Wren clapped her wings. “You did it! How do you feel now?”

Melody paused. She put her wing on her belly again. “I feel… proud. I feel calm.”

“That is emotional awareness,” said Grandma Wren kindly. “You noticed your feelings, you named them, and you chose a calm plan. Your feelings are important. Your knowledge helps you.”

Melody nodded. “When I feel frustration, I can breathe, name it, and organize.”

“Yes,” said Grandma Wren. “And you can ask for help.”

Melody tapped her button drum—tap-tap, tap. She added two light claps—clap-clap. Then she sang, “I was mad and sad, I know. Belly breaths made it slow. Sort and sing, tidy too. Now my heart feels bright and true!”

The song floated out the window and into the leaves. It sounded warm and shiny.

Grandma Wren smiled. “That is a beautiful calm song.”

Melody beamed. “Thank you for teaching me. I will keep my treehouse neat. I will listen to my feelings. I will use my breath and my brain.”

The sun slid across the floor in golden stripes. The baskets sat in a tidy row. Melody’s treehouse felt safe and happy. She tapped her drum one last time and whispered, “I can do hard things, one… two… three.”

The End.
