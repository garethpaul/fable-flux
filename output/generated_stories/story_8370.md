# Finn Listens to His Feelings

In a quiet attic under a sloping roof, a curious fox kit named Finn peeked around dusty boxes. The air smelled like old paper and cedar. Sunlight slipped through a tiny round window and made a bright circle on the wooden floor. Finn’s paws made soft tap-tap sounds as he explored.

“Oh! A jump rope!” Finn said, lifting a red-and-blue rope from a trunk. “I want to jump fast! I want to jump high!”

He placed the rope on the floor and wagged his fluffy tail. Finn took a big breath. He felt excited. His tummy fluttered like a small bird. “Ready, set, jump!” he said.

He swung the rope. Thump—tangle! The rope caught on his back paw. Finn stumbled and sat down with a little “oof.”

His cheeks felt hot. His ears drooped. “I can’t do it!” Finn grumped. “I want to be good right now.”

The attic was very quiet. Finn could hear a clock tick-tick-tick and a soft breeze whoosh at the window. His heart went thump-thump-thump a little faster. He felt tight inside, like a knot.

Just then, a tiny moth fluttered by the window. Its wings made a whispery ffffft-fffft sound. The moth landed on a box and sat very still, opening and closing its powdery wings.

Finn watched. His whiskers twitched. “Hello, little flutter friend,” he said softly. “You are patient.”

He looked back at the rope. “I feel mad,” Finn said to himself. He pressed a paw to his chest. “My chest feels hot. My belly feels twisty. My face feels scrunchy. This feeling is called mad.”

He remembered what Mama Fox always said: “Name it to tame it.” So Finn whispered, “I feel mad. I feel stuck. I want to jump, but it is hard.”

A small, warm tear slid down his fur. Plink. It made a tiny dark spot on the wood. Finn sniffed. The attic smelled a little like cinnamon from an old box. The smell made him feel cozy for a moment.

“I can take a calm breath,” Finn said. He put a paw on his belly. “Smell the flower…” He breathed in. “Blow the candle…” He breathed out. The moth fluttered once, as if cheering.

Finn noticed his body. “My shoulders are softer now,” he said. “My face is not so tight.” He waited and listened to the tick-tick-tick of the clock. He felt the smooth wood under his paws. He heard a pine cone roll in a corner, bump-bump.

He looked at the rope again. “Maybe I can try a little bit,” he said. “I can be patient with me.”

Finn decided to practice in small steps. “Step one: swing the rope slow.” He held the handles and swung the rope in a gentle circle. Swish. Swish. The rope touched the floor with a soft tap.

“Step two: bunny hops!” Finn bent his knees and did tiny hops without the rope. Hop. Hop. Hop. His paws made light pat-pat sounds. He smiled a little. “My legs are waking up,” he said.

“Step three: one jump with the rope,” Finn told himself. He swung the rope and hopped once. Tap—jump! He landed with a wobble but stayed up. His tail poofed with pride. “I did one!”

He listened to his heart. “I feel proud and a little scared. That’s okay,” he said. “Feelings can be friends that talk to me.”

He tried again. Tap—jump! Tap—jump! Tangle—plop! He fell on his bottom and frowned. The hot feeling tickled his chest again. “Hello, mad,” Finn said kindly. “You came back. I will listen.”

Finn put the rope down and walked in a slow circle, picking up his knees like marching feet. Thump-thump, thump-thump. “This is exercise to help my feelings move,” he said. He reached his paws up to the ceiling. Stretch! He touched his toes. Bend! He shook out his tail. Wiggle-wiggle!

After moving, Finn took two calm breaths. “Smell the flower. Blow the candle.” The moth lifted off the box and floated near the window again, ffffft-fffft, as if dancing with him.

“Now I am ready to try,” Finn said. He picked up the rope. “Slow and steady. Patient and kind.”

Swish. Tap—jump! Tap—jump! Tap—jump! Finn counted, “One… two… three… four!” The rope and his paws made a happy rhythm. His ears perked up. His fur felt cool now, and his tummy felt steady like a smooth stone.

“I did four!” Finn laughed. “I did four jumps!”

He set the rope down and petted it like a friend. “Thank you, rope. Thank you, body. Thank you, feelings, for talking to me.”

The attic felt bright, even though the light was the same. The air smelled like wood and a little like sunshine. Finn saw the tiny moth rest on the window again. “You were patient, too,” he said. “You waited and watched.”

Finn sat on a soft old blanket. He hugged his knees and smiled. “Today I learned something,” he said. “When I feel mad or stuck, I can name my feeling. I can take calm breaths. I can move my body. I can try small steps. With patience, I can learn new things.”

The clock went tick-tick. Finn stood up and did four more hops, just for joy. Hop! Hop! Hop! Hop! His giggle filled the quiet attic.

“I listened to my feelings,” he said. “And I kept going.”

The End.
