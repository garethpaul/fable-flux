# Kiki Joey’s Bedtime Plan

Kiki the kangaroo joey woke up in her cozy bedroom with a happy bounce in her belly. Morning light peeked through the curtains, making soft stripes on the rug. Her room smelled like clean blankets and a little bit like lemon from the fresh laundry. On the shelf, her books stood tall. On the floor, her blocks waited like tiny, colorful mountains.

“Mama, can we read my bedtime book tonight?” Kiki asked at breakfast. “The shiny star one?”

“Of course,” said Mama Roo, smiling. “But remember, we need extra calm tonight. We have an early day tomorrow.”

Kiki nodded. She wanted bedtime to be smooth and happy. She wanted to help. “I can make a plan,” she whispered to herself, feeling a warm fizz of excitement. “I’ll start now.”

After playtime, Kiki hopped back to her cozy bedroom. She looked around. Her blocks were scattered. Her pajamas were in a little heap. Her stuffed koala, Coco, was under the pillow, peeking out with a fuzzy gray ear.

Kiki took a big breath. She felt the soft carpet under her toes. She heard the gentle hum of the fan. “I can do it,” she said. “I can start without being asked.” She picked up the blocks, one by one. Clack, clack, clack, into the bin they went. She folded her pajamas and set them on the bed. She fluffed the pillow and tucked Coco on top.

Just then, her big cousin, Tessa, hopped in. “Hi, Kiki! Want to build a rocket with the blocks?” Tessa’s eyes were bright.

Kiki looked at the block bin. She wanted to play. But she also wanted bedtime to be easy. She put a paw on her chest and remembered her plan. “I started cleaning, Tessa,” she said. “I want to help Mama. We can take turns. First clean, then play a little, then read.”

Tessa nodded. “Okay! I can help. I’ll take turns with you. You put in the red blocks, and I’ll do the blue ones.”

“Deal,” said Kiki.

They worked together. Their paws moved softly. The bin made a gentle thunk as blocks landed. When they finished, Tessa grinned. “Now a tiny rocket?”

Kiki thought. She wanted to be honest. “We said clean first, then play a little,” she said. “Just five pieces. Then we put them back. Promise?”

“Promise,” said Tessa.

They built a small, shiny rocket with a silver block on top. “Zoom!” whispered Kiki. The rocket made a soft whoosh as they lifted it. They giggled, then put the five blocks back, just like they promised.

Mama Roo peeked in. “I see you took initiative,” she said. “You started helping all on your own.”

Kiki’s cheeks felt warm and proud. “I made a plan,” she said. “I want bedtime to be cozy.”

After dinner, Kiki went to her bedroom and touched the book with the shiny star on the cover. She wanted it to be ready. But then she saw something. Her crayons were on the dresser, without the lid. One purple crayon lay on the floor, like a sleepy worm.

Kiki picked up the purple crayon. “Oh no,” she said softly. She remembered drawing yesterday. “I forgot to put the lid on. I will fix it.” She snapped the lid on with a tiny click. She put all the crayons in the box. “I tell the truth,” she added. “Mama, I left the crayons out, but I fixed it now.”

“Thank you for your integrity,” said Mama Roo, rubbing Kiki’s ears. “Telling the truth and making it right is brave.”

Later, when it was almost bedtime, Kiki and Tessa both wanted to hold the shiny star book first. Their paws touched the cover at the same time.

“I want to hold it,” said Tessa.

“I want to hold it too,” said Kiki. Her tummy felt tight. She took a slow breath. “Let’s use peaceful words,” she said. “We can take turns. I will hold it to open, and you can turn the pages.”

Tessa’s ears wiggled. “Okay,” she said. “That sounds fair.”

They smiled. Kiki opened the book. Tessa turned the pages with soft, careful fingers. The paper made a whisper sound, swish, swish. The room smelled like warm soap and the sweet wool of their blankets. Outside, crickets sang a quiet song.

When Mama read the last page, Kiki felt sleepy and safe. She thought about her day. She had started cleaning without being asked. She had told the truth about the crayons. She had taken turns with Tessa and used calm words. Once, when a block tower fell, she felt sad, but she tried again. She was resilient.

Kiki hugged Coco and whispered, “I can begin. I can be honest. I can share turns.” Her heart felt like a gentle hop.

Mama tucked the blanket under Kiki’s chin. “What did you learn today, my joyful joey?”

Kiki smiled. “When I start helping on my own, everything feels better. Taking turns is fair. Telling the truth is brave. And if something falls, I can try again.”

Mama kissed her forehead. “That’s the spirit.”

The bedroom grew quiet and cozy. The fan hummed. The curtains swayed. Kiki closed her eyes, proud of her plan and her brave, kind heart.

The End.
