# Benji Bear Tries a New Story

Benji Bear was a friendly little bear cub with soft, fuzzy fur and a round, warm nose. Every night, he snuggled into his cozy bedroom. His quilt was patchy and bright. His pillow smelled like pine and sunshine. A tiny nightlight glowed like a firefly on his shelf.

“Tonight is story night!” Benji cheered, bouncing on his bed. His paws made gentle thumps on the soft blankets.

Mama Bear peeked in with a smile. “What kind of story would you like, Benji?”

Benji’s ears wiggled. “A honey story! Always a honey story!” He loved stories about honey—sticky, sweet, and golden.

Mama sat beside him and stroked his tiny ear. “Honey stories are lovely,” she said. “But would you like to try a different kind of story tonight? Maybe a story about music, or stars, or a brave little mouse?”

Benji’s nose scrunched. “I don’t know,” he said. “I only like honey stories.”

Mama Bear nodded kindly. “That’s okay. But sometimes, trying something new is good for our minds. It helps us be open-minded. Open-minded means we listen and learn, even when it’s different.”

Benji hugged his quilt. The nightlight made a soft golden glow. He could hear crickets singing outside the window. “What if the story isn’t fun?” he whispered.

Mama squeezed his paw. “We can try. If you don’t like it after a little bit, we can stop.”

Benji took a deep breath. He could smell cinnamon from the bakery downstairs. “Okay,” he said. “I can try.”

Mama smiled. “Wonderful. How about a story you help tell?”

“Me?” Benji’s eyes grew wide. “I can try to tell a story too?”

“Yes,” Mama said. “We can practice together.”

Benji sat up tall. “Okay,” he said. “Let’s try.”

Mama began in a gentle voice. “Once upon a time, in a cozy bedroom just like yours, a little bear cub found a small drum under the bed.”

Benji’s eyes sparkled. “What color is the drum?”

“You tell me,” Mama said.

“Red like a strawberry!” Benji giggled.

“Red like a strawberry,” Mama repeated. “The bear tapped it—tap, tap, tap.” She drummed her fingers on the blanket. Tap, tap, tap. The sound was soft and happy.

Benji copied her. Tap, tap, tap. It felt good. “What happens next?” he asked.

“You tell me,” Mama said again.

Benji blinked. “I’m not sure,” he whispered.

“That’s okay,” Mama said. “We practice. Practice means we try again and again. We don’t have to be perfect.”

Benji took another breath. He looked at his quilt. He felt the warm blanket on his paws. He listened to the crickets. “Maybe,” he said slowly, “the little bear hears—stars?”

“Stars?” Mama asked softly.

Benji nodded. “Yes. The stars make a tiny twinkle song.”

Mama’s eyes shone. “Beautiful. The twinkle song goes ‘ting, ting, ting.’”

Benji chimed, “Ting, ting, ting.” His voice was like a bell.

“The little bear taps the drum with the star song,” Mama said. “Tap-ting, tap-ting, tap-ting!”

Benji giggled so hard his belly shook. “Tap-ting, tap-ting, tap-ting!”

“The bear wonders,” Mama continued, “if anyone else can hear the music.”

Benji thought. He remembered being open-minded. “Maybe a shy mouse hears it,” he said. “The mouse likes the song but feels nervous.”

Mama nodded. “The little bear smiles and says, ‘Would you like to play with me?’”

Benji made his voice gentle. “Would you like to play with me?”

“The mouse says, ‘I can try,’” Mama said.

Benji whispered, “Open-minded.”

They both smiled.

Benji added, “The mouse has a tiny bell. It goes ding!”

“Ding!” Mama said. “The bear and the mouse make a new song together. Tap-ting, ding! Tap-ting, ding!”

Benji clapped softly. Clap, clap! “This is not a honey story,” he said, surprised. “But I like it!”

Mama stroked his fur. “You were open-minded and tried something new. You also practiced telling the story.”

Benji felt proud. His cheeks felt warm and happy. “Can I tell more?” he asked.

“Yes,” Mama said. “What happens next?”

Benji thought again. He wanted to be brave and try. “A gentle wind comes in the window,” he said. “It smells like pine trees. The wind whispers, ‘Whooosh.’ The wind is part of the band now!”

Mama laughed softly. “Whooosh. The nightlight glows like a tiny moon. The room feels safe and cozy.”

Benji leaned into Mama’s side. “The bear, the mouse, the wind, and the stars all play softly. They make sleepy music.”

He closed his eyes and tapped once more. Tap. “The little bear says, ‘Thank you, new story.’”

Mama kissed his forehead. “And the little bear learns that trying new things can be sweet too,” she said, “just like honey.”

Benji smiled. “I was open-minded,” he said proudly. “And I practiced. And I told a story!”

“Yes,” Mama whispered. “You did all three.”

Benji yawned a big, gentle yawn. The cozy bedroom hummed with quiet. Crickets sang. The nightlight glowed. The quilt felt like a warm hug. “Tomorrow,” he murmured, “we can try another new story.”

Mama tucked him in. “Tomorrow,” she agreed.

Benji drifted to sleep, happy and brave, with music and stars in his dreams. He knew that being open-minded helped him learn and grow. He knew that practicing made him better. And he knew that stories could come from anywhere, even from a tiny bell and a strawberry-red drum.

The End.
