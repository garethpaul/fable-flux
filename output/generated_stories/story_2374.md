# Joey Jumps to the Beat of the Baskets

In a sunny laundry room, a joyful kangaroo joey named Kip bounced in with a big smile and a tiny notebook. The room smelled like lemon soap and warm cotton. The washing machine hummed, “whirr-whirr,” and the dryer sang, “rumble-rumble.” Baskets sat like little hills, full of soft shirts, socks, and towels.

“Today I will be a leader,” Kip said, patting his notebook. “I will write my plan. I will use my time well!”

Kip flipped the notebook open. He held a short blue crayon. “Plan!” he said aloud, tapping the page. “One: Sort. Two: Wash. Three: Fold. Four: Play.” He drew simple shapes—a circle for sort, a wavy line for wash, a square for fold, and a star for play. He smiled. “I can do it!”

Mama Roo hopped in with a basket that smelled like sunshine. “Hello, Kip,” she said, kissing his head. “What is your plan?”

Kip pointed proudly. “Plan! Sort, wash, fold, play. I will lead. I will use time.”

Mama Roo nodded. “Good plan, Captain Kip. I will follow your lead.”

Kip puffed up like a soft pillow. “Team, let’s start!” He clapped. “We sort first.” He sniffed a shirt. “Smells like grass.” He giggled. “Green pile!” He listened to the socks. “Quiet socks go together.” He rubbed a towel on his cheek. “So soft!”

Kip placed a little clock on the shelf. Tick, tock. Tick, tock. “We have five little ticks to sort,” he said. “When the big hand goes to the dot, we switch.”

Mama Roo smiled. “I like your time plan.”

They sorted. Kip led. “Light clothes here. Dark clothes there. Tiny socks in the sock spot.” He made it a game. “Hop-hop to the right spot! Hop-hop to the left spot!”

The clock went tick, tock, tick, tock. Kip looked up. “Time!” he called. “We switch to washing.” He opened the washer. It smelled like clean rain. “In go the clothes,” he said. Plop, plop, plop. He pushed the buttons. “Beep!” The machine began to swish and sing.

Kip wrote in his notebook, saying the words as he wrote. “Sort—done! Wash—on!” His letters were bumpy and big, but he felt proud. “Writing helps me lead,” he said.

While they waited, Kip saw a basket of colorful socks. “I want to make a tall sock tower!” he cried. He stacked socks high. “Up, up, up!” The tower wobbled. Socks fell—flump!—onto the floor.

The clock ticked. Kip frowned. “Oh no. I forgot the time.” He looked at his plan. “After wash, we fold. I used too much time on the sock tower.” He sighed and his ears drooped. “I made a mistake.”

Mama Roo put a gentle paw on his shoulder. “It’s okay to make mistakes,” she said softly. “We can forgive ourselves. We can fix it. What does your plan say now?”

Kip took a deep breath. He looked at the clock. He wrote a new line, slowly and carefully. “Five ticks to fold,” he said. “I will try again. Practice helps.”

He clapped. “Team, folding time! I will lead with a song. Songs help us keep time.”

Kip sang, “Fold, fold, nice and neat. Fold, fold, feel the beat.” Mama Roo sang too. The dryer rumbled like a drum. The warm towels made a soft hush by Kip’s paws. He folded a tiny shirt. “Side, side, flip,” he said. He folded a towel. “Big hug fold.” He matched socks in pairs. “Two friends together.”

The clock ticked kindly. Kip watched it, then nodded. “We have two ticks left,” he said. “We can finish the last socks.” He looked at Mama Roo. “Thank you for following my plan.”

“You are a kind leader,” Mama Roo said. “You use your time. You forgive mistakes. You practice.”

Kip’s cheeks felt warm with pride. He wrote again. “Fold—done!” He drew a happy face. He looked at the last step. “Four: Play!”

He put the notebook down and checked the room. Baskets were empty. Shelves were neat. The air smelled like clean cotton and lemon. “We did it on time,” Kip said, hopping in a small circle. “Plan, practice, and kindness!”

Mama Roo opened the window. A soft breeze floated in. “Play time,” she said.

Kip giggled. “Hopscotch with towels!” He laid towels in a line. “One, two, three—hop!” He paused and looked at the clock. “I can play for three ticks,” he said. “Then I will put the towels away.”

They hopped together, laughing. The breeze tickled their ears. When the clock ticked three little ticks, Kip clapped. “Time!” he said. “We stop and clean up.” He folded the towels fast but neat. “I can have fun and still use time.”

He wrote one last line in his notebook. “Lesson: Make a plan. Watch the clock. Be a kind leader. Practice and forgive. Then there is time for play.”

Kip hugged Mama Roo. “I like my plan,” he said. “Time helps me do things and have fun.”

Mama Roo hugged back. “And you help us all.”

Kip’s tail thumped happily. He looked around the clean, warm room and took a deep breath. Lemon, cotton, and a little bit of joy.

“Plan, practice, and kindness,” he whispered. “I can do it every day.”

The End.
