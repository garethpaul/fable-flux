# Polly’s Quiet Feathers

Polly was a creative parrot who loved colors and words. She lived in a cozy treehouse high in a friendly oak. The treehouse smelled like pine and crayons. A soft rug felt fuzzy under Polly’s feet. The breeze peeked through the window and made the leaves whisper, shhh, shhh.

Polly had a bright blue notebook and a purple pen. She liked to write and draw stories in her notebook. “Squawk! Today I write a story about rainbows,” Polly chirped. She fluffed her green and gold feathers and settled on a small pillow by the window.

Polly started to write. Scritch-scratch went the pen. “Once upon a time,” she whispered. The sun made warm stripes on the floor. Polly smiled and wrote more. “A rainbow sailed in the sky like a boat.”

Suddenly, Tap! Tap! Tap! Knock-knock came from the ladder. “Polly! Can we come in?” called Max the squirrel.

“Me too!” squeaked Dot the mouse. “I brought crunchy crackers!”

Polly blinked. She liked her friends. She also liked quiet when she wrote. Her tummy felt twisty. She wanted to share, but she also wanted space. She remembered what Mama Parrot had said: “Your feelings matter. Your space matters. Use kind words to make a boundary.”

Polly took a breath. “Hi, Max! Hi, Dot!” she called. “I’m happy to see you. I am writing right now. I need quiet.”

Max popped inside anyway. His tail swished. “I won’t be loud,” he said, and he jumped on the rug with a soft thump.

Dot scooted in with her cracker bag. Crunch, crunch, crunch! “I can be quiet,” she whispered, but the bag went crinkle-crinkle.

Polly’s pen stopped. Scritch-scratch turned to nothing. Her feathers drooped. “I feel upset,” Polly said softly. “I asked for quiet, and my body feels tight.”

Max froze. Dot’s ears perked up. “Oh,” said Max. “I didn’t know your quiet was a rule.”

Polly nodded. “It is my boundary,” she said. “A boundary is a kind rule about my body, my things, and my space. It helps me feel safe. I need quiet time to write.”

Max looked at the blue notebook. Dot looked at the purple pen. “We can try,” said Dot. “But we are wiggly.”

Polly thought of a plan. She liked plans. Plans were strategies. Strategies helped everyone. “Let’s make a quiet corner and a sharing corner,” Polly said. “Quiet corner is for writing. Sharing corner is for talking and snacks.”

“How will we know?” asked Max.

Polly smiled. She drew a big red line on a sticky note and stuck it on a small sign. “Red feather means hush,” she said. Then she drew a green check on another sticky note. “Green leaf means we can talk.”

Polly put the red feather sign by the pillow near the window. She put the green leaf sign by the rug and the snack basket. “When the red feather is up, I need quiet,” she explained. “When the green leaf is up, we can share and chat.”

Max nodded. “We can do that.”

Dot nodded too. “Crunch later,” she whispered, closing the cracker bag.

Polly lifted the red feather sign. The treehouse grew calm. The breeze said shhh. Max curled like a little comma near the door. Dot hummed a tiny tune in her head but stayed still. Polly’s body unwound. Her feathers smoothed. Scritch-scratch, went her pen again. “The rainbow boat sails to a gentle sun,” she wrote.

After a little while, Polly looked up. Her story had a happy middle. She felt proud. “Thank you for helping,” she said. “Now I am ready to share.” She lifted the green leaf sign.

Max stretched. Dot grinned. “Snack time!” Dot squeaked, opening the bag. Crunch-crunch! “Can you read your story?” asked Max.

Polly nodded. “Yes. I want to share. Sharing is fun when I feel safe.” She read in a warm voice, “The rainbow boat sails to a gentle sun, and everyone listens to the quiet waves.” Max clapped his paws. Dot’s whiskers wiggled. “I love it,” said Dot. “Your words feel like soft clouds.”

“Thank you,” said Polly. “I feel happy and calm.”

Max looked at the signs. “I like your strategy,” he said. “Next time I will ask, ‘Is it red feather time or green leaf time?’”

Dot nibbled a cracker. “And I will wait until green leaf time to crunch,” she said.

Polly beamed. “My boundary helped me do my best. Boundaries are kind. Boundaries keep our hearts comfy.”

They all took crayons and drew pictures of the rainbow boat. They took turns with the purple pen. “May I use it?” asked Max.

“Yes,” said Polly, “for three minutes.” She set a tiny leaf timer. They shared and laughed. When the timer rang, Max passed the pen to Dot. “Your turn,” he said.

The sky turned peach. The leaves whispered good night. Polly put up the red feather one more time. “Quiet for tidy time,” she said. Max and Dot nodded and folded the rug. The treehouse felt neat and peaceful.

Polly fluffed her feathers. “Today I learned a big thing,” she said. “It is okay to say what I need. I can make a boundary with kind words. I can make a plan so we can all share.”

Max and Dot smiled. “We learned to listen,” said Max. “We can follow your signs.”

“We are a good team,” said Dot.

Polly tucked her notebook away and hugged her friends with her wings. “Yes,” she chirped. “When we respect each other’s boundaries, we all feel safe, we all can create, and we all can have fun.”

The End.
