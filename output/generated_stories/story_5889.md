# Tilly the Turtle Tries

Tilly was a shy turtle. She lived with her family in a cozy house. One sunny afternoon, the family dining room was full of happy sounds. Chairs scooted. Plates clinked. The big window let in warm light. On the wooden table sat a shiny silver bowl and a bright red whistle.

Mama set down a basket of soft rolls. “Game time after lunch!” she said, smiling. “We’ll play Spoonball right here in the dining room.”

Tilly peeked from behind her shell. “Spoon…ball?” she whispered.

Papa tapped a spoon on the table. It made a clear, ringing ting! “Yes, Tilly. Spoonball is a gentle, fun sport. We use spoons to tap a soft ball around the table.”

Tilly’s big brown eyes grew wide. “I’m shy,” she murmured. Her cheeks felt warm. Her tummy fluttered like tiny butterflies.

Her brother Max bounced a squishy, colorful ball in his hands. Boing, boing. “Want to try with me, Tilly?” he asked kindly.

Tilly tucked her head a little. “I don’t know.”

Grandma slid a napkin to Tilly. “You can watch first,” Grandma said in a soft voice. “Then you can choose. That is using initiative—choosing to start when you feel ready.”

Tilly rubbed the napkin between her small feet. It felt smooth. She looked at the shiny spoons. She listened to the gentle ting ting ting. The dining room smelled like warm rolls and sweet jam. The soft ball felt friendly when she touched it. It was not scary. It made a quiet thump when it landed on the table.

“Ready?” asked Mama, tying a little ribbon around the whistle. “We will be kind. We will cheer for everyone. Sports can be gentle. Sports can be fun. Sports can be for shy turtles, too.”

Tilly’s heart pitter-pattered. She wanted to hide. But she also wanted to try. She took a deep breath. In. Out. “I will watch first,” she said.

The family began. Tap. Tap. TAP! The spoons kissed the ball. The ball rolled across the smooth table, around the basket of rolls, past the salt shaker. Everyone giggled and clapped. “Nice tap, Max!” “Great try, Papa!”

Tilly watched carefully. She learned how the ball moved. She saw how to hold the spoon. She listened to the rhythm. Tap… roll… tap-tap!

Max waved. “Your turn if you want, Tilly.”

Tilly felt the butterflies again. Then she felt something else—like a tiny light in her chest. She remembered Grandma’s words. Choose to start. Try a little. That is initiative.

“I will try one small tap,” Tilly said. Her voice was quiet, but it was brave.

Mama smiled and slid a spoon to Tilly. The handle was cool and smooth in Tilly’s hand. The ball rolled toward her. It made a soft shhh across the table. Tilly lifted the spoon. She tapped—ting!

The ball rolled to Max. Everyone clapped gently. “Yay, Tilly!” said Papa. “That was a strong start.”

Tilly blinked. She felt a tiny bubble of pride. “I did it,” she whispered.

“Want to try two taps?” Max asked.

Tilly nodded. The ball came back. Tap… tap! The spoons sang again. Ting-ting! The ball rolled around the bowl and back to Tilly. She tapped with a little more pep. Ting!

Grandma blew the ribboned whistle—fwee! “Score for Team Turtle!” she cheered.

Tilly laughed. The butterflies flew away. Her shell felt lighter. She even peeked her head all the way out. “I like Spoonball,” she said.

They played again and again. They made silly rules. “Tap with your eyes closed for one count.” “Tap while humming.” The dining room was full of soft sounds and happy smells. The table was a tiny stadium. The napkins became little flags. They waved them and chanted, “Go, Tilly, go!”

Then the ball rolled off the table—plop! It bounced under a chair. Tilly paused. Everyone looked around.

“I can get it,” Tilly said, before anyone asked. She slid off her chair. Her feet touched the cool floor. She crawled under the chair. It was a little dusty. It smelled like lemon from the clean floor. She reached out. Her shell bumped the chair leg—thunk. She did not stop. She picked up the ball and held it to her chest. “Got it!”

She climbed back up and placed the ball on the table. “I took initiative,” Tilly said proudly. The word felt big, but it felt good.

Max smiled. “Thanks, Tilly. That helped the team.”

Mama nodded. “When you choose to start, you help yourself and others.”

They played one last round. Tilly tried a gentle spin tap. The ball curved around the silver bowl, rolled past the jam, and stopped right in front of Grandma. “Wow!” said Grandma. “That was clever and calm.”

When the game ended, everyone clapped for everyone. “Good taps, Papa!” “Nice saves, Max!” “Great initiative, Tilly!”

Tilly hugged the soft ball. Her shy heart felt brave now. She had watched, then tried, then helped. Sports could be gentle. Sports could be kind. And there was room for everyone at the table.

Tilly whispered, “I can start small. I can try new things. I can help.” She smiled at the shiny spoon and the happy faces.

And the dining room felt extra warm.

The End.
