# Pippa the Penguin Says “Please”

Pippa the cheerful penguin waddled into the comfortable nursery with a happy hum. The room felt cozy and warm. Sunlight made zigzag patterns on the colorful rug. Soft music played from a little speaker. Crayons, blocks, and picture books waited on low shelves. The air smelled like crayons and warm cocoa.

“Good morning!” Pippa chirped, flapping her tiny wings. “I’m ready for a kind day!”

Her friends were already there. Mateo stacked blocks. Aisha drew with bright crayons. Jun tied ribbons on a teddy bear. Mr. Ravi, the teacher, smiled and said, “Hello, Pippa. Today we will share, listen, and learn about each other.”

Pippa wiggled with joy. “I love sharing! I love listening!”

On the rug sat a round drum with a shiny top and a basket of soft scarves in many colors. Pippa’s eyes sparkled. “Ooh! Can I try the drum?”

Mateo reached for it too. “I want to try!”

Pippa paused. Her heart felt bouncy and excited. She remembered Mr. Ravi’s gentle words: respect means we care about others’ feelings. She took a deep breath. “Mateo, we both want it. Let’s take turns. I can go after you.”

Mateo smiled with relief. “Thanks, Pippa.”

Pippa smiled back. “You’re welcome.” She sat beside him and listened. Mateo tapped softly—tap, tap—like raindrops. Pippa swayed to the rhythm. The sound felt round and smooth, like a gentle heartbeat.

When it was Pippa’s turn, she asked, “Is it okay if I play now?” Mateo nodded. Pippa tapped tap-tap-tap, then paused to listen to the drum’s echo. “Thank you for waiting,” she told herself softly. “Asking and waiting is kind.”

Mr. Ravi clapped quietly. “Wonderful turn-taking. That is respect.”

Soon it was art time. The table was set with paper, crayons, and stickers. Aisha drew a bright sun and a tiny house. Jun drew a boat and wrote his name carefully. Pippa wanted to draw penguins dancing.

She reached for a shiny gold sticker. At the same time, Aisha reached too. Their flippers and fingers touched. Pippa pulled back. “Oops! You go first, Aisha.”

Aisha shook her head. “We can share. Let’s both use gold!”

Pippa felt warm inside. “Thank you.” She took one sticker and passed the sheet to Aisha with a smile. The gold glowed on the paper like a happy star.

After art, Mr. Ravi said, “Today is our World Circle. We will learn words from our homes and share snacks from different places. When we listen, we show respect. When we speak up kindly, that is being assertive.”

Pippa patted her belly. “Snacks sound yummy!”

They sat in a circle on the soft rug. Mr. Ravi rang a tiny bell that chimed like a whispering spoon. “We will pass the talking shell. When you hold the shell, you may speak. If you have a need, use your brave, kind voice.”

He handed the smooth shell to Jun. Jun held it and said, “In Korea, we say annyeong to say hello.” He bowed a little. Everyone waved and said, “Annyeong, Jun!” Jun smiled, his eyes bright.

The shell went to Aisha. “In Arabic, we say marhaba.” She stretched the sound like a friendly ribbon. Everyone tried it—“Mar-ha-ba!” Aisha giggled.

Mateo held the shell next. “In Spanish, we say hola!” He lifted his hand. “Hola!” the class sang together.

Pippa hugged her flippers around herself. She felt shy, like a snowflake hiding in a mitten. “What can I share?” she wondered. “I’m a penguin from a far, cold place.” When the shell reached her, she rubbed its smooth edge and took a calm breath.

“In my home, the air feels icy,” she said. “We slide on our bellies. We say hello with happy chirps.” Pippa chirped softly—“Chirp-chirp!” The class listened. Some friends chirped, too. The room filled with sweet, tiny sounds. Pippa’s cheeks felt tingly and proud.

Mr. Ravi nodded. “Thank you, Pippa. You were brave and kind. We respect each other by listening.”

Snack time came with crunchy crackers, sweet mango slices, and little seaweed squares. The scents mixed together—salty, fruity, crispy. Pippa reached for the mango, then paused. “Does anyone want mango first?” she asked.

Jun smiled. “You can go ahead.”

Pippa took one slice and passed the bowl. “Thank you,” she said.

Across the table, Mateo reached for three crackers at once. Aisha looked worried. “I only see two left,” she whispered.

Pippa noticed and felt a small flutter. This was a chance to use her kind voice. She stood a little taller. “Mateo,” she said gently, “I want to be fair. Could you please take two crackers so Aisha can have some, too?”

Mateo blinked, then nodded. “Oh! I didn’t see. Thank you for telling me.” He put one cracker back. Aisha smiled. “Thanks, Mateo.” The table felt calm again, like smooth water.

Later, during quiet play, Pippa cuddled a soft polar bear puppet. Jun reached for it without asking. Pippa held it close and said, clear and calm, “I’m using this now. You can have it when I’m done.” Jun froze, then relaxed. “Okay. I’ll play with blocks and wait.” Pippa smiled and added, “Thank you for waiting. I’ll tell you when it’s your turn.”

When cleanup music played, everyone sang and put toys away. Pippa found the gold sticker sheet on the floor. She picked it up and asked, “Whose is this?” Aisha raised her hand. Pippa gave it back and said, “Here you go.” Aisha beamed. “Thanks for asking.”

Before goodbye, Mr. Ravi asked, “What did we learn today?”

Pippa flapped her wings. “Respect means we listen, take turns, and care about others’ feelings. Being brave and kind is called assertive. We can say what we need without hurting anyone.”

Mr. Ravi smiled. “Yes. And we can enjoy the big, beautiful world together.”

Pippa waddled to the door with a happy heart. The nursery smelled like crayons again. The rug felt soft under her feet. She chirped to her friends, “Hola! Marhaba! Annyeong! Chirp-chirp!” Everyone laughed and waved.

Pippa whispered, “I can be kind and brave, every day.”

The End.
