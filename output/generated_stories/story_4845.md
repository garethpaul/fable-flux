# Lio the Lion Cub and the Treehouse Talk

Lio was a brave little lion cub with bright golden fur and a curious nose. He lived near a big, friendly tree. High in its branches sat a wooden treehouse that smelled like pine and sunshine. The floor felt smooth under Lio’s paws, and a soft breeze tickled his whiskers. Birds sang, “Chee-chee!” and leaves whispered like quiet rain.

“This is my favorite place,” Lio said proudly. “It is high and happy!”

One morning, Lio climbed the ladder with his strong little paws. He carried a small bucket. Inside were shiny pebbles, a red ribbon, and a tiny bell that chimed, “ting-ting!”

At the top, Lio pushed open the creaky door. “I will decorate!” he said. “I want my treehouse to sparkle!”

Lio tied the ribbon to a branch. He hung the bell near the window. He lined the pebbles along the railing. “Perfect,” he purred.

Just then, a gentle voice floated from the leaves. “Hello, little one.”

Lio blinked. “Who said that?”

A long green gecko peeked from the wall. “I’m Gigi,” she said, flicking her tiny pink tongue. “This is my sunny wall.”

Lio tilted his head. “Your wall? But this is my treehouse.”

Gigi smiled and tapped the wall with her foot. “I nap here when the sun is warm. It feels nice on my tummy.”

Lio’s ears twitched. “Oh. I didn’t know.” He looked at his ribbon and bell and pebbles. He felt a small squeeze in his chest, like a tight knot. He wanted his treehouse to be special, but he didn’t want to make anyone sad.

The leaves rustled again. A squirrel with soft gray fur and bright eyes popped up by the window. “I’m Suri! I keep acorns on this sill,” she chirped. She pointed to a neat row of short sticks wound with grass. “Please don’t move them.”

Lio glanced at the window where his bell hung. The bell touched Suri’s sticks and made a tiny “ting.”

Suri’s fluffy tail dipped. “I don’t like the bell touching my things. It makes me worry.”

Lio’s brave heart felt big, but it also felt heavy. “I’m sorry,” he said softly. “I didn’t understand.”

A thin branch tapped the roof. A little owl, round and speckled, peeked in. “Hoo-hello,” said Ollie. “I nap in the morning in the quiet treehouse. The bell is loud for my ears.”

Lio took a deep breath. He listened. The bell sang again, “ting-ting,” and the leaves hissed, “shhh.” He smelled the warm wood and the sweet sap on his paws. He noticed Gigi’s sunny wall, Suri’s careful sticks, and Ollie’s sleepy eyes.

Lio sat down. His tail curled around his paws. “I think I made a mistake,” he said. “I did not ask before I changed our shared home.”

Gigi bobbed her head. “Thank you for saying that.”

Suri nodded quickly. “We can fix it together.”

Ollie yawned and smiled. “We can make it cozy for everyone.”

Lio’s whiskers lifted. “Yes! Let’s find a way that feels good for all.”

First, Lio gently untied the bell. “I will hang it outside, by the ladder,” he said. He tapped it softly. It tinkled, far from Ollie’s nap and Suri’s sticks. “Is that better?”

Ollie blinked happily. “Much better. Thank you.”

Next, Lio moved the red ribbon. “What about the railing near the door? It will not touch the sunny wall or the window.”

Gigi stretched on her warm spot. “Perfect,” she said. “My tummy is happy.”

Suri scurried to the sill. “I will keep my acorns and sticks right here, neat and safe.” She smiled at Lio. “Thank you for leaving space.”

Lio looked at his shiny pebbles along the railing. A small ladybug crawled over one pebble. Lio thought about the ground under the tree. He thought about the squirrels needing acorns and the birds needing seeds. He thought about the tree drinking water.

“I have another idea,” Lio said. “We can make the treehouse friendly for the tree and all friends.”

“How?” asked Suri, nose twitching.

Lio pointed to a little pile of crumbs he had left from a snack. “First, we clean up food crumbs so they don’t bring ants inside. Ants like to be outside.” He brushed the crumbs into a leaf and gently shook the leaf onto the soil below. “The soil can feed tiny bugs and the tree.”

“That’s caring for our home,” Ollie hooted softly.

“Next,” Lio said, “let’s use this basket for treasures. We won’t stick things into the tree.” He touched the bark with his paw. “We will be kind to the tree’s skin.”

Gigi patted the basket. “Good boundary,” she said. “Basket for treasures, bark stays free.”

Suri clapped her little paws. “We can have a ‘quiet time’ sign for morning when Ollie sleeps.” She drew a smiley moon on a piece of bark and leaned it by the door.

Lio’s heart felt light now. “And we can have a ‘ask first’ rule,” he added. “Before we touch someone’s things or space, we ask. That shows understanding.”

Everyone practiced.
“May I sit on this step?” Lio asked Suri.
“Yes,” Suri said. “Thank you for asking.”
“May I share the sunny wall?” Gigi asked Ollie.
“Yes,” Ollie said. “Quiet feet, please.”

The treehouse seemed to glow with good feelings. The bell chimed softly by the ladder. Birds sang, “Chee-chee!” The air smelled fresh and leafy. Lio stood tall and brave, not from roaring, but from listening.

He smiled at his friends. “Today I learned that being brave also means understanding. We ask before we use, we listen when someone says ‘no,’ and we take care of our shared home.”

Everyone nodded. The tree’s leaves whispered, “Yes.”

The End.
