# Ollie Otter and the Tidy Time Shed

Ollie was a playful otter with bright eyes and quick paws. One sunny morning, he padded into the garden shed. The shed smelled like fresh wood and orange soap. Sunlight made golden dust sparkle in the air. Tools sat on hooks. Boxes rested on shelves. A big clock tick-tick-ticked on the wall.

“Today I will build a water wheel!” Ollie cheered. “Splashy, spinny fun!”

He darted to a stack of buckets, thump-thump, and bumped them. Clatter! Bucket lids rolled like cymbals. Ollie giggled. “Oops!”

Grandma Goose peeked in, her feathers soft and gray. “Good morning, Ollie,” she honked gently. “What’s your plan, dear?”

“A water wheel!” Ollie puffed his chest. “I will build it fast.”

Grandma Goose smiled. “Fast is fun, but time is a friend when we use it well. Let’s follow directions, use our time wisely, and keep things tidy.”

Ollie nodded, but his paws were already grabbing ribbon, string, and spoons. “I can do it all at once!”

He spread everything on the floor. The shed became a maze of bits and bobs. Tape stuck to his whiskers. “Mmm, sticky!” he giggled.

The clock went tick-tick-tick.

Ollie tried to stick spoons on a wheel, but the spoons slid. He tried to tie string to a bucket, but the knot twisted into a tangle. He huffed. “Why is this so hard?”

Grandma Goose handed him a simple plan on a bright blue card. The card smelled like clean paper. “Let’s follow directions, one step at a time,” she said. “We’ll use the clock to help us.”

Ollie looked at the plan. There were pictures with numbers.

“Step 1: Clear a space.” Grandma tapped the card. “We have five minutes.”

“Five minutes? I can do that!” Ollie chirped.

“Set, tidy, go!” Grandma honked.

Ollie sang a tidy song. “Pick up, put back, stack and smile!” He put tape on the tape hook. He put spoons in a tin. He stacked the buckets by size—big, medium, small. The shed felt calmer. The clock went tick-tick-tick.

“Ding!” Grandma pointed to the clock. “Five minutes done. You used your time well.”

Ollie’s whiskers wiggled. “I did it!”

“Step 2: Find the parts,” Grandma read. “Paddle sticks, a round lid, a small nail, and a clean bucket.” She set a timer egg that went click-click.

Ollie listened and looked. He opened a labeled drawer. The drawer smelled like pine. “Paddle sticks!” He tapped them together. “Tok-tok!” He found a round lid on a shelf and a small nail in a box marked NAILS. He chose a clean blue bucket. “Done before the egg goes ding!”

“Excellent,” Grandma said. “You followed directions.”

“Step 3: Build the wheel,” Grandma said softly. “We’ll take ten minutes. We do one little thing at a time.”

Ollie breathed in. He held the round lid. “Little steps,” he whispered.

He pressed paddle sticks around the lid, like sun rays. Tap-tap went the nail, steady and soft. He added more sticks, neat and even. He felt proud. The wood felt smooth. The glue smelled sweet. The clock ticked, but Ollie did not rush. He watched, he waited, he pressed, he smiled.

There was a knock on the shed door. Tap-tap.

Beni Bunny peeked in, ears tall. “Can I help?” he asked. “I am small, but I am quick.”

“Of course,” Ollie said. “Everyone can help.”

“We have a job for you,” Grandma Goose said kindly. “Beni, you count the paddle sticks. When we get to eight, say ‘Stop.’ Counting helps our time.”

Beni touched each stick. “One, two, three, four, five, six, seven, eight! Stop,” he squeaked.

“Great counting,” Ollie cheered. “We used our time well with your help.”

“Step 4: Test the wheel,” Grandma read. “Three minutes.”

They set the wheel on the bucket edge and poured a little water. The wheel turned—whirr, splash, whirr! Water drops sparkled like tiny stars. Ollie clapped his paws. “It works!”

But then—squeak—one paddle wobbled. The wheel slowed.

“Uh-oh,” said Ollie. “Problem.”

Grandma nodded. “We still have time. Step 5: Fix and organize. Two minutes.”

Ollie dabbed a bit more glue and held the paddle. He counted with Beni. “One, two, three, four, five.” The glue set. The wheel spun smooth again. Whirr-whirr!

Then Ollie looked around. The floor was tidy. The tools were back. The boxes were shut. The labels showed where things belonged. The shed felt peaceful, like a gentle hug.

Beni smiled. “I liked helping.”

“I liked working together,” Ollie said. “And using time like a friend.”

Grandma Goose chuckled. “What did we learn?”

Ollie stood tall. “I learned that time helps when we make a plan. We follow directions. We clean as we go. We do little steps. We can ask friends to help. That’s good time-management!”

Beni nodded. “And organizing makes it faster and calmer.”

Ollie looked at the clock. Tick-tick-tick. He looked at his water wheel, spinning and shining. He felt warm and proud inside. “Next time,” he said, “I will start with a plan, set a timer, and tidy as I go.”

Grandma Goose winked. “That is smart and kind.”

They rolled the wheel out to the garden. Sunlight kissed their faces. Water chuckled over stones. The wheel spun and sang, whirr-splash, whirr-splash. Ollie laughed, Beni giggled, and Grandma honked a happy honk.

Ollie whispered, “Time well used is time that feels good.”

The End.
