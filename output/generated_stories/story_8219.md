# Ollie Otter’s Car Talk

Ollie the playful otter buckled into his cozy car seat with a soft click. His whiskers wiggled with excitement. The family car smelled like warm crackers and orange peels, and the seats felt smooth under his little paws. Sunlight sprinkled through the window, painting tiny gold spots on his fur.

“Good morning, Ollie,” said Mama Otter, starting the engine. “Seat belts on?”

“Click!” Ollie said proudly. “I’m ready!”

Papa Otter looked in the mirror. “We have our morning routine. First, we sing. Then we listen. Then we share.”

Ollie clapped. “Sing, listen, share!”

As the car hummed down the street, the tires made a gentle shhh sound. Birds chirped outside, and a breeze tickled Ollie’s nose. He giggled. “The car is like a tiny boat,” he said. “We’re floating!”

Mama smiled. “A car boat on a safe road river.”

They began with their song. Papa tapped the steering wheel softly and sang, “We use kind words every day, in the car and when we play.”

Ollie sang along, his voice bright. “We take turns and listen, too, so feelings won’t feel blue!”

After the song, Papa Otter said, “Now it’s listen time.” He turned down the radio so it was quiet, like a whisper. “Ollie, what would you like to share today?”

Ollie’s paws wiggled. “I want to tell a long story! It has a dragon, a kite, and… and…” He stopped. He heard a small sigh.

Beside him sat Baby Mina Otter in her little seat. Her eyes were big and watery. “Ba-ba,” she said softly, rubbing her belly.

Mama Otter peeked back. “Mina is hungry. She needs a snack.”

Ollie felt a squiggle in his tummy. He wanted to talk first. He scrunched his nose and puffed his cheeks. “But I want to tell my story now!”

The car was quiet. The tires still went shhh. Ollie could hear Mina’s tiny sniffles. He looked at her round eyes. He took a slow breath in, like smelling a flower. He blew out slowly, like blowing a candle.

Papa Otter nodded. “Thank you for breathing, Ollie. That helps.”

Ollie remembered their car routine: sing, listen, share. He also remembered their peaceful-communication words: “I feel, I need, can we?”

He tried them. “I feel excited to tell my story,” he said, his voice gentle. “I need a turn. Can we give Mina a snack first, and then can I share next?”

Mama Otter smiled big. “Yes, Ollie. Thank you for saying how you feel.”

Papa Otter passed a crunchy rice cracker to Mama. The wrapper made a crinkle-krinkle sound. Mama gave the snack to Mina. “Here you go, little one.”

Mina nibbled, crunch, crunch. Her eyes brightened. “Mmm!” She clapped, crumbs sprinkling like tiny snow.

Ollie felt warm inside. “Mina is happier now,” he said.

Papa Otter said, “That was empathy, Ollie. You noticed Mina’s feelings.”

“What is emp-fa-tee?” Ollie asked.

“Empathy,” Mama said, “is when you try to feel what someone else might feel. Like putting on their tiny shoes in your mind.”

Ollie looked at his own tiny shoes and giggled. “Mina’s shoes are tiny-tiny!”

The car turned onto a busy road. People walked, bikes rung their bells, and buses whooshed by. Ollie watched a family at a bus stop. A child helped his grandma climb onto the bus. He waved. They waved back.

“Look,” Papa said, “Teamwork everywhere. We are all helpers in our big world.”

“Our world is big like the ocean,” Ollie said.

“And we share it,” Mama added. “That’s being a good global citizen—caring for others near and far.”

Ollie nodded. “I can care with kind words.”

“Your turn to share now,” Papa said.

Ollie sat tall. “My story is called The Dragon Kite.” He spoke slowly, so everyone could listen. “A dragon kite got stuck in a tree. The dragon felt scared. The wind was loud—whooosh! The children said, ‘We feel worried. We need a plan. Can we work together?’ They used soft words, and they took turns. One held the ladder. One watched the wind. One said, ‘Breathe in, breathe out.’ The dragon kite came down safe. Everyone cheered! The end.”

Mina clapped again. “Dra-gon!” she squealed.

Mama Otter laughed. “What a wonderful story.”

Papa Otter said, “You used peaceful-communication in your story, just like in our car.”

Ollie’s whiskers wiggled. “Peaceful words are like seat belts for feelings. They keep feelings safe.”

The car slowed by the park. Trees waved their green hands. The air smelled like grass and sunshine. “We made it,” Mama said.

Before they got out, Papa asked, “What did we learn today?”

Ollie held up his paw. “One: We follow our routine. Two: We use peaceful words—‘I feel, I need, can we?’ Three: We listen and help. That’s empathy and teamwork. Four: We care about everyone in our big world.”

Mama touched Ollie’s paw with hers. “Perfect.”

Ollie took another slow breath. “I feel proud. I need a hug. Can we hug?”

They all giggled and leaned in for a squishy car-seat hug. It felt warm and safe, like a blanket at nap time.

As they stepped out, the wind whispered softly, the birds sang, and Ollie whispered back, “Thank you, peaceful words.”

The End.
