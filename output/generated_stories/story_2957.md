# Leo’s Honest Attic Adventure

Soft dust floated like tiny stars in the quiet attic. It smelled like old books and warm wood. A little window let in a stripe of golden light. In that light sat Leo, a brave little lion cub with bright eyes and a swishy tail.

“Today I will help!” Leo said. “I will put toys in boxes before dinner.”

Mama Lion called from the stairs, “Thank you, Leo. Please clean for ten minutes, then come down when I ring the bell.”

Leo listened carefully. “Ten minutes,” he repeated. He touched his round ears. “I am listening.”

Leo opened a box. “Clack!” went wooden blocks. “Rustle, rustle,” went soft scarves. He moved his paws, tidy and quick. His tail swished in time. “I can do it,” he hummed.

But then—“Boing!” A springy jack-in-the-box peeked out. “Hello!” Leo giggled. He turned the handle. “Clink, clink, clink—Pop!” The clown jumped up, and Leo laughed so hard his whiskers wiggled.

He looked back at the boxes. “I will play just a tiny minute,” Leo said. “Then I will clean.” He hopped, rolled, and spun. His paws pat-patted on the wooden floor. His brave heart felt big and happy.

In the corner sat a small gray mouse named Pip. Pip’s nose twitched. “Squeak,” said Pip. “Hello, Leo.”

“Hi, Pip!” said Leo. “Do you want to help me move the scarves?”

Pip nodded. “Yes. But remember Mama’s bell.”

Leo’s ears heard only his own giggles. He twisted the clown again. “Pop!” The attic echoed, “Pop, pop!”

Then Pip pointed with a tiny paw. “Listen.”

Far away came a soft “Ding-ding.” It was Mama’s bell. Dinner time!

Leo froze. His tummy felt wobbly. He looked at the messy floor. Blocks, scarves, and toys lay everywhere. “Oh no,” Leo whispered. “I did not finish. I played too long.”

Pip’s eyes were kind. “You can tell the truth,” Pip said gently. “Honesty is brave too.”

Leo took a deep breath. He felt the dust tickle his nose. “I will be honest,” he said.

He trotted down the stairs. The hallway smelled like sweet soup and warm bread. Mama Lion stood by the table. “Leo,” she said, “did you clean for ten minutes?”

Leo lowered his head. “Mama, I did not. I started to clean, but then I played. I heard your bell, and I was not done. I am sorry.”

Mama knelt and looked into Leo’s eyes. Her voice was calm. “Thank you for telling the truth, Leo. That is very brave. Honesty helps me trust you.”

Leo blinked. “I want to be honest. I want to do better.”

Papa Lion smiled and set a timer on the counter. “How about this? We can try again. Ten minutes of moving and cleaning, then play time after dinner. We can use a timer to help with time.”

Leo’s tail lifted a little. “I will try again.”

Pip peeked from the stair rail. “Squeak,” he said. “I can help too.”

Mama nodded. “That is kind, Pip. Compassion means helping a friend when they feel bad.”

Back in the attic, the timer went “Tick, tick, tick.” Leo listened. He and Pip moved with purpose. “One, two, three,” Leo counted softly as he stacked blocks. “Zip-zip,” he folded scarves. “Tap, tap,” he slid books onto a shelf. His paws padded and pattered, steady and gentle.

When the timer chimed “Ding,” Leo stopped. He looked around. The attic was neat. The golden light felt warmer. He felt proud and calm.

Mama called up, “How did it go?”

Leo stood tall. “I listened to the timer. I moved my paws fast and kind. I was honest, and I worked until the bell.”

Mama and Papa came up the stairs. Papa sniffed. “It smells like clean wood,” he said. “And I can see the floor! Well done, Leo.”

Leo turned to Pip. “Thank you for helping and for reminding me to listen.”

Pip smiled. “You were brave to tell the truth.”

They all went down for dinner. The soup was warm and the bread was soft. Leo held his spoon and said, “Today I learned three things. One: Tell the truth, even when it is hard. Two: Listen to helpers, like bells and timers. Three: Use your body to move and finish your job before play.”

Mama touched his paw. “Honesty makes our hearts strong. Compassion makes our friends feel better. Listening helps us use our time well.”

Leo nodded. His brave little heart felt light. “Tomorrow,” he said, “I will listen first, work with care, and play after.”

Papa winked. “That sounds like a great plan.”

After dinner, there was time to play. Leo and Pip took out the jack-in-the-box. “Clink, clink, clink—Pop!” They laughed together. The attic felt cozy and safe, and the golden light faded softly.

Leo curled his tail. “I am glad I told the truth,” he whispered.

Pip squeaked, “Me too.”

The End.
