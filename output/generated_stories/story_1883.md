# Tall Neck, Gentle Heart

On a sunny morning, a thoughtful giraffe named Lila peeked over the railing of her balcony garden. The garden was full of soft green leaves, tiny tomatoes, and pots of mint that smelled cool and fresh. Bees hummed. A little breeze tickled her long ears. She felt calm and happy.

“I love my balcony,” Lila hummed. “I love my plants. I love my friends.”

She leaned down to sip water from a wide bowl. Her tall neck bent like a rainbow. As she lifted her head, she spotted a shiny bell hanging by the door. “Ding, ding,” it chimed softly in the wind.

Just then, Lila heard a squeaky voice. “Good morning, Lila!”

It was Pip, a tiny sparrow with bright eyes. Pip fluttered onto the railing. “I brought a seed,” Pip said proudly, dropping a sunflower seed into a pot. “It’s from my family’s tradition. We plant a seed and sing a song.”

Lila smiled. “That is a beautiful tradition, Pip. Let’s sing.”

They sang a gentle tune. Lila’s deep voice went “la-la,” and Pip’s small voice went “pip-pip.” The seed slipped into the soil like a little treasure. Lila patted the dirt with her soft nose. It smelled earthy and sweet.

Soon, another friend arrived. Tiko the tortoise clonked along, his shell shiny like a pebble. He held a tiny watering can. “I’m here for Water Wednesday,” he said. “It’s my family’s tradition. We water plants carefully, one by one.”

Lila clapped her hooves. “Wonderful! Let’s water together.”

They began watering. Pip sprinkled from above. Tiko poured slowly. Lila reached high leaves and low leaves with her long neck.

Then, Lila saw the biggest tomato. It was red and round and perfect. “I will taste just a tiny bite,” she whispered. She leaned in. Her teeth took a nibble. The tomato burst with juicy sweetness. “Mmm!” she said. Juice dripped down her chin.

But she had nibbled too much. The tomato fell off the stem and splat! It landed on Tiko’s shell, red and squishy.

Tiko froze. His eyes grew wide. “Oh,” he said softly. “I was saving that tomato. I wanted to make soup for us tonight. It is my grandma’s recipe.”

Pip fluttered back a step. “Oh no,” Pip chirped. “Lila, did you forget to ask?”

Lila’s heart sank like a stone in a pond. She looked at Tiko’s shell, sticky with tomato. She looked at the empty stem. “I made a mistake,” she said. Her voice was small. “I did not ask. I am sorry.”

The garden felt quiet. The bee’s hum sounded far away. Lila felt heat in her cheeks. Her tummy felt twisty.

She took a deep breath. The mint plant smelled cool again. “Tiko,” she said, “I want to make amends. I want to fix what I can.”

Tiko blinked. “How will you fix it?” he asked gently.

Lila thought. She touched the broken stem with her nose. Then she said, “I will share something from my tradition. In my family, when we make a mistake, we listen, we say sorry, and we help. May I help?”

Tiko nodded slowly. “I am listening.”

“First,” Lila said, “I will clean your shell.” She took a soft leaf and dipped it in water. “Is this okay?”

“Yes,” Tiko said.

Lila wiped the tomato off Tiko’s shell, slow and careful. Pip helped with a fluffy feather. The red smear faded. Tiko’s shell shone again.

“Second,” Lila said, “I will grow more tomatoes.” She opened a packet of seeds. The seeds made a tiny shhh sound. “Pip, will you sing our seed song again?”

“Yes!” Pip chirped. Together they sang, “la-la, pip-pip,” as they tucked the seeds into fresh soil. The dirt felt cool and grainy under Lila’s lips.

“Third,” Lila said, “I will share my Grandma Giraffe’s salad recipe tonight, to go with your soup. It uses mint and kindness.”

Tiko smiled a little. “Kindness is a good spice,” he said.

Pip giggled. “It goes with everything!”

They all laughed. The balcony garden felt warm again. The bell by the door chimed, “ding, ding,” like a tiny yes.

Tiko looked at the empty tomato stem and then at Lila. “Thank you for saying sorry,” he said. “Thank you for helping. I accept your amends.”

Lila’s shoulders relaxed. She felt light. “Thank you for forgiving me,” she said. “Next time, I will ask before I taste.”

Pip fluttered closer. “We can make soup with little tomatoes and mint,” Pip suggested. “We can try my family’s sunflower bread too!”

Lila’s eyes brightened. “We can share all our traditions,” she said. “We can make a peace picnic.”

That evening, the balcony smelled like soup and mint and warm bread. The sun went soft and orange. Tiko poured soup carefully. Pip set out tiny crumbs of bread. Lila mixed a fresh green salad. They ate together and talked.

“I learned to ask,” Lila said.

“I learned to listen,” Tiko said.

“I learned to share,” Pip said.

They all smiled. The garden hummed. The bell whispered, “ding.”

The lesson was clear: When we make a mistake, we can make amends with kind words, helpful hands, and open hearts. Peace grows like a seed when we care for it together.

The End.
