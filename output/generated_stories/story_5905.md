# Gilly the Giraffe Says “I’m Sorry”

Gilly the giraffe had a cozy home office. It was a sunny corner with a small desk, a leafy fern, and a big window that smelled like rain and fresh air when he opened it. His long neck reached his top shelf where he kept crayons, soft pencils, and a jar full of smooth pebbles from the park. Gilly liked to think quietly and draw pictures that helped him remember big ideas.

One morning, Gilly whispered, “Today I will make a poster to help the planet.” He loved the planet. He loved trees, birds, and the sleepy turtle who lived down the street. He took a deep breath. The room smelled like paper and lemon soap.

Knock, knock! “Hi, Gilly!” called his friend, Bea the bunny, peeking in.

“Hi, Bea,” said Gilly with a warm smile. “I am working in my home office. I’m making a ‘Save Water’ poster.”

Bea hopped closer. “Ooo! Can I help?”

Gilly’s long ears twitched. He liked help. But he also liked quiet in his office. “Yes,” he said gently, “but we will use inside voices. This is my special thinking space.”

Bea nodded. “Inside voices,” she whispered.

They dipped brushes into cool blue paint. Drip, drip. The paint felt cold and smooth when Gilly touched the brush to the paper. “Water is precious,” Gilly said. “We turn off the tap when we brush our teeth.”

“I can draw a faucet!” Bea said. She hopped, hop, hopped to the table. The table wobbled. “Oops,” she giggled.

Gilly’s fern rustled. His pencils rolled. “Please move softly,” Gilly said. “I need my desk to stay still.”

“I will be soft,” Bea whispered. She drew a silver faucet and a blue water drop with a happy face.

Soon, their friend Max the monkey swung by the window. “Hee-hee! What are you doing?” Max grinned, his tail swishing.

“We’re making a poster,” Gilly said. “We are saving water to help the Earth.”

Max clapped. “Fun! I want to help!” He climbed in. Thump! His feet made a thuddy sound on the floor. He reached for Gilly’s special pebbles.

“Those are for thinking time,” Gilly said. He hugged his pebbles close. “This is my home office. Please ask before touching my things.”

Max froze. “Oops. May I hold one pebble, please?”

Gilly smiled. “Yes. Thank you for asking.”

They worked together. Brush, brush. Swish, swish. The room was bright. The paint smelled a little like berries. The sun made warm stripes on the rug.

Then Max got excited. “I know! Let’s make it big!” He spread his arms wide. He reached for the fern to use a leaf as a paint stamp.

“Please stop,” Gilly said quickly. “That is my plant. I like it to grow. It is not for paint.”

But Max had already pressed the leaf. Squish. Green paint dripped. A leaf broke. The fern drooped.

Bea gasped. “Oh no.”

Gilly’s heart felt heavy. He loved his fern. He loved quiet, careful work. He took a slow breath. In... and out. “Max,” Gilly said softly, “I feel sad. My plant is hurt. This is my special office. I asked for gentle hands.”

Max looked at the droopy fern. His ears went low. “I... I’m sorry, Gilly,” he said, voice small. “I got too excited. I didn’t listen to your rules. I didn’t respect your space. I will fix it.”

Gilly nodded. “Thank you for saying sorry. Saying ‘I’m sorry’ helps hearts feel better. We can fix things together.”

Bea brought a soft cloth. “We can wipe the paint.”

Max took a tiny cup of water. “Water can help, but we must not waste it,” he said. He turned the tap on for a moment and then off. “On to wet... and off to save.”

They dabbed gently. Dab, dab. Gilly held the fern’s pot and whispered, “It’s okay, little plant.” The fern smelled leafy and green again. It still drooped, but just a little.

Max looked up at Gilly. “I want to make it right. How can I help more?”

Gilly thought. “Let’s make a new part of our poster,” he said. “We can add three rules: Ask First. Be Gentle. Save Water.”

Bea clapped softly. “I like that!”

Max drew three big hearts. Inside the hearts, Gilly wrote in big letters:
- Ask first.
- Be gentle.
- Save water.

Max said, “I will ask before I touch. I will be gentle in your office. And I will turn off the tap.”

Gilly smiled. “Thank you, Max. Thank you, Bea. I forgive you, Max. Your apology was kind and true. When we say sorry and change what we do, we show respect.”

They finished the poster together. The faucet had a happy drop. The fern had a little sign that said, “Please grow.” The hearts shone bright.

Gilly taped the poster to the window. Sunlight made the colors glow. Birds chirped outside, tweet-tweet, as if cheering.

Max stood tall. “Gilly, I’m glad I said sorry.”

Gilly nodded. “Me too. Today we learned that a real apology has three steps: say sorry, say what went wrong, and try to make it better.”

Bea added, “And we learned to save water!”

Gilly looked around his tidy home office. It felt calm again. “Friends,” he said, “thank you for helping my plant and my heart.”

They all took a sip of cool water from small cups. Sip, ahh. Then they turned the tap off tight.

“Next time,” Max said, “I’ll ask first.”

“And I’ll speak up if I need quiet,” Gilly said.

“And I’ll hop softly,” Bea giggled.

They smiled at each other, cozy and proud. Outside, the wind whispered. Inside, the fern stood a little taller.

The End.
