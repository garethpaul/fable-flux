# Mango Leads the Clean-Up Crew

Mango was a courageous monkey with bright, curious eyes and gentle hands. He lived with his family near a big, messy garage workshop. The garage smelled like wood, oil, and warm rubber. Sunlight made golden stripes on dusty floors. Tools clinked. Boxes thumped. A fan hummed softly.

One morning, Mama said, “We have chores today.”

Mango’s ears perked up. “Chores?” he asked, tilting his head.

“Yes,” Mama said with a smile. “We will tidy the garage workshop. It will be safer. It will be nice.”

Mango peeked inside. The workbench was covered with screws, brushes, and tape. A shiny hammer lay under a towel. Paint cans sat like little round soldiers. A red bike leaned on a stack of cardboard. There were muddy footprints on the floor.

Mango took a deep breath. He could smell lemon soap in Mama’s bucket. He could hear a gentle drip-drip from a pipe. He felt brave. He felt ready.

Then Mango heard a giggle. His little cousin Pip popped in. “Play?” Pip chirped.

Mango looked at the mess. He wanted to play. But he also wanted to help. He was a leader in his heart. He stood up tall and said, “First we do chores. Then we play. That is fair.”

“Fair,” Pip repeated, nodding.

Mango clapped his hands. “Team, listen!” he said in a clear, kind voice. “We have a plan. I will lead. We will work together.”

Mama smiled. “Good leadership, Mango.”

Mango pointed to the shelves. “Pip, you can sort the screws by size—small, medium, big. I will sweep. Mama will wipe the bench.”

Pip blinked. “How do I know small, medium, big?”

Mango crouched beside him. He picked up three screws. “Feel with your fingers,” he said softly. “Small feels tiny. Medium is a little bigger. Big is heavy. You know this. You are smart.”

Pip’s eyes shone. “I know this!” he cheered.

They began. Swish-swish went Mango’s broom. He liked the sound. Dust danced in the sun like tiny stars. Pip’s little hands clicked the screws into jars. Click, clack, clink! Mama wiped the bench in smooth circles. Wipe, wipe, shine!

Soon they found a problem.

“Uh-oh,” said Mango. “The red bike blocks the door. That is not safe.”

Pip pulled the bike. It wobbled. The tires squeaked. “It’s stuck,” Pip said with a frown.

Mango felt a twist in his tummy. He wanted to say, “I can do it alone!” But he remembered his lesson: leaders ask for help. Leaders listen.

Mango took a breath. “Let’s understand the problem,” he said. “Why is it stuck?”

He knelt and looked closely. He used his knowledge. “The wheel is caught under the cardboard,” he said. “We must lift the boxes first.”

Pip nodded. “Lift boxes!”

Mango said, “We lift together. One, two, three.” They lifted the light boxes and moved them to the shelf. Mama steadied the bike. Together, they rolled the bike to a safe corner. The tire made a soft shhh sound on the floor.

“Teamwork!” Mango cheered.

They worked more. Mango found the hammer under the towel. He held it carefully. “Tools have jobs,” he told Pip. “We must put tools in their right places. That is justice. Everything gets fair care.”

“Justice,” Pip whispered, practicing the big word.

They put the hammer on a hook. They stacked the paint cans by color. Red, blue, yellow. It looked like a rainbow. Mango smiled. “Colors are friends,” he said.

Pip pointed to the muddy footprints. “Yuck!”

Mango dipped a mop in warm, lemon water. He let Pip smell. “Mmm,” Pip said. “Smells like sunny lemons!”

“Would you like to try?” Mango asked.

Pip nodded. He held the mop. He pushed and pulled. Swoosh, swoosh. The muddy prints faded. The floor felt smooth under Mango’s feet. He wiggled his toes and laughed.

Then they heard a little cry. A tiny toy car was trapped under a toolbox. “Help,” Pip said, worried.

Mango crouched again. “We do not yank,” he said gently. “We think. We understand. We lift the tool box first.”

Together, they slid the box to the side—scrape, scrape—and freed the car. Pip hugged the car. “Thank you!”

“You did it,” Mango said. “You are brave too.”

At last, the garage looked bright. The shelves were neat. The tools were safe. The floor shined. The bike stood straight in its corner. The fan hummed, and the air smelled clean and lemony.

Mama clapped. “Look at this! We worked hard. We used understanding. We used knowledge. Mango showed strong leadership.”

Mango felt warm and proud. “Chores help us,” he said. “Chores make our space safe. We did it together. That is fair and kind.”

Pip bounced. “Now play?”

“Yes,” said Mama, smiling. “You kept your plan. Work first, then play. That is just and fair.”

Mango nodded. He set a timer. “We will play for a little while, then put toys away. Chores continue, even after fun.”

They vroomed the tiny car along the smooth floor. Zoom! The sound echoed softly. Pip giggled. Mango laughed. When the timer dinged, they parked the car on the toy shelf.

Pip whispered, “I like chores. They make things nice.”

Mango hugged him. “Me too. Chores show love.”

They turned off the light. The clean garage glowed in the soft afternoon sun. Mango held Pip’s hand. He felt strong, smart, and kind.

The lesson was clear: when we understand the problem, lead with care, and do our chores together, we make our world safe, fair, and happy.

The End.
