# Monkey Milo Leads the Quiet Office

In a cozy home office, a courageous monkey named Milo sat up straight in a spinny chair. The chair squeaked, eek-eek, as he turned to look at the neat desk. The desk smelled like fresh paper and lemony cleaner. Sunlight made warm squares on the floor. The computer hummed softly. Click, click, went the little keyboard when Milo tapped it.

Milo wore a tiny red scarf. It made him feel brave. He was helping his family today. The home office was their busy space. There were pencils, sticky notes, and a tall cup of water with ice that clinked. Milo liked the sound. Clink, clink.

He had a big job. The family was having a video class soon. Grandma needed to learn how to join the class. Dad needed quiet to talk. Baby Bella needed a place to color. Everyone needed something. It felt like a lot.

Milo took a deep breath. He filled his belly with air and let it out slowly. “I can do this,” he said. His voice was steady and clear. “I can be brave and kind. I can speak up.”

Suddenly, Baby Bella toddled in. Her feet pat-pat on the rug. She held a handful of shiny crayons. “Color! Color!” she sang. She dropped the crayons. Clatter-clatter! They rolled under the desk.

Dad walked in too. He had big headphones on. “I have a call in one minute,” he whispered. His eyes looked worried.

Then Grandma appeared on the screen. “Milo, dear, I can’t hear anything!” she said. Her voice was loud and fuzzy. “Is it the button? The little talking button?”

Milo’s heart beat fast. Thump-thump. So many things at once! He wanted to hide under the desk. But he was the courageous monkey in the home office. He remembered his scarf. He remembered his breath.

“Okay,” Milo said to himself. “Be brave. Be clear. Be kind.” He stood tall on the chair and held his hand up like a leader.

“Everyone,” Milo said, not too loud, not too soft. “I have a plan.”

Dad looked at him. Baby Bella blinked at him. Grandma squinted on the screen. Milo felt warm and strong.

Milo spoke clearly. “Dad, I can help Grandma. You can have quiet soon. Please give me two minutes.” He held up two fingers.

Dad nodded. “Thank you, Milo,” he said, and he waited.

Milo turned to Baby Bella. He crouched down so his eyes were level with hers. He kept his voice calm. “Bella, I hear you. You want to color. The crayons rolled away. I will help you get them. Then we will color at the little table over there.” He pointed to a small side table with a soft lamp. “But we will use quiet voices. Can you say, ‘quiet’?”

“Qui-et,” Bella whispered with a giggle. She puffed her cheeks and blew a soft “shhh.”

“Great job,” Milo said with a smile.

Milo slid under the desk. It smelled dusty, like old paper and warm wires. He grabbed the crayons, one by one. Click went the blue. Tap went the red. He put them in a cup and gave them to Bella. “Here you go,” he said. “Color on this paper. I will check on you in a little bit.”

Bella nodded and began to draw soft swirls. Swish, swish.

Now Milo turned to the computer. “Grandma,” he said kindly. “Please look for the small picture of a little microphone. Do you see it?” He spoke slowly. “It looks like a tiny spoon with a circle.”

“Oh! Yes, I see it,” Grandma said. “It has a line on it.”

“Please click it one time,” said Milo. “That will turn the sound on.”

Grandma clicked. “Can you hear me now?” she asked.

“I can!” Milo clapped. “Great job, Grandma.”

Dad smiled and put on his headphones. He pointed to the door, asking with his eyes if it was okay to close it. Milo nodded and held up a finger. “One more thing,” he whispered.

Milo looked around the office. He saw the tall cup of water near the keyboard. He had an idea. He walked to Dad and spoke up with care. “Dad, please move the cup away from the computer. It might spill.”

Dad moved the cup. “Good thinking, Milo,” he said. “Thank you for speaking up.”

Milo felt proud. He had used his brave voice. He didn’t shout. He didn’t hide. He said what he needed, and he helped others too.

But then—uh-oh! The printer coughed. Whirr, whirr, clunk. A paper got stuck. Dad frowned. “I need that paper,” he said.

Milo took another slow breath. “I will be persistent,” he told himself. “Keep trying. Don’t give up.”

He opened the little printer door. Warm air puffed his face. It smelled like hot paper and ink. He found the bent sheet and gently wiggled it free. “Got it,” he said. He smoothed the paper with his small hands and handed it to Dad.

Dad’s eyes were happy now. “You led us so well,” he said. “Thank you, Milo.”

Grandma waved on the screen. “You taught me something new,” she said.

Bella held up her picture. “I drew a sun!” she whispered.

Milo stood tall in his chair. “Today I used my brave voice,” he said. “I asked for quiet. I said what I needed. I helped my family. Being assertive means I speak clearly and kindly. It helps me and it helps others.”

He wrapped his red scarf snug under his chin. The office felt calm. The computer hummed. The crayons swished. Dad talked softly on his call. Grandma listened. Bella giggled.

Milo smiled and whispered, “I did it.”

The End.
