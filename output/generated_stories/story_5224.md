# Miko the Mouse Makes Peace

In a quiet house, behind a little door near the floor, lived Miko, a kind mouse. Miko loved the pantry. It smelled like warm bread and sweet apples. The shelves were tall and tidy. Tiny crumbs sometimes sparkled like stars on the floor. Miko liked to sweep them into a neat pile with his soft tail.

“Good morning, pantry,” Miko whispered each day. He liked to start with calm words and a smile.

One sunny morning, Miko heard a sharp clatter. Tap-tap-clack! A spoon trembled on the edge of a jar. A jar of honey wobbled. A bowl of oats rattled like little drums.

“Oh dear,” Miko said, ears perking up. “Something feels wiggly and worried.”

On the shelf above, he saw two tiny ants tugging on the same golden crumb. “Mine!” squeaked the first ant. “No, mine!” squeaked the second. Their tiny legs shook. Their antennae twitched. The crumb slid closer to the edge, closer and closer.

Miko took a deep breath. He put his paw over his heart. In, out. In, out. Calm breaths. Then he called, with a gentle voice, “Hello, friends. I am Miko. I hear loud voices. Can we use soft voices and talk?”

The ants froze. The crumb stopped wiggling. The pantry became quiet again. Even the honey jar seemed to stop its sticky sway.

The first ant sighed. “I found this crumb first,” she said, still a little loud.

The second ant stamped a tiny foot. “I am very hungry. I wanted to bring it to my family.”

Miko nodded. “Thank you for telling me,” he said. “I hear you. You both want the crumb. You both have good reasons.”

He sat down on a folded napkin and patted the space beside him. “Let’s sit. Let’s breathe. Let’s listen. One at a time.”

The ants looked at each other. Their antennae drooped. They sat beside Miko, tiny and tired. Together, they breathed: in through nose, out through mouth. Calm and slow.

Miko spoke softly. “We can use helpful words. We can say, ‘I feel’ and ‘I need.’ Would you like to try?”

The first ant nodded. “I feel proud because I found the crumb. I need to finish my job.”

The second ant nodded too. “I feel worried because my babies are hungry. I need to bring food home.”

Miko’s whiskers twitched with a smile. “Thank you. That was clear and kind. Now we know. Finding first feels proud. Hungry babies feel important.”

He looked at the crumb and then at the shelves stacked with oats, rice, and crackers. “Maybe there is a way for both of you to feel okay. What could we try?”

The ants looked around. The first ant said, “We could share?”

The second ant added, “We could split the crumb in half!”

Miko clapped his tiny paws. “That is a smart idea. Let’s make a plan. I have a little leaf plate.”

He scampered to his nest and came back with a small green leaf, shiny and smooth. It smelled like fresh rain. He set the crumb on the leaf and used a clean toothpick like a tiny stick. Carefully, he pressed in the middle. Crack! The crumb made a happy, crisp sound as it became two even pieces.

The ants each took a piece. They both smiled. Their antennae wiggled hello to each other. “Thank you,” they said together.

But then—wiggle, wobble, wobble—oh no! The honey jar tilted again. A sticky golden tear began to slide down the side. The spoon skittered and clinked.

Miko looked up. “It seems the shelf is still worried,” he said. “Let’s help.”

He spoke clearly, one step at a time. “Ant friends, please stand here and steady the spoon. I will push the honey jar back, slow and safe.”

“Yes, Miko!” said the ants. They placed their tiny feet on the spoon handle. They pressed, steady and strong.

Miko planted his paws, pushed his shoulder against the jar, and counted, “One, two, three… push.” The jar slid back to the center of the shelf. It made a soft, thankful thump. The sticky tear paused and stayed put.

“Teamwork,” Miko said, breathing out. “We did it. Thank you for listening and helping.”

The pantry felt calm again. The oats hummed a dry little rustle. The apples smelled sweet and sleepy. The spoon rested with a quiet ding.

The first ant looked shy. “I am sorry I shouted,” she said. “I was excited.”

The second ant nodded. “I am sorry I pulled. I was worried.”

Miko smiled. “Saying sorry is kind. Thank you. Today we learned we can stop, breathe, and talk. We can say how we feel and what we need. We can listen. Then we can find a plan together.”

The ants stood taller. “We will teach our ant friends,” they said. “We will use soft voices.”

Miko packed a tiny basket with a few oat flakes and a teeny drop of honey on a seed. He gave a little bow. “For your families,” he said. “Please travel safe.”

The ants waved their antennae and marched away, crumb halves held high, the sweet seed rolling behind like a golden moon.

Miko swept the floor with his tail again. Swish, swish. The pantry smelled cozy and calm. He curled up by a cinnamon stick. His heart felt warm.

He whispered, “When we talk kindly, we make space for peace.”

The End.
