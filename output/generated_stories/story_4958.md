# Tiko the Tortoise Listens

In a quiet study corner, Tiko the wise tortoise liked to read and think. The study corner was cozy and warm. A small lamp made a soft, golden glow. A round rug felt fuzzy under Tiko’s feet. Crayons, books, and paper were neatly stacked on a low shelf. It smelled like crayons, paper, and a little bit like cinnamon tea.

Tiko wore tiny round glasses. He moved slowly and carefully. He always said, “Slow can be smart.” Today, Tiko had a plan. He wanted to practice listening. He wanted to practice kind words. He wanted to practice empathy—feeling what others feel.

Tiko tapped a pencil on a notebook. Tap, tap, tap. “I will listen with my ears,” he said. “I will listen with my eyes. I will listen with my heart.”

Just then, Tiko heard a small sniffle. Sniff. Sniff. He looked up. Near the crayons, a little mouse named Minka sat on the rug. Her whiskers drooped. Her tail curled tight.

“Hello, Minka,” Tiko said softly. “I hear you sniff. Are you okay?”

Minka wiped her eyes. “My picture is wrong,” she squeaked. “I tried to draw the sun, but it looks messy.”

Tiko did not fix the picture. He did not say, “It’s fine.” He took a slow breath. He remembered to listen. “You worked hard,” Tiko said. “You wanted a bright sun. It looks different from your idea, and that feels sad. Is that right?”

Minka nodded. “Yes. My tummy feels twisty.”

Tiko nodded too. “I understand. When my plans change, my tummy feels twisty too.” He put a gentle claw on the paper. “May I sit with you?”

“Yes,” Minka said. Her ears lifted a little.

They sat together. The lamp felt warm on their backs. Tiko listened to the quiet crinkle of the paper. He listened to Minka’s little breaths.

After a pause, Tiko asked, “What do you like about your sun?”

Minka blinked. “I like the yellow. It is bright.”

“That is a good start,” Tiko said. “Would you like help, or would you like me to just sit and cheer?”

Minka thought. “Help, please.”

Tiko smiled. “We can practice, step by step.” He pointed to a blank corner. “Let’s make a little practice sun here. We can try round circles. Slow circles.”

Tiko drew a gentle circle. He made it slow and steady. Swish. Swish. Minka held her crayon. She tried too. Her circle was wiggly. She frowned. Tiko watched her face, not just her hands.

“You look worried,” Tiko said. “Would you like to rest your hand?”

Minka nodded. Tiko showed her how to hold the crayon close, like a hug. “Let’s count together,” he said. “One… two… three. Circle!”

They drew. The crayons made soft, scratchy sounds. Scratch, scratch. The paper smelled nice and papery. Minka’s second circle looked more round. She smiled a tiny smile.

“I did it!” she said.

“You did,” Tiko said. “You practiced. You breathed. You tried again.”

A small tap-tap came from the shelf. A cricket named Kip hopped close. “Excuse me,” Kip chirped. “I want the blue crayon, but I don’t know how to ask.”

Tiko turned to Kip. He kept his voice calm. “You can use your words. Let’s practice. Say, ‘May I please have the blue crayon?’”

Kip took a breath. “May I please have the blue crayon?”

Minka looked at Kip. She hugged the blue crayon to her chest. She had been using it. Tiko watched her face. He saw her thinking.

“Minka,” Tiko asked gently, “how does it feel to share the crayon?”

Minka bit her lip. “I want it. But I can wait a little. Kip asked kindly.” She held out the crayon. “Here you go. May I have it back when you are done?”

Kip’s wings rustled. “Yes. Thank you!”

Tiko smiled. “You both used kind words. You both listened. You both cared.”

Minka drew her sun with yellow and a little blue sky. Kip drew a tiny blue pond. The room felt peaceful. The rug felt soft. The lamp hummed a happy hum.

After a while, Kip returned the blue crayon. “Thank you for waiting,” he said.

“Thank you for asking,” Minka said.

Tiko nodded. “Do you know what we did today?” he asked.

Minka and Kip looked up.

“We practiced empathy,” Tiko said. “Empathy is when we try to feel what someone else feels. We listen with our ears, our eyes, and our hearts. When we do that, we know what to say and what to do.”

Minka touched her picture. “I felt sad. You sat with me. You helped me practice. That made me feel calm.”

Kip chirped softly. “I felt shy. You taught me how to ask. That made me feel brave.”

Tiko patted the rug. “You both learned, and I learned too. When we slow down and listen, we can help. When we talk kindly, we can share. When we practice, we grow.”

The three friends looked at their art. The sun was bright and round. The pond was shiny and blue. The study corner smelled like crayons and cinnamon. It felt safe and warm.

Tiko took off his tiny glasses and smiled. “Today, we used empathy. Today, we practiced. Today, we communicated with kind words.”

Minka and Kip grinned. “We can do it again tomorrow,” they said.

“Yes,” said Tiko. “Every day is a good day to listen and care.”

The End.
