# Ollie Otter Builds a Boxcar

Ollie was a playful otter with bright whiskers and a happy wiggle. Today, Ollie padded into the garage workshop. The floor was cool and smooth. The air smelled like wood, soap, and a tiny bit of lemon oil. Sunlight made squares on the floor. Tools hung on the wall like shiny fish.

“Splish-splash!” Ollie said, even though there was no water, because saying it made him smile.

On the workbench, there was a box with a picture of a small wooden boxcar. It had four round wheels and a blue seat. A little note on the box said: “Build Me!”

Ollie clapped his paws. “I can do it!”

He opened the box. Pieces tumbled out with a soft clack-clack. There were wooden sides, tiny screws, smooth wheels, and a little bag that jingled. There was also a paper with pictures and numbers.

Ollie poked the paper. “Directions,” he read slowly. The pictures showed Step 1, Step 2, Step 3.

“I want to ride it now!” Ollie giggled. He grabbed two wooden sides and tried to push them together. Clunk. They slid apart. He tried again. Clunk. The boards fell and tapped his paw.

“Ow,” Ollie said, rubbing his paw. His whiskers drooped. “This is hard.”

He took a big breath, but it came out fast and spiky. His tummy felt tight. His face felt warm. “Why won’t it work?” he squeaked, pushing the pieces again. Clunk. Clatter. A wheel rolled away and bumped a paint can. Ting!

Ollie’s nose twitched. He felt a big grumpy bubble in his chest. His paws wanted to throw the wood. His eyes felt wet.

Just then, Mama Otter peeked in. She wore soft blue gloves and smelled like sawdust and soap. “Hi, Ollie,” she said gently. “I hear clinks and clunks.”

Ollie sniffed. “I want a boxcar. It keeps falling. I feel mad.”

Mama nodded. “It’s okay to feel mad,” she said. “Let’s do our Calm-Down Three. Ready?”

Ollie nodded, a tiny nod.

Mama held up a paw. “One: Put your paw on your heart.”

Ollie placed his paw on his chest. He felt thump-thump.

“Two: Breathe in like you smell a flower.” Mama sniffed slowly.

Ollie sniffed. The garage smelled sweet like wood and a little like lemon.

“Three: Breathe out like you blow a bubble.”

Ollie blew softly. “Pooooof.” He felt his shoulders go down.

“Better?” Mama asked.

“A little,” Ollie said. His whiskers lifted a bit.

“Good,” Mama said. “Now, do we want to push pieces, or follow directions?”

Ollie looked at the paper. The pictures were simple and clear. “Follow directions,” he said.

Mama smiled. “That sounds like a plan.”

Ollie picked up the paper. “Step 1: Find the two long sides.” He touched the wood. Smooth. Warm from the sun. “These are long.”

“Great noticing,” Mama said.

“Step 2: Use two little screws.” The tiny screws were shiny silver. Ollie tipped the bag—ping ping! He counted, “One, two.” He placed them carefully.

“Step 3: Turn the screwdriver slow.” Ollie held the small screwdriver. He turned it—scritch, scritch. The sound was soft and sandy. The wood hugged together.

“It’s working!” Ollie grinned.

“Slow and steady,” Mama said. “If your body gets tight, what can you do?”

“Calm-Down Three,” Ollie said.

They moved to Step 4: Add the front and back. Ollie tried to hurry. The piece slipped and bonked the bench. His tummy got tight again.

“Pause,” Mama whispered. She touched the paper. “Let’s read it once more.”

Ollie breathed in like a flower, out like a bubble. He read the picture again. “Oh! The arrows show the bumps go inside.” He flipped the piece. Click! It fit.

“That’s logical thinking,” Mama said. “You looked, you thought, you tried.”

Ollie’s tail thumped with joy. They added the wheels. The wheels were smooth and cool. “Step 5: Put the rods through.” Ollie slid the rods—shhh. He tightened tiny nuts with a little twist—tik, tik.

At Step 6, he wanted to skip the seat and paint first. He reached for the blue brush.

He stopped. He looked at the picture. “Seat first,” he said. “Then paint.”

“You are improving with every step,” Mama said, smiling.

Ollie set the seat in place. Scritch, scritch went the screwdriver. Finally, the last screw was snug. He tapped the side. Knock-knock. It was solid.

Ollie rolled the boxcar on the floor. The wheels went whirr-whirr. He climbed in. It fit! He pushed with his paws. The garage felt breezy. He giggled. “I did it!”

“Would you like to paint now?” Mama asked.

“Yes!” Ollie dipped the brush in blue paint. It smelled a little like soap and rain. He painted slow strokes. Swish, swish. He added a little yellow star. Dab, dab. He wiped a tiny drip with a cloth.

When it was dry, Ollie rode again. Whirr! He turned a careful circle between the tool chest and the broom.

Mama clapped. “You didn’t give up. You calmed your body. You followed directions. You used your thinking.”

Ollie beamed. “When I feel mad, I can breathe. I can look at the steps. I can try again.”

He patted the blue seat. It felt smooth and proud. “I’m good at learning,” he said.

“Yes,” Mama said. “And learning takes time.”

Ollie parked the boxcar and put the tools away. The garage was neat and quiet again. He took one more flower breath. In. Out. His tummy felt soft. His heart felt happy.

“Tomorrow,” Ollie said, “I will make a bell. Ding-ding!”

“With steps,” Mama said.

“Step by step,” Ollie agreed, with a wiggle.

The End.
