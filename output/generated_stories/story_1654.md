# Dori the Dolphin Dances in the Attic

Dori was a playful dolphin who loved to splash, flip, and twirl. One windy day, a gentle whoosh blew open a small window in a quiet attic. The attic smelled like old wood and lemon polish. Dust danced in the sunlight like tiny stars.

Dori peeked in through a big round bowl of water set in the middle of the attic. “Hello?” she called, her voice soft and bubbly. “Is anyone here?”

No one answered. The attic was calm. It was hushed like a nap time song.

Dori wriggled her tail and smiled. “I can move here,” she said. Her tail flicked. Swish-swish! Water rippled in the bowl. Dori loved to move. Moving made her feel strong, happy, and calm inside.

Around the bowl were boxes, soft rugs, and colorful scarves. There was a basket with paper, crayons, and a little brush. A string of silver bells hung from a beam. When the breeze tickled them, they chimed, ding-a-ling!

Dori stretched her fins. “Today, I will make art with my moves,” she said. “I will make still air dance.”

She started to glide in the bowl. “Swoosh,” she whispered. Her body curved like a rainbow. She flicked her tail, and the water made gentle waves. The waves went shhhh, like the attic was saying, “Calm, calm.”

Dori hummed a tune. “La-la-la.” She reached for a scarf with her fin. “Hello, blue scarf,” she said. “Let’s dance!” The scarf brushed her cheek like a soft cloud. It smelled a little like flowers. Dori twirled the scarf in the air. It made a slow circle. The bells sang softly, ding.

“I can paint, too,” Dori said. She took a crayon in her mouth and set a big sheet of paper near the bowl. “I will draw my moves!” She flipped her tail—tap!—and the crayon touched the paper. Line, curve, line! She swished the scarf again and made gentle wind. The paper wiggled and made a scratchy sound. “Scritch-scratch,” Dori giggled.

But then, a problem came. The scarf slipped into the bowl with a plop. “Oh no!” Dori cried. The scarf soaked up water and got heavy. It slid off the rim and pulled the crayons. The crayons rolled. Clack, clack! One crayon bounced under a box. The brush tipped and left a dot on the floor.

Dori’s heart thumped fast. “This is too messy,” she said. She felt a tight knot in her tummy. The calm attic did not feel calm now.

She took a slow breath. In. Out. The bells chimed. Ding-a-ling. “I can fix this,” Dori said. “I can move to help.” She remembered: smart moves solve problems.

First, she made tiny tail wiggles to stop the bowl from splashing. “Small moves,” she told herself. “Small and slow.” The water settled. Shhhh.

Next, Dori looked around. “I need a plan,” she said. “Move, then art.” She pushed the heavy scarf to the rim with careful nudges. “Lift, lift,” she hummed. With one more gentle flick—plop!—the scarf came up. Water dripped. Drop, drop.

Dori spotted a cloth by the basket. She nudged it with her nose. “This cloth can help,” she said. She used her fins to push the cloth to the wet spot. Pat, pat, pat. The drops went away.

“The crayon,” Dori said. “Where did it go?” She listened. The attic was quiet. She moved slowly, sliding the paper aside. “Scrape.” She peeked under the box. “Aha!” The green crayon lay there like a tiny log. She tapped the box with her tail, just a little. Nudge. The crayon rolled out. “Got you!” Dori cheered.

Now the attic felt calm again. The air was warm. Sunlight made a golden square on the rug. Dori smiled. “My moves can make mess,” she said, “and my moves can make better.”

She made a new plan. “First, safe space,” she said. She moved the paper far from the bowl. She put the crayons in a small tin so they would not roll. She hung the wet scarf on a low hook. Drip, drip. She shook the bells, just a touch. Ding! “Thank you, bells,” she said. “You remind me to be calm.”

Then Dori danced again. She counted softly. “One, two, slow.” She swam in circles. The water whispered. Her tail left little waves, like smiles. She watched the waves and drew them on the paper—soft curves, big loops. She made art from movement.

Footsteps sounded on the stairs. Tap, tap. A gentle voice said, “Oh! What lovely lines.” It was Nana, the attic’s owner. She smiled at Dori. “Thank you for being careful,” Nana said. “You moved with care. You made art with your body. You cleaned up, too.”

Dori’s cheeks felt warm with joy. “Thank you, Nana,” she said. “I used small moves when I felt big feelings. Moving helped me feel calm. Moving helped me fix my problem. Moving helped me make art.”

Nana nodded. “That is improvement,” she said. “You learned and you grew.”

Dori wiggled her tail in a happy wave. “I am grateful for this quiet attic,” she said. “I am grateful for my strong body. I am grateful for you.”

Nana hung the drawing on the beam. The bells chimed. Sunlight made the paper glow.

Dori hummed. “Move, breathe, and try again,” she whispered. “My body helps my heart.”

And in the quiet attic, Dori danced, drew, and smiled, calm and thankful.

The End.
