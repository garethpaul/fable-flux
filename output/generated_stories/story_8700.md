# Pippa Panda and the Tumble of Socks

Pippa Panda was a patient panda who loved to help. One sunny afternoon, Pippa waddled into the laundry room. It smelled like clean soap and warm cotton. The washing machine hummed, “Whirr, whirr,” and the dryer went, “Hummm-bum-bum.” The floor felt cool under Pippa’s soft paws.

“Time to sort socks!” Pippa sang. She peeked into a basket full of clothes. There were blue socks, red socks, tiny socks, and fuzzy socks. “I will make pairs, two by two,” Pippa said, clapping her paws. “One for left, one for right!”

Pippa took a blue sock. “Where is your friend?” she asked. She found the match and placed them in a neat pile. “Click-clack,” went the laundry room clock.

But then, Pippa saw it. A long green sock was stuck under the dryer door! Just one, all alone. Pippa tugged gently. “Come out, little sock.” The sock wiggled but did not move.

Pippa felt a tiny grumble in her tummy. Her eyebrows squeezed. Her cheeks felt hot. “I don’t like this,” she whispered. She tugged a little harder. “Come on!”

The sock slipped free and—plop!—the sock pile fell down like a little waterfall. Socks flew across the floor. “Oh no!” Pippa cried. “My neat pile!”

Her tummy grumble felt bigger now. Her paws curled into fists. Her breath got fast. “I want to shout,” Pippa said. “I want to stomp.”

Just then, Grandma Bear poked her head in the door. She wore a soft scarf and smelled like lavender. “Hello, Pippa,” Grandma Bear said in a calm, warm voice. “I heard a plop. Are you okay?”

“No,” Pippa said, with a wobbly voice. “My socks fell. I worked so hard. I feel mad.”

Grandma Bear nodded. “It’s okay to feel mad,” she said. “Feelings are like weather. They come and go. Let’s be safe and calm. We do not pull hard near machines. We use slow hands, and we take slow breaths.”

Pippa looked at the shiny dryer door. She remembered the rule: no fingers inside the machines. “Slow hands,” Pippa repeated. “Slow breaths.”

“Let’s try my ‘Smell the Flower, Blow the Bubbles’ trick,” Grandma Bear said. “Ready?”

Pippa nodded. She held her paws up like a flower. “Smell the flower,” Grandma said.

Pippa breathed in slowly through her nose. She smelled soap and warm cotton.

“Blow the bubbles,” Grandma said.

Pippa blew out slowly through her mouth. “Pffffff,” she went.

They did it again. Smell the flower. Blow the bubbles. Again. Smell. Blow. Pippa’s cheeks cooled. Her paws relaxed. Her breath slowed down.

“How do you feel now?” asked Grandma Bear.

“Calmer,” Pippa said. “My mad feeling is smaller.”

Grandma Bear smiled. “Wonderful. When we feel angry, we can pause. We can breathe. Then we can choose what to do.”

Pippa looked at the socks. “I want to fix my pile,” she said. “But I want to do it safely.”

Grandma Bear nodded. “Let’s make it fun, too. Would you like a craft to help?”

“Oh yes!” Pippa clapped. “A sock craft?”

Grandma Bear opened a little basket. Inside were safe craft things: soft felt circles, yarn, buttons with big holes, and a dull plastic needle. “We will only use safe tools,” Grandma said. “I will help you. We keep scissors closed and far away from little paws. We sit while we craft.”

Pippa sat on a little rug. “What will we make?”

“A Sock Sorter Snake,” said Grandma Bear. “It will help you keep socks together.”

Pippa’s eyes widened. “A sock friend!”

They found a long clean sock with no partner. It was striped like a candy cane. Grandma Bear held the sock open while Pippa gently stuffed it with other clean, lonely socks. “Soft and squishy,” Pippa giggled. The sock felt like a small pillow snake.

“Now for eyes,” Grandma Bear said. Pippa stuck two big felt circles near the top. “Boop, boop!” She tied yarn around the end for a tail. “Swish, swish!” The Sock Sorter Snake looked friendly and silly.

Pippa patted the snake. “You can live by the dryer,” she said. “You can hold socks that wait for a match.”

Grandma Bear smiled. “That is smart and safe.”

Together, they picked up the socks. Pippa moved slowly and carefully. She did not put her paws near the machine doors. She made pairs and put single socks into the snake. “You can wait here,” she told the singles. “No rush.”

When they were done, the floor was tidy. The laundry room hummed softly. Pippa felt proud. Her tummy felt light.

“I had a problem,” Pippa said. “I felt mad. I took safe breaths. I made a plan. I made a craft. I feel better.”

Grandma Bear gave Pippa a gentle hug. “You were patient and safe,” she said. “You used your calm tools.”

Pippa grinned. She looked at the Sock Sorter Snake. It looked back with big silly eyes.

“Thank you, Snake,” Pippa said. “Thank you, Grandma.”

Grandma Bear winked. “Remember: when anger pops up, pause. Smell the flower. Blow the bubbles. Use slow hands. Make a safe plan.”

Pippa nodded. “I can do that,” she said. “I am a patient panda.”

The End.
