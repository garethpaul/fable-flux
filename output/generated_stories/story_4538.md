# Momo’s Good-Morning List

Momo was a kind little mouse who lived behind a tiny door in the dining room wall. The dining room was bright and warm. Sunlight made golden squares on the wooden table. The air smelled like toast and fresh apples.

“Good morning, table. Good morning, chairs,” said Momo. He stretched his small paws high. “I will take care of my body today. I will take care of the world, too.”

Momo had a small notebook. It was made from paper he saved from old envelopes. He called it his Good-Morning List. He held a short, friendly pencil. “I like to write,” he said. “Writing helps me think and feel calm.”

He wrote slowly, saying each word aloud. “Drink water. Eat rainbow food. Breathe. Rest. Be fair. Be gentle with Earth.”

His tummy made a little rumble. “First,” he said, “I drink water.” He lifted a thimble-cup and sipped. “Ahh.” The water was cool and crisp. He felt it splash-splash in his belly. “My body likes that!”

Next, he climbed a chair leg and stood on the soft seat. On the table sat a bowl of fruit. Red apples, yellow bananas, green pears! The colors shone like tiny lights. Momo wrote, “Eat rainbow food,” and then he spoke to the fruit. “May I have a little piece?”

He found a thin apple peel and a dropped seed. “Just what I need,” he said. “I take only a little. I leave the rest.” He chewed the apple peel. Crunch! Sweet juice tickled his tongue. “Mmm,” he hummed. “This is wellness for me.”

Across the table, a napkin fluttered down like a soft white cloud. “Oh!” Momo gasped. On the floor lay two things: a good cloth napkin and a torn paper napkin. A breeze from the window had pushed the paper napkin under a chair.

Momo climbed down the chair leg—pat, pat, pat. He picked up the cloth napkin and stroked it. “Soft,” he said. He picked up the torn paper napkin, too. It felt dry and crinkly. “Trash or treasure?” he wondered.

Just then, a tiny beetle crawled by. “Hello, Momo,” said the beetle. “Someone spilled crumbs. Will you share?”

“Yes,” said Momo with a smile. “We can share the crumbs. That is fair.”

They ate little crumbs together. Crunch, crunch. The beetle’s tiny feet went tippy-tap on the floor. “Thank you,” said the beetle. “You are kind.”

Momo nodded. “Justice means being fair. Everyone gets a turn. Everyone gets a chance.”

He looked at the napkins again. “I can help the Earth,” he said. He wrote in his notebook: “Use again. Fix. Not waste.” He shook the cloth napkin and folded it nicely. “You can be used again.” He patted the paper napkin. “You can be turned into new paper.” He carried it to a little box that said, “Paper to Recycle,” in wobbly Momo letters.

“Good job,” hummed the beetle.

Momo felt proud, but then he heard a sniffle. Under the table, a young ant was rubbing her knee. “Oh no,” said Momo. “Are you okay?”

“I slipped on a grape drop,” said the ant. “My knee is sore.”

Momo took a slow breath in. He took a slow breath out. “We can breathe together,” he said softly. “In... and out.” The ant breathed with him. Her shoulders relaxed.

Momo tore a tiny strip from the paper napkin edge he had saved. “May I wrap your knee?” he asked.

“Yes, please,” said the ant.

Momo wrapped it gently. “There,” he said. “You are cared for.” He wrote: “Help friends. Kind words help hearts.” The ant smiled. “Thank you. That feels better.”

“Let’s rest a moment,” said Momo. They sat under the table. It was cool and a little dark. They listened. Drip, drip went the sink. Tick-tock went the clock. The dining room smelled like cinnamon now.

After resting, Momo stood and stretched again. “My body likes to move,” he said. He did slow mouse stretches. “Reach up. Wiggle toes. Roll shoulders.” The beetle tried. The ant tried. They giggled. “We move safe and slow,” said Momo. “We listen to our bodies.”

On the table, a cup wobbled near the edge. “Careful!” cried Momo. He climbed fast and steadied the cup. He saw a puddle of juice. He wrote: “Clean safely.” Then he spoke kindly to the room. “We clean together.”

The friends used the cloth napkin to wipe the juice. The cloth made a soft swish-swish sound. “We saved the napkin for many washes,” said Momo. “That helps the Earth.”

When the room was tidy, Momo opened his notebook. “Let’s read my Good-Morning List,” he said.

He read each line in a steady mouse voice:
“Drink water.” He sipped. “Ahh.”
“Eat rainbow food.” He nibbled a blueberry bit. “Pop!”
“Breathe.” They breathed in and out.
“Rest.” They closed eyes for a count of three. One... two... three.
“Be fair.” They shared a crumb with a passing ladybug.
“Be gentle with Earth.” They folded the cloth napkin and put the paper bit in the recycle box.

Momo felt warm and happy. “Wellness is taking care of our bodies, our feelings, our friends, and our Earth,” he said. “Writing helps me remember.”

The beetle clapped his tiny hands. The ant gave a small cheer. The dining room seemed to smile in the golden light.

Momo closed his notebook. “Today was a good, gentle day,” he said. “Tomorrow, we will learn more.”

The End.
