# Ollie Otter’s Pillow Parade

Ollie the playful otter loved to wiggle and whirl. His cozy bedroom was full of soft things that made him smile: a blue blanket like the sea, a glowing nightlight shaped like a moon, and a fluffy pile of pillows that smelled like clean soap and warm sunshine.

“Tonight,” Ollie said, patting his whiskers, “I will make a Pillow Parade! I will dance, and hop, and spin!”

He clapped his little paws. Pat-pat! His heart felt fizzy with joy.

But when Ollie tried to stack the pillows into a little stage, they slipped. Fwump! One slid to the floor. Thump! Another tipped over and bonked his tail.

“Ouch,” Ollie squeaked, rubbing his tail. “I want my parade right now.”

He tried again. He threw the biggest pillow on the bottom and the smallest on top. Sloooop—down they went, like jelly. His whiskers drooped.

From the hallway, Mama’s gentle voice floated in. “What’s going on, Ollie?”

“I want to dance on a pillow stage,” Ollie said. “But it keeps falling. I want it fast.”

Mama peeked in. The room smelled like lavender from the bedtime spray. The moonlight made little silver dots on the carpet. Mama smiled. “That looks like a brave plan. It might take time.”

“I don’t want time,” Ollie huffed. “I want now.”

Mama knelt and touched his paw. “When we hurry too fast, things can tumble. Let’s try patience. Patience is taking small steps and a slow breath. Can you try a slow breath?”

Ollie puffed his cheeks. He breathed in—sloooow. He breathed out—soft. His body felt less jumpy, like a calm river instead of splashy waves.

“Good,” Mama said. “Now, you can try on your own. I’ll be nearby if you need help.”

Ollie nodded. He felt a little brave. He wanted to try by himself. That was self-reliance—doing small parts on his own.

He pushed the pillows gently. He tested each one with his paw. Squish. Squish. “This one is wobbly,” he said. “This one is firm.” He put the firm one on the bottom. He pressed the corners with his tiny paws until it stayed. He smiled.

He added two medium pillows. He took his time. When they leaned, he did not cry. He breathed again. In. Out. He watched how the pillows liked to sit. He listened to the soft hush-hush of the nightlight. He felt the tickle of the blanket on his feet. He was patient.

Soon, his pillow stage stood tall and safe. Not too tall. Not too small. Just right.

“Hooray,” Ollie whispered.

Then he tried a spin. He slid a little. “Oh!” He sat down. The stage held steady. He laughed. “My tail is a good helper. It keeps me steady!” Ollie’s tail was wide and flat, not like Mama’s slim tail. It was different, and it helped him balance. That made him proud.

He stood up and tried small steps. Tap, tap. Swish, swish. He made a dance he called the River Wriggle. He tapped his paws. He swished his tail. He hummed a quiet song. Da-da, doo-doo. The room sounded like a gentle drum—the soft thump of his steps and the cozy hum of the nightlight.

Mama clapped softly at the door. “I love your River Wriggle.”

Ollie beamed. “I was patient, Mama. I did it myself.”

“You did,” Mama said. “And you used your special tail. We all dance in our own way.”

Ollie’s friend, a stuffed turtle named Tika, sat on the bed with a stitched-on smile. Ollie set Tika on a small pillow by the stage. “Tika has short legs,” Ollie said. “Tika can do a slow sway.”

Ollie swayed Tika left. Sway. Right. Sway. “We are different,” Ollie told Tika. “That is good. We can still dance.”

He tried a new step. He bent his knees. He stretched his paws. He lifted his whiskers like tiny flags. He felt brave and calm at the same time. That was courage—trying something new, even if it felt a little wobbly.

Then his tummy made a rumble. Grumble-grumble.

“I think my dancer needs a snack,” Mama said with a wink. She brought a small bowl of apple slices. They smelled sweet and crisp. Ollie took a bite. Crunch! The sound made him giggle.

“After my snack,” Ollie said, “I will try the Spin-and-Stop.”

He placed his paws just so. He wiggled. He spun in a little circle. Round once. Round twice. He stopped with a soft hop. The pillows did not fall. He did it!

“I did the Spin-and-Stop,” he cheered. “Patience helped me learn.”

Mama hugged him. Ollie felt warm and safe. “I am proud of you,” she said. “You waited. You tried small steps. You used your own way. You were brave.”

Ollie tucked Tika into the blue blanket. He climbed into bed. The nightlight glowed like a tiny moon on the sea. The room was quiet and cozy. He took one more slow breath. In. Out.

“Tomorrow,” he whispered, “I will learn a new step. I will go slow. I will be patient.”

Mama kissed his nose. “Good plan, my otter.”

Ollie closed his eyes. He felt the soft pillow under his head, the blanket on his shoulders, and the happy beat of his heart. He smiled.

The Pillow Parade would be here again tomorrow. And Ollie knew just what to do: breathe slow, take small steps, and celebrate his own special way.

The End.
