# Toby Tortoise and the Click-Click Belt

Toby Tortoise was small and green with a shiny, bumpy shell. He was very wise, and he liked to talk slowly and think carefully. One sunny morning, Toby climbed into the family car with his friends, Mia and Max. The car smelled like warm apples and clean soap. The seats were soft, and the windows felt cool to touch.

“Today we go to the park!” said Mia. She bounced on her toes. “Swings! Slides!”

Max clapped. “Zoom! Let’s go fast!”

Toby looked at the seat belt. It was smooth and a little chilly. He patted it with his tiny tortoise hand. “First,” he said in his calm, gentle voice, “we click.”

Mia reached for her belt and made it go click! Max reached for his belt, but it felt twisty. It would not slide. He pulled and pulled. “Ugh! Not working!” Max huffed. His face felt hot. He kicked his feet. “I don’t want it!”

The car made a soft ding-ding sound. The air smelled like the orange peel Mia was holding, and the sound made Max grumpy. “I want to go now!” he cried.

Toby took a slow, deep breath. He closed his eyes, and then he opened them. “When I feel huffy,” Toby said, “I breathe. In like smelling a flower.” Toby sniffed: ssssnnniff. “Out like blowing a bubble.” He blew: whoo.

Mia sniffed and blew too. Max watched. His shoulders went down a little. “Flower and bubble?” he asked.

“Yes,” said Toby. “We can choose calm.” His voice was soft like a blanket. “We can make a good choice.”

Max tried. Ssssnnniff. Whoo. His tummy felt softer. His hands stopped squeezing the belt so tight.

Toby nodded. “Now we problem-solve. What is the problem?”

“The belt is twisty,” said Max, frowning. “And I’m mad.”

“Good noticing,” said Toby. “Twisty belt. Mad feelings.” He put one tiny hand on his shell, which felt cool and smooth. “Step one: pause. We did our breathing. Step two: ask for help.” Toby smiled. “I can help.”

Max looked at the belt. “Please help, Toby.”

“Thank you for asking,” said Toby. He looked carefully at the silver buckle and the strap that was flipped. “Step three: check the strap.” He spoke slowly so everyone could learn. “We untwist. We make it flat.”

Toby and Mia gently turned the strap. It made a soft swish against the seat. “Swish, swish,” Mia sang.

Max giggled a little. The giggle felt like fizz in soda. The strap lay flat now, smooth like paper.

“Step four,” said Toby, “sit back. Bottom back. Back back.” He tapped the seat with a tiny knock knock. Max scooted back. The seat felt comfy and hugged his back.

“Step five,” Toby said, “pull and click.” Max pulled the belt across his tummy. It hugged him like a seatbelt hug. Click! The sound was bright and happy.

The car stopped ding-ding. The car felt quieter. Max smiled and wiggled his toes in his sneakers. “I did it!”

“You did it,” Toby agreed. “You made a safe choice.”

Mia pointed to Toby’s shell. “Toby, why is safety important?”

Toby touched his shell. “My shell keeps me safe when I walk slowly,” he said. “In a car, the seat belt is our shell. It keeps our bodies safe, even if the car stops fast.” He tapped the belt. “Click-click belt. Safe, safe shell.”

Max nodded. “Click-click belt is like a shell.”

“Wise choice,” said Mia.

The engine hummed like a sleepy bee. The tires whispered on the road: shhhh. The sun danced on the window, making warm stripes on Toby’s shell. But soon, the car stopped. Red light. Outside, a truck honked. Honk! The honk was loud. Max wrinkled his nose. “I want to go now,” he groaned. His legs kicked again. “This is taking too long!”

Toby felt the little bump of the road under the car. He felt his body want to wiggle too. He put a hand on Max’s arm. “Frustration is okay,” he said. “It visits. We say hello, and then we help it go.”

“How?” asked Max. His voice was small, like a whisper in a shell.

“We use our tools,” said Toby. “Breath. Words. Choices.” He pointed. “Tool one: flower and bubble.” They all breathed together. Ssssnnniff. Whoo.

“Tool two: kind words,” Toby said. “We say, ‘It’s hard to wait, but I can do it.’”

Max repeated, “It’s hard to wait, but I can do it.”

“Tool three: waiting game,” said Mia. “Can we play ‘What do I hear?’”

“Yes,” said Toby. “Good choice.” He closed his eyes. “I hear the engine hum.”

“I hear the turn signal click-click,” said Mia. Click. Click.

“I hear my tummy grumble,” said Max, giggling.

They laughed. The red light turned green. The car rolled forward like a slow river. Max’s body felt calm again. “I made a good choice,” he said.

“You did,” Toby nodded. “You used calm. You used words. You used a safe seat belt.”

At the park, they unclicked. The belt popped free with a soft pop. The door opened, and the air smelled like grass and sunshine. Birds chirped. Cheep, cheep! Toby stretched his legs and felt the warm sun on his shell.

Before they ran to the swings, Max turned to the car and put his hand on the seat. “Thank you, click-click belt,” he said. “You are my shell.”

Toby smiled his slow, wise smile. “Safety first, calm hearts next, good choices always,” he said. “That is how we ride.”

Mia took both their hands. “We did it together.”

“Yes,” said Toby. “We can always choose safe and calm.”

They played and laughed and felt proud. And when it was time to go home, the first thing Max did was pull the belt across, make it flat, sit back, and click.

He grinned. “Look, Toby! I remembered.”

Toby nodded. “Wise and safe,” he said.

The End.
