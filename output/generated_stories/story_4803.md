# Owl Hoots and Quiet Notes

On a peaceful front porch, a wise old owl named Olive sat in a rocking chair. The chair went creak, creak, creak. The sun felt warm on her feathers. A soft breeze smelled like pine and cookies. Olive sipped cool water and hummed a gentle tune.

“Good morning, porch,” Olive hooted. “What a calm, cozy day.”

Little feet thumped up the steps. Thump, thump, thump! It was Pip, a small squirrel with bright eyes and a fluffy tail. Pip’s cheeks were puffed, and his tiny paws were tight.

“Olive!” Pip squeaked. “I feel mad! My block tower fell. It went crash! And my tail got stuck in a knot! And my pencil broke!”

Olive blinked her golden eyes. She patted the step beside her. “Come sit, dear Pip,” she said softly. “Let’s take care of you.”

Pip plopped down. His tail twitched. “I don’t like this feeling,” he said. “It’s hot and big in my tummy. I want to shout, ‘Grr!’”

Olive nodded. “Feelings can be big,” she said. “It’s okay to feel mad. We can help your body. We can help your heart. We can help your thoughts.”

“How?” asked Pip. He scrunched up his nose.

“First, we rest,” Olive said. She handed Pip a small, soft pillow. “Put this on your belly. Feel the in and out.”

Pip lay back and felt his tummy rise and fall. In. Out. In. Out. The pillow moved like a little boat on a gentle lake.

“Can I try owl breaths?” Pip asked.

“Yes,” Olive smiled. “In through your nose. Out with a soft hoot. Hoo… hoo…”

Pip breathed in. He breathed out. “Hoo… hoo…” The porch felt quiet and cool. The breeze kissed his ears.

“My tummy is not so hot now,” Pip said.

“Good,” Olive said. “Now we shake out the grumps.” She wiggled her wings. “Shake, shake, shake!”

Pip giggled and wiggled his paws and tail. Shake, shake, shake! The grumps fell away like leaves.

Olive picked up a little blue notebook and a short pencil. “Then, we write,” she said. “Writing can lift heavy feelings. We put words on paper. The paper can help carry them.”

Pip peered at the notebook. “But my pencil broke,” he said.

Olive gave him the short pencil. “This one is small, like a nut,” she said. “Small can be strong.”

Pip smiled. “What do I write?”

“Start with how you feel,” Olive said. “You can draw too.”

Pip pressed the pencil on the page. Scratch, scratch, scratch. He drew a tall block tower. He drew it wobbly. He drew his tail in a big twist. He drew a thunder cloud with zig-zag lines.

He wrote in big letters, “MAD.”

Olive watched with kind eyes. “Would you like to write what helps?” she asked.

Pip nodded. He wrote, “BREATHE.” He drew little puffs: hoo, hoo. He wrote, “SHAKE.” He drew tiny paws dancing. He wrote, “WATER.” Olive poured him a cup. He sipped. Sip, sip. Cool and nice.

Pip looked at his page. “My mad is not so big now,” he said. “It feels smaller. Like a pebble.”

Olive rocked and creaked. “That is self-care,” she said softly. “You cared for your body with breaths and water. You cared for your feelings with shakes and writing. You cared for your thoughts with kind words.”

Pip’s ears perked up. “Self-care,” he repeated. He touched his chest. “I can take care of me.”

A bird sang tip-tip-tip in a nearby tree. The porch smelled sweet, like wood and sun. A butterfly floated past. Pip’s shoulders softened.

“Can I write a calm plan?” Pip asked.

“What a bright idea,” Olive said.

Pip turned the page. He wrote, “MY CALM PLAN.” He said each word as he wrote. “One: Stop. Two: Owl breaths. Three: Shake. Four: Sip water. Five: Write or draw. Six: Try again.”

He drew a happy acorn next to each step.

“I like your plan,” Olive said. “Let’s practice two more owl breaths, so your body remembers.”

Together they breathed. In. Hoo… Out. Hoo… The world felt gentle and slow.

Pip stood up. “I want to try my tower again,” he said. “I’ll go slow. I’ll be kind to me.”

Olive nodded. “Slow is strong,” she said. “Kind is strong, too.”

Pip tiptoed to the porch corner and stacked three blocks. Tap. Tap. Tap. He checked his belly. Calm. He checked his tail. Loose and fluffy. He smiled.

“Look, Olive!” he cheered. “It’s standing!”

Olive clapped her wings softly. “Well done, Pip. You used your calm plan. You cared for you. You wrote your feelings. You tried again.”

Pip hugged the blue notebook. “When I feel mad again, I will write. I will breathe. I will shake. I will sip. I can help me.”

Olive’s eyes shone. “Yes, dear Pip,” she said. “Your feelings are visitors. Your care is the home.”

The sun warmed the porch. The chair went creak, creak, creak. Pip and Olive watched the butterfly float and twirl. The day felt safe and bright.

Pip whispered, “Thank you, Olive.”

Olive smiled. “Thank you for trying, Pip.”

The End.
