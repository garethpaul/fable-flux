# Leo’s Colorful Courage

Leo was a brave little lion cub with soft golden fur and a tiny tuft of a mane. One sunny morning, he padded into the creative art room. The room smelled like sweet paint and fresh paper. Sunlight danced on jars of bright colors—red, blue, yellow, and green. Brushes stood like tall grass in a cup, and smooth clay felt cool under his paws.

“Hello!” chirped Pip the parrot, flapping green and gold wings. “We’re making art today!”

“Hi,” said Tessa the turtle, sliding slowly across the table. “We can paint, draw, or make shapes with clay.”

Leo’s heart thumped with excitement and a tiny bit of worry. “I want to make a painting,” he said. “A big, brave painting!”

Leo dipped a brush into yellow paint. Drip, drop! The paint felt silky and wet. He made a big sun. Then he tried to paint his family. The brush wobbled. The lines were squiggly. His lion looked more like a potato with ears.

“Oh no,” Leo whispered. His whiskers drooped. “It doesn’t look right.”

Pip hopped closer. “Squawk! Art is about trying. Art is about you.”

Tessa peeked up. “Slow and steady, Leo. We can try again.”

Leo took a deep breath. He listened to the soft hum of the room. He heard the gentle scratch of crayons. He smelled sweet glue and orange slices from snack time. He felt the brush tickle his paw.

“I want to improve,” Leo said. “How do I get better?”

“Practice,” said Tessa, smiling. “Little steps.”

“And be brave to try new things,” Pip added, fluffing his feathers. “We all make art in different ways!”

Leo looked around. Pip painted feathers and swirls. Tessa made neat dots and tiny hearts. A giraffe friend used long strokes. A bunny friend made stampy shapes with her paw. Everyone’s art looked different, and everyone was smiling.

Leo’s ears perked up. “Different is good,” he said. “I will try again.”

He got a fresh paper. The paper was smooth and bright. He held the brush gently. He made a light, soft line. Then another. He used small steps, like Tessa said. He practiced circles for faces. He practiced triangles for ears. He practiced lines for whiskers. He hummed a brave little tune.

“Nice work,” Tessa said. “You’re improving.”

Pip bobbed his head. “Look at that brave brush!”

Leo tried mixing colors. He swirled red and yellow. “Wow! It makes orange,” he said, eyes wide. He dabbed it into a warm sunset sky. He mixed blue and yellow to make green grass. He pressed his paw into brown paint and made a paw print. Squish! It felt cool and soft.

He still made a wiggly line by mistake. His lion’s tail went swoop! “Oops,” Leo said.

Pip grinned. “Squawk! Oops can be art too.”

Leo looked. The swoop looked like a dancing tail. “Yes,” he said, smiling. “My lion is dancing!”

He added music notes with a tiny brush, tap-tap-tap. He gave his lion a brave smile. He painted friends—Pip with bright wings, Tessa with a shiny shell, the giraffe with tall spots, the bunny with big ears. He made each friend look the way they were, each one special. He used many colors to show how different friends make the room bright.

When he finished, he stepped back. His heart felt warm and proud. “I did it,” he said softly. “I improved.”

Ms. Star, the art teacher, came by. She wore a paint-splattered apron and smelled like lemon soap. “Leo,” she said kindly, “tell me about your art.”

Leo stood tall. “My lion is brave. He keeps trying. His tail swoops because he dances when he practices. These are my friends. We are all different, and that makes our art beautiful.”

Ms. Star’s eyes shone. “That is wonderful. You worked in small steps. You tried new things. You turned an oops into a dance. That is improvement! How do you feel?”

Leo touched his chest with a painty paw. “I feel proud.”

Pip squawked happily. “Proud and brave!”

Tessa nodded. “Slow and steady wins the day.”

Other friends gathered around. “I like your sunset,” said the bunny. “It looks warm.”

“I like your dancing tail,” said the giraffe. “It makes me want to dance too.”

Leo’s whiskers lifted in a big smile. “Thank you,” he said. “Your art is wonderful too.” He pointed to Pip’s swirls. “Your colors fly.” He pointed to Tessa’s dots. “Your dots make a song.” He pointed to the giraffe’s long strokes. “Your lines reach high.” He pointed to the bunny’s stamps. “Your shapes hop.”

They all laughed and clapped with their paws, wings, and hooves. The room filled with happy sounds—tap, flap, clap!

Ms. Star hung Leo’s painting on the wall. “This wall shows our growth,” she said. “Every picture tells a story of trying, learning, and being ourselves.”

Leo looked at the wall. He saw many colors and many styles. He felt brave and calm. He knew he could keep improving, one little step at a time.

He whispered, “I am me. I can learn. I can grow.”

Then he said out loud, “Let’s make more art together!”

“Yes!” cheered everyone.

And they did—painting, drawing, and shaping clay, each in their own way, helping each other, and celebrating every little step.

The End.
