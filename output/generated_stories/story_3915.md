# Tiko's Brave Big Beat

The musical practice room was warm and bright. Sunlight made tiny sparkles on the shiny drums. The room smelled like lemon polish and new strings. Soft chairs sat in a circle. A small turtle named Tiko peeked in. Tiko was shy. His shell felt safe, like a tiny house. He moved slowly, tap-tap, on the wooden floor.

Inside the room, friends were getting ready. A rabbit tuned a violin. A bird blew a gentle toot on a flute. A bear pat-patted a big drum. Their sounds floated like bubbles. Pop, pop, pop!

“Welcome, Tiko!” said Ms. Melody, the kind music teacher. She wore a scarf with bright notes on it. “Today we will make a band.”

Tiko’s head tucked back into his shell a little. “I… I listen first,” he whispered.

“That’s okay,” Ms. Melody said softly. “Listening is part of music.”

They began to play. The rabbit’s violin sang, “La-la.” The bird’s flute whispered, “Woo-woo.” The bear’s drum went, “Boom-boom.” Tiko sat on a small cushion and felt the drum beat in his belly. Boom-boom. It felt like a big heart.

“Now it’s your turn, Tiko,” Ms. Melody said. She held out a small hand drum. It was light. It felt smooth and warm.

Tiko shook his head. “I am too shy,” he said, his voice like a tiny leaf. “My beats will be wrong.”

The bear puffed. “Just hit it loud,” he said. “Loud is best.”

The rabbit twitched her ears. “No, fast is best,” she said. “Play fast, fast!”

The bird chirped, “High notes are best! Make it high!”

Tiko’s tummy felt twisty. Loud? Fast? High? He wanted to hide. It did not feel fair. He wanted a turn to try his own way. But he was shy to say so.

Ms. Melody raised her hands. “Friends, let’s be fair,” she said. “Everyone gets a turn. And everyone can try in their own way. We keep our minds open, like windows. We listen with kind ears.”

The room got quiet. Tiko took a slow breath. The drum was in his lap now. He could feel the soft skin of it under his little hands. He looked at his shell. He looked at the drum. An idea bubbled up.

“What if…” he began, very soft. “What if the drum is a story?”

“A story?” the bird asked, tilting her head.

Tiko nodded. “In my head, the drum can be rain on my shell. Tip-tap. Then the rain is a river. Swish. Then it is little feet. Pitter-pat. Then it is big clouds. Boom.”

Ms. Melody smiled. “That is beautiful imagination, Tiko. Let’s try your story.”

The rabbit blinked. “I never thought of that.”

The bear frowned, but then his face softened. “Okay,” he said. “I will listen.”

Tiko closed his eyes. He could smell lemon polish and hear his slow breath. He tapped the drum very softly with one finger. Tip. Tap. Tip. Tap. “This is rain on my shell,” he whispered.

The room leaned in. The rabbit’s ears relaxed. The bird’s wings tucked close. The bear stopped tapping completely.

Tiko used two fingers now. Pitter-pat, pitter-pat. “The rain is a river!” He brushed his palm across the drum. Swishhhh. It sounded like water sliding over smooth stones. He felt brave. His heart beat steady. Boom… boom…

He lifted his little hands and made one big soft thump. Boom. “A cloud,” he said. “A kind cloud.”

The bird smiled. “I can be wind with my flute!” She blew a gentle “whooooo” that curled around the river sound.

The rabbit said, “I can be leaves with my violin.” She played tiny notes, like green leaves laughing.

The bear grinned. “I can be distant thunder. Soft, not scary.” He tapped the big drum very slow. Boom… pause… boom…

Tiko opened his eyes. The room was full of his story. He felt tall, even in his little shell. His cheeks warmed with happy.

Ms. Melody clapped quietly. “That was fair and open-minded,” she said. “We listened to Tiko’s idea. We tried it. And look—our music grew!”

The bear looked at Tiko. “I thought loud was best,” he said. “But your soft beats were best for the rain. I learned something.”

The rabbit nodded. “I thought fast was best,” she said. “But slow can be lovely.”

The bird chirped, “High is not always best. Wind can be low and soft.”

Tiko tucked his chin with a shy smile. “Thank you for listening,” he said. “My imagination made me brave.”

Ms. Melody said, “And your imagination helped us all. That is fair sharing. That is open hearts and open minds.”

They played the story again. This time, everyone added a little idea. The room smelled sweet and sounded like rain and wind and laughter. Tiko tapped his drum, not too loud, not too fast, not too high—just right for the story in his mind.

When the music stopped, the friends hugged. Tiko’s shell felt light. He did not want to hide. He wanted to think of more stories. Maybe next time, the drum could be tiny feet on a moon path. Or popcorn. Or star bubbles!

Ms. Melody wrote on the board: “We imagine. We are fair. We keep open minds.”

Tiko read the words and whispered, “Yes.”

The End.
