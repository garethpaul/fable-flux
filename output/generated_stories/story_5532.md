# Momo’s Helpful Hands

Every morning, the sun peeked into a small, busy garage workshop. The air smelled like warm wood and lemon soap. Tools hung on the wall in neat rows—hammers, wrenches, and bright blue tape. In the corner was a soft rug with picture books and a little bell.

This was Momo’s favorite place. Momo was a courageous monkey with big curious eyes and gentle hands. He lived with Tia, a kind mechanic who fixed bikes, wagons, and scooters. Tia had a calm voice and a wide, warm smile.

“Good morning, Momo,” Tia said each day. “Let’s start our routine!”

Momo loved the routine. First, he rang the bell—ding-ding!—so everyone knew the day had begun. Next, he wiped the workbench with a damp cloth—swish, swish—smelling the lemon soap. Then he counted the tools. “One, two, three,” he said softly, tapping each hook.

One sunny day, the workshop door creaked open—creeeak—and new neighbors rolled in. There was a red tricycle with squeaky wheels, a wagon with a wobbly handle, and a scooter missing a bell. The neighbors were a group of kids—Ava, who wore purple glasses; Sam, who used a small, shiny leg brace; Leela, who liked to hum; and tiny Omar, who held a toy dinosaur.

“Welcome!” Tia said. “We can fix your rides. And we can help you learn how to care for them too.”

Momo waved. “Hello! I’m Momo. I can help.”

Ava smiled. “I’ve never been in a workshop.”

Sam looked around. “Is it okay if I go slow?”

Momo nodded. “We go at our own pace here.”

They began their routine—together. Momo handed out little cotton aprons. He showed the counting chart on the wall. “Count with me,” he said. “It helps us feel ready.” They counted—and giggled when Omar’s dinosaur tried to count too. “Rawr! One!” Omar said.

But soon there was a problem. The wrench hooks were high. Ava couldn’t reach. The workbench stool was wobbly for Sam. The red tricycle was stuck on a bump in the floor and Leela looked worried.

“We don’t fit,” Ava whispered.

“We do fit,” Momo said, but his voice felt small. He wanted everyone to feel welcome and strong. His tail curled with worry. He loved their routine, but the routine did not fit everyone yet.

Tia knelt beside him. “Momo,” she said gently, “you are brave. What new ideas can make our routine include everyone?”

Momo took a deep breath and listened: the soft hum of Leela’s song, the squeak of the tricycle, the tiny tap of Sam’s brace, the whisper of the lemon cloth. The sounds reminded him that everyone brought something special.

“I have an idea,” Momo said, eyes bright. “We can change the routine! We can make a plan that helps each friend be independent.”

He drew a picture chart with crayons. He drew a sun for “Start,” a bell for “Hello,” a ramp for “Roll In,” a star for “Try,” and a heart for “Help.”

“Step one,” Momo said, pointing. “We move tools lower.” He slid a basket onto a lower shelf. “Now Ava can reach.”

“Step two,” he said. “We make things steady.” He folded a small towel to make the stool firm. Sam sat on it and grinned. “Comfy,” Sam said.

“Step three,” Momo said. “We smooth the floor.” He placed a wide board like a little bridge over the bump. “Roll on, trike!” Ava rolled the tricycle forward—smooth and easy.

“Step four,” Momo said. “We add sounds.” He tied tiny bells to the scooter. Leela’s eyes shone. “I can hear the scooter sing,” she said, humming along.

“Step five,” Momo whispered. “We ask and listen.” He looked at each friend. “What do you need to do it by yourself?”

Ava said, “I need a picture to follow.” Momo drew a simple picture of tightening a bolt—turn-turn-stop.

Sam said, “I need to take breaks.” Momo put a blue cushion on the rug. “Breaks are part of the routine,” he said.

Leela said, “I like to hum while I work.” Momo smiled. “Music helps us focus.”

Omar lifted his dinosaur. “Dino needs a tiny wrench!” Everyone laughed. Momo gave Omar a safe, soft toy wrench. “Dino can help too,” he said.

They worked together. “Lefty-loosey, righty-tighty,” Tia sang. Momo squeaked the oil bottle—squeak, squeak. The workshop felt bright and busy. The air smelled like lemon and warm metal. The scooter bell jingled. The tricycle wheels spun—whoosh! Sam tightened the wagon bolt by himself. “I did it,” he said proudly.

Ava tested the tricycle. “I can ride!” she cheered.

Leela rang the scooter bell to the beat of her hum. “Ting-ting, hum-hum.”

Omar gave his dinosaur a ride in the wagon. “Rawr! All aboard!”

Momo’s heart felt big and soft. He had been brave. He had been kind. He had changed the routine to include everyone. That was real courage.

At the end of the day, they gathered by the bell. Tia said, “Momo, your new routine helps everyone belong.”

Momo nodded. “In our workshop, everyone can help. Everyone can learn. Everyone can do things their way.”

They rang the bell together—ding-ding!—and the sound felt like a hug.

The lesson was clear: When we listen, make space, and try new ideas, everyone can be included and be independent. Routines can change so everyone belongs.

The End.
