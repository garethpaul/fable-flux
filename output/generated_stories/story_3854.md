# Sunny Jumps and Soapy Bubbles

Pip was a joyful kangaroo joey. He loved to jump, wiggle, and giggle in his sunny backyard. The sun felt warm on his fur. The grass was soft under his big feet. Birds chirped, “Cheep-cheep!” and a gentle breeze whispered, “Whooo.”

“Today, I will write a big thank-you note for Nana Roo,” Pip said. He held a bright blue crayon and a small notepad. He felt proud. “I can do this by myself!”

Pip hopped to the picnic table. His tail swished happily. He smelled sweet flowers and fresh cut grass. He drew a big heart and wrote, “Th—” But then his tummy went grumble-grumble. He saw a mud patch by the garden. Splash! Pip hopped right into it.

“Squish-squash!” Mud slid between his toes. Pip laughed and bounced. “So bouncy! So fun!” Mud dotted his nose. Mud dotted his ears. Mud dotted his little paws.

Pip hopped back to the table. He picked up the blue crayon. “I will finish my note.” He pressed the crayon down. Smear! The paper turned brown and sticky. The blue line got muddy and blurry.

“Oh no,” Pip said softly. He felt a little sad. “My note looks messy.”

Mama Roo peeked out the back door. She wore a wide sun hat and a kind smile. “Hello, Pip! I see big jumps and big fun. I also see big muddy paws.”

Pip showed his paper. “I tried to write by myself. But the mud made my writing messy.”

Mama Roo came closer. She sniffed the air. “I smell grass, flowers, and… muddy paws!” She winked. “What could help?”

Pip thought. He looked at his paws, brown and sticky. He touched his face and felt grit. He looked at the paper, smudged and wet. He took a deep breath. “I need clean paws.”

“That sounds smart,” Mama Roo said. “How will you do it?”

Pip stood tall. “I can wash my paws by myself. I can do it now.”

Mama Roo nodded. “Peaceful plan. First, ask for what you need.”

Pip turned to the little outdoor sink by the hose. “Water, please,” he said with a smile. He turned the handle. The water sang, “Splish-splash!” It felt cool on his fur.

He added soap. The bubbles went, “Pop-pop-pop!” They smelled like lemons. Pip rubbed his paws, front and back, between his fingers, around his thumbs, and under his nails. “Scrub-a-dub, one-two-three, clean, clean, clean for me!” he sang.

He rinsed with a swish. The mud slipped away like chocolate syrup in a stream. He shook his paws gently. “Drip-drip-drip!” Then he patted them dry with a soft towel. “All clean,” Pip whispered. He felt proud and calm.

Pip hopped back to the table. He looked at the messy paper. “I feel sad about this paper,” he said in a small voice.

Mama Roo listened. “Thank you for telling me your feelings.”

Pip took a steady breath. “Mama, may I please have a new paper?”

“Yes, you may,” Mama Roo said. She set a clean sheet on the table.

Pip smiled. “Thank you.” He set his paws flat to keep them still. He held the blue crayon softly. He wrote, slow and careful: “Thank you, Nana Roo. Love, Pip.” The letters were wiggly, but they were clear. Pip added a bright heart and a smiling sun.

He looked at his paws again. Still clean! He felt warm in his chest. “I did it by myself,” he said.

Mama Roo clapped softly. “You made a plan. You asked with kind words. You washed well. You wrote your note.”

Pip giggled. “Hygiene helps my writing!”

He hopped to the garden path, then paused. “If I jump in the mud again, my note will get messy again.”

Mama Roo tilted her head. “What can you do?”

Pip thought. He touched his chin. “I can play after I finish my writing. And if I get dirty, I can wash again.”

“That is self-reliance,” Mama Roo said. “You can care for your body. You can solve problems.”

Pip felt big and brave. He went to the fence where his neighbor, Tilly Turtle, peeked. “Hi, Tilly!”

“Hi, Pip,” Tilly said. “Can I see your note?”

“Yes,” Pip said. “Please look with your eyes, not your hands. My paws are clean, and I want to keep the paper clean, too.”

“Okay,” Tilly said. She smiled. “It is beautiful.”

Pip beamed. The sun felt even warmer. The lemon soap smell still danced on his paws. The birds sang a happy tune.

Pip hopped back to the table and slipped the note into an envelope. He drew a tiny bubble on the corner. “For Nana Roo,” he said. “Clean paws, kind words, careful writing.”

Mama Roo gave him a gentle hug. “That is a wonderful lesson.”

Pip nodded. “Wash first. Write well. Speak kindly. I can do it!”

The End.
