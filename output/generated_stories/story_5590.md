# Runa Rabbit’s Gentle Guest Room Plan

Runa was a small, determined rabbit with velvet-soft ears and a brave little heart. One sunny afternoon, Runa hopped into the guest room to get it ready. Grandma Owl was coming to visit and would sleep there tonight. The guest room smelled like clean sheets and pine soap. The curtains were mint green and fluttered like leaves. A round window let in warm, golden light.

On the soft bed lay two blankets. One blanket was Runa’s family quilt. It was patchy and bright, with red squares and yellow stars that Grandma Rabbit had sewn long ago. It felt warm and bumpy under Runa’s paws. The other blanket was a smooth, silky shawl with tiny silver birds. It belonged to Tiko, Runa’s best friend, a gentle turtle. Tiko’s family used the shawl every time a guest came. It was their tradition.

Runa and Tiko both wanted to make the guest room special. But they had different ideas.

“We should use the family quilt,” said Runa, puffing her chest. “Grandma Owl will love the stars! It is our family tradition.”

Tiko shook his shell and smiled softly. “The silver bird shawl is for guests,” he said. “It is my family tradition. Guests feel calm with it.”

Runa’s whiskers twitched. “But the quilt is warm,” she said.

Tiko tapped the shawl with one careful toe. “The shawl is cool and smooth.”

They both looked at the bed. One bed. Two traditions.

Runa took a deep breath and felt her heart beat fast. She wanted to win. She wanted to be right. Her ears felt hot. Tiko’s eyes blinked slowly. He wanted to help, but he wanted to honor his family too.

Runa remembered what Mama Rabbit always said: “When you feel hot inside, pause and cool your thoughts.” Runa rubbed her ears and counted softly. “One… two… three.” She felt her body calm, like a lake getting still.

“Tiko,” Runa said, “I feel upset. I want Grandma to feel our love.” Her voice was small but clear.

Tiko nodded. “I feel worried. I want to honor my family too.”

Runa listened to his words. The room seemed to sigh with them. The mint curtains swished. A dust mote danced in the light.

“What if we fight?” Runa asked.

Tiko shook his head. “I don’t like fighting,” he said. “We can find a gentle way.”

Runa’s eyes brightened. “A peaceful solution,” she whispered. She smiled a tiny smile. “Let’s share our traditions.”

“How?” Tiko asked.

Runa looked around. She touched the quilt. She stroked the shawl. She sniffed the clean air and thought of Grandma Owl’s kind eyes. “We can use both,” Runa said.

Tiko tilted his head. “Both?”

“Yes,” said Runa. “We can lay the family quilt on the bed so it is warm and soft. Then, we can fold your silver shawl at the foot of the bed. Grandma can choose to pull it up. She will feel calm from your birds and cozy from our stars. Two traditions, one bed.”

Tiko’s slow smile grew big. “That is peaceful,” he said. “And kind.”

They worked together. Runa smoothed the quilt, pat-pat-pat. Tiko laid the shawl across the end, swish-swish. They tucked the corners until they were neat. The silver birds and yellow stars shone in the same sunbeam. Runa added a small bowl of warm carrot tea on the night table. Tiko placed a tiny bell with a ribbon, a welcome sign from his family.

“Let’s practice greeting Grandma,” Runa said. She stood tall. “Welcome, Grandma Owl,” she said softly. “In our family, we share a quilt for warmth.”

Tiko bowed a little. “And in my family,” he added, “we share a shawl for calm. You may use both.”

They both smiled. It felt good, like honey on toast.

Evening came. The door creaked open. Grandma Owl stepped in, her feathers smelling like pine trees and crisp air. “Hoo-hoo, what a lovely room,” she said. Her voice was mellow and warm. She touched the quilt with one wing and the shawl with the other. “Stars and birds together,” she murmured. “I feel loved and peaceful.”

Runa’s heart did a happy hop. Tiko’s shell seemed to shine.

Grandma Owl sipped the carrot tea. “Did you two have a problem?” she asked gently.

Runa nodded. “We both wanted our traditions. We felt big feelings.”

“But we listened,” Tiko said. “We shared and made a calm plan.”

Grandma Owl smiled, her eyes like tiny moons. “That is true wisdom,” she said. “When traditions meet, let them dance together.”

Runa and Tiko curled up on the soft rug. They felt the coolness of the floor and the warmth of the lamp. The mint curtains whispered a lullaby. The guest room was ready, and their hearts were light.

Lesson: When we have different ideas, we can pause, share our feelings, listen, and make a plan that uses the best of both. Sharing traditions can make one room, one bed, and one world feel big enough for everyone.

The End.
