# The Little Song in the Closet

In a quiet house, there was a cozy closet hideaway. It smelled like clean cotton and a little like cedar wood. Soft scarves brushed like feathers. A tiny light glowed from a night-light shaped like a moon.

Inside the closet sat Pip, a small singing bird. Pip had bright green feathers and a yellow beak. He loved making songs. He hummed, “Tweet-tee, tweet-tee,” and the hangers clicked like little claps.

Pip was practicing a new song. “La-la, li-li,” he sang. But his song sounded wobbly. His voice cracked like a dry leaf. Pip’s tummy felt tight. “Oh no,” he whispered. “My song is not ready.”

Pip wanted to sing for his friend, Momo the mouse, and Lila the ladybug. They were waiting right outside the closet hideaway. He heard their soft voices.

“Is Pip ready?” asked Momo, his whiskers twitching.

“I hope so!” said Lila, wings sparkling in the night-light.

Pip puffed his feathers. “I can do it,” he told himself. Then he made a choice. He flew to the door and chirped too fast. “I’m ready!”

He hopped onto a shoebox stage. The scarves made a curtain. Pip began to sing, “La-la—oh!” His voice squeaked. His notes tumbled like socks from a shelf. He forgot the middle part. His cheeks felt hot. He made a big sound to hide his mistake. “LAAAA!”

The loud note startled Momo and Lila. The hangers swung. A hat fell with a soft thump. Pip gasped. “I didn’t mean to shout,” he said. His heart thumped like a drum.

Momo peeked from behind a boot. “My ears felt a little scared,” he squeaked.

Lila rubbed her wings. “It was a tiny bit too loud,” she said gently.

Pip’s feathers drooped. “I wanted to sing perfect,” he whispered. “But I sang too loud, and I forgot the words. I made a mess.”

He felt a prickly tear. He wanted to fly away. But he stopped. He took a slow breath. The closet smelled like cozy sweaters. The air felt warm and safe.

Pip remembered something his grandma bird once said. “When a song is hard, take a small step. Try again. Little by little makes it better.”

Pip blinked and made a decision. “I can grow,” he told himself. “I can learn.” He turned to Momo and Lila. “I’m sorry I scared you,” he said. “I made my voice too big. I will make it gentle now.” He peeped softly, “Can we try again?”

Momo’s whiskers wiggled. “Thank you for saying sorry,” he said.

Lila smiled. “We believe in you,” she said. “We can help, too.”

“How can you help?” asked Pip.

“We can use word seeds,” said Lila.

“What are word seeds?” asked Pip.

“They are tiny words that help songs grow,” said Momo. “Like soft, slow, and sip.”

“Sip?” Pip tilted his head.

Momo nodded. “Sip air. Not a gulp. Just a sip.” He demonstrated with a tiny sip sound. “Sip makes a soft start.”

Pip giggled. “Sip.” He breathed in, just a little. The air felt cool and sweet, like a tiny bubble in his chest.

“Soft,” said Lila. “Soft like scarf.” She touched the silk scarf. It whispered shhh.

“Slow,” said Momo. “Slow like a sleepy snail.” He traced a little spiral in the dust.

Pip felt the words in his beak. “Sip. Soft. Slow.” He repeated them like a gentle drumbeat. “Sip. Soft. Slow.”

He tried again. “La-la,” he sang, soft as a scarf. “Li-li,” slow as a sleepy snail. He took small sips of air. His song floated like a feather. It was not perfect. One note still wobbled. Pip paused. “Hmm,” he said. “That note is tricky.” He smiled. “Tricky means I can practice.”

“That’s a growth song,” said Lila. “You are learning!”

Pip kept going, one small step at a time. He sang the middle part. He tried the wobbly note again. It was still a little shaky, but less than before. “I can try again,” Pip told himself. “Little by little.”

When he finished, the hangers were still. The hat stayed on the shelf. Momo and Lila clapped their tiny paws and wings.

“That was kind,” said Momo. “My ears feel happy.”

“Thank you for making amends,” said Lila. “You listened and tried again.”

Pip felt warm and proud. His heart was a soft drum now. He looked at his friends. “Thank you for your word seeds,” he said. “Sip, soft, slow helped my song grow.”

Lila nodded. “Good choices grow good songs.”

Pip hummed a small, sweet tune as the moon night-light glowed. He knew his song would keep growing, step by step, sip by sip.

He fluffed his feathers and whispered, “When it’s hard, I try again. When I make a mistake, I say sorry and make it right. I can learn. I can grow.”

The End.
