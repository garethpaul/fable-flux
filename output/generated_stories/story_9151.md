# Porch Parade of Gentle Steps

On a peaceful front porch, a gentle elephant calf named Elo sat with her Grandma Nandi. The porch smelled like sweet flowers and warm tea. A soft breeze tickled Elo’s big ears. Birds chirped, “Peep-peep!” and the porch floor felt smooth and cool under Elo’s little feet.

Grandma Nandi smiled and said, “Today, I will share our family’s Gentle Steps, a special porch parade. It is our family tradition. We move, we breathe, and we share feelings.”

Elo’s eyes grew wide. “A porch parade? Can I do it too?”

“Yes,” Grandma said. “I will teach you, and then you will teach someone else. That is how our tradition keeps growing.”

Elo nodded, excited. But just then, Elo’s little drum rolled off the porch and thumped into the grass. “Oh no!” Elo squeaked. She felt a hot bubble of mad in her belly. Her face scrunched. Her feet stamped. Thump-thump!

Grandma Nandi put a warm, gentle trunk around Elo. “I see big feelings,” she said softly. “Let’s use our Gentle Steps.”

Elo sniffled. “I feel mad.”

“That feeling is okay,” Grandma said. “Step one is Elephant Breath. In through the trunk, out nice and slow.”

Grandma showed Elo. “Snoop in the flower,” she said, sniffing the sweet porch air. “Blow out the candle,” she said, whooshing a slow breath. Elo copied: snoop, blow. The hot bubble felt smaller.

“Step two is Stomp and Soften,” Grandma said. “We use our strong feet to move the mad out. Then we soften our steps.”

They stomped together: stomp-stomp-stomp. The porch boards hummed. Then they made softer steps: tip-tip-tip. The breeze brushed Elo’s cheeks. Her belly felt calm.

“Step three is Share and Care,” Grandma said. “We say our feeling. We ask for help. We listen.”

Elo looked at her drum in the grass. “I feel mad because my drum fell. Can you help me?” she asked.

“Yes,” Grandma said. “You did it. You shared your feeling. Now, let’s add a fun exercise to our parade: Trunk Twirls!”

Grandma and Elo twirled their trunks in slow circles. Swish-swish! Elo giggled. “It tickles!”

They marched in a little line on the porch: two elephants in a gentle parade. Stomp, soften, twirl. Breathe in, breathe out. The birds watched. A butterfly landed on the rail and fluttered like a tiny flag.

Just then, Elo’s friend, a small bunny named Tiko, hopped up the porch steps. “Hi, Elo! Can we play?” Tiko asked. He wiggled his nose. “Oh! Your drum is in the grass.”

Elo felt a small flicker of mad again. Her ears warmed. Her foot lifted to stomp. She remembered Grandma’s words. “Gentle Steps,” she whispered.

Elo took an Elephant Breath. Snoop in the flower. Blow out the candle. She did Stomp and Soften: stomp-stomp, tip-tip. Then she did Trunk Twirls, swish-swish. The flicker went out.

“I feel mad because my drum fell,” Elo said to Tiko. “Can you help me?”

“I can help!” Tiko said. He hopped down, picked up the drum, and brought it back. “Here you go.”

“Thank you,” Elo said. Her heart felt warm, like sunshine on the porch.

Grandma Nandi clapped softly. “Elo, you used our tradition. Now, would you like to share it?”

Elo nodded. “Yes! Tiko, do you want to learn our porch parade?”

Tiko’s ears perked up. “Yes, please!”

Elo stood tall and gentle, just like Grandma. “Step one: Elephant Breath. Snoop the flower. Blow the candle.” They did it together. In, out. Slow and calm.

“Step two: Stomp and Soften,” Elo said. They stomped. The porch made a happy thrum. Then they tiptoed. Tap-tap-tap.

“Step three: Share and Care,” Elo said. “We say our feeling. We ask for help. We listen.”

Tiko thought. “Sometimes I feel mad when my carrot rolls away,” he said. “Can you help me chase it next time?”

“Yes,” Elo said. “I can help.”

“Last, Trunk Twirls,” Elo said, giggling. “If you don’t have a trunk, you can do Ear Swishes.” Tiko swished his long ears in circles. Swish-swish! They both laughed.

They made a new porch parade together. Breathe, stomp, soften, twirl, swish. Their feet moved, their bodies warmed, and their feelings felt light and free. This was exercise that felt like play.

Grandma Nandi poured cool water. The cups clinked. “You have learned and shared,” she said. “This is our tradition. We pass it on with love.”

Elo’s eyes shone. “Grandma taught me. I taught Tiko. Tiko can teach someone too!”

“I will teach my little sister,” Tiko said. “We will have a porch parade at my house.”

The sun slid low and painted the porch gold. The breeze smelled like flowers and fresh grass. Elo hugged Grandma. “Thank you for our tradition.”

Grandma hugged back. “Thank you for sharing it.”

Elo smiled. “When I feel mad, I can breathe, move my body, and share my feelings. I can teach others too.”

On the peaceful front porch, the gentle elephant calf and her friend watched the butterfly flutter away. Their hearts felt calm, their legs felt strong, and their family tradition was ready to travel to many porches, one gentle step at a time.

The End.
