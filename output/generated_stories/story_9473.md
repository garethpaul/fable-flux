# Beep-Boop and the Boundary Book

Beep-Boop was a helpful robot dog with soft, whirring ears and a tail that glowed gentle blue. He lived in a cozy bedroom full of warm blankets, twinkly star stickers on the ceiling, and a little desk by the window. The desk held crayons, thick paper, and a pencil that smelled like fresh wood. Beep-Boop’s person was Mia, a small child with big curious eyes and a giggly laugh.

One sunny morning, light sprinkled through the curtains like golden dust. Beep-Boop padded on soft paws that made a quiet hum. “Good morning, Mia,” he chirped. “Beep! I am ready to help.”

Mia yawned and smiled. “Hi, Beep-Boop.” She hugged her pillow. It was fluffy and smelled like laundry soap. “Today I want to draw and write.”

Beep-Boop wagged his glowing tail. “I love writing! I can help with letters and lines.”

They sat at the desk. Mia drew a big red heart and wrote M-I-A with neat, careful lines. Beep-Boop’s ears went click-click with joy. “Wonderful letters,” he said.

Just then, a knock sounded on the bedroom door. Tap-tap. It was Mia’s cousin, Leo. He popped his head in and grinned. “Hi! Can I come play?”

Mia looked at her paper. She felt happy to see Leo, but also a little unsure. She wanted to finish her special drawing first. Her tummy felt a tiny twist. She didn’t know what to say.

Leo walked in fast. He reached for the red crayon. “I want to draw too!” he said.

Mia pulled back her hand. “Oh,” she whispered.

Beep-Boop noticed Mia’s quiet face. His tail dimmed a little. He said, softly, “Mia, do you want to keep your drawing just for you right now?”

Mia nodded. “Yes. But I don’t want to make Leo sad.”

Beep-Boop blinked a warm blue blink. “We can use kind words to tell Leo how you feel,” he said. “Communication helps everyone understand. We can write the words first to practice.”

Beep-Boop slid a fresh paper gently toward Mia. The paper was smooth and cool. He pushed a purple crayon next to it. “Let’s make a Boundary Book,” he suggested. “A book of our words about space, sharing, and feelings.”

Mia’s eyes grew bright. “Okay!”

Beep-Boop drew a tiny line for each page. “Page one: I feel. Page two: I need. Page three: I will. Page four: Thank you.”

Mia smiled and wrote big, wobbly letters with Beep-Boop guiding her paw.

On Page One, she wrote: I FEEL PROUD OF MY DRAWING.
She added a happy face with rosy cheeks.

On Page Two, she wrote: I NEED TIME TO FINISH.
She drew a little clock with short hands.

On Page Three, she wrote: I WILL SHARE LATER.
She drew two crayons next to each other.

On Page Four, she wrote: THANK YOU FOR LISTENING.
She drew a red heart.

Beep-Boop’s tail glowed brighter. “Beep! These are clear words,” he said. “Now let’s say them out loud.”

Mia took a breath. She turned to Leo, who was bouncing on the soft rug. “Leo,” she said gently, “I feel proud of my drawing. I need time to finish it. I will share later. Thank you for listening.”

Leo froze and looked at her. He blinked. He saw her Boundary Book. He saw the red heart and the tiny clock. He heard her calm voice.

“Oh,” Leo said. He held the red crayon behind his back. “Okay, Mia. I didn’t know. Thanks for telling me.” His cheeks turned pink. “Can I draw with the blue crayon on another paper for now?”

Mia smiled big. “Yes! You can use the blue crayon. When I’m done, we can draw together.”

Beep-Boop wagged. “Excellent communication,” he chimed. “You used your words and your writing. You told your feelings and needs. That is a strong boundary.”

Leo sat on the fluffy rug with a fresh sheet. The crayon made a soft scratch-scratch sound. Mia finished her heart, adding tiny stars around it. The bedroom smelled like crayons and warm blankets. The window showed a tiny bird singing peep-peep.

After a little while, Mia put down her pencil. “I’m done!” she said. She held up her drawing. “Leo, do you want to see?”

Leo scooted closer slowly. “Can I?” he asked.

Mia nodded. “Yes. Thank you for asking.”

Leo looked and smiled. “It’s beautiful.” He handed the red crayon back. “Can we draw a rainbow together now?”

Mia clapped. “Yes! Let’s make a big one.”

Beep-Boop gently placed a new paper between them. “Teamwork time,” he beeped happily.

They took turns with the crayons. Mia chose red and orange. Leo chose green and blue. Beep-Boop drew tiny silver stars with his robot pencil that went zzz-zzz. They talked as they drew.

“I like taking turns,” Leo said.

“I like finishing my picture first,” Mia said. “It makes me feel calm.”

“I like hearing your words,” Beep-Boop said. “When we share our feelings, we help each other.”

When the rainbow was done, it curved across the page like a happy bridge. They taped it on the wall above the bed. The tape made a soft rip sound. The rainbow made the room feel even cozier.

Mia hugged Leo. “Thanks for listening,” she said.

Leo hugged back. “Thanks for telling me.”

Beep-Boop wagged and gave a friendly robo-bark. “Today we learned,” he said. “We can use kind words to set boundaries. We can write our feelings to practice. We can listen and take turns.”

Mia nodded. “Communication helps everyone feel safe,” she said.

Leo nodded too. “And it helps us have fun.”

They all smiled in the warm, sunny room. Beep-Boop’s tail glowed a gentle blue, and the star stickers twinkled like tiny cheers.

The End.
