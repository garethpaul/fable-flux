# Whiskers and the Wiggly Wrench

In a sunny little house, there was a clever cat named Whiskers. Whiskers had bright green eyes, soft gray fur, and a curious nose that liked to twitch. One morning, Whiskers padded into the garage workshop. The air smelled like pine wood and lemon soap. Sunbeams made dust sparkle like tiny stars.

“Wow,” Whiskers purred. “So many tools!” There were shiny screws, colorful tape, and a silver wrench that looked wiggly on the pegboard. On the workbench, a small red wagon waited with one wheel off.

Mia, Whiskers’s friend, wore a blue apron and a gentle smile. “Good morning, Whiskers,” she said. “I’m fixing the wagon. Do you want to help?”

“Yes, yes!” Whiskers meowed, tail swishing. “I can fix it fast!”

Mia laughed softly. “Helping is wonderful. But we must follow directions. We will go step by step.”

Whiskers bounced on his paws. “Step by step,” he agreed, though his whiskers tingled with hurry.

Mia showed a paper with pictures. “First, gather the parts,” she said. “Wheel, bolt, washer, nut.” She pointed to each picture.

Whiskers sniffed the parts. The wheel smelled like rubber. The washer felt cool and smooth. The nut was small and round like a shiny cookie. He stacked them in a neat line. “I did it!”

“Great,” said Mia. “Now, step two: slide the wheel onto the axle.”

Whiskers tried to push the wheel on. It wobbled, went sideways, and slipped to the floor with a boop. “Oh!” he squeaked. His ears drooped.

“It’s okay,” Mia said kindly. “Let’s try again. Hold the wheel straight.”

Whiskers took a slow breath. In… and out. The wheel slid on with a soft shhh. “I did it again!”

“You did,” Mia smiled. “Step three: put on the washer.”

Clink. The washer went on. Easy.

“Step four: twist on the nut,” Mia said. “Righty-tighty. Lefty-loosey.”

Whiskers squinted. “Righty-tighty,” he repeated. He turned the nut to the right. It tightened a little, but not enough. The wheel still wiggled.

“I will use the wiggly wrench!” Whiskers cried. He grabbed the silver wrench and tried to twist fast. The wrench slipped. Clank! It tapped his paw. “Ow!” Whiskers pulled back, fur puffing. His tummy felt hot and tight. “This is too hard!” he hissed.

Mia knelt beside him. “I see you feel frustrated,” she said softly. “Frustration is a big feeling. Let’s pause.”

Whiskers blinked. “Pause?”

“Yes,” said Mia. “We can be mindful. That means we pay attention to right now. Put your paw on your belly. Take three slow breaths.”

Whiskers placed his soft paw on his tummy. He breathed in like smelling warm cookies. He breathed out like blowing dandelion fluff.

“In…” Mia said with him.
“And out,” Whiskers whispered.
“In… and out.”

Whiskers’s fur smoothed. His tummy felt calm. The garage felt quiet again. He could hear the click of a clock and the gentle hum of the fridge.

Mia smiled. “Good. Now, let’s follow the directions again. Slowly. We can use the wrench the right way.”

Whiskers nodded. “Righty-tighty,” he said in a brave little voice.

Mia showed him how to open the wrench so it fit the nut. “Make it snug,” she said. “Not too tight. Not too loose. Hold the wagon steady. Turn to the right.”

Whiskers watched closely. He pressed his paw to keep the wagon still. He turned the wrench slowly. The metal made a quiet creak, like a tiny door. “Creak,” he said, giggling.

“Great job,” Mia said. “Check the wheel.”

Whiskers nudged the wheel. It spun with a happy whirr. “It works!” he cheered, whiskers dancing.

“Almost done,” Mia said. “The last step is to test it outside.”

They rolled the wagon to the driveway. The sun felt warm on Whiskers’s back. A breeze smelled like fresh grass. Whiskers climbed in, and Mia gave a gentle pull. The wagon rolled smooth and straight. Whiskers lifted his paws in joy. “Whee!”

Then Whiskers spotted the pegboard again. His eyes landed on a bottle of glue. “Maybe I can glue a bell to the wagon!” he meowed. He reached for the glue.

Mia touched his shoulder. “Let’s check the directions,” she said. “Is there a step for glue?”

Whiskers looked at the paper. No glue picture. He paused, took one mindful breath, and set the glue down. “No glue,” he said. “We follow directions.”

Mia nodded. “You are clever and careful.”

Whiskers felt proud. His tummy felt light like a bubble. “When I got upset, I breathed,” he said thoughtfully. “When I listened and followed steps, I fixed the wagon.”

Mia patted his head. “Yes. Sometimes we want to rush. But slow steps help us do things well. When we feel frustration, we can stop, breathe, and then try again.”

Whiskers curled his tail. “Stop, breathe, try again,” he repeated. “Righty-tighty, lefty-loosey. Follow directions.”

They parked the wagon and cleaned the tools. The wrench clicked back onto the pegboard. The nut and washer pictures were checked off. The garage felt neat and cozy.

As they finished, Whiskers whispered to the wagon, “Thank you for waiting.” He smiled at Mia. “Thank you for helping.”

“You helped too,” Mia said. “You listened, you cared, and you kept trying.”

Whiskers purred, a soft rumble like a tiny engine. “I can fix things—with steps, breaths, and patience.”

The sun slipped lower, and the tools glowed golden. Whiskers stretched, happy and calm. He had learned something big in the little garage workshop.

Follow directions. Breathe when it’s hard. Try again.

The End.
