# Dori the Dolphin in the Garden Shed

Dori was a playful dolphin with bright, curious eyes. She loved to click and squeak and spin in the water. But one windy day, a big splashy wave lifted Dori and whoosh! carried her far from the sea. The wave rolled and rolled until—bump!—Dori slid into a cozy garden shed near a little house.

The shed smelled like fresh wood and wet leaves. Sunlight peeked through dusty windows, making tiny golden sparkles float in the air. Dori wiggled her tail. “Eee-eee!” she chirped, blinking. “This is not the ocean.”

Inside the shed were boxes, a tall broom, soft soil in a bag, and many shiny things. There were old tin cans, colorful bottle tops, and a broken watering can that made a clink-clank sound when it moved.

“Hello?” Dori called. “Is anyone here?” Her voice echoed, “Here… here…”

A small girl named Maya opened the door slowly. “Oh!” she gasped. “A dolphin? In my garden shed?” Her eyes shone. She wasn’t scared. She was surprised and curious.

Dori flapped her fins. “I’m Dori. I’m lost. I want to go home to the sea.”

Maya knelt beside her. “I’m Maya. I want to help. But how?” She looked at Dori’s smooth, shiny skin and her wiggly tail. “You don’t have legs,” Maya said kindly. “It’s okay. You are different. Different is special.”

Dori smiled with her eyes. “Thank you. I can sing,” she said, and she made happy dolphin clicks: “Eee-eee-eee!”

Maya giggled. “Your voice is beautiful.” She looked around the shed. “We need water to keep you comfy. We need a plan to get you back to the sea.”

Just then, a neighbor boy peeked in. “What’s going on?” he asked. His name was Sam. He held a bucket and wore muddy boots. “Wow! A dolphin?”

“Shh,” Maya said softly. “We have a guest. We must be gentle and kind.”

Sam nodded. “Hi, Dori.”

Dori squeaked, “Hi, Sam.”

Maya said, “Let’s use what we have. We can make a splashy path. We can recycle old things to help.”

Sam looked at the pile of cans and bottles. “We can use these,” he said. “Recycle means use things again in a new way!”

Dori listened, hopeful. “Can I help?”

“You can cheer us on,” said Maya. “You can keep trying, even if it’s hard. That is called resilience. It means we don’t give up.”

Dori took a big breath. “I will try. I will not give up.”

They got to work. Sam filled buckets from the garden hose. Swish, swish—the water sounded like soft rain. Maya spread an old tarp on the floor and poured water over it. “Slip and slide,” she sang.

“Eee-eee!” Dori clapped her fins. The cool water felt nice on her skin. Drip, drop, drip—the shed was full of watery music.

Maya said, “Let’s make the sides safe.” She lined the edges of the tarp with clean, empty cans and soft towels, so Dori wouldn’t bump herself. “We can make a little river,” she said.

Sam found the broken watering can. “It doesn’t pour well, but we can use it like a scoop.” Clink-clank! He scooped and poured, scooped and poured, making the tarp very wet and slippery.

Maya tied old bottle tops to a rope. “These can jingle so we know where the path goes.” Jingle-jingle, the bottle tops sang.

Dori wriggled forward a little. Slip! She slid back. Her eyes got worried. “I’m trying,” she whispered. “It’s hard.”

Maya patted her gently. “You are doing great,” she said. “Let’s take small moves. Small moves are brave.”

Sam nodded. “We will go slowly. We respect your feelings.”

Dori breathed in and out. In and out. She tried again. Wiggle. Slide. Wiggle. Slide. “Eee—eee,” she hummed to keep calm.

Outside, the wind whooshed, and the smell of wet grass came through the door. A robin chirped. The shed felt like a tiny, busy boat.

Step by small step—or wiggle by small wiggle—Dori reached the doorway. The sunlight felt warm on her back. “I’m closer,” she said, her voice bright.

Maya smiled. “Yes! You are strong and brave.”

They kept going. Sam laid more wet towels on the garden path. Maya slid the tarp forward and poured more water. Swish, swish. Jingle-jingle. Clink-clank. Dori wriggled along the cool, slippery path the children made from recycled bits and kind ideas.

At the garden gate, they all paused. “Almost there,” said Sam. The sea was not far. They could hear the soft hush of waves, like a big breath. Hush… hush…

Neighbors came to see. Some looked worried. Some looked amazed. “A dolphin in a garden?” one lady said.

Maya stood tall. “We are helping her go home,” she explained. “We respect her. She is different from us, and that is wonderful.”

The neighbors nodded. “We can help too,” they said. They brought more buckets, more towels, more smiles. Together, they made the path wet and safe.

Dori kept trying. When she slipped, she tried again. When she felt tired, she rested, then tried again. “I am resilient,” she whispered. “I don’t give up.”

At last, the path reached the sandy shore. The salty air tickled Dori’s nose. The ocean sparkled like a giant blue blanket.

“One more wiggle,” said Maya.

“Go, Dori!” cheered Sam.

“Eee-EEE!” sang Dori. She gave one big, brave push and slid into the cool, loving waves. Splash!

The ocean hugged her. She leaped and twirled. “Thank you, friends!” she called. “You used your hearts. You used your heads. You used old things in new ways. You helped me because you care.”

Maya waved. “We learned too,” she said. “We learned resilience. We learned to celebrate differences. We learned to respect others. And we learned to recycle to help our world.”

Dori blew a happy fountain of water. “Keep trying, keep caring, keep helping,” she sang. “Little moves can do big things.”

The sun shone warm. The waves whispered. Everyone smiled.

The End.
