# Dori the Dolphin Visits the Porch

Dori was a playful dolphin with bright eyes and a sunny smile. One calm morning, Dori splashed up the river, following the gentle sound of music. Plink-plink-plunk went the notes. Tap-tap-clap went the beat. The air smelled like warm bread and sweet flowers.

At the end of the river was a peaceful front porch. A big shade tree whispered its leaves. A rocking chair creaked softly. On the porch sat neighbors from many places. They were getting ready for a porch picnic.

“Hello!” Dori chirped, poking her nose over the porch rail. “I heard music!”

“Welcome, Dori,” said Mrs. Rivera, waving. “Today we are sharing songs and snacks from our homes.”

Dori bounced with excitement. “I love to play! I can splash and squeak. I can teach a dolphin dance!”

Mr. Kofi strummed a small wooden kora. The strings sang like sunshine. “We’d love to learn,” he said, smiling.

Dori wiggled happily, but then she frowned a little. “I want to join, but I don’t know these songs. What if I do it wrong?” Her tummy felt fluttery, like tiny bubbles.

That was the problem.

“Take a slow breath,” said Grandma Mei, handing Dori a cool, damp cloth. “Self-care first. Breathe in… and out.” Dori pressed the cloth to her forehead. It smelled like mint. She felt calmer.

“Thank you,” Dori whispered. “I will try.”

Mina, a little girl in a bright scarf, clapped softly. “We can learn together,” she said. “We can try a little, and try again. That is a growth mindset!”

Dori nodded. “Try a little, and try again,” she echoed.

First, Mrs. Rivera sang a lullaby from her childhood. Her voice was warm like a blanket. “La-la-la, duérmete,” she sang. The syllables were smooth and round.

“It is new to me,” said Dori. “Can you say it slow?”

“Of course,” said Mrs. Rivera. “Du-er-me-te.” She smiled. “These words are Spanish.”

Dori repeated, “Du-er-me-te,” and clapped the gentle beat. Clap… clap… clap. Everyone clapped with her, and Dori’s cheeks felt bright with pride.

Next, Mr. Kofi plucked the kora. “This rhythm is from my home,” he said. “Listen.” The notes danced like raindrops. Dori closed her eyes to hear the pattern. Tap tap-ta-tap. Tap tap-ta-tap.

“I will count it,” Dori said. “One-two, pause, two!” She tried the beat with her tail. Swoosh-tap, swoosh-tap. She missed once and splashed a little too big.

“It’s okay,” Mina giggled. “We try again.” She patted Dori’s flipper. “You are learning something special from my friends.”

Then Grandma Mei brought out a small drum and tiny buns that smelled of sesame. “This song is from my family,” she said. “We share food and music to show love.”

Dori took a tiny nibble of a bun. It was soft and sweet. “Mmm,” she hummed. “Sharing helps me understand.” She tapped the drum, dum-dum, dum-dum. The sound felt like a heartbeat.

“Would you like to teach us the dolphin dance now?” asked Mr. Kofi.

Dori looked at the rocking chair, the tree, the friends. She thought of the soft lullaby, the sunny strings, the steady drum. She took another slow breath. “Yes,” she said. “But I want to blend our ways. We can make a porch dance!”

She showed a simple wave move with her flipper. “Wave like water,” she said.

Mina added a gentle clap on the lullaby beat. Clap… clap… clap.

Mr. Kofi added the raindrop notes. Tap tap-ta-tap.

Grandma Mei tapped the drum, dum-dum, dum-dum, and the porch hummed with happy sounds.

Dori led the dance. “Wave, clap, tap-tap-ta-tap, dum-dum!” The wind carried the music. The porch smelled like tea and fruit. The floor felt warm on everyone’s feet. Laughter bubbled up like little fountains.

Dori made a small mistake and spun the wrong way. She giggled and tried again. “It’s okay to be new,” she said. “Trying is how I grow.”

After the dance, everyone rested. They sipped cool water and stretched their shoulders. “Rest is good self-care,” said Grandma Mei. “Our bodies need water and quiet.”

Dori nodded and took small sips. The water tasted like fresh rain. Her heartbeat slowed, steady and calm.

Mrs. Rivera handed Dori a bright ribbon. “For your brave trying,” she said. “And for honoring our songs.”

Dori’s eyes shone. “I learned your words and beats,” she said. “And we danced together. Your ways are special. My ways are special. When we share and listen, we make something new and beautiful.”

Everyone smiled. The rocking chair creaked, creak-swish, like it agreed.

That was the solution.

On the peaceful front porch, the neighbors clapped one more time. Clap… clap… clap. Dori waved her flipper. “Thank you for teaching me,” she said. “I will remember to listen, to try, and to care for myself.”

“And we will remember to welcome and learn,” said Mina.

The porch glowed with warm light. The river whispered. The tree leaves sang a soft shhh. The friends felt close and proud.

Lesson: We can honor each other’s cultures by listening, learning, and sharing. With a growth mindset and good self-care, we can try new things and make beautiful music together.

The End.
