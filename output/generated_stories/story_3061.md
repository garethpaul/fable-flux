# Benny Beaver Builds His Brave

Benny was a busy beaver with bright, curious eyes. He loved to use his little paws and his big, flat tail. Today, he sat on the peaceful front porch of his wooden home. The porch smelled like fresh pine. The sun felt warm on his fur. Birds sang soft songs from the trees. The porch boards felt smooth under his feet.

Benny had a new plan. “Today,” he said, tapping his tail gently, “I will learn to whistle and tie a bow all by myself!” His voice sounded excited and a tiny bit wobbly. He set a red ribbon on the porch rail. He took a deep breath. He tried to whistle.

“Pfffff!” He blew air. No whistle sang back.

Benny’s cheeks puffed. He tried again. “Pff-pff-pffff!” Still no whistle. His shoulders drooped.

Mama Beaver opened the porch door. She brought a cool cup of apple slices. “Good morning, Benny,” she said softly. “I hear your brave breath.”

Benny looked up. “Mama, I can’t whistle. Maybe I’m not good at it.”

Mama knelt beside him and stroked his fur. “Learning takes time. Your first tries are steps. Every step is practice.”

Benny nodded. The apple slices were sweet and juicy. He felt the cool juice on his tongue and smiled a little. “Okay. I will practice.”

He took a sip of water. He put his lips together, soft and round. He breathed out slowly. “Ffffff… fffwee?” A tiny sound flickered like a baby bird.

Benny’s eyes grew big. “Did you hear that?” His heart felt light, like a leaf in the wind.

“I did,” said Mama, smiling. “Small sounds grow into songs.”

Benny giggled. He tried again. “Fffwee! Fwee!” The little sounds came and went. Some tries were quiet. Some tries squeaked. Benny clapped his paws. He felt proud. The warm sun felt like a hug.

Then he looked at the red ribbon. “Now I will tie a bow,” he said. He picked up the ribbon. It felt smooth and silky. He looped one end and then the other. The ribbon wriggled and slipped away.

“Oh no,” said Benny. “My bow looks like a spaghetti worm!” He giggled at the funny shape, but his cheeks felt hot.

Just then, his friend Dotty the Duck waddled up the path. “Quack! Hi, Benny. Can I see?” Dotty hopped onto the porch, her little feet making soft tap-tap sounds.

Benny held up the wiggly ribbon. “I am trying to tie a bow. But the ribbon runs away.”

Dotty fluffed her feathers. “I can help!” She grabbed the ribbon with her bill and pulled. The ribbon slid fast and knocked over Benny’s little cup of water. Splash! The water ran over the porch and right onto Benny’s drawing paper.

“My picture!” cried Benny. The colors blurred. His eyes filled with tears. “Dotty, I didn’t want help like that!”

Dotty’s wings drooped. “Oh,” she said in a small voice. “I am sorry, Benny. I did not ask. I was too quick.”

Benny took a deep breath. He felt the cool air go in and out. He looked at Dotty’s sad eyes. He remembered how he felt when he could not whistle. He remembered Mama’s warm words.

“It’s okay to make mistakes,” Benny said softly. “Thank you for saying sorry.” He set the wet paper in the sun. “I can draw again later.”

Dotty nodded. “Can I sit with you? I can cheer. I can quack softly. Quack-quack.”

Benny smiled. “Yes, please.” He wiped the porch dry with a towel. The towel felt fluffy and smelled like sunshine.

He picked up the ribbon again. “Loop, swoop, and pull,” he whispered. His paws moved slow. The ribbon slipped once. It slipped twice. Benny felt a tiny frown. He remembered: first tries are steps.

“Loop, swoop, and pull,” he said again. He pulled the ends carefully. The bow tightened.

Dotty leaned close. “Quack-quack! You did it!”

Benny looked down. A real bow sat in his paws. It was neat and red and bright. His heart felt big and brave. “I did it! I tied a bow!”

Mama stepped onto the porch with a soft smile. “I see a helper, a good apology, and a lot of practice,” she said.

Benny wiggled his tail happily. “I practiced. I kept trying. I listened to my breath. I forgave Dotty. I can learn new things.” He put the bow on his tail and laughed when it tickled.

“Now,” said Benny, “I will try to whistle my bow song.” He put his lips together and breathed. “Fffwee… fwee… FWEET!” A clear little whistle flew into the air. Birds in the trees answered with happy chirps.

Dotty clapped her wings. “Quack-hooray!”

Mama hugged Benny. The hug was warm and gentle. “You showed self-confidence,” she said. “You believed in you. You practiced. And you were kind when someone made a mistake.”

Benny smiled at the quiet porch, the shining sun, and his red bow. “I can do hard things when I practice. I can say sorry when I make a mess. I can forgive. I can try again.”

The porch felt extra peaceful, like the world was taking a sweet, calm breath with Benny. He took one more slow breath and whispered, “Fwee.”

The End.
