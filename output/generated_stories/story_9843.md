# Pippa Parrot and the Painty Pause

Pippa Parrot loved colors. She was a creative parrot with bright green feathers and a beak that clicked when she thought. Click, click! She lived near a sunny school with a special creative art room. The art room smelled like crayons and orange peels. Soft music hummed. Light danced on jars of brushes and cups of water. Warm paper lay in a neat stack, smooth as a pond.

“Hello, art room!” Pippa chirped one morning as she fluttered to the big window. “Time to make something bright!”

Inside, Mr. Diego, the art teacher, waved. “Good morning, Pippa! We are painting ocean animals today,” he said. “Please remember our safety rules and personal space. Ask before you touch someone or their tools.”

Pippa nodded. “Ask first. Got it!” Her feathers puffed with excitement.

Children in aprons stood at easels. Blue paint swished like waves. The brushes made whispery sounds: swish, swish. Pippa loved the sound. She loved the colors. She loved everything! She zipped from one easel to the next.

“Oh! A purple whale!” Pippa chirped to Maya. “Can I try a dot?” Without waiting, Pippa dipped her feather tip in purple and tapped the whale’s tail. Dot!

Maya’s smile faded. “Pippa, that was my painting. You didn’t ask.”

Pippa blinked. “Oh. I forgot.” Her beak clicked, click. Her tummy felt wiggly.

Pippa flitted to Jamal’s table. “Green seaweed! My favorite!” She reached for a shiny, silver scissors.

Jamal pulled back. “That’s my scissors. Please ask. And scissors are sharp.”

Pippa froze. “Sharp? Oh.” She put the scissors down. Tap. Her heart fluttered fast.

Mr. Diego walked over. His voice was calm and warm. “Pippa, remember personal boundaries. We ask for permission before we touch someone or their things.”

Pippa shuffled her feet. “I got too excited,” she said softly. “I wanted to help.”

“It’s kind to want to help,” Mr. Diego said, “and it’s also kind to ask. Asking keeps everyone safe and happy.”

Pippa looked around. Colorful paint. Busy hands. Careful scissors. Smiling friends—well, almost smiling. “I want my friends to feel safe,” she whispered.

Just then, Pippa saw a green paint cup wobble near the edge of the table. Drip, drip—plop! The cup tipped, and green water slid like a snake toward the floor.

“Uh-oh!” Pippa squawked. “Spill!” The water oozed toward the recycling bin full of paper scraps. A strong paint smell puffed into the air.

“Safety first,” Mr. Diego called. “Everyone, step back.”

Pippa flapped her wings, but she remembered not to touch anyone without asking. “May I help?” she asked Mr. Diego.

“Yes, thank you,” he said. “Please hold the bin steady while I get the towels.”

Pippa wrapped her wings around the bin, careful and gentle. “I’m holding it,” she told the children. “I won’t move you.”

Maya came close. “Pippa, can I stand next to you?”

“Yes,” said Pippa. “Thanks for asking.”

Together they kept the bin safe while Mr. Diego blotted the green puddle. Squish, squish. The towels turned leafy green.

“Now,” Mr. Diego said, “let’s care for our room and our Earth. We’ll put paint water in the sink, not on the floor or in the trash. That keeps our space clean and our planet happy.”

Pippa nodded. “Clean room, clean Earth.”

After the spill, Pippa took a slow breath. “I need to fix my mistake,” she said. She fluttered to Maya’s easel. “Maya,” she began, “I am sorry I touched your painting. I did not ask. That was your space and your art.”

Maya’s eyes softened. “Thank you for telling the truth,” she said. “That is honest. Next time, ask.”

Pippa turned to Jamal. “I am sorry I grabbed your scissors. I did not ask, and scissors are sharp. That was not safe.”

Jamal nodded. “Thanks for being honest. We can share tools when we ask and when a grown-up says it’s safe.”

Pippa felt lighter, like a paper kite. “May I make my own painting now?” she asked Mr. Diego.

“Yes,” he smiled. “Here is your paper, brush, and your very own cup for rinsing. Remember to keep your wings to yourself unless someone says yes.”

Pippa set up her space. She lined up her colors. She kept her cup at the center of the table so it couldn’t tip. She whispered, “Safe and tidy.” The water made soft clinks as she cleaned her brush. Clink, clink.

She painted an ocean full of polite fish. Each fish had a tiny speech bubble. “May I swim here?” one fish asked. “Yes, thank you,” another replied. Pippa giggled. “My fish have good manners!”

Maya came by. “Pippa, your fish are cute. May I add one small bubble on the corner?”

Pippa looked at her painting. She looked at Maya. She smiled. “Yes, you may,” she said. “Thank you for asking.”

Maya added a tiny bubble with a blue dot inside. “Pop!” she said, and they laughed.

When the paintings dried, the children and Pippa cleaned up. They put lids on paint, rinsed cups in the sink, and sorted paper in the recycling bin. Pippa hummed a happy tune.

Mr. Diego clapped softly. “Today we learned big lessons,” he said. “We asked before touching. We told the truth. We stayed safe. We cared for our art room and our Earth.”

Pippa puffed her feathers with pride. “Asking shows care. Honesty fixes mistakes. Clean rooms help the planet. And safe tools keep us well.”

She looked at her friends. “May I give a wing wave?” she asked.

“Yes!” everyone cheered.

Pippa waved her wing. “Thank you for asking and thank you for saying yes,” she chirped.

The End.
