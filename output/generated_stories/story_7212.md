# The Owl in the Guest Room

In a quiet house with soft carpets and sunny windows, there was a cozy guest room. The room smelled like clean sheets and warm vanilla. Sunshine made golden shapes on the floor. The guest bed was puffy like a white cloud. A small lamp gave a gentle glow, like a night star.

One day, a wise old owl named Olwyn visited. Olwyn had soft gray feathers, bright round eyes, and a calm hoot like a lullaby. He wore a tiny blue scarf and carried a little notebook in his wing.

“Whoo-hoo,” Olwyn said kindly as he fluttered onto a chair by the window. “What a peaceful place.”

But then, a little quarrel began.

Two children, Mia and Leo, came into the guest room with a box of colorful building blocks. The blocks clicked and clacked as they made towers on the soft rug.

“I want the red blocks,” Mia said, holding them tight.

“But I need them for my bridge,” Leo said, frowning. “Red is strong!”

Mia crossed her arms. “Red is for my rocket!”

Leo stomped his foot. “That’s not fair!”

Olwyn blinked slowly. He listened. He watched. He smelled the warm vanilla air and felt the gentle sun on his feathers. He heard the click-clack of blocks and the shaky voices of Mia and Leo.

“This room is usually quiet,” Olwyn whispered to himself. “But today, there is a problem. Let’s find a fair and kind way.”

Olwyn hopped closer and spoke softly. “Whoo-hoo, friends. I see big feelings. I hear loud words. Let’s take a slow breath together.”

Mia and Leo looked at the owl. They breathed in and out. In and out. The warm air made their shoulders relax.

“Good,” said Olwyn. “Now, let’s use owl steps. Step one: We say our feelings. Step two: We listen. Step three: We find a fair plan.”

Mia nodded. “I feel mad,” she said. “I really want the red blocks for my rocket.”

Leo nodded too. “I feel upset,” he said. “I want the red blocks for my bridge.”

Olwyn wrote in his little notebook. Scratch, scratch. “Thank you. You said your feelings. Now, step two: we listen. Mia, can you tell me what Leo said?”

Mia looked at Leo. “You feel upset. You want red blocks for your bridge.”

“Good listening,” said Olwyn. “Leo, can you tell me what Mia said?”

Leo looked at Mia. “You feel mad. You want red blocks for your rocket.”

“Excellent,” Olwyn hooted gently. “You listened with your ears and your hearts.”

The room felt calmer. The lamp glowed warm. The blocks waited quietly.

“Now,” said Olwyn, “step three: we find a fair plan. Fair means everyone is heard. Fair means we share and take turns. Fair means justice.”

“Justice?” asked Mia. “What is that?”

Olwyn smiled. “Justice means we make things right for everyone. Not just one. It means we look for a plan that helps both of you.”

Leo rubbed the red blocks with his fingers. They felt smooth and cool. “Maybe we can split them?”

Mia counted the blocks. “There are six. We can share!”

Olwyn nodded. “That is one fair plan. Are there others?”

Mia tapped her chin. “We can use red blocks for the rocket first. Then Leo uses them for the bridge.”

Leo thought. “Or we can mix colors. Red and blue can be strong. Red and yellow can be fast.”

Olwyn chuckled. “You are both leaders today. Leaders listen. Leaders share. Leaders help.”

Mia smiled. “I like the take-turns plan.”

Leo smiled too. “I like the mix-color plan.”

Olwyn fluffed his feathers. “We can blend the plans. First, choose two red blocks for the rocket and two for the bridge. Then take turns with the last two. One turn for Mia. One turn for Leo.”

Mia and Leo looked at each other. Their eyes were bright. “Yes!” they said together.

They set to work. Click-clack went the blocks. Mia built a rocket with red, yellow, and blue. Leo built a bridge with red, green, and blue. The rocket stood tall like a lighthouse. The bridge stretched wide like a rainbow path.

Sometimes Mia needed a red block. She asked, “May I have a turn, please?”

Leo checked the plan. “Yes,” he said. “After I finish this part.”

He finished, then handed the block to Mia. “Your turn.”

“Thank you,” said Mia. “Here is your turn back.”

Olwyn watched with happy eyes. The guest room felt bright again. The air smelled sweet. The sun made the blocks shine.

When the rocket was done, it had a red nose and yellow wings. When the bridge was done, it had strong red posts and blue rails. Mia and Leo clapped.

Olwyn hooted softly. “You did it. You solved the problem. You used words. You listened. You made a fair plan. That is justice. That is leadership.”

Mia hugged Leo. “I’m proud of us.”

Leo hugged Mia back. “We shared. We can do it again.”

Olwyn wrote one more line in his notebook. Scratch, scratch. “Lesson learned: When we have big feelings, we breathe, we speak, we listen, and we make a fair plan.”

He tucked his notebook away and opened his wings. “Whoo-hoo. Remember the owl steps.”

Mia and Leo said, “We will!”

Olwyn gave a warm wink. “Peace feels nice in this guest room.”

The lamp glowed. The blocks rested. The room was calm and happy again.

The End.
