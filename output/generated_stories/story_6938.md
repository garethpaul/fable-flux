# Nia the Giraffe and the Pantry Plan

In a cozy house with sunshine stripes on the floor, a thoughtful giraffe named Nia peeked into a big pantry. The pantry smelled like warm oats and sweet bananas. Tiny jars clicked when she moved them. Crinkly bags rustled like leaves in the wind.

Nia loved this pantry. She loved the crunchy crackers, the round apples, and the soft bread. But today, Nia had a busy day. She wanted to make picnic snacks, help a friend, and still go outside to play. Her tail swished with worry.

“I want to do everything,” Nia said softly. “But I don’t know when to do it.”

Her friend, a little mouse named Momo, poked his whiskered nose from behind a cereal box. “Squeak! What’s the plan, Nia?”

Nia took a deep breath. She felt the cool pantry floor under her hooves. “I don’t have a plan,” she said. “I just have a lot to do. I want to make sandwiches. I want to help you fix your tiny wagon. And I want to go ride my scooter outside.”

Momo’s ears twitched. “Maybe we can make a plan. A kind plan with time for everything.”

Nia blinked. “A kind plan?”

“Yes,” said Momo. “Kind to you. Kind to me. Kind to time!”

Nia smiled. “That sounds nice.”

She looked around. The pantry light was soft like morning butter. “First, we make a list,” she said. She found a big notepad and a blueberry-blue crayon. The crayon smelled a little fruity.

She wrote with slow, careful letters:

1. Make picnic sandwiches.
2. Help Momo fix wagon.
3. Go outside for scooter play.

Nia tapped her hoof. “Now we give each thing a time.”

Momo nodded. “Squeak! How long for sandwiches?”

Nia listened to the bag rustles and the clock’s gentle tick from the kitchen. Tick… tock… tick… tock. “Sandwiches need fifteen minutes,” she said. “We will spread peanut butter, slice bananas, and pack apples.”

Momo clapped his tiny paws. “My wagon needs ten minutes. The wheel is wobbly.”

Nia giggled. “Then twenty minutes for outside scooter play. I want to feel the breeze.”

She drew three big boxes next to the list. “We color a box when we finish,” Nia said. “That will help our plan stay friendly.”

Momo pointed. “When do we start?”

Nia looked at the clock again. “We start now. Click!” She pretended to press a button with her hoof. “Sandwich time!”

They got to work. The pantry smelled sweet when Nia peeled the banana. “It’s soft like a cloud,” she said. The peanut butter made a gentle sticky sound when she spread it. “Shloop, shloop.”

Momo handed her slices. “Here you go!”

“Thank you,” said Nia. “Helping is kind.” She packed apple slices into a little box. “Crunchy and red,” she said, sniffing. “They smell fresh like morning.”

Tick… tock… tick… tock. Nia peeked at the clock. “We have three more minutes for sandwiches,” she said. “Let’s clean as we go.”

They wiped crumbs. They closed jars. The pantry looked neat and calm. Nia colored the first box on the list in blueberry-blue.

“Sandwiches done!” she cheered.

“Wagon time!” Momo squeaked.

They rolled Momo’s tiny wagon into the pantry. The wheel wiggled like a loose tooth.

Nia crouched low. “I will hold it steady,” she said. “You tighten the tiny screw.”

Momo twisted with a small shiny tool. “Twist, twist.”

Nia watched the clock. “Four minutes left,” she said kindly. “You can do it.”

Momo breathed in. He listened to the clock with Nia. Tick… tock. “Almost… done!” he squeaked.

The wheel stopped wobbling. Nia and Momo smiled. Nia colored the second box. “Helping a friend feels warm inside,” she said. She patted her chest. “Like sunshine.”

Momo nodded. “Squeak! Thank you.”

Nia looked at the third box. “Outdoor time!” she said. “We have twenty minutes.”

They carried the picnic bag outside. The air smelled like grass and orange flowers. A soft wind tickled Nia’s nose. “Ah-choo!” she giggled.

She put on her helmet. “Safety first,” she said. “Time for scooting.”

Nia pushed her scooter gently. The wheels hummed on the path. “Whirr… whirr.” She felt the breeze on her cheeks. Momo rode in his tiny wagon behind her, waving.

Birds chirped. Leaves whispered. The sky was bright and blue, like Nia’s crayon. Nia laughed. “This is fun!”

After a few happy loops, Nia checked the little kitchen timer she had clipped to her basket. It beeped softly. “Beep-beep.”

“That means time is done,” Nia said. “We did our plan.”

They sat by a sunny spot and ate their sandwiches. “Yum,” said Nia. “Soft, sweet, and crunchy!”

Momo nibbled an apple slice. “Squeak! We did everything. We didn’t rush. We didn’t forget.”

Nia leaned back and listened to the trees. She felt calm and proud. “Our kind plan helped us. We made time for snacks, helping, and play,” she said. “Time is like a friend. When we make a plan, time is happy, and we are happy too.”

Momo smiled. “Next time, we can plan nap time and story time.”

Nia nodded. “Yes. We can make boxes for that too.” She colored a tiny heart on the bottom of the page. “A heart for compassion. We were kind to each other.”

They packed up, cleaned up, and went back inside. The pantry smelled tidy, like vanilla and fresh bread. The list hung on a little hook, waiting for the next day.

Nia whispered, “Plan, help, play, and rest. One thing at a time.”

She felt brave and peaceful. She had learned that making a simple plan, watching the clock, and being kind made the day bright and fun.

The End.
