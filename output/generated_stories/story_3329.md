# Benny Beaver's Basement Balance

In a busy basement full of boxes and bright bins, a busy beaver named Benny bustled about. The floor was cool and smooth. The air smelled a little like paint and laundry soap. A tiny window let in a stripe of sunshine that made dust sparkle like stars.

Benny loved to build. He stacked blocks, rolled tape, and clicked little toy tools. “Build, build, build!” he hummed. His flat tail thumped softly on the floor. Thump, thump. Benny’s whiskers wiggled with excitement.

Today, Benny wanted to make a brave beaver fort. He pulled a big blue bin. He tugged a tall cardboard tube. He pushed a pile of pillows. “I can do it all!” he said.

But then the basement buzzed with too much busy. The blocks clattered. The tape got stuck on his fur. The tall tube wobbled—whoa! Benny’s tail thumped louder. Thump, THUMP. His heart felt rumbly, like a drum in his chest.

“I want it done now!” Benny squeaked. He grabbed more blocks. They slipped and spilled. Clackety-clack! A tower tipped and fell with a crash. Benny’s cheeks felt hot. His eyes got wet. He stamped his webby feet. “Grrr. It’s too hard!”

Mama Beaver peeked down the stairs. “I hear big sounds,” she said softly. “Is my busy beaver feeling too big inside?”

Benny sniffed. “Everything is wiggly. My body is jumpy. I feel like a storm.”

Mama nodded. “Let’s try our calm plan. Remember: stop, breathe, and move.”

Benny blinked. “Stop, breathe, and move,” he whispered.

Mama held up a gentle paw. “First, we stop. Put the tools down.”

Benny put the tape and blocks on the floor. His tail stopped thumping, just a little.

“Next, we breathe,” Mama said. “Smell the warm cocoa.” She pretended to hold a mug. Benny copied her. They sniffed in. Mmmm. “Blow the cool cocoa.” They blew out slowly. Ffffff.

In, out. In, out. Benny’s shoulders dropped. His tummy felt softer.

“Last, we move,” Mama said. “Let’s do beaver exercises to shake out the storm.”

Benny nodded. “Beaver exercises!”

They did five tail taps. Tap, tap, tap, tap, tap. They did five log rolls on the carpet. Roll, roll, giggle, roll, roll. They did five strong wall pushes. “Push the wall, feel the power,” Mama said. Benny felt his muscles work. Warm and steady.

The basement didn’t feel so noisy now. Benny’s heart was a quiet drum. “I can think again,” he said.

Then he saw the mess. Blocks spread like raindrops. The tall tube had bumped a small paint tray and made a tiny splat on the floor. “Oh no,” Benny whispered. “I made a mess.”

Mama handed him a damp cloth. “You can fix it. When we make a mistake, we clean up, and we say sorry.”

Benny took a brave breath. He wiped the little splat. Swipe, swipe. He stacked blocks back into the bin. Clink, clink. He patted the pillows into a neat pile. “I’m sorry for the mess, Mama,” he said, looking up.

“Thank you for your good apology,” Mama said, smiling. “That was kind and strong.”

Benny felt taller. He had a new idea. “Maybe my fort can be smaller and steadier,” he said. “I can make a cozy corner fort!”

He drew a simple plan on a scrap of cardboard with a stubby blue crayon. “Door here. Pillow wall here. Tube becomes a periscope!” He made a soft whooshing sound, “Whooosh,” and smiled.

Benny began again, slowly. He put four big blocks for the base. He tried one step at a time. When his tail started to wiggle too fast, he paused. “Stop, breathe, and move,” he reminded himself. He did three wall pushes. Push, push, push. Calm came back.

He taped a blanket to a chair. Sticky-sticky. He set the pillows in a snug line. Puff, pat, puff. He slid the cardboard tube through a blanket hole. “Periscope ready!” He peered through. The world looked round and silly. “I see Mama! Hi, Mama!”

Mama waved. “Hi, Captain Beaver.”

Benny crawled inside. The fort smelled like clean blanket and crayons. It felt cozy and safe. “I did it!” he cheered. “My body listened. My brain helped. My heart is happy.”

Mama ducked her head into the fort. “What helped most, Benny?”

Benny tapped his nose. “I stopped. I breathed cocoa. I moved my body. Then I said sorry and tried a new way. That is self-reg… self-regu… self-regulation!”

Mama clapped softly. “Yes. That means you choose what to do with your big feelings.”

Benny grinned. “When I feel like a storm, I can be my own umbrella.”

They snuggled in the cozy fort. The little window’s sunshine stripe slid across the floor. The basement hummed softly, not too busy, just right.

Benny whispered, “Tomorrow, I’ll build a boat.” He smiled and added, “And if I feel wiggly, I know what to do.”

The End.
