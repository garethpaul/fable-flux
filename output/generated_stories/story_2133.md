# Poppy Panda and the Fair-Turn Treehouse

High in a breezy bamboo forest, there sat a cozy treehouse. The treehouse smelled like warm wood and sweet leaves. Birds sang soft songs, and a yellow ribbon fluttered on the door.

Inside lived Poppy Panda. Poppy had soft black-and-white fur and the calmest eyes. She liked to breathe slow, count to three, and think kind thoughts. Her friends loved to visit.

One sunny morning, Poppy hung a sign on the wall. It said, “Fair-Turn Game Day.” She set out toys: a tiny slide for plush animals, a drum, a paint corner, and a basket of shiny marbles. She also set a bell on a stool. “The bell can help us take turns,” Poppy said to herself, patting it gently. “Ding means next.”

Soon, her friends climbed up the ladder. Tiko the tiny tiger bounced in first. Miri the monkey swung in with her tail. Lila the lamb tiptoed in, and Benji the bluebird fluttered to the window perch.

“Welcome!” Poppy smiled. “We will share. We will listen. We will try our best.”

“Yes!” chirped Benji.

“Let’s go!” giggled Miri, reaching for the drum.

Tiko also reached for the drum at the same time. Their paws bumped. “Mine!” Tiko growled. “I saw it first!”

Miri hugged the drum to her chest. “No, I did!”

The treehouse felt tight and prickly, like a storm in a small room. Lila shrank back. Benji fluffed his feathers. Poppy felt her tummy twist a little. She wanted to shout, “Stop!” But she remembered her practice.

Poppy put a paw on her chest and breathed in through her nose. She counted, “One… two… three.” She breathed out slowly. The tight feeling got softer.

She spoke in a warm voice. “Friends, I see big feelings. Let’s use the bell.”

“It’s not fair!” Tiko said. His stripes looked droopy.

“I’m mad,” Miri said. Her tail twitched.

Poppy nodded. “I hear you both. You both want the drum. Let’s make a plan that is fair for everyone. That’s justice.”

“What is just-ice?” Lila asked, peeking from behind a cushion.

“Justice means we treat everyone in the same fair way,” Poppy said. “No favorites. No pushing. We follow the same rules together.”

Benji hopped closer. “How do we do that?”

Poppy held up the bell. “We can take turns. We can use a sand timer. Each friend gets a turn. When the sand is done, the bell goes ding, and the next friend plays.”

Tiko crossed his arms. “But I want it now.”

Poppy felt the twist in her tummy again. She squeezed a soft bamboo pillow. She counted, “One… two… three.” Then she asked, “Can we listen to each other? Tiko, tell us why you want the drum.”

Tiko’s voice got small. “I like the boom. It sounds like my heart.” He patted his chest. “Boom, boom.”

Miri blinked. “I did not know that.”

Poppy turned to Miri. “Miri, why do you want the drum?”

Miri shrugged. “I want to make silly jungle songs. I was excited.”

Poppy smiled. “Thank you for sharing. I feel warm in my heart when we listen. Here is the plan: Miri can have the first turn for one sand timer. Then we ring the bell. Tiko gets the next turn for one sand timer. While we wait, we can choose another fun thing.”

Miri and Tiko looked at each other. Tiko’s tail swished. Miri’s ears wiggled. “Okay,” they said together.

Poppy flipped the sand timer. Golden sand began to fall. Miri tapped the drum softly. Tap, tap… TAP. Benji hummed along. Lila painted tiny stars. Tiko squeezed the bamboo pillow and breathed like Poppy. “One… two… three,” he whispered. “I can wait.”

When the sand ran out, Poppy rang the bell. “Ding!” Miri hugged the drum to her chest, then paused. “Tiko, I’m sorry I grabbed. I can forgive you for shouting if you forgive me for grabbing.”

Tiko blinked. “I forgive you. I am sorry I yelled.”

Poppy’s eyes shone. “That is forgiveness. Thank you.”

Tiko played next. Boom… boom… boom-boom! The drum sounded warm and round. The treehouse felt light again, like sunshine on bamboo leaves.

Later, a new problem popped up. The basket of shiny marbles slipped off a shelf. Marbles rolled everywhere—plip, plip, plip—like tiny rain. Lila bleated, “Oh no! My stars!”

“Stop!” Tiko said, reaching fast.

“Wait!” Miri cried, running in front.

The room started to buzz again.

Poppy felt the tight twist return. She put her paws on the floor. She breathed in, “One… two… three.” She breathed out and spoke clearly. “Freeze feet. Soft voices. We will make a fair plan.”

Everyone froze. The marbles sparkled on the floor.

Poppy said, “We will each pick up five marbles. We will count out loud. Then we will put them back together. That is cooperation. We help each other.”

“One… two… three… four… five,” they counted. Little paws and wings moved gently. The basket filled up again.

When they were done, Poppy clapped softly. “We did it. We waited. We listened. We were fair. We forgave. We helped.”

Benji chirped, “Poppy, how do you stay so calm?”

Poppy touched the bamboo pillow. “I feel my feelings. I breathe. I count. Then I choose kind actions.”

The sun slid lower. The treehouse glowed golden. Poppy hung a new sign: “We Can Do Hard Things, Calmly and Fairly.”

Miri smiled. “Today I learned to wait.”

Tiko nodded. “I learned to share.”

Lila said, “I learned what justice means.”

Benji fluffed his wings. “I learned to count and breathe.”

Poppy hugged them all. “I learned we can do it together.”

They watched the birds sing and the leaves dance. The treehouse smelled sweet and safe.

The End.
