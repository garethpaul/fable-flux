# Milla Mouse and the Treehouse Cleanup

High in a cozy oak, a kind mouse named Milla lived in a sunny treehouse. Her walls smelled like pine. The floor was smooth and warm. A tiny bell chimed when the breeze came through the round window.

One bright morning, Milla peeked outside. “Good morning, tree!” she squeaked. She stretched, rubbed her whiskers, and listened. Birds chirped. Leaves rustled. But Milla heard something else too—crinkle, crinkle.

She looked down. “Oh no,” Milla said softly. “There is litter on the forest path.”

Little wrappers lay by the roots. A bottle rolled and bumped the trunk. The tree seemed to sigh. Milla’s heart felt heavy, like a pebble in her pocket.

“I care about you, tree,” Milla whispered, patting the bark. “I will help.”

Milla tied on her green leaf apron. She took three small baskets. On each basket, a tiny label showed a picture: one with a leaf, one with a bottle, and one with a banana peel. “Leaves and sticks, recycling, and compost,” Milla said. “We can sort. We can save. We can share.”

She rang her bell—ding-a-ling! “Friends! Help the tree!” Her voice was gentle but brave.

Soon, friends climbed the rope ladder, one by one. Pip the squirrel scrambled up, his tail flick-flick-flicking. Tulla the turtle came slow and steady, tap, tap, tap on the rungs. Buzz the bee hummed up beside them, bzz-bzz, light as a whisper. Last came Lolo the ladybug, tiny and bright, landing with a soft tick on the rail.

“What’s wrong?” asked Pip, sniffing the air.

Milla pointed. “The forest is messy. Let’s clean together. We will use teamwork. We will be kind to the Earth.”

Buzz nodded. “I like flowers. Flowers like clean soil.”

Tulla blinked. “I am slow, but I am strong.”

Lolo lifted her little wings. “I am tiny, but I can reach teeny spots!”

Milla smiled. “Everyone can help. Everyone is included.”

They climbed down to the forest floor. The ground felt cool and damp. Sunlight made golden speckles on the leaves. A soft wind made the ferns sway, swish-swish.

Milla set the baskets in a row. “We will sort as we go,” she said. “Recycling in the bottle basket. Food scraps in compost. Leaves and sticks in the leaf basket. If we are not sure, we ask.”

Pip zipped to a shiny can. “Here!” he chattered. “Recycling?”

“Yes,” said Milla. “Thank you.”

Lolo found a tiny apple core by a rock. “Compost?” she asked.

“Compost,” Milla said. “The worms will make rich soil. New plants will grow.”

Tulla pushed a big paper bag with her shell. “Heavy,” she said. “But I can do it.” She nudged it to Milla, step by careful step.

“You are strong and steady,” said Milla. “Thank you, Tulla.”

Buzz hovered over a puddle. “I see a wiggly straw,” he buzzed with a worried hum. “Straws can hurt little legs.”

Milla nodded. “We will take it away for safety.” She pinched it gently and dropped it into recycling.

They worked together. They giggled when the bottle rolled and Pip chased it. They sang a simple sorting song: “Sort, sort, sort it right! Make the forest clean and bright!” Their voices mixed with the birds. It felt like the whole forest was singing, too.

Then Lolo patted Milla’s sleeve. “I am small,” she said. “Sometimes I can’t carry big things.”

Milla kneeled so her eyes met Lolo’s. “That’s okay,” she said softly. “Small eyes see small things. Can you look under the ferns?”

Lolo perked up. “Yes!” She found tiny bits of paper and a lost button. She felt proud and warm, like sunshine on her back.

After a while, Tulla paused. “I am tired,” she said. “May I rest?”

“Of course,” Milla said. “We take care of the Earth, and we take care of ourselves.” She poured Tulla a thimble of cool water. The water tasted fresh, like rain.

When the baskets were almost full, Buzz pointed to a little bird with a string around its foot. The bird chirped, cheep-cheep, frightened.

Milla’s whiskers twitched with worry. “We will help,” she said.

“Hold steady,” said Tulla, making a calm, firm spot with her shell.

“I will keep watch,” buzzed Buzz, circling slow and gentle.

“I will bring soft moss,” said Lolo.

“I can nibble the knot,” said Pip.

Milla stroked the bird’s wing. “It’s okay,” she whispered. “We are here.” Working together with care—snip, snip—they freed the bird. It shook its feathers, then sang a sweet, sweet song and flew to a safe branch.

Milla’s eyes shone. “This is why we clean,” she said. “We protect our friends.”

At last, the path was clear. The tree seemed to stand taller. The air smelled like rain and leaves. Sunlight danced on the clean ground.

Back in the treehouse, Milla spread a cloth. They shared crunchy apple slices and nut crumbs. The snacks made happy munch-munch sounds. Everyone smiled.

Pip said, “Teamwork made it fun.”

Tulla said, “Resting helped me help more.”

Buzz said, “The flowers will be glad.”

Lolo said, “I am small, but I helped a lot.”

Milla put a tiny sign by the door. It said, “Love the Earth. Sort and Share.” She placed the baskets in a neat row for next time.

She looked at her friends. “Today we learned three things,” Milla said. “We care for the Earth by sorting and reusing. We help each other with kind hearts. And everyone belongs when we work together.”

They all touched paws, wings, and shell. “We can do it again tomorrow,” they cheered.

The bell chimed—ding-a-ling—and the tree rustled like a happy hug.

The End.
