# Owl Writes on the Balcony Garden

On a sunny morning, a wise old owl named Olive sat on the railing of a small balcony garden. The garden smelled like mint and wet soil. Little tomatoes hung like red buttons. Basil leaves tickled Olive’s toes. Birds chirped, and a soft breeze made the wind chimes sing, ting-ting-ting.

Olive loved stories. She loved reading them, and she loved writing them, too. She kept a tiny notebook and a short, smooth pencil tucked under her wing. Every day, Olive had a routine. She would:
- stretch her wings,
- sip cool water,
- nibble a crunchy carrot and a sweet strawberry,
- then sit and write on the balcony from the first bell of the wind chimes to the second bell.

But today, the wind was stronger. The chimes clanged, CLANG-CLANG! Olive’s pencil rolled. Her notebook fluttered. Pages flipped like little wings. Olive blinked. “Hoo-hoo,” she said softly. “My writing time is wobbly today.”

She tried to write one line. The pencil made a squiggle. Olive frowned. “I want to write a story,” she whispered, “but I feel stuck.”

Just then, a tiny snail named Sori peeked out from under a lettuce leaf. “Hello, Olive,” Sori said in a slow, smooth voice. “Why are you frowning?”

“My story won’t come out,” Olive said. “The wind is loud. My pencil rolls. My words feel shy.”

Sori inched closer, his shell shining in the sun. “Maybe your routine can help,” he said. “What do you do first?”

Olive took a deep breath. She knew her routine well. “I stretch,” she said. She stood tall, wings wide. The stretch felt good, warm, and steady.

“What next?” asked Sori.

“I drink my water,” Olive said. She sipped, sip-sip, the water cool on her tongue. She felt calm.

“And then?” Sori smiled.

“I eat a crunchy carrot and a sweet strawberry,” Olive said. She nibbled. Crunch! Juicy! The carrot tasted crisp like a snap. The strawberry tasted like sunshine. She felt strong and happy.

Sori nodded. “Good nutrition gives brave energy,” he said. “Now you can try to write.”

Olive placed a smooth stone on the corner of her notebook so the page would not fly. She tucked the pencil behind her ear. The wind still blew, but Olive felt ready.

She whispered to the page, “Hello, story.” Then she wrote, one careful word at a time. “Once… there… was… a… tiny… snail… on… a… brave… balcony.”

Sori giggled. “That sounds like me!”

But just then, a big shadow passed. A crow landed on the railing with a FLAP. “Caw! What are you doing?” the crow asked.

Olive’s heart thumped. She felt a little scared. “I am writing,” she said. “I write every day.”

“Caw! The wind is wild. You can’t write now,” said the crow.

Olive looked at Sori. Sori looked at Olive. Olive felt her brave energy from the carrot and strawberry. She remembered her routine. She also remembered her kind goal: write a little every day.

In a gentle but firm voice, Olive said, “I can write even with the wind. I will do it slowly. I will be brave.”

The crow blinked. “Caw?” He hopped closer. The chimes tinkled. The basil leaves waved hello. Olive put another smooth stone on the page. She began to write again.

She wrote about wind that tried to push stories away.
She wrote about a snail who moved slowly but never stopped.
She wrote about an owl who used routine, healthy snacks, and courage to keep going.

The crow watched. “Caw,” he said softly. “You are calm. You are steady.”

Olive smiled. “Writing can be small steps. One word, then another. We can be brave in small ways.”

Sori clapped his tiny snail feelers. “Yes!”

The crow tilted his head. “Can I listen?”

“Yes,” said Olive. “I will read what I wrote.”

Her voice was warm and cozy like a blanket. She read:
“Once there was a tiny snail who loved a balcony garden. The wind blew, but he kept going. His friend, a wise old owl, stretched, drank water, ate good food, and wrote a brave story. They did it together, one little step at a time.”

When Olive finished, the balcony felt calm. The wind softened. The chimes went ting-ting-ting again. The crow smiled. “Caw. I learned something,” he said. “Routine helps. Good food helps. And brave hearts write good stories.”

Olive tucked her pencil away. She patted her notebook. “I learned something, too,” she said. “When my words feel shy, I follow my routine, I take care of my body, and I take one small, brave step.”

Sori yawned a tiny snail yawn. “That is a good lesson.”

Olive looked at the garden. She smelled mint. She heard the soft city sounds below. She felt proud and peaceful.

“Tomorrow,” Olive said, “I will write again.”

The End.
