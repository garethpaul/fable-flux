# Tortoise Tim's Quiet Guest Room Gallery

In a small, sunny house, there was a cozy guest room. The bed had a soft blue quilt. The curtains were yellow like warm butter. A little table stood by the window, and on the table were paper, crayons, glue, and a tiny clock that went tick-tick-tick.

A wise tortoise named Tim lived there. His shell was smooth and green with shiny swirls. Tim liked to make art in the quiet guest room. He liked how the room smelled like clean sheets and lemon soap. He liked how the light made gentle stripes on the floor.

One morning, Tim took a deep breath. He looked at the paper. He looked at the crayons. He felt both excited and a little worried.

“I want to make a picture for my friend,” Tim said. “But I don’t know where to start.”

The tiny clock said tick-tick-tick. Tim touched a red crayon. It was warm from the sun. He touched a blue crayon. It was cool like the shade.

From the hallway came a soft tap-tap. It was Bee the little neighbor, a friendly bumblebee who wore a tiny scarf. “Hi, Tim!” buzzed Bee. “Can I see your art?”

Tim smiled. “Hi, Bee. I haven’t started yet. My mind feels fuzzy.”

Bee peered at the blank paper. “It looks so big,” Bee said. “What will you draw?”

Tim blinked slowly. “Let’s think,” he said in his calm, wise voice. “We can use our minds like a toolbox.”

“How?” asked Bee, tilting her head.

Tim pointed to the tiny clock. “First, time. We can make a plan. We will draw for five ticks, then rest for two ticks, then color for five ticks. That helps our brains.”

Bee buzzed in a happy circle. “A plan!”

Tim nodded. “Next, questions. We can ask small, helpful questions. What colors does my friend like? What shapes can I make? What fits on this paper?”

Bee said, “I like purple and yellow! Your friend likes gardens, I think.”

Tim’s eyes shone. “A garden picture! Leaves and flowers and a tiny path. Simple is good.”

They listened to the clock: tick-tick-tick. The room felt calm.

“Let’s start,” Tim said. “Step one: sketch.”

He held a pencil. It made a soft scritch-scratch on the paper. Tim drew a round hill. He made three big flower shapes—circle, circle, circle. He drew a wiggly path like a smile.

Bee hovered close. “It looks wobbly,” she said.

Tim smiled. “Wobbly is okay at the start. Art grows.”

The clock ticked five times. “Rest,” Tim whispered. He stretched his short legs. Bee hummed a tiny song. They smelled the lemon soap and felt the cool shade by the curtains.

“Step two: check,” said Tim. “Does it fit? Is there space for the sky?”

Bee looked. “Yes! The sky can be up here,” she said, pointing with a buzz.

“Good thinking,” said Tim. “Step three: color.”

Tim chose green for the hill. The crayon felt smooth and waxy. He colored slowly. Shh-shh-shh. Bee chose yellow and purple for flowers. Dab-dab with crayon tips.

The clock said tick-tick-tick. Tim paused to sip water. “Slow and steady,” he said. “Patience helps the colors stay neat.”

Bee nodded. “Patience,” she echoed.

Tim added blue to the sky. He left light spaces for clouds. He glued a little strip of brown paper for the path. The glue smelled sweet and sticky.

Bee gasped. “It’s pretty!”

Tim tilted his head. “Let’s solve one more problem,” he said. “How do we make the flowers look soft?”

He tried tiny circles with the crayon. Round and gentle. “That works,” he said. “We tested it.”

Bee tried too. “Soft!” she cheered.

They listened again: tick-tick. “Rest,” Tim said. They looked out the window. A warm breeze touched the curtains. The guest room felt safe and quiet.

“Step four: tiny details,” Tim said. He used a white crayon to make little light dots on the petals. He used a darker green for shadow under the leaves. “Light and shadow make the picture feel real,” he said softly.

Bee buzzed low. “It looks like we can walk on the path.”

Tim smiled. “Almost done.” He wrote a small note at the bottom: For a dear friend. He set the picture by the window. The colors glowed in the sun.

Bee clapped her tiny hands. “You did it!”

“We did it,” said Tim. “We used our plan. We asked good questions. We took breaks. We were patient. That is smart art.”

Bee looked proud. “Smart art!”

That afternoon, Tim’s friend came to visit. The guest room smelled like fresh paper and warm light. Tim gave the picture to his friend.

“It is beautiful,” said the friend. “It feels calm and happy.”

Tim’s heart felt warm. “I learned that art takes time,” he said. “When a problem feels big, I make a plan, ask questions, and go step by step.”

Bee buzzed, “And take breaks!”

They all laughed. The tiny clock kept ticking, soft and steady. The guest room felt like a small gallery full of care.

The End.
