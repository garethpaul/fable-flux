# Otter Milo’s Car-Ride Traditions

Milo was a playful otter with bright eyes and soft whiskers. He wore a tiny red scarf that smelled like sunshine and soap. He loved to wiggle, giggle, and learn new things. Today, Milo hopped into the family car with Mama Otter and Grandma Otter.

The car seats were warm and cozy. The windows felt cool when Milo pressed his paws on the glass. He could hear the soft rumble of the engine and the squeak of his seatbelt. Click! The belt hugged him safe.

“Seatbelt first,” said Mama Otter with a smile.

“Click!” Milo said, clicking his belt by himself. “I can do it!”

Grandma Otter clapped. “Self-reliance, Milo. Doing things you can do, all by yourself.” Her voice was gentle, like a soft blanket.

Milo wiggled. “Where are we going?”

“We’re going to the park,” said Mama Otter. “It’s Snack-and-Story Day.”

Milo’s whiskers twitched. “What is Snack-and-Story Day?”

Grandma Otter’s eyes twinkled. “It’s our family tradition. Every weekend, we ride in the car, share a healthy snack, and tell stories from long ago.”

Milo felt the seat hum as the car began to roll. He saw trees swish by and smelled the sweet orange slices in a lunch box. His tummy rumbled like a tiny drum. “I love snacks,” he said.

Mama Otter passed him a small napkin. “First, clean paws,” she said.

Milo took a wipe from the little pack and cleaned his paws. “Rub, rub, rub. All clean!” The wipe felt cool and smelled like lemons. He put the wipe in the little trash bag by his seat. “I can do it by myself,” he said proudly.

“Healthy habits,” said Mama Otter. “Clean paws, safe seatbelt, and water to sip.”

Milo took a careful sip from his water bottle. Glug, glug. “Now the story?” he asked, eyes wide.

Grandma Otter nodded. “Long ago, when I was a young otter, our family had this same ride. In our old family car, we shared orange slices, sang a little song, and told a ‘Remember Story.’ That is our tradition.”

“What is a ‘Remember Story’?” Milo asked. His whiskers tickled his nose.

“It is a story from our family,” Grandma said. “We share it so we remember who we are and how we care for each other.”

Milo leaned back and listened. The seat was soft. The air smelled like oranges and a tiny hint of new car. Grandma began:

“Once, on a rainy day car ride, I felt worried. The rain tapped the windows—tip-tap, tip-tap. Your great-grandpa shared orange slices and said, ‘We can be brave together.’ We sang our car song, and I felt safe.”

Milo hugged his scarf. “What is the car song?”

Grandma smiled. Mama Otter cleared her throat. Together they sang, soft and happy:

“Click goes the belt, snug and right,
Sip some water, feeling light,
Share our stories, one by one,
Family love is bright as sun.”

Milo giggled and clapped. “Again!” They sang a second time. Milo joined in, his little voice squeaky and sweet.

When the song ended, Mama Otter opened the snack box. She handed Milo orange slices and carrot sticks shaped like tiny boats. “Healthy snack time,” she said.

Milo sniffed the orange. “It smells sunny!” He peeled a slice. Ssssk! Juice tickled his paws. “May I share?” he asked.

“Yes,” said Mama Otter. “Sharing is caring.”

Milo gave an orange slice to Grandma and one to Mama. He kept two for himself. “We all have some!” He crunched a carrot boat. Crunch, munch. The taste was fresh and happy.

Grandma dabbed his paws with the napkin. “You’re learning our tradition—song, snack, and story. And you’re also helping yourself.”

Milo puffed his chest. “I can peel, I can sip, I can click.” He thought for a moment. “Can I tell a Remember Story too?”

Mama Otter nodded. “Yes, Milo. Our tradition grows when each of us shares.”

Milo took a brave breath. The air felt cool in his nose. “Remember when I was little, and I didn’t like the loud car wash? The brushes went whoosh-whoosh and splashed. I felt scared. Mama held my paw, and we sang the car song. Then I felt okay.”

Grandma smiled warm and bright. “That is a beautiful Remember Story.”

The car rolled past tall trees. A breeze whispered at the window. Milo looked at the snack box and saw one last orange slice. He picked it up and held it out. “This one is for the car,” he said with a giggle.

Mama laughed. “The car can’t eat, silly otter.”

“But we can share it when we stop,” Milo said. “We can make a wish. That can be part of our tradition too!”

Grandma tapped her chin. “A wish slice at the end. I like it.”

The car parked at the park. The seatbelt went click again as Milo unbuckled carefully, all by himself. He held the orange slice high. “I wish we always share our stories, our songs, and our snacks,” he said.

Mama and Grandma each took a tiny nibble. “We will,” Mama said. “Traditions help us remember love.”

Milo felt warm and brave and happy. He wiped his paws, sipped his water, and tucked his napkin into the trash bag, all by himself. “I can do it,” he whispered.

As they walked toward the playground, Milo hummed the car song. He knew he was part of something special. He could help, he could share, and he could tell stories too.

The End.
