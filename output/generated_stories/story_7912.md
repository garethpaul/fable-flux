# Birdie Finds Her Brave Song

Birdie was a tiny singing bird with bright blue feathers and a soft, warm chest. She lived in a playful living room with a cozy rug that felt like clouds, a big couch that gave friendly squishes, and colorful blocks that clicked and clacked. Sunlight made little sparkles on the floor, and the smell of fresh cookies drifted from the kitchen.

Every morning, Birdie liked to sing hello to the toys.

“Good morning, Sunny Ball!” she chirped.

“Boing-boing! Good morning, Birdie!” the bouncy ball replied.

“Hi, Puzzles! Hi, Teddy! Hi, Paints!” Birdie sang as she hopped from the window to the bookshelf.

But today, Birdie’s tummy felt fluttery. She had a special plan. She wanted to sing her new song for her friends. It was a song about being brave.

Birdie took a deep breath. “I will sing now,” she said softly. “I can do it.”

She opened her beak and sang, “Tweet-tweeet… be brave and sweet…”

But her voice came out tiny and squeaky. “Eep!”

Teddy tilted his fuzzy head. “Are you okay, Birdie?”

Birdie ruffled her feathers. “I wanted to sing my new song. But my voice sounded funny. Maybe I’m not a good singer.”

Sunny Ball rolled closer, making a gentle thump. “I liked your try. Trying is brave!”

Birdie shook her head. “I feel small. I don’t feel brave at all.”

Just then, the living room wind chime hanging by the window tinkled, “Ting-ting!” The sound was soft and friendly. Birdie listened. The chime was not loud. It was gentle and pretty.

Paints whispered from the art table, “Sometimes colors are soft and still beautiful.”

Teddy smiled. “We can help. Let’s solve this together.”

“How?” asked Birdie, her eyes wide.

“Step one,” said Teddy, holding up a plush paw, “We make a plan.”

Sunny Ball bounced once. “Plan! Plan! Plan!”

Teddy nodded. “Step two: We practice.”

Paints added, “Step three: We cheer for each other.”

Birdie blinked. “A plan sounds good.”

Teddy spread a little blanket like a stage on the soft rug. The rug felt warm under Birdie’s tiny feet. Sunbeams hugged her feathers. “This is your safe stage,” said Teddy. “Only friends here.”

Birdie took a small breath. “Tweet… tweet… I am brave… I am me…”

“Yay!” cheered Sunny Ball. “Boing! I hear your song!”

Birdie’s heart beat fast, like tiny drum taps. “I’m still scared.”

Teddy gently patted the blanket. “It’s okay to be scared. Being brave means trying even when you feel scared.”

Birdie nodded slowly. “Okay. I will try again.”

She tried a new plan. “I will sing to one friend first.” She looked at the wind chime. “Hello, Chime. This song is for you.”

The chime answered with a happy “Ting!”

Birdie sang softly, “Tweet… little chime… we shine…”

Her song floated like a warm feather. It sounded sweet and clear.

Sunny Ball clapped with a soft thump. “That was pretty!”

Birdie felt a glow in her chest. It felt like a tiny light turning on. “I did it,” she whispered.

Teddy said, “Now try singing to two friends.”

Birdie looked at Paints and Teddy. “This song is for you. Tweet… I am me… I can be… happy, kind, and free…”

The room felt calm and cozy. Birdie smelled the cookie sweetness and heard the wind chime answer. She smiled.

“Wonderful!” said Paints, splashing a little yellow joy onto a paper. “Your song is you.”

Birdie stood taller. Her feathers were smooth and shiny now. “I feel brave,” she said.

Sunny Ball bounced in place. “Sing for all of us now!”

Birdie took a deep, slow breath. She felt her feet on the rug. She felt the warm sun. She heard her heartbeat. She remembered the plan: plan, practice, cheer.

Then she sang her brave song, louder and stronger:

“Tweet-tweet, I am me,
Brave and kind as I can be.
If I’m small, that’s okay,
I will try, day by day.”

Her voice filled the playful living room like a hug. The couch gave a proud squish. The blocks clicked happily. The wind chime danced, “Ting-ting-ting!” Teddy hugged her. Sunny Ball spun in a joyful circle.

Birdie laughed. “I did it! My song is mine.”

Teddy said softly, “Your voice is special because it is yours.”

Sunny Ball added, “And we helped each other. That’s friendship!”

Paints chimed in, “You solved the problem with a plan.”

Birdie felt the glow in her chest become warm and steady. “Today I learned something big,” she said. “I don’t have to be perfect. I can practice, ask for help, and be proud of me.”

Teddy nodded. “That’s self-esteem. It means you believe in yourself.”

Birdie hopped to the window and looked at the sunny day. “I believe in me,” she sang, softly and sure.

The friends all cheered, and the living room felt extra bright, like a smile.

The End.
