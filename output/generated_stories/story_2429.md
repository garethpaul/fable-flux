# The Owl Who Sorted Feelings

In a quiet study corner, a wise old owl named Olive perched on a high-backed chair. The chair felt smooth and cool under her talons. A lamp glowed warm and yellow. Books lined the shelves like rainbow feathers. The room smelled like paper and lemon polish.

Children from many places visited Olive’s study corner. Today, three friends came in. Sami wore a bright red scarf. Mei had glittery shoes that squeaked. Tadi held a tiny backpack with a drum key inside.

“Welcome,” Olive hooted softly. Her voice was like a blanket. “Please come in.”

On the little table sat a basket of chores. Inside were cards with jobs: Tidy books. Wipe the table. Sort crayons. Each card had a picture and a word.

“We will tidy the study corner,” said Olive. “We will also tidy our feelings.”

Sami’s eyebrows squeezed together. “My tummy feels tight,” he said. “I don’t know why.”

Mei stared at the crayon box. “The crayons are all mixed up,” she said. “I like them in rows.”

Tadi tapped his fingers on his backpack. Tap, tap, tap. “I want to drum,” he said. “But this is a quiet place.”

Olive blinked her big golden eyes. “We all feel many things,” she said. “Let us name them. When we name a feeling, it gets lighter.”

Sami put a hand on his tummy. “I think I feel worried,” he whispered.

Mei took a deep breath. “I feel fussy,” she said. “I want neatness.”

Tadi looked at his hands. “I feel excited,” he said. “My fingers want to dance.”

Olive nodded. “Thank you for being honest,” she said. “Honesty helps our hearts breathe.”

She lifted the first chore card. “Tidy books,” Olive read. “Who would like this?”

Mei’s hand shot up. “Me! I like lines and rows.”

“Thank you, Mei,” said Olive. “You can make a pattern you love.”

She lifted the next card. “Wipe the table.”

Sami sniffed the lemon polish. “I can do that,” he said. “The smell is fresh. It might help my tummy feel calm.”

Olive smiled. “Good choice, Sami.”

She lifted the last card. “Sort crayons.”

Tadi looked at the box. Colors tumbled like confetti. His eyes shone. “I can sort,” he said, “and I can drum in my head.”

“That is thoughtful,” said Olive. “We respect the quiet here. Thank you for finding a gentle way.”

They began. Mei moved the books. Thump, thump, thump. She lined them up from small to tall. “Ah,” she sighed, “this looks just right.”

Sami dipped a cloth in warm, soapy water. Swish, swish. He wiped slow circles. The table became smooth and shiny. His tummy felt a little softer.

Tadi held the crayons. Red, blue, gold, green. He listened with his eyes. “You go with warm colors,” he told the reds and oranges. “You go with cool colors,” he told the blues and greens. He tapped one finger softly on his knee. Tap… pause… tap.

Then something happened. A shiny black crayon rolled off the table. Plop! It left a dark smudge on the floor.

“Oh no,” whispered Tadi. His cheeks grew hot.

Sami’s eyes widened. Mei’s mouth made a small O.

Olive tilted her head. “What’s the feeling, Tadi?”

Tadi looked down. “I feel scared,” he said. “I made a mess. I want to hide.”

Olive’s feathers rustled gently. “Thank you for telling the truth,” she said. “That is honesty. Honesty helps us fix things.”

Mei stepped closer. “We can wipe it,” she said. “I have a tiny cloth in my pocket.” She pulled out a soft square with rainbow stitches.

Sami knelt. “I will get warm water,” he said. Swish, swish went the bucket.

Tadi blinked. “You’re not mad?”

“We all make messes,” Mei said. “All people. All the time.”

“And all feelings are welcome,” Olive added. “When we share them, we can help each other.”

They worked together. Dab, dab. Wipe, wipe. The smudge faded like a small cloud in the sun.

Tadi took a deep breath. “I feel relieved,” he said.

Sami nodded. “I feel proud. The table shines.”

Mei smiled. “I feel calm. The books stand like quiet soldiers.”

Olive opened a little wooden box. Inside were smooth stones. Each stone had a simple face. Happy. Sad. Worried. Calm. Excited. Proud.

“These are Feeling Stones,” said Olive. “Choose one that matches your heart.”

Sami picked the proud stone. “It feels warm in my hand,” he said.

Mei chose calm. “It is cool and smooth,” she said.

Tadi chose relieved. “It feels like a gentle breeze,” he said.

Olive tucked one last card into the basket. It said: Say Thank You.

They gathered in a circle. The lamp hummed softly. The lemon smell floated in the air.

“Thank you for being honest,” said Olive.

“Thank you for helping me,” said Tadi.

“Thank you for caring about quiet,” said Mei.

“Thank you for naming your feelings,” said Sami.

Olive blinked slowly. “In this study corner,” she said, “we accept all feelings and all people. We are different, and that makes us strong. When we tell the truth and help with chores, our hearts feel light.”

Sami touched his tummy. “It feels calm now,” he said.

Mei looked at the neat shelves. “I feel happy.”

Tadi peered at the crayons in tidy groups. “I feel ready to drum outside later,” he said.

Olive fluffed her feathers and gave a soft hoot. “You learned to listen to your feelings, to accept each other, and to be honest. That is wise.”

They all smiled. The study corner glowed warm and bright.

The End.
