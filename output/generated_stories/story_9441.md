# Tessa Turtle’s Treehouse Tune

High in a gentle green tree, there was a cozy wooden treehouse. It smelled like pine needles and warm sunshine. Inside lived Tessa, a shy little turtle with a smooth, shiny shell. Tessa loved quiet things—soft moss, slow clouds, and the whisper of leaves. But there was one sound that made her heart feel big: music.

Down below, the forest friends met every afternoon to make music together. They called it Treehouse Band Time. Tessa had made a small drum from a hollow gourd and two short sticks. She kept her drum in the treehouse corner, near a window where the breeze sang.

“I want to play with them,” Tessa whispered to herself. “But what if I’m too slow? What if I make a mistake?”

One bright morning, a note fluttered up on a string. It was from Maestro Meadowlark, the little bird who led the band. The note said, “Treehouse Band meets today. Listen, then play. Follow the beat. You can do it!”

Tessa hugged her drum and peeked over the window sill. She saw Maestro Meadowlark hop onto a branch like a tiny conductor. She saw Benny Bunny with carrot-shaped maracas, Lila Ladybug with a tiny bell, and Milo Mouse with a soft, blue flute.

“Welcome, friends!” sang Maestro Meadowlark. “Today, we will learn a new song called Treetop Thank You. We will listen. We will follow directions. We will make our own little bits of music, too!”

Tessa’s toes tingled. She wanted to join so much.

Maestro tapped his wing. “First, listen.” The forest grew quiet. Even the leaves seemed to wait. Then Maestro sang, “Tap-tap, rest. Tap-tap, rest.” He tapped his twig on the branch—tap-tap—and then paused.

Benny Bunny shook his maracas—rattle-rattle—then stopped with a giggle. Lila chimed her bell—ding-ding—then rested. Milo blew a soft note—whoooo—then took a breath.

Tessa felt the pattern in her belly. Tap-tap, rest. Tap-tap, rest. “I can try,” she whispered. She lifted her drumsticks, but her hands trembled. “What if I’m too loud? What if I don’t stop on the rest?”

Maestro called, “It’s okay to try. We learn together.”

Tessa took a deep breath. She watched Maestro’s wing. Tap-tap—she tapped her drum softly—tup-tup—and then she held still. The quiet rest felt like a warm hug. Tap-tap—tup-tup—and rest. The rhythm sounded like a friendly heartbeat.

“Wonderful!” Maestro chirped. “Now step two: follow directions with words. I will say the steps. We will do them.”

He sang, “Hands ready. Eyes on me. Tap two times. Stop. Count to three.”

Everyone sang the steps back. “Hands ready. Eyes on you. Tap two times. Stop. Count to three.”

Tessa smiled. She liked knowing the steps. She lifted her sticks. Tap-tap. Stop. One, two, three. She felt the music and the quiet both.

“Step three,” said Maestro. “We add our own sound. That is called creativity. After we follow the beat, we each share a small idea.”

Benny bounced. “I will add a soft hop sound—boing!”

Lila waved her bell. “I will add a tiny twinkle—ting!”

Milo wiggled his whiskers. “I will add a squeak note—eek!”

Maestro turned to the treehouse window. “Tessa Turtle, would you like to join us?”

Tessa peeked out, cheeks rosy. “I’m shy,” she whispered. “But I want to try.”

“Come as you are,” said Maestro. “Soft is beautiful. Slow is beautiful. Your sound belongs.”

Tessa tucked her drum under one arm and slowly climbed down the ladder. The bark felt rough under her feet. The air smelled like sweet sap. Her heart went thump-thump, like two gentle taps.

Maestro lifted his wing. “Ready, band? Listen, then do.”

He sang the steps again. “Hands ready. Eyes on me. Tap two times. Stop. Count to three.”

Everyone played together. Tap-tap. Rest. Tap-tap. Rest. The music floated up like butterfly wings.

Now it was time to add ideas. Benny added a quiet boing. Lila sprinkled a ting. Milo squeaked a tiny eek.

Tessa squeezed her drum. What could she add? She looked at the leaves and listened to the wind. The breeze made a soft shhh. Tessa whispered, “My idea is a shell rub.” She gently rubbed her drumstick across the side of her shell—shrrr—like wind in grass.

The sound was soft and warm. Everyone smiled.

“That is beautiful,” said Maestro. “You listened. You followed directions. You added your creative sound.”

Tessa’s shy smile grew. “Thank you,” she said. “Thank you for waiting for me. Thank you for the steps. Thank you for the music.”

Maestro bowed. “And thank you, Tessa, for sharing your sound. Music is for everyone.”

They played the song again. Tap-tap. Rest. Tap-tap. Rest. Boing. Ting. Eek. Shrrr. The treehouse felt full of friendly sounds. Even the sun seemed to clap with its warm, golden hands.

When the song ended, the band put their instruments down. They placed a tiny leaf-sticker on Tessa’s drum. It said, “Good Listener.”

Tessa touched the sticker and took a deep breath of piney air. She felt calm. She felt proud. She felt grateful.

Maestro asked, “Friends, what did we learn today?”

Benny said, “Listening helps us play together.”

Lila said, “Following directions keeps the music smooth.”

Milo said, “Being creative makes the music special.”

Tessa added, “Being brave and saying thank you makes our band kind.”

They all nodded.

Tessa looked up at her cozy treehouse. She knew she could practice there, slowly and softly, any time she wished. And when she was ready, she could come down and play with her friends. The music would wait. The music would welcome.

That afternoon, the forest hummed with a happy tune, and Tessa’s shy heart hummed along.

The End.
