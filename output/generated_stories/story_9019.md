# Leo Mends the Clock

In a quiet attic, where dust danced in the sunbeams, a brave little lion cub named Leo padded on soft paws. The attic smelled like old wood and warm straw. Boxes made little hills. A tiny window let in golden light. Tick-tock, tick-tock went a small, round clock on a shelf.

“I’m brave,” Leo whispered. His whiskers twitched. “I can explore!”

He saw shiny buttons in a jar, ribbons in a basket, and a pile of clean cardboard rolls. He touched a roll. It felt smooth and light. “Ooh,” he said. “Craft things!”

Leo loved to make things. He loved to help, too. But today, he forgot the time. His Grandma had asked, “Leo, please come down when the clock says three. We will make soup.”

Leo nodded then, but now he sniffed a glue stick and hummed. He did not look at the clock.

He began to craft. He glued a button to a roll. He tied a blue ribbon around it. He cut paper stars. Snip, snip. His tail swished with joy. “Look, a rocket!” he cheered.

Then his brave paws bumped the shelf. Clink. The small, round clock wobbled. Oh no. It tipped. It fell.

Clatter! The clock split open. A little hand popped off. The tick-tock stopped.

Leo’s ears drooped. His tummy felt twisty. “I broke the clock,” he whispered. He looked at the tiny hand on the floor. He looked at the hole in the clock face. He felt sad. He felt worried. “Grandma needs the clock to know when it is three.”

He took a deep breath. In, out. “I must make amends,” he said softly. “I must fix my mistake.”

He remembered what Grandma said: “When we break something or hurt someone, we say sorry, we fix what we can, and we use our time well.”

Leo looked around. The attic was quiet. The sunbeam had moved. He had spent a long time. He needed to be quick and careful now.

First, Leo said the words out loud. “I am sorry, clock. I bumped you. I will try to fix you.”

Then he made a plan. “Step one, find all the pieces. Step two, fix the clock. Step three, clean up. Step four, go to Grandma by three.”

He found the little hand on the floor. He found a tiny screw. He found a loose nail. He picked them up gently and put them in a jar so they would not roll away.

He thought about time. The light in the window told him the day was moving. Leo whispered, “I will count to help my time.” He set a simple rhythm in his head. “One lion minute, two lion minutes,” he said, and tried not to rush.

Leo used what he had. He was resourceful. He did not throw things away. He recycled. He used a clean cardboard roll to make a tiny tube to hold the screw. He used a paper clip he found to make a small pin. He used ribbon to make a soft strap so the clock could hang steady.

“Can I do it?” he asked himself.

“You can try,” he answered.

Snap. He pressed the little hand back on its tiny pin. Click. He turned the pin gently. Tick... tick... The clock made a small sound. Leo smiled a little. “Good job, paws,” he said.

He straightened the face with a bit of cardboard, cut to fit. Snip, snip. He slipped it under the frame like a patch. He did not use too much glue. He waited. He counted. “One lion minute, two lion minutes.” He used his waiting time to tidy. He put buttons back in the jar. He tied ribbons and rolled them up. He swept tiny paper stars into a box to use later.

“Leo?” called Grandma from below. Her voice was warm. “Is it three?”

Leo’s heart thumped. He was brave. He picked up the clock with his ribbon strap. It ticked, soft but steady. He went to the attic stairs.

“I am coming, Grandma,” he said. His voice trembled a little. “I need to tell you something.”

Down the stairs he came, step by careful step. He held the clock. He looked at Grandma’s kind eyes.

“I am sorry,” he said. “I forgot the time. I bumped the clock. I broke it. I tried to fix it. I used a roll, a paper clip, and ribbon. I cleaned up, too. I want to make amends. Can I help more?”

Grandma knelt and gave Leo a gentle hug. He smelled her soup—carrots and warm onions. “Thank you for telling me the truth,” she said. “You were brave to say sorry. You were wise to fix what you could and to use your time to clean up.”

She took the clock and listened. Tick-tock, tick-tock. “It works,” she smiled. “It looks a little different, but it works. I like your ribbon strap. It is recycled and strong.”

Leo’s tail swished. “I will set a time plan next time,” he said. “Craft first for two lion minutes, then check the clock, then craft again.”

Grandma laughed softly. “That is time-management,” she said. “We can even set a little bell to help.”

They hung the clock with the ribbon. It looked proud on the wall.

“Now,” said Grandma, “will you help me chop carrots?”

“Yes,” said Leo. “Helping is part of making amends.”

He washed his paws. He chopped soft carrots with a safe, small knife. Tap, tap, tap. He put the paper scraps from the carrots in the compost. He saved the ribbon ends in a jar. He felt calm and happy.

When the soup was ready, they sat and ate together. The clock ticked. The attic was quiet above them. Leo took a warm spoonful and smiled. “I learned,” he said. “When I make a mistake, I say sorry, I fix what I can, I clean up, and I use my time well.”

Grandma nodded. “That is how we grow,” she said.

Leo purred. He felt brave and kind.

The End.
