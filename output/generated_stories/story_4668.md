# Pip’s Shower Song Games

Pip was a small singing bird with bright blue feathers and a tiny golden beak. Pip lived near a house with a shiny, tile bathroom. Every morning, steam puffed like little clouds, and the mirror got foggy and soft like milk. Pip loved to perch on the window ledge and sing, “Tweet-a-doo, tweet-a-day!” The bathroom smelled like soap bubbles and mint toothpaste.

One rainy morning, Pip peeped into the bathroom. The laundry basket was full and heavy. Empty shampoo bottles lay on the floor. A roll of toilet paper was squished and soggy. The trash can was stuffed. “Oh dear,” said Pip. “It’s messy! It’s hard to play and sing here.”

Pip fluttered his wings. “I can help,” he said. “I will use my own idea! I will start something new.”

Pip pecked on the glass. “Hello? Anybody home?” he chirped.

Little Lina, who loved to splash and skip, ran in with her dad. “Hi, Pip!” Lina waved. “We are getting ready for bath time.”

Pip tilted his head. “Can we play a game?” he asked. “A sport game! A clean-up sport!”

Lina’s eyes got big. “A sport? In the bathroom?”

“Yes!” Pip sang. “A safe and gentle sport. We will toss, sort, and score! We will use only soft throws. We will keep feet on the dry mat. We will cheer and sing! And we will recycle, too.”

Dad smiled. “That sounds fun and careful. What do we do?”

Pip hopped to the edge of the sink. “First, we get three bins. One bin for recycling. One bin for laundry. One little bin for trash.”

Lina clapped. “I will get the bins!” She ran and brought a blue bin, a white basket, and a small green pail.

Pip sang, “Bin-bin-bird begin!” The note rang bright and clear.

Lina giggled. “What next?”

Pip fluffed his feathers. “Now, we play Shower Song Games! This is my family’s old bird tradition. Long ago, my grandma taught me. We sing as we clean. We share our song. We make work into play.”

“Wow,” said Lina. “I want to learn your tradition.”

Pip took a breath. His song was soft and warm. “Tweet, tweet, tidy feet. Sort and sing, clean and greet!”

Lina copied, “Tweet, tweet, tidy feet!” Dad hummed along.

Pip pointed with his beak. “Round One: The Gentle Toss. Empty bottles go in the blue bin. One point for each bottle. No big throws. Only little plops.”

Lina picked up a plastic bottle. “Plop!” It landed in the blue bin. “I did it!”

“Tweet-tastic!” sang Pip.

Dad found another bottle and plopped it in. “Two points!”

Pip tapped the soggy toilet paper. “Round Two: The Save and Dry. Paper is wet. We cannot recycle that. But we can save the roll tube! We peel off the wet paper and put the brown tube in the recycle bin. The wet paper goes in the trash.”

Lina peeled slowly. “It feels squishy,” she said. “It smells like rain.”

“Good care,” Pip chirped. “That is smart sorting.”

Pip hopped to the laundry basket. “Round Three: Towel Toss. Toss the towels into the white basket from the mat. Only little throws. Ready?”

Lina swung her arm gently. “Whoosh!” A towel landed inside. “Score!”

Dad tossed washcloths. Plop, plop. “We’re a team,” he said.

Pip sang louder. “Tweet, tweet, tidy feet. Teamwork makes it neat!”

The mirror was still foggy. Lina traced a heart on it. “I can’t see well,” she said.

Pip nodded. “Round Four: The Clear View. We use a dry cloth to wipe the mirror a little. Not too hard, just a gentle swipe. Safety first—no climbing, no slipping.”

Dad handed Lina a soft cloth. Lina wiped in small circles. “It’s shiny!” she said. The mirror smelled like lemon.

Soon the floor was clear, the bins were sorted, and the basket was ready for the washer. The bathroom felt light and fresh. The faucet dripped, drip-drop, like a tiny drum.

Lina bounced on her heels. “What about sports?” she asked. “We did tossing. What else?”

Pip flapped. “Final Round: Brush and Balance! Stand on the mat with knees soft. Brush your teeth and count to ten. Then balance on one foot and hum our song.”

Lina brushed. “One, two, three…” Minty bubbles tickled her tongue. She balanced on one foot. “Mmm-mmm-mmm!” Dad balanced too and pretended to wobble. They all laughed.

When they were done, Lina looked around. “Pip, your idea worked. We cleaned up. We played safe sports. We learned your family’s cleaning song. And we recycled!”

Pip’s chest felt warm. “You took action right away,” he said. “That is called initiative. You saw a problem. You started helping. You made a plan, and you did it.”

Lina nodded. “I can do that again. I can use my own good ideas!”

Dad gave a thumbs-up. “We can make this our family tradition, too. Shower Song Games every week!”

Pip sang a happy high note. “Tweet-a-doo, tweet-a-day! Try your idea, lead the way!”

The bathroom sparkled. The blue bin was full of clean bottles to recycle. The white basket was ready to wash. The little green pail was not so full anymore. The mirror shone. The air smelled like soap and lemons and mint.

Lina wrapped herself in a soft towel. “Thank you, Pip,” she said. “Today I learned: When I see a mess, I can start helping. I can share, sort, and play safe sports. I can share traditions. I can care for the Earth.”

Pip bowed. “Yes,” he chirped. “Small helpers make big change.”

They all hummed the tidy song one more time.

The End.
