# Cubby Solves the Nursery Puzzle

In a comfortable nursery with soft rugs and sunny windows, a friendly bear cub named Cubby blinked awake. The room smelled like clean pine and warm blankets. Sunlight made little golden squares on the floor. “Good morning,” Cubby yawned, rubbing his fuzzy eyes. His ears perked up at a gentle hum from the air purifier and the soft tick-tock of the clock.

Today was Tidy-and-Play Day. On the wall, a chart showed pictures: a toy basket, a watering can, a recycling bin, and a book. Cubby’s caregiver, Miss Maple, smiled. “Good morning, Cubby. Today we have four jobs. We can think first, then do. Thinking helps us choose the best way.”

Cubby nodded. “I can think and do!”

Miss Maple pointed to the first picture. “One basket is too full. Toys are everywhere. What should we do first?”

Cubby looked around. Blocks were under the chair. Crayons were on the rug. A toy truck was parked on a pillow. “Hmm,” Cubby said, tapping his chin. “If we walk, we might step on blocks. Ouch. First, we should make a safe path.”

“That’s smart,” said Miss Maple.

Cubby scooted on his knees and gently pushed the blocks into a neat line along the wall. “I’m making a path so we don’t trip,” he said. He listened to the soft click-click of blocks touching. He felt proud. “Now we can move.”

“Great thinking,” Miss Maple said.

Next was the watering can. A big green plant by the window drooped a little. “The plant is thirsty,” Cubby said. He held the can, but it was heavy. Water sloshed. “This is too heavy for me,” Cubby told himself.

Cubby took a deep breath. “Miss Maple,” he said, standing tall. “I need help. The can is heavy. Can we pour some out or use a smaller cup?”

Miss Maple smiled. “That is self-advocacy, Cubby. You asked for what you need. Let’s use a small cup.”

Cubby dipped the cup and watered slowly. Drip, drip, sip went the soil. The plant’s leaves looked happier already. “We used just enough,” Cubby said. “Not too much. That saves water.”

“You used your brain and your voice,” Miss Maple said. “And you cared for the plant.”

They moved to the next job. A box held paper, plastic bottles, and tiny scraps. “What goes where?” Cubby wondered. He wrinkled his nose and sniffed. The paper smelled like crayons. The bottles smelled a little like soap.

Cubby lined up the items. “Paper is flat,” he said. He slid the paper into the blue bin. “Bottles are round,” he said, placing them in the green bin with a little clink. “Tiny scraps can go in the trash,” he decided, then paused. He held up a cardboard tube. “Wait! Is this trash?”

He thought. “Cardboard is like paper. It can be recycled.” He smiled and put the tube in the blue bin. “I used clues,” he said. “Shape and feel.”

“Critical thinking at work,” Miss Maple cheered. “You asked a question, you looked, you chose.”

Now the last picture: a book. “Story time,” Cubby said happily. He reached for a book about forest animals. The cover felt smooth. He opened it, and the pages whispered. But the room still had toys out. The truck was still on the pillow. The crayons were still on the rug.

Cubby looked at the chart again. “First we tidy. Then we read.” He made a plan. “I will sing a tidy song to make it fun.”

He sang, “Pick up, pick up, little and big, put them in the basket, swish and swig!” He giggled at his silly words. He rolled the truck to the basket: vroom, bump. He gathered crayons with a gentle clatter and put them in their box. He stacked the blocks into a tower, but one wobbled.

Cubby stopped. “If I put the big block on the bottom, the tower will not wobble,” he thought. He switched the blocks. The tower stood tall and steady. “I figured it out,” he said.

“Problem solved,” Miss Maple said, clapping softly. “You used your brain, again!”

At last, the nursery felt calm. The rug was smooth under Cubby’s paws. The air smelled fresh. The plant stood tall by the sunny window. The bins were sorted and neat. Cubby’s belly gave a tiny rumble. “Snack?” he asked with a smile.

“After our story,” Miss Maple said. “Would you like to read or listen?”

Cubby puffed his chest. “I would like to read the parts I know. And I will ask for help if I get stuck.”

“That is brave and smart,” Miss Maple said.

They curled up in a cozy chair. Cubby traced the pictures with his paw. “Bear cubs live in forests,” he said. “They need clean water and tall trees.” He paused. “We help the world when we save water and recycle.”

Miss Maple nodded. “And we help ourselves when we stop, think, and ask for help.”

Cubby smiled, warm and calm. “Today I made a safe path. I watered with care. I sorted things to recycle. I made a tower that did not wobble. I asked for help. I can think before I do.”

Miss Maple gave him a gentle hug. “That is the lesson: Use your brain, use your voice, and care for your world. One small step at a time.”

Cubby looked at the tidy nursery. It felt good. It felt safe. It felt like home. “I did it,” he whispered.

“Yes,” said Miss Maple. “You did it, and you learned how.”

They turned the last page together, and the paper made a happy rustle.

The End.
