# Tully the Shy Turtle’s Treehouse Day

Tully was a shy turtle who liked quiet places. His shell was smooth and green, and it felt cool like a leaf in morning shade. One sunny day, Tully walked slowly to a little treehouse by the pond. The treehouse sat in a big, friendly tree. The wooden steps smelled like warm bark. Leaves whispered, “Shhh, shhh,” as the breeze moved through them.

“Today, I will try to do things by myself,” Tully whispered. He took a deep breath. He moved one foot, then the next, up the steps. Cre-eak, cre-eak, cre-eak went the wood. Tully’s heart thump-thumped, but he kept going.

Inside, the treehouse felt cozy. Sunlight made tiny gold dots on the floor. A small window showed the blue pond and tall grass. On the wall hung a simple sign painted by Tully’s Grandma Shell. It said, “Slow is still brave.” Grandma Shell had told Tully old turtle ways—how to care for the pond, how to share, and how to try.

Tully touched the sign. “I can try,” he said.

On a shelf sat a basket with tools: a short broom, a little cloth, a wooden scoop, a string, and a tin cup. Tully read the note in Grandma Shell’s neat letters: “Our turtle tradition—care for our home and share what you can. Start small. Do it with love.”

Tully nodded. “I can do this myself,” he said softly.

He started with the window. It looked dusty. He dipped the cloth into the tin cup of water. Drip, drip. He wiped in slow circles. Swish, swish. Sunlight became brighter. The treehouse smelled like clean rain.

“Good job, me,” Tully whispered. His shy smile grew.

Next, Tully looked at the floor. A few leaves had blown in. He picked up the tiny broom. Sweep, sweep. The straw made a gentle whisper. Tully liked the sound. It was kind and soft, like a lullaby for the floor.

Then Tully saw a small tear in the window curtain. He blinked. “Oh,” he said. “Can I fix this?” He remembered Grandma Shell’s lesson: “Use what you have. Mend before you toss. That is conservation.” Tully found his string. He tied little loops, one, two, three. The curtain stayed together. It looked neat.

“I did it,” Tully said, proud and surprised. Warm joy bubbled in his tummy, like tiny pond bubbles.

Outside, a robin chirped, “Cheer up! Cheer up!”

“Hello,” Tully said. “Would you like some water?” He filled the tin cup and set it on the rail. The robin hopped closer, sipped, and sang a sweet note. Tully laughed softly. “Sharing feels nice,” he said.

Suddenly, he heard rustling and a little squeak. A field mouse peeked into the doorway. Her whiskers twitched. “Hello,” she squeaked. “I’m Mina. I’m lost. May I rest a moment?”

Tully’s shy heart thumped again. He wanted to hide in his shell. But he looked at Grandma Shell’s sign and took a slow breath. “I can help,” he said to himself. Out loud, he said, “Welcome, Mina. Come in.”

Mina stepped inside. “Oh! It smells like fresh rain. Your home is lovely.”

“Thank you,” Tully said. “I cleaned it myself today.”

Mina touched the neat curtain. “You fixed this?”

“Yes,” said Tully. “I used string. We turtles try to mend things. We call it caring for our home.”

“That is a good tradition,” Mina said, smiling.

Tully showed Mina the basket. “These are small tools. I use them to take care of the treehouse and the pond. I sweep, I wipe, and I share water with birds. That’s conservation. It helps our world.”

Mina’s eyes shined. “Can I learn?”

“Yes,” said Tully. “First, we listen.” They both stood quiet. The tree whispered. The pond plopped. A dragonfly buzzed. “When we listen, we know what to do,” Tully said.

Tully handed Mina the broom. “Gentle sweeps,” he said. “Sweep, sweep.” Mina copied. “Sweep, sweep.” They giggled. Dust twirled like tiny dancers in the sun.

Next, Tully showed her the scoop. They gathered a few fallen leaves into a small basket. “We can add these to the garden on the ground,” he said. “They will make the soil rich.”

“That is smart,” Mina said. “You are a good teacher.”

Tully blushed under his shell. “I am trying,” he said. “Trying by myself first helped me feel brave. Now I can share.”

Mina sat by the window. “Thank you for helping me,” she said. “You were shy, but you still helped.”

“I was shy,” Tully said. “But I can be shy and strong. Slow is still brave.”

They both smiled. The robin sang again. A soft breeze made the leaves clap, clap, clap.

“Would you like to take some water for your walk?” Tully asked. He poured water into a tiny leaf cup and tied it with string to a little stick so Mina could carry it. “This is my small gift,” he said. “Generosity is sharing what you can.”

Mina hugged Tully’s shell. “You are kind,” she said. “I will remember your turtle ways. I will care for my home too.”

Mina waved and climbed down. “Goodbye, Tully!”

“Goodbye!” Tully called. He looked around the treehouse. The window sparkled. The floor felt smooth. The basket was neat. The sign glowed in the golden light.

Tully took a calm breath. “I did many things by myself,” he said. “I cared for my home. I shared with a friend. I used old turtle ways to help our world.”

His shy heart felt strong. He watched the pond ripple and the leaves dance.

“I can try,” he said again, with a brave smile. “And when I try, I grow.”

The End.
