# Roo and the Gentle Gears

In a cozy garage workshop, a joyful kangaroo joey named Roo bounced in with a hop-hop-hop. The air smelled like lemon soap and warm wood. Sunlight made bright squares on the floor. Tools hung on the wall like shiny soldiers. The workbench hummed with little sounds: click, whirr, tap.

“Morning, Roo,” said Uncle Max, wiping his hands. He wore big blue goggles and a smile. “Want to help me fix the gentle gears? They make the garden gate open soft and slow.”

“Yes, please!” Roo’s tail thumped with happy thumps. He loved helping. He loved the cheer of the workshop, the smooth feel of sanded wood, and the friendly squeak of the stool.

Uncle Max set a small box on the bench. “These are special screws,” he said. “They need careful hands.”

Roo’s eyes grew wide. “I can do careful,” he said.

Uncle Max showed Roo how to hold the little screwdriver. “Twist slow. Breathe steady,” he said. “Like this. In through the nose, out through the mouth.”

Roo copied him. In… out… The air smelled like oranges from Uncle Max’s hand soap. Roo giggled. “It tickles my nose!”

They started to work. The gentle gears were round and silver. They made a soft shhhhhh when they turned. Roo liked the sound. He liked it so much that he wanted to make it faster. He wanted to be the best helper ever.

“Watch me!” Roo said. He twisted quick-quick-quick.

“Sloooow,” Uncle Max reminded kindly.

Roo tried to slow down. But his paws were bouncy. Twist-twist—clink! A tiny screw slipped and rolled. It dropped to the floor and hid under the red toolbox.

“Oh no,” Roo gasped. His ears drooped. He felt hot in his cheeks. “I made a mess!”

Uncle Max knelt beside him. “It’s okay. Let’s look together.”

They looked under the toolbox. The floor smelled dusty. Roo saw a glint like a star. “There you are!” he said. He reached, but his paw bumped the oil can. The can tipped. Glug! Dark oil splashed on the floor. Splat on Roo’s toe. Splat on the mat. A little droplet kissed the gear.

Roo’s heart beat fast. Boom-boom-boom. “I ruined it,” he whispered. His throat felt tight. He wanted to run out of the garage and hide behind the lemon tree.

Uncle Max took a slow breath. In… out… He patted the mat. “Let’s pause, Roo.”

Roo wiggled, but he nodded.

“Hands on belly,” Uncle Max said. Roo placed his little paws on his round belly. “Smell the flower.” They breathed in together. “Blow the candle.” They breathed out together.

In… out… In… out… The boom-boom-boom in Roo’s chest began to slow.

Uncle Max smiled. “That’s self-regulation,” he said. “It means helping your body and feelings get calm so your brain can think.”

Roo blinked. “Self-regu-la-tion,” he said carefully. The big word felt like a marble on his tongue. “I can do that.”

Uncle Max handed Roo a soft cloth that smelled like soap. “We can fix spills. And we can fix feelings. Ready?”

Roo nodded. “Ready.”

They wiped the oil. Swish, swish. Roo liked watching the shiny floor come back. They washed the gear with warm, bubbly water. The bubbles made tiny rainbows. Roo giggled when one popped on his nose.

“I’m sorry, Uncle Max,” Roo said. “I went too fast.”

“I forgive you,” said Uncle Max. His voice was warm like toast. “Thank you for telling me. We all make mistakes. Forgiveness helps us move forward.”

Roo felt his cheeks cool. A soft glow filled his chest. “Thank you for forgiving me,” he whispered.

Uncle Max nodded. “Now, what’s our plan?”

Roo thought. He put a paw on his belly. In… out… He pictured slow hands. He pictured careful twists. “Go slow,” he said. “Smell the flower. Blow the candle. Then twist.”

“Great plan,” said Uncle Max.

Roo climbed onto the stool again. He held the screwdriver with steady paws. “Smell the flower.” In. “Blow the candle.” Out. Twist… twist… The screw slid into place with a tiny happy click.

Roo’s tail wiggled, but his hands stayed calm. He did one gear. Then another. The gentle gears turned with a soft shhhhhh, like a quiet wave.

“You did it,” Uncle Max said.

“I did it!” Roo beamed. His heart felt strong. His head felt clear. He was proud—not for being fast—but for being calm and careful.

They carried the gears to the garden gate. Uncle Max set them in, and Roo handed each screw like a helper hero. The gate opened. Creeeak—no! It moved smooth and sweet. Sshhh… The lemon tree leaves rustled. A soft breeze smelled bright and sunny.

Roo bounced once, then remembered. He put his paws on his belly. In… out… He smiled. “Self-regulation helps me do my best,” he said.

Uncle Max ruffled Roo’s ears. “And forgiving yourself helps you try again.”

Roo nodded. “I forgive me, too,” he said. “I can learn. I can slow down. I can be careful.”

They high-fived with a gentle pat. The gate whispered open, and two butterflies floated through like little yellow boats.

Roo laughed. “Slow and steady is special,” he said.

Uncle Max winked. “And so are you.”

The End.
