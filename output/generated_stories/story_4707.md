# Pico Parrot Saves the Bits and Pieces

Pico was a small, bright parrot with rainbow feathers and a clever mind. He lived in a quiet attic at the top of a cozy house. The attic smelled like old books and warm wood. Sunlight puffed in through a round window, making dust sparkle like tiny stars.

“Squawk! Good morning, attic!” Pico chirped each day.

The attic had boxes, baskets, and jars. There were buttons, ribbons, paper scraps, and little twigs from the window ledge. Pico liked to make things. He glued shapes, tied strings, and painted dots. He called it “making magic from little bits.”

One windy day, Pico hopped to a jar and found it empty. “Oh!” he said. He checked a basket. Empty. He looked in a box. Empty again. He fluffed his feathers. “Where did all the bits and pieces go?”

Downstairs, he heard people say, “Throw it away. It’s just trash.” The wind carried the words up the stairs like a whisper.

Pico’s heart thumped. “Trash? But bits can be useful!” he said. He wanted to show that small things could be saved and used again. That is called conservation—taking care and not wasting.

He looked around. The floor had tiny paper triangles from an old craft. The window ledge had two twigs and a soft feather. A jar held lost buttons: red, blue, and a shiny gold one.

“I will gather and make,” Pico said. “I can do this!” He stood tall. He would work by himself. He would try again and again.

He found a little box, but it was missing a side. “Hmm,” Pico said. “I can fix it.” He pressed a flat card to the box. It fell out. Flop.

He tried again. “Press, press.” Flop.

Pico took a deep breath. He listened to the quiet attic. Tick-tock went a clock. The air smelled like crayons. He felt calm. “One more try,” he told himself. “I can be patient.”

He used a ribbon to tie the card in place. He poked the knot with his beak. “Tug, tug.” It held! Pico cheered, “Squawk! It works!”

He made little pockets from paper triangles. He glued them to the box. He dropped in buttons, twigs, and tiny beads. Tap, tap. Plink, plink. The sounds made him smile.

Next, Pico made a sign. He used a marker and wrote big simple words: “Save Bits. Make New.” He drew a happy earth with a smile.

He placed his box by the attic stairs. The sun warmed his feathers. He called down, “Hello! Please don’t throw away good bits. We can use them!”

A child peeked up the stairs. “Hi, Pico,” said the child. “What are you doing?”

“I am saving,” said Pico. “Conservation means we take care. We do not waste. Little things can be used again.”

The child looked at the pockets. “These are neat,” the child said. “Can I help?”

Pico bobbed his head. “Yes! Put bottle caps here. Put clean paper scraps there. Twigs go in this pocket.”

The child ran to get things. Soon, soft steps and small hands brought clean caps, paper, string, and cloth. Plink! Rustle! Swish!

But then, the ribbon on the box sagged. The card slipped. The pockets drooped. Oh no!

Pico felt a lump in his tummy. “It’s falling apart,” he whispered.

He sat by the round window. The wind kissed his feathers. He watched leaves dance. He felt sad, but he did not give up.

“I will try again,” he said. “I will be persistent.” He fluffed up, shook off the worry, and looked for a better way.

He found a roll of strong tape under a book. It smelled like pine. He also found two cloth strips and a wooden clothespin.

“New plan,” said Pico. He taped the card inside the box so it could not slip. Press, press. Smooth, smooth. He used cloth strips to hold the pockets tighter. He clipped the top with the clothespin. Click!

“Let’s test,” Pico said. He put in three buttons. Plink, plink, plink. The pockets held. He added a bottle cap. No droop. He smiled. “We did it!”

The child clapped. “Yay, Pico!”

More family members came to see. “What a smart parrot,” said one. “What a good idea,” said another.

Pico puffed with pride. “We can all help the earth,” he said. “We can save. We can sort. We can make new things from old things.”

They all made a little craft together. Pico used a twig and a ribbon to make a tiny flag. The child glued a button sun to a paper sky. The attic filled with happy sounds: snip, snip; tap, tap; giggle, giggle.

At the end of the day, the box pockets were full of useful bits. The sign smiled from the stair rail. The attic felt cozy and bright.

Pico looked around. “We did not waste,” he said softly. “We saved. We made. We cared. That is conservation.” He felt calm and strong. He had done it with his own ideas, and he kept trying until it worked.

He tucked his head into his soft feathers and whispered, “Small steps help our big world.”

The End.
