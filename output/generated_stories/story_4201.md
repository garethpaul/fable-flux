# Roo and the Rolling Snack

Roo was a joyful kangaroo joey with bright eyes and a bouncy tail. Today, Roo hopped into the family car with Mama and big sister Kip. The car smelled like warm seats and sweet oranges. Sunlight made little sparkles on the window. The seatbelt clicked. The engine hummed, “Vroom, vroom.”

Mama smiled in the front. “Buckled?” she asked.

“Buckled!” Roo and Kip said together.

Roo patted a small snack cup in his lap. Inside were crunchy carrot sticks. They smelled fresh and a little earthy, like the garden after rain. Roo loved carrot sticks.

Kip held a book. “I brought a story,” she said. “It’s about a brave bird.”

Roo wiggled. “I want the first carrot!” he said, feeling excited. He opened the cup. But the car went over a small bump—ka-thump! The cup tipped. Carrot sticks rolled like orange pencils under the seat.

“My carrots!” Roo cried. His tummy felt tight. His face got hot. He could hear his heart thump-thump. A little growl bubbled in his chest.

Kip looked over. “We can pick them up at the red light,” she said kindly.

“No!” Roo shouted. “They’re mine! I want them now!” His voice sounded loud in the small car. The air felt sticky and heavy. Roo made his paws into fists.

Mama’s voice was calm, like soft music. “Roo, I hear your big feelings. Take a slow breath with me. In through your nose... out through your mouth.”

Roo didn’t want to breathe. He wanted carrots. But he tried. In… the air smelled like orange peels. Out… his shoulders felt a tiny bit softer.

Kip held up the book. “While we wait, I can share my story,” she offered. “Would you like to listen?”

Roo huffed. “No! I want my carrots.” A little tear wiggled down his cheek. He felt hot and prickly. He felt like a shaken soda.

Mama said, “Roo, your feelings are real. But yelling makes our car feel unsafe. We can be brave and try helpful tools. Let’s try three belly breaths. Then we can find a solution.”

Roo felt the seatbelt across his belly. He put his paws on it. He breathed with Mama. One… two… three… His tummy went up and down like a small wave. His body felt calmer. His hands opened.

“Better,” he whispered.

At the red light, Mama pulled the car to a gentle stop. Kip reached down and scooped up the runaway carrot sticks. “Found them!” she said. The carrots clicked together in her cup. She held them out. “I can share with you. I have apple slices, too.”

Roo blinked. “Share? With me?”

Kip nodded. “Yes. I know it’s hard to wait. We can share and make it fair.”

Roo felt his cheeks cool. “Thank you,” he said softly. He took one carrot and one apple slice. The carrot was crunchy. The apple was sweet and juicy. He listened to the munch-munch and the soft hum of the car. His tummy felt happy again.

Mama smiled in the mirror. “Roo, you used courage to calm down. Kip, thank you for sharing.”

Roo thought. “I can share, too,” he said. He held the cup toward Kip. “Do you want a carrot?”

“Yes, please,” Kip said. “And I’ll share my story now. Ready?”

Roo nodded, eyes bright. “Ready.”

Kip began. “Once, a brave bird wanted a shiny seed. But the seed rolled away, just like your carrots. The bird felt mad. But the bird took belly breaths. Then the bird asked a friend to help. The friend shared seeds. They both ate. The end.”

Roo giggled. “That’s a good story. It sounds like us!”

Mama laughed softly. “Stories help us learn.”

Roo looked out the window. Trees whooshed by. The car felt cozy again. “I like when we share,” he said. “Sharing makes more happy.”

A few minutes later, the car slowed again. Mama said, “We’re stopping at the park. We can have a picnic.”

At the park, Roo climbed out. The breeze smelled like grass and sunshine. The blanket felt soft. Kip laid out the snacks—carrots, apples, and little crackers. Roo remembered the car. He remembered the hot, prickly feeling. He remembered the belly breaths.

Two small birds hopped near the blanket. Roo watched them share a crumb. One bird pecked, then hopped back, and the other bird pecked. Roo smiled. “Even birds share,” he said.

Kip nodded. “Sharing is caring.”

Roo puffed his chest. “I am a brave sharer!” he said. He put two crackers on Kip’s napkin. “One for you. One for me.”

Kip grinned. “Thank you.”

On the ride home, Roo leaned back. The seat felt warm. The car hummed. He held the snack cup carefully with two paws. “I’m going to tell a story,” he announced. “Once, a joyful kangaroo joey had big feelings in a small car. He wanted all the carrots. His body felt hot. But he breathed brave breaths. He listened to his sister. He was open-minded. He tried a new idea. He shared. Then everyone felt safe and happy. The end.”

Mama clapped the steering wheel softly. “Beautiful story, Roo.”

Roo smiled a big, proud smile. He felt light like a balloon. He looked at Kip. “Will you share a high-five?”

Kip tapped his paw. “High-five!”

Roo said, “I learned that sharing is caring. Sharing helps feelings get softer. Sharing takes courage, but it makes more joy.”

Mama nodded. “That’s a wonderful lesson.”

Roo munched a carrot. Crunch. “I can do it again,” he said. “When I feel mad, I can breathe. I can listen. I can share.”

The car hummed. The sun warmed Roo’s fur. The world felt bright and friendly. Roo held his cup carefully and smiled, ready to share again.

The End.
