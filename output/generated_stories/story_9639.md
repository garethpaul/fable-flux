# Leo’s Little Lab Adventure

Leo was a brave little lion cub with bright, curious eyes. One rainy afternoon, he padded down the creaky steps to the basement lab. Drip, drip, drip went the rain outside. Click, hum, buzz went the lab inside. The lights glowed soft and warm. Shelves were full of colorful jars, buttons, and blinking machines.

“I want to make a rolling rumble cart,” Leo said, swishing his tail. “I can do it myself.”

He found a small wooden board, four round wheels, and a tin pail. He sniffed the clean wood. He knocked the wheels—tap tap tap. He felt his paws tingle with excitement.

He looked at a simple picture on the wall: Board. Wheels. Axle. Tighten. Roll!

“I can try,” Leo told himself. “I can move my body. I can try again.”

Leo pushed the board with his paw. He tried to put on a wheel. The wheel slipped and clattered to the floor. Clink! It rolled under a table.

“Grrr,” Leo rumbled. His whiskers drooped. “This is hard.”

He took a breath in, slow and big. He held it. He let it go. Whooo. “I can calm my body,” he whispered. “I can try again.”

He crawled under the table. The floor felt cool on his belly. He stretched his paws out long and reached. “Almost,” he said. “I can move closer.” He scooted forward and picked up the wheel. “Got it!”

Back at the bench, Leo tried again. He lined up the wheel with a little round stick. He pressed. It wobbled. It fell off. Plop.

His ears tilted back. His tail twitched. He felt hot in his face. “I feel frustrated,” he said out loud. “I want it to work now.”

He stamped his paws—tap tap tap—and then he paused. He remembered a song Mama Lion taught him for big feelings.

“Stop, breathe, and move,” Leo sang softly.
“Stop, breathe, and move.”
“Shake it out, stretch it tall,
Try again—don’t fear the fall.”

Leo stopped. He took three belly breaths. In… and out. In… and out. In… and out. He shook his paws. He rolled his shoulders. He stretched up tall, tall, tall, touching the warm light. He felt calmer. He felt ready.

“I can do one small step,” he told himself. “Just one.”

He put the board on a cloth so it would not slide. He held the wheel with one paw and the stick with the other. Slowly, slowly, he pushed. Click! The wheel stayed on.

His eyes went wide. “It worked!” he giggled. “I did it!”

He did a happy wiggle dance. His claws made a tiny scritch-scratch on the floor. He bent his knees and bounced. Boing, boing.

“Now the other wheels,” Leo said. He tried the next one. This time it didn’t click. It squeaked. It slipped. It rolled away again.

Leo felt a grumble in his tummy. He stopped. “How do I feel?” he asked. “I feel a little mad.” He placed a paw on his chest. He took a slow breath. He blew it out like a gentle wind. “I can choose to try again.”

He tried another way. He used a stretchy band to hold the stick steady. He pushed with gentle pressure. Click! Then another. Click! Then the last one—click!

Leo smiled so wide his whiskers curved. He set the tin pail on the board. He tied it on with a ribbon. He gave the cart a tiny push. It rolled a little, then stopped.

“Hm,” he said. “It does not roll far.” He tapped his chin. “What can I do?”

He checked the wheels. One wheel rubbed the ribbon. “Ah-ha!” he said. “I can move the ribbon.”

He untied it and tied it again, a little looser. He tested the cart. He crouched low, wiggled his hips, and gave a steady push. The cart rolled across the smooth floor. Rrrrroll! The tin pail rattled—ratta-tatta-tat!

Leo laughed. “I did it! I made a rolling rumble cart!”

He took the cart for a spin around the lab. He moved his body with joy—step, step, step, push! He steered around a stack of boxes. He scooted under a hanging cord. He ducked under a shelf, tail swishing. He parked the cart by the stairs.

Mama Lion peeked down. “I heard rolling,” she said with a smile. “What did you do, little one?”

“I made this cart,” Leo said proudly. “I felt frustrated, but I stopped, breathed, and moved. I tried one small step at a time. I did it by myself.”

Mama Lion nuzzled his head. “You used calm, clever paws,” she said. “You trusted yourself.”

Leo puffed his chest. He felt warm and strong inside. He patted the cart. “When it is hard, I can slow down. I can move my body. I can try again. I can help myself.”

He put a soft blanket in the pail. He placed a toy lion in the cart. “Ready?” he asked. He pushed gently. The cart rolled smooth and steady. Rrrrroll. Rattta-tat. The toy lion smiled up at him.

Leo grinned. “I am brave. I am patient. I can do hard things!”

The End.
