# Tiko the Shy Turtle Says “I’m Sorry”

In a quiet attic, where dust danced like tiny stars, a shy turtle named Tiko lived in a cozy shoebox home. The attic smelled like old paper and warm wood. Soft light peeked through a small round window. The floor creaked in kind, friendly squeaks.

Tiko liked the hush of the attic. He liked to listen. He listened to the tick-tock clock. He listened to the wind whoosh past the window. He listened to the faraway hum of people below. The attic had treasures from around the world: a painted fan from Japan, a tiny drum from Africa, a bright scarf from India, and a snow globe from a place with tall mountains.

Tiko was shy. He hid in his shell when people came. But he loved to look at the attic treasures. He whispered, “Hello, fan. Hello, drum. Hello, scarf. Hello, globe,” as he shuffled by.

One sunny morning, Tiko saw his friend, Mina the mouse. Mina squeaked, “Good morning, Tiko! Do you want to play museum? We can set the treasures on the table and tell stories.”

Tiko’s eyes sparkled. “Yes,” he whispered. “Let’s play.”

They moved carefully. Tiko carried the tiny drum. Thump-thump, it made a soft sound. Mina twirled the scarf. Swish-swish, it made a silky sound. They put the treasures in a line like a little world parade.

“Look,” said Mina. “We can be kind and gentle with the world.”

Tiko nodded. “We take care of things from many places,” he said. “We are good helpers.”

They both smiled. But then, Tiko saw the snow globe. It had a little village inside and friendly people painted on tiny houses. When Tiko shook it, white flakes swirled like a slow, quiet storm. He loved it.

“I want to see it closer,” Tiko whispered. He reached his small turtle hands up, up, up. His shell bumped the table. Clonk.

The snow globe wobbled. Wibble wobble. Mina gasped. “Careful, Tiko!”

“Oh no,” Tiko whispered. The globe tipped and slipped. It fell on the soft scarf, but it still made a sharp crack. The base chipped. A tiny leak started. Drip. Drip. Drip.

Tiko’s heart thumped. He felt hot and small. He pulled into his shell. “I didn’t mean to,” he said in a tiny voice.

Mina touched the globe. “It’s hurt,” she said. “We have to tell Grandma Mei. These treasures are special. They are gifts from friends far away.”

Tiko shook in his shell. “I’m shy,” he said. “I’m scared.”

Mina’s whiskers twitched kindly. “Being shy is okay,” she said. “But telling the truth is strong. Making amends is kind. We can do it together.”

Tiko took a slow breath. He peeked out. He looked at the little world in the globe. He thought about friends far away. He thought about taking care of things that do not belong to us. He thought about being honest.

“I will tell,” he said. “I will fix what I can.”

Together, they wrapped the globe in the scarf to stop the drip. Swish, tie, pat. They carried it carefully. Step, step, step, down the creaky stairs. The attic door opened with a soft groan.

Grandma Mei sat at a table with tea that smelled like flowers and a cookie tin that clicked shut. She looked up and smiled. “Hello, little ones.”

Tiko felt his tummy flip. He held the snow globe with both hands. “Grandma Mei,” he said, voice a little wobbly. “I made a mistake. I bumped the table. The snow globe is cracked. I am sorry.”

Grandma Mei’s eyes were gentle. “Thank you for telling me the truth, Tiko,” she said. “That is integrity. That means being honest even when it is hard.” She touched the globe. “Things can break. Hearts feel better when we make amends.”

“What is ‘make amends’?” Tiko asked.

“It means you say sorry and you try to fix the hurt,” Grandma Mei said. “You can help repair. You can be careful next time. You can show you care.”

Tiko nodded. “I want to help,” he said. “I want to be careful. This globe is from a friend who lives far away. We are part of one big world. We can take care of each other’s things.”

“That is global citizenship,” Grandma Mei said with a smile. “We take care of the world and the people in it.”

Mina squeaked, “We can dry it! We can put gentle glue! We can make a little note.”

Grandma Mei brought a soft cloth and a tiny brush. Brush, dab, pat. Tiko held the globe steady. Mina read the simple steps. Grandma Mei added a drop of clear glue to the chip. The drip stopped. The crack was small now, like a little line of ice.

Tiko wrote a note with big, careful letters:
“I am sorry. I bumped it. I helped fix it. I will be gentle.”

They set the globe back in the attic, safe on a steady shelf. Tiko touched the table and whispered, “I will move slowly. I will be careful.”

Mina smiled. “You did it, Tiko! You told the truth. You made amends.”

Tiko felt warm and brave. He looked out the round window at the blue sky. “I am shy,” he said softly, “but I can be strong and kind.”

Grandma Mei hugged them both. “That is integrity,” she said. “And that is how we care for our big, shared world.”

The attic was quiet again. Tick-tock. Whoosh. Swish. Tiko curled in his shoebox home with a happy heart. He whispered, “Say sorry. Fix the hurt. Be gentle. We are all neighbors.”

The End.
