# Ollie Otter’s Tasty Teamwork

Ollie was a playful otter with shiny whiskers and bright, curious eyes. One sunny afternoon, Ollie slid across the smooth floor into the dining room. The table smelled warm and sweet, like cinnamon and oranges. Sunlight made little squares on the floor, and Ollie’s whiskers twitched with excitement.

“Today I’ll make a special snack,” Ollie said, clapping his little paws. “I will try and try, no matter what!”

On the table, there were small bowls with colorful things: green cucumber slices, orange carrots, red tomatoes, and soft rice. There was a little pot that smelled like coconut and ginger. There were also wrappers that were thin and round like the moon.

Ollie looked at a paper on the wall with pictures of different foods from around the world. “These are rice rolls,” he read slowly. “Friends in many places make them together. They dip, roll, and share.” He smiled. “I want to try. I can learn!”

Ollie dipped a wrapper in warm water. It felt slippery and cool. “Ooo, squishy!” he giggled. He put it on a plate. Then he tried to add rice. Plop! The rice fell in the middle like a little snow hill. He added cucumber and carrot sticks. “Roll, roll, roll,” he whispered.

But the wrapper stuck to his paws. It ripped a tiny bit. The rice rolled right out and went pitter-patter on the plate.

“Oh no,” Ollie sighed. His whiskers drooped. “This is hard.”

Just then, his friend Mia Mouse peeked in with a soft, spicy smell swirling around her. “Hi, Ollie! It smells yummy in here. What are you making?”

“Rice rolls,” said Ollie. “But I can’t do it. My roll fell apart.”

Mia smiled and touched the smooth wrapper. “In my family, we make tortillas,” she said. “We press and flip and try again. It takes practice. Want to try together?”

Ollie nodded. “Yes, please!”

Mia washed her tiny paws. “First, let’s make a little line of rice,” she said. “Not too much.” She showed Ollie how to press the rice gently. “Soft paws,” she said.

“Soft paws,” Ollie repeated. He pressed gently. The rice made a neat line. His whiskers lifted a little.

They added cucumber and carrot sticks. “Color parade!” Ollie cheered. He sniffed the ginger. “Spicy tickle!”

“Now we tuck,” Mia said. “Fold the bottom up, and the sides in, like a blanket hug.”

Ollie tried. The wrapper stuck again. “Oops!” he said. It tore a tiny bit, but not much. He took a breath. “I will try again.”

“Good job,” Mia said. “Trying is how we learn.”

They made another roll. Ollie dipped. He pressed softly. He folded like a blanket hug. But when he rolled, it wriggled away and plopped. “Oh no!” Ollie’s shoulders drooped.

Mia patted his paw. “One more time. We can do it. We can be patient.”

Ollie nodded. “I will keep trying.” He looked at the picture again. He thought about families rolling together, laughing and helping. He could almost hear the happy sounds: roll, dip, crunch, share.

Ollie dipped a new wrapper. He waited one, two, three seconds so it was not too wet. “Just right,” he said. He made a small line of rice. He added carrot, cucumber, and a tiny leaf of mint. The mint smelled cool, like a breeze.

“Fold, fold,” he whispered. “Blanket hug.” He rolled slowly, with soft paws. The roll stayed together. It was round and shiny.

“I did it!” Ollie squeaked. His eyes sparkled. “I kept trying!”

Mia clapped. “You did it! Now let’s make more. We can make a rainbow plate.”

They worked side by side. Ollie rolled carefully. Mia added slices of mango that smelled sweet and sunny. “In my grandmother’s kitchen, we share stories while we cook,” Mia said.

Ollie listened and smiled. “In my river home, we crunch shells and splash. Today we roll and share. I like learning about your food.”

They made green rolls with cucumber, orange rolls with carrot and a tiny bit of mango, and white rolls with rice and coconut flakes. The dining room was full of sounds: soft splashes, tiny taps, little laughs. The air smelled fresh, sweet, and spicy.

“Let’s make a dip,” Mia said. She mixed soy sauce with a drop of honey and a squeeze of lime. It smelled tangy. “In some places, families use sauces like this.”

Ollie dipped a corner of a roll. Crunch! “Yum!” he said. “Sweet and tangy. Thank you, Mia.”

“You kept trying,” Mia said. “That’s why it worked.”

They set the rainbow plate in the center of the table. Ollie looked at the bright colors. His chest felt warm and proud. “We did this together,” he said. “I learned from you. We shared. We tried again and again.”

“Persistence,” Mia said slowly. “That means you keep going.”

Ollie nodded. “Persistence. I kept going, even when it was hard.”

They shared the rolls, one for Ollie, one for Mia, and one saved for a friend. They chewed and smiled. The crunchy sounds were like tiny drums. The mint felt cool. The mango tasted like sunshine.

Ollie licked a grain of rice from his paw and giggled. “Today I learned to roll rice, to be patient, to ask for help, and to enjoy foods from different places. Trying again made me strong.”

Mia grinned. “And cooking together made it fun.”

Ollie looked around the cozy dining room. The sun squares on the floor were softer now. “Let’s remember: keep trying, help each other, and enjoy the flavors of our big world.”

They high-fived with soft paws and tiny hands.

The End.
