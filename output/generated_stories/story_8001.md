# Leo Learns in the Bathroom

Leo was a brave little lion cub with bright eyes and a soft, golden mane. One sunny morning, he padded into the bathroom. The tiles felt cool under his paws. Drip, drip, went the faucet. Swish, swish, went the shower curtain when he touched it.

“Today, I learn something new,” Leo said. “I am brave. I am smart.”

On the counter, his toothbrush stood like a tiny blue flag. His soccer ball, the size of a melon, rested by the door because Leo loved to practice kicks everywhere. His mama had said, “Leo, the bathroom is for washing. Balls belong in the basket.” But Leo had forgotten.

Leo picked up his toothbrush. “Brush, brush,” he sang. Bubbles tasted minty and cool. He watched them pop in the mirror. “Mmm! Tingly!” He used small circles like Mama had shown him. “Up and down, round and round,” Leo whispered. He counted slowly, “One, two, three…” He knew he should brush to the count of twenty. He took a deep breath. “Four, five, six… I can do it!”

When he reached twenty, his teeth felt smooth like shiny pebbles. He smiled at his reflection. “Knowledge helps me brush right,” he said proudly. He rinsed his mouth and listened to the water gurgle down the drain. Glug, glug.

Then Leo saw his soccer ball again. “I can practice kicks while the floor dries,” he said. “Just one gentle tap.”

He tapped the ball with his paw. Thump. The ball rolled and bumped the soap dish. Clatter! The soap slid onto the floor. Sliiiide! Leo stepped back. Whoops! His paw slipped on a tiny soap bubble. He wobbled. “Oh!” His tail puffed like a dandelion.

He caught his balance and put the ball down. His heart went thump-thump. “Sports need safe spaces,” he said softly. He looked around at the sudsy floor and the messy counter. His brush lay sideways. The soap was on the tiles. Towels were half on the rack, half on the floor.

Leo’s ears drooped. “I forgot to be organized,” he admitted. He remembered Mama’s gentle voice, “We learn by looking, thinking, and trying again.”

Leo took a slow breath. He decided to use his knowledge. “First, I clean. Then, I put things where they belong. Then I ask Mama about safe places to play.”

He picked up the soap and set it in the dish. He hung the towels carefully. He wiped a small puddle with a dry corner. Swish, swish. He stood the toothbrush back in its cup. “Everything has a home,” he said. He put his soccer ball in the hallway basket, snug and safe. Plop.

Mama Lion peeked in. Her fur was warm and soft, and she smelled like sunshine. “Hello, brave Leo,” she said. “I heard a clatter. Are you okay?”

Leo looked up with big eyes. “Mama, I’m okay. I made a mess. I tried to kick my ball in the bathroom. The soap slid. I slipped a little. I felt scared. I cleaned it up. I am sorry.”

Mama knelt and gave Leo a gentle nuzzle. “Thank you for your apology,” she said. “Thank you for cleaning. That was very brave and very smart.”

Leo’s whiskers perked. “Mama, I learned something. Bathrooms are for washing and brushing. Sports need space. Knowledge helps me choose. Can you teach me more?”

Mama smiled. “Of course. Let’s use our minds like flashlights. We look. We think. We plan.”

She pointed to the sink. “What do we know about water?”

Leo thought. He tapped his chin. “Water is wet and slippery. So we walk, not run.”

“Good,” said Mama. “What do we know about balls?”

“Balls roll. They can bump things,” Leo said. “So we play with balls outside or in the playroom.”

“What do we know about things and homes?” Mama asked.

“Everything has a place,” Leo answered. He looked around proudly. “The brush sleeps in the cup. The towel sleeps on the rack. The ball sleeps in the basket.”

Mama clapped her paws softly. “That is great knowledge, Leo. Knowledge keeps us safe, helps us play, and helps us care for our things.”

Leo felt warm and tall inside. “Can we have a mini sport time now?” he asked. “Maybe stretches?”

“In the bathroom, we can do quiet stretches,” Mama said. “Show me.”

Leo put his paws on the soft bath mat. It felt fluffy like a cloud. He reached up high. “Stretch to the sky!” He bent down low. “Touch your toes!” He giggled. “Hello, toes!” Mama stretched too. Their tails swished in time. Swoosh, swoosh.

“Now,” Mama said, “we put the mat straight and the curtain closed. That is organization.”

Leo carefully lined up the mat’s corners. He drew the curtain smooth. Swish. “Organized!” he cheered.

They walked to the basket. Leo patted his soccer ball. “I will play outside after breakfast,” he said. “I will kick in the grass.”

Mama nodded. “That’s a wise plan. You used your knowledge to make a good choice. I am proud.”

Leo hugged Mama. “I am brave. I am smart. I can learn every day,” he said. He felt calm and happy, like a purr inside his chest. Rrrr.

At breakfast, Leo told his stuffed giraffe about his morning. “I brushed for twenty. I said sorry. I cleaned up. I learned where to play. Knowledge helped me!”

The giraffe smiled with button eyes. Leo grinned back.

And from that day, when Leo saw the bathroom, he remembered: use knowledge, be organized, say sorry when needed, and keep sports in safe spaces. He felt ready for new adventures, one smart step at a time.

The End.
