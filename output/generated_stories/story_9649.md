# Tiko the Tortoise Sets a Sky Goal

On a sunny day, a wise tortoise named Tiko climbed slowly, step by careful step, up to the rooftop garden. The roof felt warm under his feet. He could smell fresh mint and tiny tomato leaves. A soft breeze tickled his nose. “Mmm,” Tiko hummed, “the sky smells like sunshine.”

Tiko loved the rooftop. He could see clouds like puffy sheep, birds swooping and singing, and kites floating like bright fish in the air. His shell felt cozy and safe, and his eyes sparkled with curiosity.

Across the rooftop, a little girl named Mina waved. She wore a blue hat and carried a red ribbon. “Hi, Tiko!” she called. “Do you want to see my kite fly high?”

Tiko blinked slowly. “Yes, Mina,” he said in a warm, gentle voice. “I would like to see your kite reach the big blue sky.”

Mina ran, and the kite lifted up. It fluttered, then dipped, then swooshed. “Up, up, up!” Mina laughed. The kite climbed higher and higher. Tiko watched with wide eyes. He felt a happy thump in his chest. The wind brushed his face, cool and fresh. The kite’s tail clicked softly against the rooftop tiles.

“I have a wish,” Tiko said. “I want to reach the top of the big planting box, the one by the tall sunflower. From there, I can see the whole sky. That is my goal.”

Mina kneeled beside him. “That box is tall,” she said. “It is as high as my knees.”

Tiko nodded. “It is tall for me. But I am wise, and I will make a plan. A plan helps a goal come true.”

Mina’s eyes shone. “What is your plan, Tiko?”

Tiko looked at the path. “First, I will rest. Then I will move to the brick step. Then I will stretch to the wooden board. Then I will climb to the top of the planting box. I will go slow and steady. I will check my body. I will breathe.”

Mina tapped her chin. “We can make little stops,” she said. She set three smooth stones in a line. “Stone One, Stone Two, Stone Three. Each stone is a small goal.”

Tiko smiled. “Small goals help the big goal.”

He took a deep breath. The mint leaves smelled sweet and cool. He touched Stone One. “Step one,” he said. His feet felt sure.

A gust of wind whooshed. The kite tugged hard. “Whoa!” Mina giggled as the ribbon danced. Tiko paused. He listened to his heart. Bum, bum, bum. “I am okay,” he told himself. “I can keep going.”

He moved to the brick step. It felt rough and warm. He wiggled his toes to grip. “Step two,” Tiko said. He stretched his neck like a slow, careful crane. “I can do hard things when I have a plan.”

Mina clapped softly. “You are doing it!”

Tiko reached Stone Two. He rested in the shade of a tiny tomato plant. He could smell the green, peppery scent. A bee buzzed by, zzz, zzz. Tiko watched the bee with curious eyes. “Hello, little worker,” he whispered. “You have a goal, too.”

The wooden board was next. It creaked a little under Tiko’s weight. He paused. “Hmm,” he said, “this part is wobbly.”

Mina crouched and held the board steady with her hand. “I can help,” she said. “Helpers are part of a good plan.”

“Thank you,” said Tiko. He stepped onto the board. Tap, tap, tap. “Step three,” he said, and smiled.

Now came the tall planting box. The sunflower’s face was bright and yellow. It looked like a little sun. Tiko felt a tiny worry in his tummy. “This is the hardest part,” he told Mina.

“It is the last part,” Mina said kindly. “You are strong and patient.”

Tiko took three slow breaths. In, out. In, out. In, out. The breeze felt cool. The sun felt warm. He placed one foot, then another. He pushed with his back legs. He lifted with his front legs. His shell scraped, scritch, scritch. He wiggled. He pressed on.

“I will not rush,” Tiko said. “Slow and steady meets the goal.”

At last, Tiko pulled himself up. Plunk! He was on top of the planting box! The rooftop spread out below him. The sky looked even bigger. The kite floated high, shining red against the blue. Tiko’s heart felt light.

“I did it!” Tiko laughed. “I reached my goal.”

Mina cheered. “Hooray for Tiko! You made a plan. You took small steps. You asked for help. You kept going!”

Tiko looked at the sunflower and the wide, wide sky. He felt proud and calm. “Now I can set a new goal,” he said. “I want to read the clouds. I will find three cloud shapes.”

Mina pointed. “I see a bunny!”

“I see a boat,” Tiko said.

“And I see a tiny kite,” Mina grinned.

Tiko nodded. “Goals can be big or small. We can play outside, be curious, and try new things. When we make a plan and take little steps, we can reach the sky, too.”

Mina tied the red ribbon gently around the sunflower’s stem, just a small bow. The ribbon danced in the wind. Tiko closed his eyes for a moment and breathed in the rooftop smells—mint, soil, sunshine. He felt safe, happy, and brave.

That day, on the warm rooftop, Tiko learned and taught: Set a goal. Make a plan. Take small steps. Ask for help. Keep going.

The End.
