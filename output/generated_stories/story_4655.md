# Benny Beaver Builds His Best

Benny was a busy beaver with bright, shiny teeth and a fuzzy, flat tail. He lived with his family in a playful living room full of soft pillows, a bouncy rug, and blocks in a rainbow basket. Sunlight made warm squares on the floor, and music hummed softly from a little radio. Benny loved to build. He loved to move. He loved to try.

One morning, Benny clapped his paws. “Today,” he said, “I will build the Tallest Tower!”

Mama Beaver smiled from the couch. “That sounds exciting, Benny. Remember to plan.”

Papa Beaver stretched his back. “And remember to take little exercise breaks,” he added. “Stretch, breathe, and wiggle!”

Benny nodded fast. “I can do it! I am brave. I can try.”

He pulled out the blocks. Red, blue, yellow, green—clink, clack, clunk went the blocks on the bouncy rug. The rug felt soft under Benny’s paws. The room smelled like warm toast. Benny’s heart felt fizzy and happy.

Benny stacked one, two, three blocks. “Look, Mama! It’s growing!”

“It sure is,” Mama said. “Use your plan. Where will the big blocks go?”

Benny tapped his chin. “Big ones on the bottom. Small ones on the top.” He placed the big blocks low and the tiny blocks high. The tower grew taller, and Benny stood on his tippy toes.

But then—uh-oh! The tower wobbled. Wobble, wobble—crash! Blocks tumbled like rain. Clickety-clack! A blue block rolled under the chair.

Benny’s ears drooped. “My tower fell,” he whispered. His tummy felt tight and small.

Papa Beaver came over and knelt beside him. “It fell this time,” Papa said gently, “but you can try again. Let’s breathe together.”

Benny put his paws on his belly. In—slow sniff. Out—soft blow. “I can try again,” Benny said. His voice shook a little, but his eyes felt warm and brave.

He reached under the chair and found the lost blue block. “Hello, block,” he giggled. “We still need you.”

Benny thought about his plan. “Big on the bottom. Small on the top. And…spread the blocks out, not too close.” He pressed the blocks together with his flat tail to make them snug. He listened to the little squeak as the blocks rubbed. Squeak, squeak. He smoothed the rug so it was flat.

“Break time!” Papa called. “Let’s move our bodies.”

Benny and Papa did tiny jumps on the bouncy rug. Boing, boing! They stretched tall like trees and wiggled like worms. Benny felt strong and light. His cheeks tingled.

“I feel ready,” Benny said. “I can learn. I can grow.”

He stacked again: thump, clack, click. The tower swayed a little but did not fall. “It’s working!” Benny sang.

Just then, Little Squirrel from next door peeped in the door with a shy smile. “Hi, Benny,” she said in a squeaky voice. “Can I play?”

Benny looked at his tower. He looked at Little Squirrel. He took a breath. “Yes,” he said kindly. “You can hand me blocks.”

Little Squirrel’s tail twitched. She held out a yellow block. “Here you go.”

“Thank you,” Benny said. “I’m making the Tallest Tower. I had a fall, but I am trying again.”

Little Squirrel nodded. “Sometimes I fall, too,” she said. “I keep trying.”

Benny smiled. “We can try together.”

They kept building. Benny planned. Little Squirrel helped. Mama hummed a happy tune, and the room felt warm and safe. The tower reached Benny’s nose…then his eyes…then his ears!

The tower wobbled again. Benny’s heart skipped. “Oh no,” he whispered.

“It’s okay,” Mama said softly. “Use your plan. You know what to do.”

Benny pressed the blocks gently. He moved one big block to the bottom. He added a wide block in the middle. He did one more belly breath. “I am brave,” he said. “I can do hard things.”

Little Squirrel gave him a tiny block with a star on it. “Top piece,” she whispered.

Benny lifted the star block high. He set it on top—so soft, so steady. The tower stood tall and proud. It did not wobble. It did not fall.

“We did it!” Benny cheered. “I did it! I believed in me.” His tail swished with joy. The room filled with claps and happy squeals.

Papa gave a thumbs-up. “Strong body, strong plan, strong heart,” he said.

Mama hugged Benny. “I’m proud of how you kept trying,” she said. “That is self-confidence. You trusted yourself. You made a plan. You moved your body. You improved each time.”

Benny beamed. “When I feel wobbly,” he said, “I can breathe, plan, and try again. I can be kind to friends, too.”

Little Squirrel smiled. “And we can be patient with each other,” she said. “That is tolerance.”

Benny nodded. He put his paw on his chest. “I am brave inside. I can learn. I can grow.”

They took a tiny bow to the tower. Then they did a fun cool-down: shake-shake hands, stretch-stretch arms, and a big, soft hug. The living room glowed in the afternoon sun, and the tower twinkled with the star on top.

Benny whispered, “Tall tower, tall courage.” He grinned. “Tomorrow, I will build again—with a plan, with movement, and with me believing in me.”

The End.
