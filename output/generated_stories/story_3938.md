# Koby's Green Dream

Koby the koala sat in his cozy home office, surrounded by colorful papers scattered across his wooden desk. The morning sun streamed through the window, making the papers sparkle like tiny rainbows. Koby loved working in his special room, but today something bothered him.

"Oh my," Koby said softly, looking at all the crumpled papers around him. "I've made such a mess trying to draw the perfect picture for Mama's birthday."

The gentle koala picked up a paper with a wobbly tree drawing. "This one's trunk is too skinny," he sighed. Then he held up another with a lopsided heart. "And this heart looks like a potato!"

Koby's fluffy ears drooped as he counted fifteen crumpled papers on his desk. His home office, usually neat and tidy, looked like a paper snowstorm had blown through it.

"I want to make something beautiful for Mama," Koby whispered to himself. "But I'm not very good at drawing."

Just then, Mama Koala peeked her head through the doorway. Her warm brown eyes twinkled as she saw the paper mess.

"What's happening in here, little one?" she asked, her voice gentle as a summer breeze.

"I wanted to draw you the most wonderful birthday picture," Koby explained, his voice small and sad. "But I keep messing up. Now I have all these wasted papers, and I feel terrible about throwing them away."

Mama Koala stepped into the office and gave Koby a soft, eucalyptus-scented hug. "Oh, sweetheart, those papers aren't wasted at all! Let me show you something magical."

She pointed to a blue bin in the corner of the office. "See that recycling bin? Those papers can become new papers, or even cardboard boxes! When we recycle, we help trees stay in the forest where they belong."

Koby's eyes grew wide with wonder. "Really? The papers can become new things?"

"Absolutely!" Mama smiled. "And you know what? Sometimes the most beautiful art comes from trying many times. Each 'mistake' teaches us something new."

Together, they gathered all the crumpled papers. Koby carefully placed each one in the recycling bin, listening to the satisfying *crinkle* sounds they made.

"Now," said Mama, "what if we set a small goal? Instead of trying to make the perfect picture, what if you just tried to make one that shows how much you love me?"

Koby nodded eagerly. He picked up his green crayon and started drawing again. This time, he didn't worry about making it perfect. He drew a simple tree with a heart carved in it, and inside the heart, he wrote "MAMA" in his best letters.

The tree was a little wobbly, and the heart was still lopsided, but when Koby looked at it, he felt proud and happy.

"It's not perfect," Koby said, holding up his drawing.

"But it's made with love," Mama replied, "and that makes it absolutely perfect to me."

Koby beamed with joy. He had learned that recycling helped the Earth, that setting smaller goals made big dreams possible, and that sometimes "not perfect" was actually just right.

"I love you, Mama," Koby said, handing her his special drawing.

"I love you too, my thoughtful little koala," she replied, hanging the picture on the office wall where the morning sun made it glow.

The End.
