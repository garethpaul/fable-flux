# Squeak’s Words in the Guest Room

Squeak was a small, resourceful squirrel with bright eyes and a fluffy tail. He loved to learn. One sunny morning, Squeak scampered into a cozy guest room. The room smelled like clean sheets and lemon soap. A soft rug tickled his paws. Sunlight made little squares on the floor.

“Hello?” squeaked Squeak. “Is anyone here?”

A gentle voice answered, “Hello, little friend.” It was Nana Rosa, a kind guest with silver hair and warm hands. She was visiting for the weekend. She smiled and patted the quilt. “Come sit. I am making a welcome basket.”

Squeak hopped onto the quilt. It felt smooth and cool. The basket held a small red scarf, a blue book, and a tiny jar of honey. Squeak’s nose twitched. “Mmm, sweet!”

Nana Rosa chuckled. “Where I come from, we say miel for honey.”

Squeak’s ears perked up. “Miel,” he tried. “Meeee-yell.” He giggled. “That sounds like a song.”

“It is a beautiful word,” said Nana Rosa. “Different words can tell the same thing. It’s like having more colors to paint with.”

Squeak’s tail swished. He liked colors. He liked words too. “Can I help with the basket?”

“Yes, please,” said Nana Rosa. “You are a helpful friend.”

Squeak picked up the scarf. It felt soft and a little warm. “Scarf,” he said.

“In my language, we say bufanda,” Nana Rosa said gently.

“Boo-fan-da,” Squeak said slowly. “Bufanda.” He smiled big. “Bufanda keeps necks cozy.”

“Very good!” said Nana Rosa. “Would you like to learn how I say book?”

Squeak hugged the small blue book. It smelled like paper and stories. “Yes, please!”

“We say libro,” said Nana Rosa.

“Lee-bro,” Squeak repeated. “Libro. Book. Libro.” His whiskers danced. “Two ways to say one friend.”

They laughed together. The guest room felt extra warm now, like a hug. Squeak placed the scarf and the book back in the basket. He saw a little card and a short pencil on the night table.

“I can write a welcome note!” Squeak said. “I can help.”

“That is very kind,” said Nana Rosa. “Let us share a tradition too. In my family, we leave a little poem for guests. It is a way to say, ‘We are happy you are here.’”

Squeak’s eyes shone. “I want to share a tradition too. My squirrel family gives a tiny nut for luck.”

“Beautiful,” said Nana Rosa. “We will write the poem and add a lucky nut.”

Squeak thought and wiggled his nose. “But I do not know many words,” he said softly. His tail drooped.

Nana Rosa patted his paw. “You know more than you think. We will use simple words. We will use kind words. Words can be small and still big with love.”

Squeak took a brave breath. He smelled lemon and honey. He heard the gentle tick-tock of the guest room clock. “Okay,” he said. “Let’s try.”

Nana Rosa smiled. “We can write in two languages. That is sharing and caring.”

Squeak squeaked with joy. “Yes! That is language-appreciation. I like that.” He sounded out the big idea slowly, “Lan-gwage ap-pre-ci-a-tion.”

They began. Squeak held the pencil. Scratch, scratch went the tip on the card. The sound was soft and friendly.

“What is the first line?” asked Nana Rosa.

Squeak said, “Welcome, friend.”

“Muy bien,” said Nana Rosa. “In my language: Bienvenida, amiga. Or Bienvenido, amigo.”

Squeak wrote carefully:
“Welcome, friend.
Bienvenido, amigo.”

He smiled. “What next?”

Nana Rosa said, “Rest and smile.”

Squeak added, “Descansa y sonríe.”

He repeated each word. “Des-can-sa y son-ríe.” The sounds felt round and sweet in his mouth, like little candies.

They read it together:
“Welcome, friend.
Bienvenido, amigo.
Rest and smile.
Descansa y sonríe.”

Squeak tapped the card. “One more line!” He thought of the honey. He thought of hugs. “Your heart is at home here.”

Nana Rosa nodded. “Tu corazón está en casa aquí.”

Squeak wrote slowly. “Tu co-ra-zón está en ca-sa aquí.” He traced a tiny heart.

He placed the card in the basket with the scarf, the book, and the honey. Then he pulled a shiny acorn from his pouch.

“This is for luck,” Squeak said. “A squirrel tradition.” He tucked the acorn beside the honey jar.

Nana Rosa clasped her hands. “We shared words. We shared traditions. We were helpful together. That is beautiful.”

Squeak felt warm pride in his chest. “Words are friends,” he said. “Words help us be kind.”

Just then, a breeze moved the curtains. They whispered, swish, swish. The clock said, tick-tock. The guest room looked soft and ready. Squeak and Nana Rosa stood back and smiled.

A little later, a guest came in. She saw the basket and the card. She read the words out loud, first in one language, then the other. Her eyes grew bright. “Thank you,” she said, voice gentle like a lullaby. “I feel welcome.”

Squeak waved. “We are happy you are here.”

The guest hugged the scarf, held the book, and dipped a finger near the honey. “Mmm,” she said. “Miel.”

Squeak’s tail swooshed with joy. “Miel!” he chirped. He felt brave, kind, and smart.

That night, Squeak curled up on the quilt. He whispered both words for book. “Book. Libro.” He patted his heart. “Two words. One love.”

Lesson: Learning and sharing words can bring people together. Helping and sharing traditions make everyone feel at home.

The End.
