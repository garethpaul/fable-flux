# Kiki the Calm Koala Says Thank You

Kiki the calm koala lived in a comfortable nursery with soft rugs, cozy pillows, and a window that let in warm gold light. The air smelled like clean blankets and eucalyptus leaves. Little toys rested in tidy baskets. A small fan hummed a sleepy song.

Kiki loved the quiet. She hugged her tiny blue blanket and sighed. “Mmm, soft,” she said. She felt safe.

One morning, the nursery buzzed with new plans. Teacher Mia clapped her hands gently. “Friends,” she said, smiling, “today we will build a Thank-You Garden. We will make paper flowers. We will share kind words. We will practice gratitude.”

“Gra-ti-tude?” Kiki asked. She tasted the big word. It sounded like a bubble.

“Gratitude means saying ‘thank you’ for the good things we have,” Teacher Mia said. “It helps our hearts feel calm and strong.”

Kiki nodded. Calm and strong sounded just right.

The friends began to work. Snip-snip went the child-safe scissors. Rustle-rustle went the colored paper. Kiki chose a soft green circle for a leaf and a bright yellow circle for a flower. She liked how the paper felt smooth and cool on her paws.

Just then, Lolo the lemur bounced over. “I want the yellow!” Lolo chirped. He grabbed Kiki’s yellow circle. The paper made a tiny tear sound.

Kiki’s ears drooped. Her chest felt tight, like a small knot. She took a slow breath in and a slow breath out, like Teacher Mia had shown. In… 1… 2… Out… 1… 2…

“Lolo,” Kiki said softly, “that was my flower.”

Lolo’s stripey tail twitched. “I need yellow to make my sun flower,” he said.

Teacher Mia came closer. “Let’s pause,” she said gently. “I see a problem. We can solve it together.”

Kiki pressed her paw to her heart. “I feel sad,” she said. “I worked hard. My yellow is torn.”

Lolo looked at the torn circle. His eyes grew round. “Oh,” he whispered. He rubbed the paper edge with one finger. “I didn’t think.”

Teacher Mia nodded. “Thank you for saying how you feel, Kiki. Thank you for noticing, Lolo. Let’s take three calm breaths.” They breathed together. The fan hummed. The room felt steady again.

“Now,” said Teacher Mia, “how can we fix this?”

Kiki thought. She looked around. The craft shelf gleamed with colors: pink, purple, blue, and more yellow too. She felt a tiny warm glow in her tummy. “Maybe,” she said, “we can share. Maybe we can say thank you for the colors we have.”

Lolo peered at the shelf. “I see more yellow,” he said. “I can use a different circle and say thank you for that. And I can say sorry for grabbing.”

Kiki’s knot loosened. “Thank you for saying sorry,” she said. Her voice was soft, like a blanket. “I can use this torn piece as a petal.”

“Great ideas,” Teacher Mia said. “You used calm breaths. You used kind words. You found a way to share. That is wellness. That is gratitude. That is conflict resolution.”

They each picked new pieces. Kiki glued the torn yellow to a pink flower. It made a funny shape like a little moon. She giggled. “I like it,” she said. The glue smelled sweet, like vanilla.

Soon, paper flowers bloomed along a cork board labeled Thank-You Garden. Each flower had a leaf for kind words. Kiki wrote, with careful letters, “Thank you, blanket, for warm snuggles.” She patted the leaf. “You help me rest.”

Lolo wrote, “Thank you, Kiki, for sharing.” He stuck the leaf near Kiki’s flower. “You helped my heart feel better,” he said.

Kiki smiled. Her chest felt light, like a bubble floating up. “Thank you, Lolo, for saying sorry,” she said. “That helped me too.”

After snack time—crunchy apple slices and cool water—Teacher Mia rang a tiny bell. “Circle time,” she sang. They sat on the soft rug. The bell’s sound was clear like a raindrop.

“Let’s share gratitude,” Teacher Mia said. “What are you thankful for today?”

Kiki held her blue blanket. “I am thankful for soft things,” she said. “They help me feel calm.” She pressed her nose to the blanket. It smelled like sunshine and soap.

Lolo raised his hand. “I am thankful for friends who use gentle words,” he said. He glanced at Kiki and grinned.

Others shared too. “I’m thankful for the fan,” said Muna the mouse. “It makes a sleepy song.” “I’m thankful for glue,” said Pip the penguin. “It sticks things together like hugs.”

Teacher Mia nodded. “When we say thank you, we notice good things. Our bodies feel safe. Our minds feel kind. Gratitude is like a little light inside.”

Kiki felt that little light. It glowed in her belly and warmed her cheeks. She thought about the problem, the breath, the words, and the fix.

At rest time, Kiki curled on her pillow. She whispered, “Thank you, day, for lessons. Thank you, breath, for calm. Thank you, friends, for trying again.” The room was quiet. The fan hummed. The paper flowers rustled softly.

Lolo tiptoed over. He placed a tiny yellow petal next to Kiki’s blanket. “A gift,” he said. “For your moon flower.”

Kiki smiled with closed eyes. “Thank you,” she murmured. Her heart felt big and peaceful.

The Thank-You Garden glowed on the wall, bright and friendly. In the comfortable nursery, small voices learned big kindness. The problem was solved with calm, sharing, and gratitude.

And everyone rested well.

The End.
