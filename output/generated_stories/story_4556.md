# Pippin's Puzzle Problem

High up in a tall oak tree, a cheerful penguin named Pippin lived in the most wonderful treehouse. It had red walls, a yellow roof, and a rope ladder that swayed gently in the breeze. Pippin loved his cozy home, but today he had a big problem.

"Oh no!" Pippin waddled around his treehouse, flapping his little wings. All his favorite toys were scattered everywhere! His wooden blocks were under the table. His colorful puzzle pieces were mixed up in three different boxes. His books were upside down on the shelf.

Pippin's friend Mama Bird had asked him to clean up before she came to visit. But looking at the messy room made his tummy feel wobbly.

"This is too much!" Pippin said, plopping down on the soft rug. "I don't know where to start!"

He took three deep breaths, just like Mama Bird had taught him. *In... out... in... out... in... out.* His flippers felt less shaky now.

"Think, Pippin, think," he said to himself. "What would be the smartest way to do this?"

Pippin looked around carefully. He noticed something important - there were three different types of messes! 

"Aha!" Pippin smiled. "I can sort this into groups!"

First, he decided to pick up all the blocks. They were heavy and made satisfying *click-clack* sounds as he stacked them in their wooden box. The smooth blocks felt cool under his flippers.

Next came the puzzle pieces. These were trickier! Some pieces had straight edges - those belonged to the outside of puzzles. Some had bumpy edges that fit together in the middle.

"The straight pieces go in this box," Pippin said, carefully sorting. "The bumpy pieces go in that box. And the corner pieces - the ones with two straight sides - go in the special box!"

As he worked, Pippin heard a sad chirping sound outside. A baby bird sat on a branch, looking confused.

"What's wrong, little friend?" Pippin called through his window.

"I can't find my way home!" the baby bird peeped. "Everything looks the same up here!"

Pippin felt sorry for the scared little bird. He remembered feeling confused about his messy room too.

"Let me help you think about this," Pippin said kindly. "What does your nest look like?"

"It's... it's brown and round, and it's near some red berries!"

Pippin looked around from his treehouse window. He could see far in every direction. "I can see three nests from here. Let's use our thinking skills! Is your nest in the sunny spot or the shady spot?"

"Shady!" chirped the baby bird.

"Is it high up or low down?"

"Low down!"

"Perfect!" Pippin pointed with his flipper. "There's only one nest that's brown, round, near red berries, in the shade, AND low down. It's right there!"

The baby bird flew home happily, singing "Thank you, Pippin!"

Feeling proud and helpful, Pippin finished organizing his books. He put the big books on the bottom shelf where they wouldn't fall, and the small books on top where he could reach them easily.

Just as he placed the last book, he heard Mama Bird's gentle knock.

"Come in!" Pippin called.

Mama Bird gasped with delight. "Pippin! Your treehouse looks wonderful! How did you clean up so quickly?"

Pippin's chest puffed up with pride. "I used my thinking brain! I sorted everything into groups, and I helped a friend too!"

"That's very smart," Mama Bird said, giving him a warm hug. "When we think step by step and stay calm, even big problems become small ones."

Pippin learned that taking time to think, staying calm, and helping others made everything better - and much more fun too!

The End.
