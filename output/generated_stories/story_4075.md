# Tiko the Turtle Builds a Snack Bridge

Tiko was a shy turtle who lived near a big, cozy kitchen. One day, he peeked into the pantry. The door was open just a tiny bit. Tiko sniffed the air. It smelled like warm bread, sweet apples, and crunchy crackers. His tummy made a small rumble. “Oh,” whispered Tiko, pulling his head into his shell for a moment. “I am a little hungry.”

The pantry was busy. Little Mice Mimi and Max zipped along the floor. Bee Beebo hummed near a jar of honey. Squeaky the Squirrel patted a bag of nuts. Everyone wanted a snack.

Tiko stepped very slowly. His shell rubbed softly on the floor, shh-ssh, shh-ssh. He didn’t like noise. He didn’t like crowds. He liked quiet, gentle places.

Mimi tugged a cracker box. “Move, move! I want the crackers now!”

Max stretched up to a shelf. “I want them first!”

Beebo buzzed, “Honey for me! Buzz-zzz!” He bumped the honey jar, and it wobbled.

Squeaky chattered, “Nuts! Nuts! Me first!”

Tiko’s eyes grew wide. The sounds were loud. The jar shook. The cracker box tipped. The bag of nuts slid, scritch-scratch.

Tiko took a deep breath. He wanted a snack too, but he did not want anyone to get hurt. He felt small and shy, but he also felt caring. He remembered what his Grandma Turtle had told him, in a soft voice: “Respect others, little Tiko. Look, listen, and share space and time. Patience is kind.”

Tiko looked around. “Excuse me,” he whispered. No one heard. He tried again, a little louder. “Excuse me, friends.”

Beebo paused. Mimi and Max stopped tugging. Squeaky froze with a nut in his paws.

Tiko felt his heart thump, thump. He made a choice. He would be brave and kind. “The honey jar is wobbly,” he said. “If it falls, it will break. We can be patient. We can take turns. We can keep things safe.”

Mimi frowned. “But I am hungry now.”

Max nodded fast. “Me too.”

Tiko nodded slowly. “Me too. We are all hungry. We can respect each other. That means we listen and share. We can make a plan.”

Beebo buzzed softer. “A plan?”

Squeaky tilted his head. “What plan?”

Tiko looked at the boxes and jars. He saw a row of soup cans like little drums. He saw flat cracker boxes like bridges. He saw a long wooden spoon like a walkway. “We can build a Snack Bridge,” he said. “We can build a safe path to each shelf. Then we can line up and take turns. No bumps, no breaks.”

Mimi’s eyes sparkled. “Build?” she squeaked.

“Building sounds fun,” said Max.

Beebo buzzed a happy note. “I like bridges!”

Squeaky bounced. “Let’s build!”

Tiko smiled a small smile. “First, we move the wobbly things to the back. That keeps them safe.” He pointed with a tiny toe. “Together?”

They all nodded. Carefully, they slid the honey jar to the back. They pushed the bag of nuts to a steady spot. They placed apples in a neat row. Tiko showed them how to go slow and quiet: “Shh-ssh, shh-ssh,” he said, moving like a soft song.

Next, Tiko said, “We will build the bridge.” He placed two soup cans side by side. Mimi and Max laid a cracker box across like a flat road. Beebo brought a little napkin for a flag. Squeaky held the spoon steady as a ramp.

Tap, tap. Slide, slide. The Snack Bridge grew. It felt good to work together. It sounded like gentle clinks and soft pats. It smelled like apples and a tiny bit of cinnamon.

“Now,” Tiko said, “we line up. We wait. We share. That is respect.”

“Who goes first?” asked Mimi.

Tiko thought again. He made a careful decision. “Let’s let the smallest go first, then the next, and so on. We will each take one snack and say ‘thank you.’”

Beebo buzzed, “I am small!” He zipped to the bridge, then stopped and waited. “May I go first?”

“Yes,” said everyone.

Beebo crossed the bridge and took one little drop of honey on a cracker. “Thank you,” he hummed, licking his lips.

Mimi went next and took one cracker. “Thank you.”

Max followed and took a slice of apple. “Thank you.”

Squeaky took two little nuts. He paused, then put one back. “I will share,” he said. “Thank you.”

Tiko waited at the end. His tummy rumbled again, but he breathed slowly. Patience, he reminded himself. When it was his turn, he took one cracker and one apple slice. “Thank you,” he said, smiling.

They all sat in a small circle on the pantry floor. Crunch, munch, hum, nibble. It was quiet. It was kind. It felt warm and happy.

Mimi looked at Tiko. “You are brave,” she said.

Max nodded. “And smart.”

Beebo buzzed, “Your plan was sweet!”

Squeaky grinned. “You helped us respect each other.”

Tiko tucked his head out, a little taller now. “We all helped,” he said. “We looked. We listened. We waited. We shared. We were safe.”

They placed a tiny sign on the Snack Bridge. Beebo wrote the words in sticky honey letters: “Respect Bridge.”

Every time they went to the pantry after that, they used the bridge. They took turns. They said “please” and “thank you.” They listened. They smiled.

Tiko was still shy, but he felt strong inside. He had learned that respect means giving space, using kind words, and waiting your turn. Patience helps us make good choices. Working together helps us build good things.

And the pantry stayed a happy, safe place for everyone.

The End.
