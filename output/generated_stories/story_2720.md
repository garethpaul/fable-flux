# The Quiet Brush of Benny Bunny

Benny Bunny was a small, determined rabbit with soft gray fur and big, bright eyes. He loved to make art. One sunny morning, he hopped into the creative art room at Sunshine School. The room smelled like sweet glue and fresh paper. Sunlight danced on jars of paint—red, blue, yellow, and green. Brushes stood like tiny trees in a cup. The floor felt cool under Benny’s paws.

“I am ready to make my best painting,” Benny whispered to himself. He wiggled his whiskers and smiled.

Miss Lila, the gentle teacher, clapped her hands softly. “Good morning, artists,” she said. “Today we will paint our feelings. Remember our art room rule: respect. We show respect by using calm voices, sharing tools, and giving each other space.”

Benny nodded. “I can do that,” he said. He felt brave and steady, like a small drum going bum-bum, bum-bum.

Benny found a spot at the table and picked up a brush. “Hello, little brush,” he said. “We will paint a big sky.” He dipped the brush in blue. Swish, swish. The brush sang softly on the paper. Benny felt happy.

Next to Benny sat Turtle Tim, a slow and quiet friend. Tim reached for the blue paint, too. Their paws touched the jar at the same time.

Benny’s ears stood up. “I had it first,” he said quickly. His voice was not calm. He pulled the jar closer.

Tim blinked. “I need blue too,” he whispered.

Benny’s heart thumped faster. “But I want a big sky,” he said. His words came out sharp. The brush made a splat on his paper. Blue dripped down like a tiny waterfall.

Miss Lila looked over. “Artists, what is our rule?” she asked, her voice soft like a feather.

“Respect,” the class said.

Benny felt his cheeks grow warm. He looked at Tim’s slow, careful eyes. He heard the scratch-scratch of crayons and the quiet hum of the lights. He took a small breath in and a small breath out. “I can be calm,” he told himself. “I am a determined rabbit. I can try again.”

Benny put the blue jar in the middle. “We can share,” he said, softer now. “We can take turns.”

Tim smiled. “Thank you,” he said.

Benny watched as Tim dipped his brush. He counted in his mind, “One, two, three,” and then took his turn. Swish, swish. The paint moved like a tiny wave. Benny felt softer inside.

Soon, Foxy Faye bounced over. “I want the big brush!” she said, grabbing the biggest one.

Benny had wanted the big brush for his sunshine. He felt a pinch in his tummy. He rubbed his paws together. He remembered Miss Lila’s words: calm voices, share, give space.

Benny stood tall on his little feet. “Faye,” he said, “I also want the big brush. Can we use it in turns?”

Faye tilted her head. “Hmm. I can use it for my clouds first. Then you can use it for your sun,” she said.

Benny smiled. “Yes,” he said. He waited and used a smaller brush to paint a circle. When it was his turn, the big brush felt soft and heavy in his paw. Swish—big sunshine rays! His paper glowed yellow and warm.

Then, a drip of green paint fell on Benny’s sky. Plop! It made a spot where the blue was smooth. Squirrel Suri gasped. “Oh no! I spilled!”

Benny’s eyes widened. He took a deep breath. In and out. He looked at Suri’s worried face. Her tail trembled.

“It’s okay,” Benny said gently. “Spills happen. We can fix it.” He dabbed a sponge, pat-pat, and wiped the drip. The spot turned into a tiny tree. He added a brown line—trunk! He even painted a little bird with a red dot for a beak.

Suri’s eyes sparkled. “You made it better,” she said.

Miss Lila smiled at them. “Benny, you showed respect to your friends and to your art,” she said. “You used calm words. You shared. You helped.”

Benny felt a warm glow in his chest. He felt proud and tall, even though he was small. “I did it,” he whispered. “I can be respectful.”

When it was time for sharing circle, the room smelled like tempera and oranges from snack time. Everyone held up their art. Tim showed a slow, blue river. Faye showed fluffy, gray clouds. Suri showed a big green forest.

Benny held up his painting. The sky was blue. The sun was bright. The little tree stood where the spill had been. A tiny red bird sang in the branches.

“What do you see?” Miss Lila asked.

“I see sharing,” said Tim.

“I see taking turns,” said Faye.

“I see helping,” said Suri.

Miss Lila nodded. “And I see respect. Respect means we listen, we wait, we share, and we speak with calm hearts. When we respect others, we feel good inside.”

Benny’s ears wiggled. He looked at his friends. “I respect you,” he said. “Thank you for painting with me.”

“And we respect you,” they said back.

Benny hugged his painting to his chest. The paper felt cool and smooth. He felt calm and strong. He learned that respect makes the art room bright and makes hearts feel safe.

On the way out, Benny whispered to the brushes, “Thank you for helping.” He set them gently in the wash cup. Swish. He wiped the table and put the lids back on the paints with a soft click.

Miss Lila gave him a small sticker shaped like a star. “For being respectful and helpful,” she said.

Benny beamed. “I am a determined rabbit,” he said. “I can be calm. I can share. I can help.”

And the art room seemed to smile back, full of color, kindness, and respect.

The End.
