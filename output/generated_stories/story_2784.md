# Rusty’s Gentle Fix

Rusty was a helpful robot dog with shiny metal ears and a soft whirr in his chest. He lived beside a small garden shed. The shed smelled like fresh soil and sweet wood. Inside, tools hung on hooks. A straw hat rested on a nail. Sunlight made warm, golden squares on the floor.

One morning, Rusty wagged his little antenna tail. “Beep-boop! Time to help,” he said in a friendly voice. He stepped into the shed. His metal paws made a tiny tap-tap on the wooden floor.

Grandpa Tom waved. “Good morning, Rusty,” he said. “We need to sort the seed packets. We will learn where each seed belongs.” He smiled and patted Rusty’s head. “Learning is like watering our brains.”

Rusty loved to learn. He sniffed with his sensor nose. “I smell dirt and apples,” Rusty said. “Beep! I want to help!”

On the table, there were packets of seeds: carrots, beans, and sunflowers. There was also a small green book with pictures. “This book tells us how seeds grow,” said Grandpa Tom. “Let’s read.”

Rusty’s eyes glowed bright. He listened as Grandpa Tom read, “Carrot seeds are tiny. They like soft, loose soil. Bean seeds are bigger. They like a little space. Sunflower seeds love sunshine.”

Rusty wagged. “Learning helps us know what to do,” he said.

A sudden squeak came from a corner. “Squeee!” A little mouse peeked out from behind a bucket. The mouse had shiny eyes and tiny gray whiskers. It sniffed the air and shivered.

Rusty tilted his head. “Hello, little friend. Are you okay?” he asked gently.

The mouse squeaked again and darted to the table leg. Then—woops!—it bumped the seed packets. The carrots, beans, and sunflowers tumbled to the floor. Little seeds scattered like rain. Tap-tap-tap!

“Oh dear,” said Grandpa Tom. “Our seeds!”

Rusty’s antenna drooped. The mouse hid behind a broom and squeaked softly. Rusty took a slow, quiet breath. He remembered the book. Learning helps us know what to do.

“I will not chase. I will not shout,” Rusty said softly. “We will solve this with calm and care.”

Rusty walked over to the mouse and sat down, making his gears purr like a sleepy bee. “It’s okay,” he whispered. “You look scared. Are you hungry?”

The mouse peeped out. It nodded just a tiny nod.

Rusty looked at the mess of seeds. He looked at the green book. He had an idea. “Grandpa Tom, may I set out a little snack for our friend? Then we can clean up together.”

Grandpa Tom smiled. “A peaceful plan. Yes.”

Rusty found a small jar lid. He put a few oat flakes in it. He placed the lid near the mouse’s hiding place. “Here you go,” he said.

The mouse stepped out, sniff-sniff, and nibbled. Crunch, crunch. Its whiskers twitched. It stopped shaking.

Rusty wagged. “Thank you for coming out,” he said. “I am sorry if we scared you. Do you feel better now?”

“Squee,” said the mouse, with a happy blink.

“Now,” said Rusty, “let’s learn where each seed goes, and then we can put them back in their packets.” He looked at the book again. “Carrot seeds are tiny. Beans are big. Sunflowers are stripey.”

Grandpa Tom nodded. “Yes. Let’s sort them.”

Rusty and Grandpa Tom gently picked up the seeds. Rusty used his soft magnet paws to gather the sunflower seeds first. Click, click. He placed them in a small bowl. He scooped the tiny carrot seeds with a folded paper, like a little slide. Shhh. He counted, “One, two, three,” in a slow, kind voice. The mouse watched, tail curled around its toes.

“Here are the big bean seeds,” said Grandpa Tom, placing them in another bowl. “We will learn by looking, touching, and listening.”

Rusty tried to scoop too fast once, and a few seeds fell again. Plip, plip. He stopped and took a breath. “Oops. I rushed. I am sorry, seeds,” he said. He slowed down. He remembered: Take your time. Learn as you go.

Soon, all the seeds were sorted and back in their packets. Rusty held up the sunflower packet. “Sunflower seeds love sunshine,” he said proudly. “I learned that.”

The mouse squeaked and tapped the packet with a tiny paw. “Squee!”

Rusty smiled. “Would you like a safe, cozy spot?” he asked. He found a small shoebox, lined it with soft straw, and set it in a quiet corner. He even added a bottle cap of water.

Grandpa Tom nodded. “You solved the problem with learning, kindness, and peace,” he said. “And you said sorry when you rushed. That was brave.”

Rusty’s chest whirred warm. “Learning helps us do better,” he said. “When we understand, we can help.”

The mouse curled up in the straw and blinked sleepy eyes. The shed felt calm. It smelled like clean earth and warm wood. Sunlight made a bright path across the floor.

Rusty wagged his antenna tail. “Beep-boop! Today I learned to look, listen, and be gentle,” he said. “And to make things right with a kind apology.”

Grandpa Tom patted Rusty’s head. “That is the best kind of growing,” he said.

The End.
