# Miko Mouse and the Rainbow Helpers

Miko was a kind little mouse with bright eyes and soft whiskers. Miko loved to ask questions. Miko lived near a sunny school with a creative art room full of colors, paper, brushes, and glue. The room smelled like crayons and lemon soap. The tables felt smooth, and the paint water went swish-swish in cups.

One morning, Miko stood on a tiny stool and peeked into the art room window. “What is that bright box?” Miko asked, nose twitching. Inside, a big box sat on the floor with a sign. The sign said, “Art for Everyone.”

“Hmm,” Miko said. “Art for everyone? How?” Miko’s heart felt bubbly with curiosity.

Miko tiptoed inside. “Hello?” Miko called softly. The room was quiet, but the sun made rainbow dots on the floor. A friendly teacher named Ms. June walked in. She wore a paint-splashed apron.

“Oh! Hello, little friend,” Ms. June said, smiling. “What brings you here?”

“I am curious,” Miko said. “What is the Art for Everyone box?”

Ms. June knelt down. “Great question! We are making art kits for the park. Some children do not have art supplies at home. We want to share. Would you like to help?”

Miko’s whiskers wiggled. “Yes, please! I want to help and learn.”

“Wonderful,” said Ms. June. “We will make kits here, and then we will go outside to the park and give them to families.”

Miko clapped tiny paws. “A daily adventure!”

Ms. June set bowls of crayons on the table. The crayons smelled sweet and waxy. There were soft brushes, safe scissors, and sticky glue sticks.

Miko asked, “Why do we put many colors in one kit?”

“So everyone can choose and make their own ideas,” Ms. June said.

Miko nodded. “Curiosity helps me ask good questions.”

They packed paper, crayons, and brushes into small bags. Miko counted, “One, two, three, four,” and placed a rainbow sticker on each bag. “Stickers make it cheerful,” Miko said.

Soon, children came into the art room. “Hi, Miko!” said Lily. “What are you doing?”

“We are making art kits to share,” Miko said. “Do you want to help?”

“Yes!” said the children together.

They worked as a team. They listened to the soft rip of tape and the gentle click of marker caps. They heard the rustle of paper and felt the cool, smooth glue sticks in their hands. The room buzzed like a happy bee.

Miko kept asking questions. “How many kits do we need?”

“As many as we can make today,” Ms. June said.

“What will we say when we give a kit?” Miko asked.

“We can say, ‘This is for you. Make something beautiful,’” Ms. June said.

Miko practiced, “This is for you. Make something beautiful.” The words felt warm and kind.

When the box was full, Ms. June said, “Let’s go to the park!” Everyone cheered.

Outside, the air smelled like fresh grass and warm dirt. The sun felt gentle on Miko’s fur. Birds sang, “Tweet-tweet,” and leaves whispered, “Shhh-shhh.” The park was busy with families. Children ran and laughed. A breeze tugged at the rainbow stickers.

Miko’s tummy fluttered. “I feel a little shy,” Miko said.

“It is okay,” Ms. June said softly. “When we help others, we can be brave together.”

Miko took a deep breath. “I can do it.”

Miko saw a small boy sitting on a bench with his grandma. The boy was watching the other children. Miko walked over. “Hello,” Miko said. “This is for you. Make something beautiful.”

The boy’s eyes grew wide. “For me?” he asked.

“Yes,” Miko said. “Art is for everyone.”

The boy opened the bag and smiled. “Thank you! I will draw my grandma and me.”

“That is lovely,” Miko said. The grandma smiled and patted the bench. “Thank you, little helper,” she said.

Miko’s heart glowed warm, like sunshine on a red apple.

All afternoon, Miko and the children shared art kits. They saw happy faces. They heard “thank you” and laughter. Some children started drawing in the grass. One child painted a big yellow sun. Another made a blue river with a tiny boat. A girl drew a picture of friends holding hands.

Miko watched with wide, curious eyes. “What made you choose a yellow sun?” Miko asked one child.

“Because today feels bright!” the child said.

“What made you draw a boat?” Miko asked another.

“I want to visit my uncle,” the child said. “He lives across the water.”

Each answer helped Miko learn. Curiosity brought understanding. Every question opened a new door.

When the box was empty, the sky turned peach and pink. Ms. June clapped. “We did it! Thank you, helpers.”

Miko felt tired and happy. The park smelled like evening flowers. The wind felt cool and soft.

Back in the creative art room, Miko sat on the stool. “Today I learned that asking questions helps me understand,” Miko said. “Curiosity helped me help others.”

Ms. June nodded. “Yes. Your questions helped us make better kits. Your kind heart helped our community. And going outside helped us share art in the park.”

Miko smiled. “I will stay curious. I will keep helping.”

The friends cleaned up together. They washed brushes that went swish-swish. They stacked paper in neat piles. They put the “Art for Everyone” sign back on the shelf for tomorrow’s adventure.

Miko waved goodnight. “See you soon,” Miko said. “Let’s make more bright days.”

And the art room glowed softly, like a friendly lantern, ready for new colors, new questions, and new ways to help.

The End.
