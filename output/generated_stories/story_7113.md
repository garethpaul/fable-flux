# Joey Jumps to His Own Drum

In a warm, comfortable nursery, a joyful kangaroo joey named Kip woke up to soft morning light. The curtains were sunny yellow. The rug felt fluffy under Kip’s little feet. He sniffed. “Mmm,” he giggled. “It smells like clean blankets and banana muffins!”

“Good morning, Kip,” said Miss Dot, the gentle teacher. She had a kind smile and jingling bracelets that sounded like tiny bells. “Today, you can try doing some things all by yourself.”

Kip’s ears perked up. His tail gave a happy thump. “By myself?” he asked.

“Yes,” Miss Dot nodded. “You can choose. If you want help, you can say, ‘Help, please.’ If you want space, you can say, ‘No, thank you.’”

Kip felt a warm flutter in his chest. “I can choose,” he whispered. That made him feel big and brave.

First, Kip looked at the shelf. There were bright blocks, soft books, and little drums. He reached for his favorite blue drum. It was smooth and cool. Tap, tap! The sound was soft like raindrops. Then, TAP-TAP! It was loud like a happy thunderclap.

“Would you like me to hold the drum while you play?” asked Tia the turtle, who moved slowly and smiled slowly, too.

Kip thought. He put a paw on his belly. “Let me try by myself first,” he said. “No, thank you, Tia.” His voice was kind. He smiled.

“Okay,” Tia said. “I will watch. If you need help, you can ask.”

Kip felt proud. He made a steady beat. Tap-tap, tap-tap. He hummed a little tune. His tail swished in time. “I’m making my own song,” he said. “It’s my Kip song.”

After music time, it was snack time. The room smelled sweet and toasty. Miss Dot put out banana muffins and apple slices. “Who would like to serve their own snack?” she asked.

Kip’s tummy rumbled. He lifted the tongs. They were a little heavy. He squeezed and picked up one muffin. Plop! It landed on his napkin. He tried for apples. Slip! One slice fell. Kip’s cheeks felt warm. He looked at Miss Dot.

Miss Dot’s voice was calm. “You’re learning,” she said. “Would you like help, or do you want to try again?”

Kip took a deep breath. In and out. “I want to try again,” he said. He held the tongs steady. Squeeze. Lift. Plop! The apple slice landed just right. Kip grinned. “I did it!”

“You made a good choice,” Miss Dot said. “You noticed your feelings. You decided what to do.”

Kip nodded. His chest felt light, like a bubble. He chewed his muffin. It was soft and warm. He crunched his apples, juicy and crisp. “I can do things,” he said.

After snack, it was play time. Kip saw a basket of colorful scarves. Red, blue, green, gold! They felt silky and cool. He wrapped a blue scarf around his shoulders. “I am Super Kip!” he laughed. He hopped, hop, hop, hop, across the rug.

Just then, Benny the bear cub lumbered over. He reached for the blue scarf. Tug!

Kip’s belly flipped. He did not like the tug. His tail tucked close. He remembered Miss Dot’s words. “I can use my voice.”

Kip stood tall. His ears were high. “Stop,” he said, calm and clear. “That is my scarf. I am using it. You can have a turn when I am done.”

Benny blinked. He let go. “I want a turn,” he said softly.

Kip took a breath. “You can use the green one now,” he offered, pointing. “I will share the blue when I’m finished.”

Benny picked up the green scarf. He smiled. “Okay.”

Miss Dot clapped quietly. “Kip, you set a boundary,” she said. “You used strong, kind words.”

Kip felt safe. His body relaxed. He and Benny danced together, swish-swish, swish-swish, the scarves fluttering like little flags.

Later, it was tidy-up time. The bell on Miss Dot’s wrist jingled. “Time to clean,” she sang.

Kip looked at the blocks. There were many. His nose wrinkled. “That looks hard,” he said.

“Would you like a plan?” Miss Dot asked.

Kip thought. He tapped his chin. “Yes, please,” he said. “A plan will help.”

“Good choice,” Miss Dot smiled. “How about: red blocks first, then blue, then green?”

Kip liked that. He started with red. Clack, clack, into the bin. “I can do this,” he told himself. Blue next. Clack, clack. Green last. Clack! When he finished, the shelf looked neat. Kip felt a warm glow. “I did my jobs!”

The nursery smelled like crayons and soap. The light was soft. Kip curled on a beanbag. Miss Dot sat nearby with a storybook.

“Kip,” she said, “how did it feel to do things by yourself today?”

Kip put a paw on his belly. He noticed his feelings. “I feel proud,” he said. “I asked for help when I needed it. I said ‘No, thank you’ when I wanted space. I used my words. I made good choices.”

Miss Dot nodded. “You were independent, and you were kind. That is a strong way to be.”

Kip hugged his blue scarf one more time and smiled at Benny. “I can try new things,” he whispered. “I can listen to my feelings. I can keep my body safe. I can ask for help. I can say ‘Stop.’ I can decide.”

He looked around the comfortable nursery—the sunny curtains, the fluffy rug, the tidy shelf, the friendly faces. His tail gave a happy thump-thump.

“I can do it,” Kip said.

“Yes,” Miss Dot said. “You can.”

The End.
