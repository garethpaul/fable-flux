# Tilly Tortoise Paints a Quiet Rainbow

In a sunny study corner, a wise tortoise named Tilly kept her art box on a little shelf. The shelf smelled a bit like crayons and fresh paper. The window let in warm light that made tiny dust sparkles dance. Tilly smiled and patted her shell. It felt smooth and safe.

“Good morning, brushes,” Tilly said softly. “Good morning, colors.” Her voice was calm and kind.

Today, Tilly had a plan. She wanted to paint a rainbow. She loved art. Art made her heart feel big and bright. She knew art could help her be calm, too. And later, she would move her body to stay strong and happy.

She opened her art box slowly. Squeak. Inside were soft brushes, fat crayons, and little pots of paint. Red. Orange. Yellow. Green. Blue. Purple. They looked like tiny stars in colorful coats.

Tilly touched the paper. It felt smooth and cool. She took a deep breath in through her nose. In. Out. In. Out. “I breathe like the wind,” she whispered. “I see like the sky. I paint with care.”

She dipped a brush into red paint. Drip, drip. “Hello, red,” said Tilly. “You are warm like a tomato.” She made a slow, curvy line. Swish. The brush tickled the paper. Tilly giggled. Her shell shook a little.

Then she dipped into orange. “Hello, orange,” she said. “You smell like sweet oranges in my mind.” Swish, swish. The color glowed like a tiny sunset. She paused to listen. The room was quiet. She could hear the clock go tick-tock, tick-tock. She could hear birds outside say cheep-cheep.

“Listening helps my art,” Tilly said. “I can hear my feelings.”

Tilly took another deep breath. In. Out. “Hello, yellow,” she said next. “You look like sunshine.” She painted a soft yellow stripe. It made her eyes feel warm. She blinked slowly. Blink, blink.

Just then, Tilly noticed her legs felt wiggly. Sitting still for a while made her want to move. “Time to exercise,” she said with a smile. “Little moves help my big art.”

She set her brush down. Plop. She stretched her legs. Stretch, stretch. She lifted her front legs up and down. Up, down, up, down. “March, march,” she sang. Her shell went tap-tap on the floor as she took tiny steps in place. She rolled her shoulders in slow circles. Circle, circle. She wiggled her toes. Wiggle, wiggle.

“Ahh,” she sighed. “My body feels awake. My mind feels quiet.”

Tilly went back to her painting. “Hello, green,” she said. “You are like soft grass.” Swish. She thought about lying in the grass and watching clouds. She smiled. “Hello, blue,” she whispered. “You are a calm sky.” Swish, swish. The blue stripe was cool and smooth. “Hello, purple,” she said finally. “You are grape jelly!” She laughed and made the last stripe. Swish.

The rainbow was almost done, but something felt missing. Tilly looked closely. She smelled the paint—soft and a little sweet. She listened to the tick-tock and the birds. She put her hand on her chest and felt her slow heartbeats. Thump… thump… thump.

“What does my rainbow need?” Tilly asked kindly.

She closed her eyes. She took three slow breaths. In. Out. In. Out. In. Out. She felt quiet. In the quiet, she had an idea. “My rainbow needs a soft, peaceful sky,” she said.

Tilly washed her brush in a cup of water. Swirl, swirl, swirl. The water turned a little purple. “Thank you, brush,” she said. She dipped the brush in light blue and white and made tiny clouds. Dab, dab, dab. The clouds looked fluffy, like cotton candy without the sticky. She added a small sun peeking from the corner. Dot, circle, smile.

Then Tilly added herself, very small, under the rainbow. She painted a tiny tortoise with a tiny shell and a tiny smile. “Hello, tiny me,” Tilly giggled. “You are brave and calm.”

She sat back and looked. Her eyes felt happy. Her shell felt warm in the sun. She could almost taste the sweet oranges she imagined. “I did it,” she said. “I made art with my heart, my body, and my breath.”

Just then, a little breeze came in the window. It made the paper flutter. Fffft. Tilly held the corners and waited. Waiting was part of art. Waiting helped paint dry. She hummed a little tune. Mmm-hmm, mmm-hmm.

She cleaned her brushes carefully. Swish in water. Pat on cloth. “Thank you, tools,” she said. “You helped me make something kind.”

When the paint was dry, Tilly stood up slowly. She did five tiny steps in place. One, two, three, four, five. She reached up tall, then touched her toes. “I can move. I can rest. I can make art,” she said.

Tilly hung the picture on the wall of her study corner. The rainbow seemed to glow. The clouds looked soft. The tiny tortoise smiled back at her. Tilly put her hand on her shell and whispered, “Art helps me feel. Breathing helps me think. Moving helps me grow.”

She looked out the window at the bright day. “Today, I learned a gentle way,” she said. “First, breathe. Then, create. Then, move. I can do it again tomorrow.”

Tilly took one more deep breath. In. Out. She gave a little nod. “I am proud,” she said. “And I am calm.”

The End.
