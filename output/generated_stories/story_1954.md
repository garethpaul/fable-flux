# Pippa Parrot’s Splashy Plan

Pippa was a small, bright-green parrot with sunny-yellow cheeks. She loved to make things. Snip, snip! Glue, glue! Pippa liked to craft and create. One morning, she hopped into the bathroom. Drip, drip, drip went the faucet. The mirror was foggy and cool. The floor mat felt soft and squishy under her toes.

“Hello, bathroom!” Pippa chirped. “Today, I have a big plan.”

She looked at her toothbrush cup. It had fun blue swirls. She looked at the towels. They smelled like lemons. She looked at the window. Sunlight poured in, warm and golden.

Pippa slid the shower curtain with a swish. She saw the tub toys—ducks, boats, and cups. “I can use these to make something,” she said. “I will make a Water Saver Sign! It will help the planet.”

Drip, drip, drip went the faucet again. Pippa tilted her head. “Water is precious,” she said. “We need water for birds, for trees, and for fish. We can play outside and learn from nature. We can save water, too.”

She took a clean cardboard roll from the recycling basket. “This is perfect!” she said. She used a bit of tape to make a round frame. She cut paper gently—snip, snip—and glued on a big blue drop—glue, glue. She added green leaves and a tiny sun. She hummed a happy tune.

“Pippa, what are you doing?” asked a rubber duck with a squeak. Pippa giggled. “I’m making a sign to remind us: Turn off the tap. Then we can go outside and play!”

She stuck the sign near the faucet. It read in big, simple letters: “Turn Off. Go Outside!”

Pippa puffed up with pride. She liked her craft. But the faucet still went drip, drip, drip. Pippa took a deep breath. “I need help,” she said. “I will speak up. I can be brave and kind.”

She called, “Excuse me! Can someone help me with the faucet?”

Mama came in. She smelled like cinnamon toast. “Hi, Pippa,” she said. “What a lovely sign!”

“Thank you, Mama,” said Pippa. “The faucet is dripping. That wastes water. Can we fix it, please?”

Mama smiled. “Yes, Pippa. Thank you for telling me. That is very helpful.” Mama turned the handle tight. The drip stopped. The bathroom got quiet.

Pippa listened. No drip. She felt proud and calm. “I was assertive,” she said softly. “I asked for what I needed.”

Mama nodded. “You were brave and kind. Now, would you like to go outside?”

“Yes!” chirped Pippa. “Let’s go on a nature walk!”

But first, Pippa had one more idea. She gathered the tub cups and an empty soap box. She used a crayon to draw a big green leaf on the box. She poked tiny holes in one cup with Mama’s help. “This will be a rain cup,” she said. “We can use it to water plants slowly. We will not waste water.”

They carried the rain cup and the soap-box leaf outside. Warm air hugged Pippa’s feathers. The sky was bright blue. A soft breeze tickled her cheeks. Birds sang, “Tweet! Tweet!” The grass smelled fresh and sweet.

“Hello, yard!” Pippa chirped. “We are here to explore.”

Pippa and Mama walked the garden path. They felt the bumpy stones under their feet. They saw a line of ants, tiny and busy. They heard the rustle of leaves. “Outdoor time is fun!” Pippa said. “We can move our bodies, breathe fresh air, and learn from nature.”

At the flower bed, Pippa saw a droopy plant. “It looks thirsty,” she said. She filled the rain cup from a small watering can. Drip, drip, drip went the cup—slow and gentle. The soil drank it up. “We are saving water and helping the plant,” Pippa said.

A wind gust came—whoosh!—and her soap-box leaf blew away. “Oh no!” Pippa cried. She felt a sting of tears. Then she remembered. “I can try again,” she said. “I am resilient.” She chased the box, flap-flap, flap-flap, and caught it by the gate. “Got it!” She laughed. “I kept going!”

They picked up a few bits of paper from the sidewalk. “We are taking care of our world,” Pippa said. “This is conservation.” She placed the trash in a bag. She left leaves and sticks for bugs and birds.

On the way back, Pippa saw a neighbor boy with a ball. “Hi,” she said. “We made a water sign and a rain cup. Do you want to try?”

“Yes!” he said. They took turns watering the plants with the slow-drip cup. They counted, “One, two, three,” then switched. They cheered for the plants. They stretched their wings—well, Pippa did—and ran in the grass. They felt strong, happy, and free.

Back in the bathroom, Pippa looked at her sign again. “Turn Off. Go Outside!” She smiled. “We did both today.”

Mama hugged Pippa. “You crafted with care. You spoke up kindly. You kept trying. You helped the earth. And you played outside.”

Pippa puffed her feathers. “Today was a splashy plan,” she said. “I learned: Save water, ask for help, keep trying, and enjoy the outdoors.”

She looked at the faucet. Quiet. She looked at her sign. Bright. She looked at her rain cup. Ready for tomorrow.

“Tomorrow,” Pippa chirped, “we can make a bird feeder from a box, and then go on another nature walk!”

The End.
