# The Guest Room Gallery

In a quiet house with creaky wooden floors, a resourceful squirrel named Nib was visiting. Nib had soft gray fur, bright curious eyes, and tiny paws that loved to make things. He lived in the big oak tree outside, but today he was inside the guest room with permission. The guest room smelled like clean sheets and lemon soap. A gentle lamp made the room feel warm and safe.

Nib looked around. “This room is so calm,” he whispered. The bed had a quilt with blue squares. There was a little desk by the window. A small bin sat under the desk, filled with clean paper scraps and empty cardboard tubes.

Nib’s whiskers twitched. “I have an idea!” he said. “I can make a picture. I can use recycling!” He touched the paper scraps. They felt smooth and crinkly. He listened to the quiet tick-tock of the hallway clock. His heart pitter-pattered with happy excitement.

Nib spread the paper on the desk. He found a glue stick that smelled a bit like sweet berries. He found safe scissors that made a soft snip-snip sound. He picked up a cardboard tube and rolled it. “This can be a tree trunk,” Nib giggled. “And the green paper can be leaves.”

Just then, a voice came from the hallway. It was Mina, the child who lived in the house. “Nib? Are you in the guest room?” she asked gently.

“Yes,” Nib called back. “I am making art. I am using recyclables.” He spoke clearly and kindly, just like Mina’s mom had taught him: use calm words, say what you are doing, and listen.

Mina walked in with soft steps. She smelled like fresh strawberries. “Oh!” she said, eyes wide. “You’re making a picture!”

Nib nodded. “I want to make a forest. But I do not know where to put the big tree. Can you help?”

Mina smiled. “We can plan together.” She sat beside Nib. Their shoulders touched lightly, warm and friendly.

Nib placed a big blue paper sky. “Here?”

Mina tilted her head. “I think the sky looks good. But I feel the tree could be more to the side. What do you think?”

Nib paused. He felt a tiny swirl in his tummy. He liked his idea. But he wanted to practice peaceful words. “I hear your idea,” he said softly. “I like the sky here. I also want the tree to be tall. Maybe we can put the tree to the side and make it very tall?”

Mina nodded. “Yes! That sounds fair. We can do both.”

They worked together. Snip-snip went the scissors. Swish-swish went the glue. Nib added a round, golden paper sun. Mina cut small leaf shapes that smelled faintly like crayons. The window breeze moved the curtains and made the paper flutter like butterflies.

Then Nib saw a shiny button on the desk. It looked like a tiny moon. Nib reached for it.

“Wait,” Mina said. Her voice was calm. “That button belongs to my grandma’s coat.”

Nib’s paws froze. He felt a little hot under his fur. “I am sorry,” he said honestly. “I did not know. I will not use it.” He carefully put the button back. His tummy settled. He felt proud that he had told the truth.

Mina smiled gently. “Thank you for being honest. We can use this bottle cap instead.” She showed a clean, silver cap from the recycling bin.

Nib’s eyes sparkled. “It can be a pond that shines!”

They glued the cap near the bottom of the picture. The cap clicked softly as it touched the paper. The picture looked more alive.

Soon, Mina’s little brother, Jo, peeked in. “Can I help?” he whispered.

Nib waved. “Yes. We are making a forest. We are talking and listening. That helps us agree.”

Jo nodded and tapped his chest. “I feel happy when I help.”

“Thank you for telling us how you feel,” Nib said. “That helps me understand.” He handed Jo a cardboard tube. “Would you like to make a tiny house for a ladybug?”

Jo’s eyes shone. “Yes!” He pressed the tube flat and drew a door with a gentle scratch-scratch pencil sound.

Nib looked at the picture. “I think it is done,” he said softly.

Mina looked too. “I think it is almost done.” She pointed to the side. “I feel the picture needs a title.”

Nib took a deep breath. He remembered peaceful words. “I like your idea. I think the title can be ‘Guest Room Forest.’ What do you think?”

Mina smiled wide. “I agree.” Jo clapped.

They lifted the picture together. It rustled like leaves. They hung it on the wall with a little strip of tape. The lamp light made the pond shine and the leaves glow.

Nib felt warm and light inside. “We listened. We shared. We told the truth. We made art with recycling,” he said.

Mina nodded. “We used peaceful words. We used calm voices. We used kind hearts.”

Jo hugged them both. “We made a gallery!” he cheered.

Nib’s tail swished with joy. “Yes,” he said. “Peaceful talking helps us make beautiful things.”

The End.
