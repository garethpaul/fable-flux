# Tiko Tortoise Listens First

In a sunny school, there was a musical practice room. The room smelled like fresh wood and shiny brass. Drums sat in a row. A piano smiled with white and black teeth. Shakers and little bells waited on a shelf. In the middle sat Tiko, a wise tortoise with a smooth, green shell.

Tiko loved music. He liked the soft tap of his small drum. He liked the gentle hum of the piano keys. He liked the tickle of the triangle’s ring. But most of all, Tiko loved to listen.

One bright morning, friends came to practice. Mei carried a bamboo flute from China. Ayo rolled a djembe drum from Africa. Luna held maracas from Mexico. Sam had a violin tucked under his arm. The room filled with new smells—warm drum skin, sweet wood, and a little bit of rosin from the violin.

“Let’s play!” Sam said, tucking his violin under his chin.

“Me first!” Ayo boomed, tapping his djembe.

“I want to show my song,” Mei whispered, holding her flute close.

“I want to shake, shake, shake!” Luna giggled, maracas ready.

Everyone started at once.

Boom-boom! Squeak-squeak! Tweet-tweet! Shake-shake-shake!

It was loud. It was messy. It was not music. The sounds bumped into each other. The violin squeaked. The flute gasped for air. The drum pushed. The maracas rattled over everything.

Tiko blinked and tucked his head a little into his shell. His ears felt full. His heart felt tight. He saw his friends’ faces. Mei’s eyebrows crunched. Ayo frowned. Luna’s smile fell. Sam’s cheeks turned red.

Tiko took a slow breath. In… and out… He lifted his head and spoke in a calm, warm voice.

“Friends,” Tiko said, “I love your sounds. They are special. But we cannot hear each other. We must listen. We must respect each other’s turn.”

Ayo tapped softly. “Respect? What does that mean?”

“It means we care,” Tiko said. “We look and listen. We let each person have a turn. We share the space and the sound. That is fairness.”

Mei nodded. “I like that.”

Luna hugged her maracas to her chest. “How do we do it?”

Tiko smiled. “We will discover a way. Watch.”

He took out a bright, red scarf. It felt smooth and cool. He held it up. “When I hold the scarf,” Tiko said, “one friend plays. The rest listen. We listen with our ears and our hearts. Then we take turns. We will hear new songs from around the world. We will learn.”

Sam took a breath. “Okay.”

Tiko raised the scarf toward Mei. “Mei, would you like to share first?”

Mei’s eyes shone. “Yes.” She lifted her bamboo flute. The room grew quiet. Mei blew a gentle note. It sounded like a soft breeze moving through leaves. The air tasted like sweet wood. Everyone listened. Ayo leaned in. Sam’s face relaxed. Luna swayed slowly.

When Mei finished, Tiko lowered the scarf. “Thank you, Mei,” he said. “What did we hear?”

“A whisper wind,” Luna said softly.

“A calm river,” Ayo said.

“A soft hello,” Sam added.

Mei smiled. “It is a song my grandma taught me.”

Tiko lifted the scarf toward Ayo. “Ayo, your turn.”

Ayo placed his hands on the djembe. He played dum… da-dum… dum. The drum sounded like footsteps on warm earth. It felt round and deep. The floor hummed under their feet. Everyone listened.

When Ayo finished, Tiko asked, “What did we hear?”

“Footsteps,” Mei said.

“A big heart,” Sam said.

“Booms like thunder far away,” Luna added.

Ayo grinned. “This beat is for gathering. It says, ‘We are together.’”

Next, Tiko lifted the scarf to Luna. “Luna?”

Luna shook her maracas very softly. Chika-chika… chika… The sound was like rain on leaves. It made their shoulders relax. Luna smiled. “This rhythm is my happy walk.”

“What did we hear?” Tiko asked.

“Tiny raindrops,” Mei said.

“Little stars,” Ayo said.

“Happy feet,” Sam said.

Then Tiko lifted the scarf to Sam. “Sam?”

Sam drew his bow. The violin sang a long, sweet note. It sounded like a bird saying good morning. It made the room feel warm, like sunshine. Sam’s eyes were bright.

“What did we hear?” Tiko asked.

“A bird and the sun,” Luna said.

“A bright string path,” Mei added.

“A hello hug,” Ayo said.

Sam laughed. “It is my welcome song.”

Tiko nodded. “You see? We listened. We respected each friend. Now, let’s play together. But we will use fairness. We will take turns to lead. We will leave space for each other. We will watch and listen.”

Tiko tapped a gentle beat on his small drum. Dum… dum… He looked at Mei and raised his eyebrows. Mei added soft flute notes—tweet… tweet… Ayo heard the space and added a light heartbeat—dum… da… Luna sprinkled tiny chika… chika… Sam slid a sweet note across the top.

The music grew like a garden. The sounds fit. They did not bump. They danced.

They played a song of the world—flute wind, drum earth, maraca rain, violin sun, and Tiko’s steady shell-drum. The room felt big and kind. It smelled like warm wood and happy air.

When the song ended, they were quiet. It was a good quiet. The kind that feels like a smile.

Tiko spoke softly. “What did we learn today?”

Mei said, “We learned to listen.”

Ayo said, “We learned respect.”

Luna said, “We learned fairness and turns.”

Sam said, “We learned songs from our homes.”

Tiko nodded. “When we respect others, we can hear the whole world. When we listen first, our music becomes one big song.”

They all put their hands on the drum and said together, “We listen. We share. We care.”

The friends packed up. The maracas made a happy shake. The violin gave one more sweet sigh. The flute whispered good-bye. The drum thumped a soft, final dum.

Tiko smiled and tucked into his shell for a tiny rest. His heart felt light. He had helped his friends make music and find fairness. He had helped them discover the joy of respect.

The End.
