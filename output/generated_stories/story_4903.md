# Bubbles, Bear, and the Big Drip

Baby Bruin, a friendly bear cub with soft, fuzzy fur, padded into the bathroom one morning. The floor tiles felt cool under his paws. He sniffed the soapy air. It smelled like lemons and warm towels. “Bath time!” he cheered, his little ears wiggling.

Mama Bear set a blue towel on the hook. “I’ll get your pajamas, honey,” she said. “Please wait and make good choices.”

Baby Bruin nodded. “I can wait,” he said proudly. He looked at the shiny faucet, the bubbly bottle of soap, and the mirror that showed his round, curious eyes.

Drip. Drip. Drip.

Baby Bruin’s ears perked up. The faucet was dripping. A tiny line of water trickled toward the edge of the tub and plopped onto the floor.

“Oh no,” said Baby Bruin. He felt a flutter in his tummy. “What if the water makes a puddle? What if someone slips?” He took a deep breath, like Mama taught him. “In and out,” he whispered. “I can think. I can try.”

He looked around. “What is the problem?” he asked himself, tapping his chin with a paw. “The faucet is dripping. The water could make a mess. I want to keep the bathroom safe.”

He wiggled his nose. “What can I do?” He remembered Mama’s three steps. “Step one: Stop and breathe. Step two: Look and listen. Step three: Try a safe plan.”

Baby Bruin stopped and took another breath. He looked and listened. Drip. Drip. The knob was shiny and smooth. A small stool stood by the sink. A stack of washcloths sat near the tub. The towel on the hook was big and fluffy.

He made a little plan. “Plan A,” he said softly. “Put a washcloth under the drip.” He pulled a washcloth with careful paws and laid it on the edge of the tub. Plip! The drops landed on the cloth instead of the floor. “Good,” he said. “The water is not running away.”

He smiled and felt brave. “Plan B,” he said. “Try to turn the knob—just a tiny twist.” He pulled the stool close to the tub. The stool made a soft scrape sound on the tile. He climbed up slowly. His claws clicked a little. “Slow and safe,” he reminded himself.

Baby Bruin put one paw on the faucet knob. It felt cool and slippery. His heart thumped. “I am a little scared,” he admitted to himself. “But I can be careful. I can be brave and smart.”

He tried a tiny turn. The knob squeaked. Drip… drip… The drips slowed but did not stop.

“Hm,” he said. “Plan C.” He thought of one more thing. “A rubber band!” He remembered a rubber band in the drawer. He climbed down. He opened the drawer carefully—slide, creak—and found a rubber band and a small wooden spoon.

He giggled. “This is like a tool,” he said. He looped the rubber band around the knob. He put the spoon in the loop to make a little handle. “Now I can turn it slowly.”

He climbed back on the stool. He held the spoon with both paws. He turned—just a tiny bit—slow and steady. Squeeeak.

He listened. The bathroom was quiet. No more drip. He waited. Still quiet. “I did it,” he whispered. His chest felt warm with proud feelings.

Just then, his foot slipped a little on the stool. “Whoops!” He grabbed the tub with his free paw and steadied himself. His heart beat fast, but he took a slow breath. “It’s okay,” he said, patting his chest. “I will climb down now.”

Mama Bear walked in with pajamas. Her eyes got wide. “Oh! The floor is dry. The faucet is quiet. Baby Bruin, what happened?”

Baby Bruin pointed to the washcloth and the spoon. “The faucet went drip, drip,” he explained. “I felt worried. I stopped and breathed. I used my eyes and ears. I made a plan. First, I caught the drips. Then I turned the knob a tiny bit, with a tool I made. I tried to be safe and brave.”

Mama Bear knelt and hugged him. Her fur was warm and comforting. “I am so proud of your thinking,” she said. “You were careful. You used steps. You fixed a problem and kept the bathroom safe.”

Baby Bruin’s tail stubby-wiggled. “I also almost slipped,” he said honestly. “But I held on and went slow.”

“That is resourceful and brave,” Mama Bear said. “Next time, you can also call for help. Working together is smart, too.”

Baby Bruin nodded. “We can be a team,” he said. He took the washcloth to the laundry basket. He put the stool back. He took off the rubber band and spoon and put them away. The bathroom smelled like lemons again. It felt calm and cozy.

Mama Bear turned on the water for bath time. Bubbles rose and popped. “What did you learn today?” she asked.

Baby Bruin dipped a paw into the warm, swishy water. “I learned to stop and breathe,” he said. “I learned to look and listen. I learned to make a plan and try it safely. That is logical thinking! And if I feel scared, I can be brave and ask for help.”

Mama Bear smiled. “Yes,” she said. “Thinking steps help little bears do big things.”

Baby Bruin giggled and splashed. “Big, smart, brave things,” he said. He settled into the soapy bubbles, happy and proud, as the quiet bathroom sparkled.

The End.
