# Milo Monkey Gets Ready

Milo was a courageous monkey with bright eyes and quick little hands. He lived with his family in a small house with a big garage workshop. The workshop smelled like wood and warm rubber. Tools hung on the wall like shiny silver stars. There was a soft hum from a small fan, and sunlight made dust sparkle in the air.

One sunny morning, Milo clapped his hands. “Today I will build a rolling cart!” he said. His tail curled with excitement.

Mama Monkey smiled. “That sounds fun. Remember, great builders prepare,” she said gently. “What do you need?”

Milo hopped to the workbench. “I need wheels! I need wood! I need… um… everything!” He grabbed a big board, a tiny screw, and a blue rope all at once. The board slid. The screw rolled. The rope fell. Clatter, clink, flop!

Milo’s face scrunched. His cheeks felt hot. “This is hard!” he said loudly. His heart thumped fast.

Mama touched his shoulder. “Let’s breathe first. In like you smell a flower.” She sniffed slowly. “Out like you blow a bubble.” She blew softly.

Milo copied her. “In… smell a flower.” He sniffed. “Out… blow a bubble.” He puffed. He felt his chest feel softer, like a pillow.

“Good,” said Mama. “Now, we prepare. We make a plan.”

Milo nodded. “Plan!” He liked that word. It sounded like a clap.

Mama pointed to the pegboard. “First, we gather. Cart needs four wheels, a board for the top, a handle, and safe tools. Let’s make a checklist.”

Milo found a chalk and wrote big, simple words on a little slate:
- Wheels: 4
- Board: 1
- Handle: 1
- Screws: 8
- Screwdriver: 1
- Washers: 8
- Helmet: 1

He read it out loud, tapping each word with his finger. “Wheels: 4.” Tap. “Board: 1.” Tap. “Helmet: 1.” Tap.

“Helmet?” Mama smiled.

Milo patted his head. “Safe heads think clear!” He giggled and put on his bright yellow helmet. It smelled like new plastic and bananas. He liked that.

They moved slowly, step by step. Milo counted the wheels, “One, two, three, four.” He placed them in a blue bin. He measured the board with a strip of soft tape. The tape felt cool. “Perfect,” he said. He found screws and washers and put them in a tiny cup so they wouldn’t roll away.

“Now we line up our tools,” Mama said.

Milo placed the screwdriver and a small wrench on a clean towel. He liked how the towel felt warm from the sun.

“Ready?” Mama asked.

Milo took another flower-bubble breath. “Ready!”

Milo set the first wheel on the board. He reached for a screw—oops! It slid off the table again. Clink! Milo’s tail twitched. “Oh no!”

Mama pointed to the cup. “Preparation helps. Keep the cup close.” She moved it near his hand.

Milo smiled. “Thank you, cup.” He tried again. This time the screw stayed. Twist, twist went the screwdriver. The sound was like a little song: tikk-tikk-tikk. Milo liked the song. His shoulders relaxed.

The second wheel went on. Twist, twist. The third wheel went on. Twist, twist. When he reached for the fourth wheel, the wrench slipped, and a tiny washer fell to the floor, ping! It hid under a stool.

Milo felt a little spark of anger. His mouth made a tight line. He wanted to yell.

Mama knelt down. “What do we do when we feel mad?”

Milo touched his tummy. “We stop. We breathe. We use words.” He nodded. “I feel mad. I can be calm.” He breathed in flower, breathed out bubble.

“Good,” Mama said. “And we accept small slips. Slips happen. We can look for help.”

“I accept,” Milo said softly. He looked around. “I need a magnet stick.”

He checked his checklist. “Do we have a magnet stick?” He drew a little star and added it. Then he opened a drawer and found a long, skinny magnet wand. It felt cool and smooth. He slid it under the stool. Click! The washer snapped to the magnet.

“I found it!” Milo cheered. His anger floated away like a popped bubble. He smiled wide and finished the last wheel. Twist, twist, tikk-tikk.

“Now the handle,” Mama said.

Milo lined up the handle and marked little Xs with chalk. He checked his list again. “Screws? Check. Washers? Check. Calm breath? Check.” He laughed at himself and breathed anyway.

Soon the cart stood on four round wheels. Milo gave it a gentle push. The wheels spun with a soft whirr. He stepped onto the board, holding the handle tight, and rolled a little. Whee! He felt the floor smooth under the wheels. He stopped and checked. “Safe? Helmet on? Clear path?” He moved a loose rope off the floor. “Now safe.”

They took turns rolling across the workshop. Sunlight warmed Milo’s fur. He smelled wood shavings and oil and something sweet—Mama had sliced bananas for a snack.

“You did it,” Mama said. “How do you feel?”

“I feel proud,” Milo said, patting his chest. “Preparation helped me. Calm breaths helped me. Accepting slips helped me. I can handle big feelings.”

Mama hugged him. “Yes. When we prepare, we work smarter and feel safer.”

Milo set the chalkboard on the shelf. “Tomorrow,” he said, “I will prepare to clean the workshop. Step by step!”

Mama laughed. “That sounds like another great plan.”

Milo took one more flower-bubble breath and rolled his cart to the corner, neat and ready. He waved to the tools on the wall. “Good job, team!”

The End.
