# Tully’s Brave Little Shell

In a quiet house, there was a small study corner. It had a soft rug, a little desk, a bright lamp, and a basket full of crayons, paper, and glue. It smelled like pencil shavings and warm paper. This was Tully’s favorite place.

Tully was a shy turtle. His shell was smooth and green, and it made a tiny tap-tap sound when he moved on the wooden floor. He liked to sit in his study corner every morning. First, he would take a deep breath. Then he would open a book, pick a crayon, and make a small plan for the day.

“I like my routine,” Tully whispered. “Read, draw, learn.”

One sunny day, Tully found a big book on the desk. The cover showed brave animals wearing little badges. The book felt heavy in his hands. He stroked the pages and smelled the fresh paper. He wanted to open it. He wanted to learn. But he was shy. What if the book had big, loud words? What if he did not know them?

His heart went thump-thump. Tully pulled his head into his shell. “Maybe later,” he said in a tiny voice.

Just then, a gentle breeze from the window made the pages flutter. Flip, flap, flip! A page stopped at a picture of a tiny turtle wearing a paper star on its shell. The turtle in the picture was smiling.

Tully peeked out. “A turtle like me,” he said. “What does it say?”

He took another deep breath. “I can be brave,” he whispered. “Brave doesn’t mean big. Brave can be small.”

He tapped his shell twice, the way he did when he needed courage. Tap, tap. Then he read the little words under the picture, slowly and softly. “Be… brave… try.” He smiled. He could read them! The words felt like warm tea in his tummy.

“I want a star, too,” Tully said. “I can make one.”

He pulled the craft basket close. It smelled like glue and crayons and a little like oranges from his hand soap. He took yellow paper and soft, safe scissors. Snip, snip. The paper made a gentle sound. He cut one triangle, then two, then three. “Oops,” he giggled. “That’s not a star yet.”

His hands shook a little. The star looked wiggly. Tully felt his cheeks get warm. “What if it looks silly?” he whispered. “What if I can’t do it?”

He closed his eyes and listened. He heard the hum of the lamp. He felt the soft rug under his feet. He smelled the glue stick. He took another deep breath. “Brave means try again,” he told himself. “I can learn.”

He tried again, carefully. Snip, snip, snip. He glued the pieces together. Press, press. This time the star looked more like the picture. Not perfect, but bright and happy.

“Hello, little star,” Tully said. “You are my brave star.”

He stuck the paper star on his shell with a loop of tape. It felt light. He stood up tall. Tap-tap went his feet as he walked in a little circle. He looked in the shiny side of the desk lamp and saw a tiny reflection of himself—a shy turtle with a bright star. He smiled.

Next, Tully opened the big book again. Flip. A page showed a picture of a magnifying glass and a ladybug. The words said, “Look close. Ask why.” Tully read them slowly. His voice sounded stronger now. “Look close. Ask why.”

“I can do that,” he said. He picked up his own little magnifying glass from the desk drawer. He knew where it was because of his routine. “Read, draw, learn,” he whispered. “And look close.”

He looked closely at a crayon. He saw tiny bits of color, bumpy like little hills. He looked at the paper edge. It was not straight. It looked like tiny teeth. “Wow,” said Tully. His eyes felt big with wonder. “Why is it like that?”

He opened his notebook and drew what he saw. He made little dots and little teeth. He wrote one word: “Why?”

Then he heard a sound at the door. Tap. Tap. Tap.

It was Pippa the puppy from next door. She wagged her tail and sniffed. “Hi, Tully!” she barked softly. “What are you doing?”

Tully felt shy again. He pulled his head in a little. “I am… learning,” he said. His voice was small. He wanted to share, but his tummy felt fluttery.

He felt the paper star on his shell. He touched it with his hand. “Brave,” he whispered. Tap, tap went his shell.

“I am looking close,” he said a little louder. “Do you want to see my drawings?”

“Yes!” said Pippa. She stepped on the soft rug and sat. Her fur smelled like sunshine.

Tully showed her the crayon hills and the paper teeth. Pippa tilted her head. “That is cool!” she said. “You are very smart.”

Tully’s cheeks warmed, but this time it felt good. “Thank you,” he said. “I was scared. But I tried. I can learn new things when I am brave.”

Pippa smiled. “Can we craft something?” she asked.

“Yes,” said Tully. “Let’s make brave badges.”

They cut more stars. Snip, snip. They colored them with bright crayons. Scribble, scribble. They glued them. Press, press. They laughed when glue got on their fingers and felt sticky. They made a simple plan and followed it: cut, color, glue. Routine helped them remember each step.

When they were done, Tully gave Pippa a star. “For trying,” he said. “You came in and asked. That is brave.”

Pippa gave Tully a lick on the cheek. “You read and shared. That is brave, too.”

Tully sat back in his study corner. The lamp felt warm. The room was quiet and safe. He looked at his book, his badges, and his notebook full of “why.” He felt proud.

He took one last deep breath. “Brave means I try, even when I feel shy,” he said. “When I try, I learn. When I learn, I grow.”

Pippa wagged her tail. “Let’s be brave again tomorrow.”

Tully nodded. “Yes. Read, draw, learn. Look close. Ask why. Try.”

The End.
