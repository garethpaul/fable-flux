# Nono the Elephant Calf Helps at the Home Office

Every morning, Nono the gentle elephant calf padded softly into the home office on his round, quiet feet. The room smelled like warm paper and lemony cleaner. Sunlight made tiny sparkles on the shiny desk. The computer hummed like a sleepy bee. Tap-tap went the keyboard. Tick-tock sang the clock.

Mama worked at the big desk. “Good morning, Nono,” she said with a smile. “I have calls today.”

Nono lifted his small trunk and waved. “Good morning, Mama. I want to help.”

He looked around. There were pencils, sticky notes, and a little plant with bright green leaves. On the floor sat a basket of soft cords. The printer blinked a yellow light.

Nono’s ears flapped. “The printer is saying, ‘Help, please!’” he whispered.

Mama nodded. “It needs paper. I can fix it after my call,” she said. Her screen showed a meeting about helping the neighborhood. People were talking about sharing a park and keeping it clean. “We are working together,” Mama said. “That is called civic responsibility.”

Nono liked those big words. He wanted to help all by himself. He wanted to be self-reliant.

He took a deep breath. “I can try,” he told himself softly. “Slow and gentle.” He remembered Mama’s rule: “Gentle trunk. Gentle feet.”

Nono peeked inside the printer. It smelled warm and papery. The paper tray was empty. He spotted a stack of paper on the low shelf. He wrapped his trunk around the stack. Oops! Too much. The paper wobbled like a little white tower.

Nono set the stack down. “Try a little,” he whispered. He took five sheets instead. He felt proud. Slip, slide—he guided the paper into the tray with careful wiggles. The printer blinked green. Whirr! It printed one page with a happy sound.

“I did it!” Nono squeaked. His tummy felt fizzy with joy.

Mama looked over and made a heart with her hands. “Thank you, Nono. I am proud of you,” she mouthed, to be quiet and respectful on her call.

Nono looked around for more ways to help. He saw messy pencils. He saw sticky notes stuck on the floor like yellow leaves. He wanted the office to feel calm for Mama and for everyone who called.

He thought, “What can I use? What can I make?” He spotted an empty tissue box. It was soft and colorful. An idea popped like popcorn. Innovation!

“I can make a pencil holder,” Nono whispered. He nudged the tissue box onto its side. He used his trunk to pull out the last tissue—so soft and tickly! He put the pencils in the box, one by one. Tap. Tap. Tap. The pencils stood like little trees.

He found a small cardboard tube from tape. He rolled rubber bands around it, stretch, snap, stretch, snap. “This can hold cords,” he said. He slid the cords through the tube. The cords stayed snug and neat. He smiled.

Suddenly, he heard voices from the computer. Two people were talking fast. “My turn,” said one voice. “No, my turn,” said the other. The voices sounded bumpy, like rocks.

Nono’s ears drooped. He wanted everyone to feel peaceful. He tiptoed closer and softly placed a sticky note near Mama’s hand. He drew a picture: two circles and an arrow going back and forth. Share. Take turns.

Mama saw the picture. She nodded and raised her hand on the call. “Let’s take turns,” she said gently. “You go first, then you.” The voices slowed. “Thank you,” one voice said. “I feel heard,” said the other. Nono’s heart felt warm, like sunlight on his back.

Just then, the little plant leaned toward the window. Its soil looked dry and dusty. Nono remembered not to use too much water. “Gentle trunk,” he told himself. He filled a tiny cup at the sink. Drip, drip, drip. He watered the plant until the soil looked dark and happy. The plant leaves perked up like smiley hands.

Mama finished her call and clapped quietly. “Nono,” she said, “you helped the office and the people. You were self-reliant. You solved problems peacefully. You showed respect by being quiet and kind.”

Nono blushed under his soft gray cheeks. “I did small steps,” he said. “I used what I had. I listened. I took turns.”

Mama nodded. “That is innovation too. You made tools from boxes and tubes. You helped our little office community. That is civic responsibility at home.”

Nono looked around. The pencils were tidy. The cords were safe. The printer was ready. The plant glowed green. The room felt calm, like a quiet song.

He placed a final sticky note on the desk. He drew a big heart and a tiny elephant. “I love helping,” he said.

Mama hugged Nono with her arms and a gentle trunk-to-trunk touch. “When you try first, ask kindly, and think of others, you grow strong inside,” she said.

Nono nodded. His tummy felt steady and bright. He had learned a big little lesson: I can try by myself. I can fix small things. I can help everyone feel calm and cared for.

He took a deep breath. The computer hummed. The clock ticked. The plant smelled like fresh rain. Nono smiled.

“I can do it,” he whispered. “One gentle step at a time.”

The End.
