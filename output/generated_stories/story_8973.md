# Roo's Rhythm in the Garage

Roo was a joyful kangaroo joey with bright eyes and a happy hop. One sunny afternoon, Roo bounced into Grandpa Mick’s garage workshop. The garage smelled like fresh wood and warm metal. Dust danced in the light. Tools hung on the wall like shiny toys.

“Boing, boing! Let’s make music!” Roo chirped.

Grandpa Mick smiled. “Music in the workshop? That sounds fun, Roo. But we must be careful and kind.”

Roo nodded and wriggled his paws. On the workbench were jars of buttons, a small drum, a wooden box, and old cans. There were also colorful ribbons and a little bell.

Roo’s best friend, Mei, walked in. She had a small bamboo flute from home. “Hi, Roo! I brought my dizi,” she said softly. “It makes a gentle sound.”

Roo’s ears perked up. “Let’s play together!” he said.

Mei smiled. “We can learn from each other. We can take turns.”

Roo was very excited. He grabbed the little drum and went, “Boom, boom, boom!” He shook the bell. “Ring-a-ling!” He banged two cans together. “Clang, clang!”

Mei put her dizi to her lips. “Fwee-fwee,” it sang, like a bird in the trees.

But Roo was so loud that he could not hear Mei. He hopped faster and drummed harder. “Boom, boom, BOOM!”

Mei’s eyes looked wet. She lowered her flute. “I can’t hear my song,” she whispered.

Grandpa Mick touched Roo’s shoulder. “Roo, look at Mei’s face.”

Roo looked. Mei’s smile was gone. His tummy felt twisty. “Oh,” he said softly. “Did I make it too loud?”

Mei nodded. “I wanted to share my song from home. It is special.”

Roo’s ears drooped. He took a slow breath. The garage hummed quietly. He could smell the sweet wood again. He could feel the smooth drum in his paws.

“I’m sorry, Mei,” Roo said. “I was too loud. I didn’t listen. I want to be a good friend. Can I try again?”

Mei’s smile came back, small but warm. “Thank you, Roo. Let’s try together.”

Grandpa Mick clapped gently. “How about a plan?” he said. “Plans help friends play fair.”

Roo nodded. “Yes, a plan!”

Grandpa Mick held up three fingers. “One: we listen first. Two: we take turns. Three: we make space for each other’s sounds.”

Roo repeated, “Listen, take turns, make space.”

Mei added, “And we can learn new sounds. My dizi is from my family. It has smooth, windy music.”

Roo’s eyes shone. “I want to learn! What does it feel like?”

Mei held out the flute. “Feel the bamboo,” she said.

Roo touched it with gentle paws. “It’s cool and smooth. Like a leaf after rain.”

Mei put the flute to her lips. “Fwee-fwee.” The sound was soft and sweet. Roo stayed quiet and listened. He could hear the bell ticking on the wall, the whisper of air, and Mei’s pretty notes like a breeze.

When she finished, Roo clapped softly. “That was beautiful,” he said. “Thank you for sharing your music from home.”

Mei beamed. “Now your turn.”

Roo lifted the drum, but this time he tapped it gently. “Boom… boom.” He listened for space. He made room for a “fwee” between each “boom.” He shook the bell only a little. “Ring… ring.”

Mei joined with her dizi. “Fwee… fwee.” Their sounds began to dance. Boom, fwee. Ring, fwee. The old cans became a soft “tink.” The wooden box made a “tok,” like a friendly knock.

Grandpa Mick kept a slow beat with his foot. “Tap, tap,” went his boot on the floor. He grinned. “Now that is a team!”

Roo felt light and happy. He could smell warm oil and hear their music weave together. He could see Mei’s eyes sparkle. His heart said, This is friendship.

They tried a pattern. Roo said, “I go boom-boom.” Mei said, “I go fwee-fwee.” They counted, “One, two, share!” They laughed when they got mixed up, and tried again. Practice felt good. They were building a skill. They were building care.

After the song, Roo looked at Mei and said, “I learned to listen. I learned your song is special. I like your dizi.”

Mei nodded. “And I like your drum. When we share, our music is bigger.”

Roo bounced once, softly. “I’m sorry I did not listen before. Thank you for being my friend.”

“I forgive you,” said Mei. “Friends say sorry. Friends forgive. Friends learn.”

Grandpa Mick handed them colorful ribbons. “Tie these to your instruments,” he said. “Ribbons to remind you: listen, take turns, make space.”

Roo tied a ribbon to the drum. Mei tied one to her dizi. The colors fluttered like little flags of friendship.

They played one more song: boom, fwee, ring, tink, tok. It felt like sunshine and hugs. When they finished, they bumped noses and giggled.

Roo whispered, “We made a new rhythm together.”

Mei whispered back, “We made a friendship song.”

The clear lesson filled the quiet garage: When we listen, say sorry, and share, we make something beautiful together.

The End.
