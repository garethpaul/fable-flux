# Buzzy Colors in the Shed

In the corner of the garden, under a warm sun and a buzzing breeze, there was a little garden shed. It smelled like fresh wood, mint leaves, and a tiny bit like rain. Inside lived brushes, boxes, pots, and bright paint. One morning, a friendly bear cub named Bramble padded up to the shed. His paws were soft and fuzzy, and his nose wiggled with curious sniffs.

“I want to paint a big happy picture,” Bramble said, his eyes shining. “I want to make a rainbow tree!”

He opened the squeaky door. Eeeek. The shed was cozy and dim, with light dancing through tiny dusty windows. The shelves were full of treasures: buttons, string, shiny lids, and jars of paint. Tiny motes of dust twirled in the sunbeams like glitter.

Bramble picked up a brush. It was a little drippy. “Oops!” he giggled as a drop fell on his paw. It felt cool and tickly.

But when he looked around, his smile slipped. The paints were all mixed up. The red was hiding in a blue jar. The blue had no lid. The yellow was tipped over, making a little puddle. Paper was crinkled and torn. The rainbow tree in Bramble’s head felt like it was wobbling.

“I feel… ugh,” Bramble said, pressing his paw to his chest. “My tummy is tight. My eyebrows feel squished. I think I feel frustrated.”

His ears drooped. He sat on a little stool and took a slow, big bear breath in and out. In… and out. “I can name my feeling,” he whispered. “I feel frustrated.”

Outside, a bee buzzed by the window. Bzzzz. It sounded like a tiny song. Bramble listened. He put his paw on his heart. Thump-thump. “Hello, feeling,” he said softly. “You can stay, but I will be kind to you.”

He tried again to paint, but the brush made muddy swirls. “Oh no,” Bramble sighed. “Now I feel sad, too. My chest is heavy. My eyes feel watery. I can say it. I feel sad.”

He blinked and took another slow breath—smell the flowers, blow the bubbles. In… out… “What can I try?” he asked himself. “I can ask for help. I can make a plan.”

Just then, a soft voice floated in. “Bzz-hello!” It was the bee from the window, tiny and bright. “I am Beeta. I like tidy flowers. I like neat paths. I can help.”

Bramble’s ears perked up. “Hi, Beeta! I want to make art, but the shed is messy. The colors are mixed. I feel frustrated and sad.”

Beeta landed on a paint jar and tapped it with her tiny feet. Tap-tap. “Let’s sort. Red, yellow, blue. We can sing a color song. Ready?”

Bramble smiled a little. “Okay.”

They sang as they worked, with gentle hums and happy bzzzes.
“Red for roses, warm and bright,
Yellow for sunshine, morning light,
Blue for sky, so calm and true,
Colors tidy, me and you!”

Bramble’s paws felt busy, and his heart felt lighter. He lined up the jars, clicked the lids, and dabbed each brush clean in a cup of water that went swish-swish. He smoothed fresh paper. He washed his sticky paws in a tiny bowl—splish, splash, drip!

“How do you feel now?” Beeta asked.

Bramble paused and checked inside. He touched his tummy. “My tummy is soft now. My mouth is smiley. I feel calm. I feel proud.”

Beeta buzzed a happy circle. “That’s emotional-awareness,” she said. “You noticed your feelings, named them, and took care of them.”

Bramble nodded. “I did. I listened to my body. I took breaths. I made a plan.”

Now the art could begin. Bramble dipped his brush in yellow. The color looked like warm pancakes in sunshine. Swipe! He painted a big circle for the sun. He added red apples that smelled, in his mind, like sweet crunchy bites. He brushed cool blue for the sky—whoosh, whoosh—like a gentle wind.

He imagined the tree could be rainbow leaves and button blossoms. He glued shiny lids for shimmering fruits. Click, clack, stick! He dotted white for fluffy clouds that felt like soft cotton. He added a tiny bee with golden stripes. “That’s you, Beeta,” he giggled.

“Bzz-yes!” Beeta cheered.

But then—oh!—a drip of blue slid down toward the sun. Plip. “Uh-oh,” Bramble said. “I feel worried. My shoulders got tight.”

He paused and breathed. “Hello, worried. I see you.” He tilted the paper and dabbed the drip with a cloth. Dab, dab. He turned the drip into a playful blue bird with a pointy beak. Chirp!

“I used my imagination,” Bramble said. “I used my problem-solving.”

Beeta clapped her tiny feet. “You turned a mistake into art!”

At last, the rainbow tree was done. Leaves of red, orange, yellow, green, blue, and purple shimmered. The sun smiled. The blue bird sang. The tiny bee buzzed near a flower. The whole shed felt warm and bright.

Bramble put a paw on his heart again. “I feel happy. I feel peaceful. I feel brave for trying.”

He hung the picture on the wall with a gentle tap. Tap. “Thank you, Beeta.”

“Anytime,” Beeta hummed. “Remember: name it, breathe it, plan it.”

Bramble repeated, “Name it, breathe it, plan it.” He wiggled his toes on the wooden floor. He smelled the sweet paint, the mint leaves, and the sunshine. He smiled big.

The lesson glowed like the sun on his painting: Feelings are helpful friends. When we name them, breathe with them, and make a kind plan, we can imagine, make art, and solve little problems.

The End.
