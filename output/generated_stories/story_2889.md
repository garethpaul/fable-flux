# Milo Monkey and the Backyard Build

Milo was a courageous monkey with bright eyes and quick hands. He lived in a sunny backyard full of warm grass, dancing butterflies, and the sweet smell of oranges from a nearby tree. The birds sang, “Tweet-tweet!” and the wind whispered, “Whoosh,” over the soft green leaves.

One morning, Milo stretched his arms and said, “Today, I want to build something amazing!” He wiggled his toes in the grass. “I want to build a rocket ship!”

Milo looked around. He did not have a real rocket. He had no big tools. But he did have a brave heart and a thinking brain. He saw a box of clean recycling by the back door. Inside were cardboard boxes, plastic bottles, paper tubes, and a shiny pie tin.

Milo’s eyes sparkled. “I can use these! I can recycle and build,” he said. He clapped his hands. “I can solve this problem. I can make a rocket from things I already have.”

He pulled the big cardboard box into the sun. The box felt warm and scratchy. He pressed his cheek to it and giggled. “You will be my rocket body,” he whispered to the box.

“Hello, Milo,” chirped a robin from the fence. “What are you doing?”

“I’m building a rocket ship,” said Milo. “But I need wings, a nose, and buttons!”

The robin hopped closer. “Tweet! You can use the pie tin for a shiny nose,” she chirped.

“Great idea!” said Milo. He taped the pie tin to the top of the box. It gleamed like a new moon. “Now I need wings.”

Milo looked and looked. He saw two long, flat cereal boxes in the recycling. He held them up. “These can be wings!” He taped them to the sides. The tape made a sticky “rriiip” sound. The wings wobbled a little.

“Oh no,” said Milo. “They are too wiggly.” He felt a tiny worry in his tummy. “Can I fix this?”

Milo sat on the warm step and took a deep breath. He put his hand on his chest. “I am brave. I can solve problems,” he said softly to himself. “Think, Milo, think.”

He remembered the paper tubes. He slid one tube under each wing and taped them tight. “Press, press, press,” he sang. He tried the wings again. They were strong!

“Yes!” Milo cheered. “I did it!”

“Tweet-tweet!” sang the robin. “Smart thinking!”

Milo still needed buttons. He wanted to pretend he could beep and boop and blast off. He found colorful bottle caps in the bin. Red, blue, and green. He glued them onto the front of the box. “Beep. Boop. Beep,” he said as he pressed them on. He drew little stars with a crayon and stuck a paper towel tube on the side for a telescope.

He climbed into the box and held the sides. The grass tickled his toes. The sun warmed his back. “Five, four, three, two, one… blast off!” he shouted.

He bounced a little. “Boing! Boing!” The box did not fly, but Milo’s imagination did. He looked through his telescope. “I see a purple planet! I see a tiny moon!” He steered with the bottle-cap buttons. “Beep-beep! Turn left!”

Then the wind blew. “Whoooosh!” The cardboard rocket tilted. The cereal box wing started to peel off again.

“Oh no,” said Milo. “Another problem.” He felt his cheeks get hot. He took another breath. “I can do hard things,” he told himself. “I can try again.”

He checked the tape. It was not enough. He looked around. He saw an old scarf on the porch. He also saw two wooden clothespins in a basket. Milo smiled. “I can use the scarf as a strap. I can use the clothespins like little hands!”

He tied the scarf gently around the rocket to hold the wings close. He clipped the clothespins on the scarf and the wings. “Clip! Clip!” The wing stayed on. The rocket felt snug and safe.

Milo climbed back in. “Thank you, wind,” he said. “You helped me make it better.”

“Tweet!” said the robin. “You solved it again!”

Milo grinned. “I did. I am brave and I am smart.” He patted his chest. He felt proud. His heart felt warm, like sunshine inside.

He tried again. “Five, four, three, two, one… blast off!” He bounced and steered, and this time the wings held strong. He flew past pretend stars that sparkled like glitter. He smelled the sweet oranges. He heard bees hum, “Bzzzz.” He felt the soft cardboard under his hands. He laughed. “I am an astronaut monkey!”

After a long adventure, Milo parked his rocket under the shade. He gently took off the scarf and the clothespins. He put the clean pieces back in the recycling box. “I used them, and I can use them again,” he said.

The robin fluffed her feathers. “What did you learn today, Milo?”

Milo looked up at the blue sky. He smiled a big, brave smile. “I learned that when I have a problem, I can stop, breathe, think, and try. I can use what I have. I can recycle and make new things. I can believe in me.”

“Tweet-tweet!” sang the robin. “That is a good lesson.”

Milo nodded. “Yes. I am courageous. I am a problem solver. And I take care of my backyard.”

The sun shined. The wind whispered. Milo gave the rocket a gentle pat. “Tomorrow,” he said, “I will build a boat!”

The End.
