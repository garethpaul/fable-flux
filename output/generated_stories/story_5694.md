# Ellie’s Calm Colors

Ellie was a gentle elephant calf with soft, floppy ears and a tiny, wiggly tail. Today, she stepped into the creative art room. The room smelled like fresh paper and fruity, non-toxic paints. Sunlight made little squares on the floor. Brushes stood like flowers in jars. Ellie’s eyes grew big. “Wow,” she whispered, her voice like a soft trumpet. “So many colors!”

Ms. Star, the art teacher, smiled. “Hello, Ellie! Today we’ll make calm color collages. We’ll listen to our bodies, use our brains, and make art that helps us feel good.”

Ellie’s friends were there too—Milo the mouse, Bella the bird, and Nia the newt. They all wore tiny aprons. Ellie put on a big, blue apron. It felt smooth and cool on her tummy.

“First,” said Ms. Star, “we take a slow breath.” She breathed in like smelling a flower. “In.” Then she blew out like blowing a bubble. “Out.”

Ellie tried. In… the room smelled like fresh oranges from the paint. Out… she could feel her shoulders drop. “That feels nice,” Ellie said, swishing her tail.

“Today’s project,” Ms. Star said, “is to make a calm place picture. Use colors and shapes that help your body feel peaceful.”

Ellie looked at the table. There were papers in soft blues, gentle greens, and sunshine yellows. There were fuzzy pom-poms, smooth buttons, and glitter that sparkled like tiny stars. Her trunk tingled with excitement. She wanted to use everything!

“I will make the biggest picture ever!” Ellie trumpeted softly. She gathered many pieces—too many. Her pile grew tall. It wobbled. It tipped. WHOOSH! The pile slid. Bits of paper fluttered down like leaves. A jar of blue paint wiggled on the edge. Plop! It tipped and made a thick blue puddle on Ellie’s picture.

Ellie’s eyes got watery. “Oh no,” she whispered. “My picture is messy. I don’t feel calm at all.”

Milo squeaked, “It’s okay, Ellie.” But Ellie’s heart thumped fast. Her ears felt hot. She stomped one big, scared stomp, and the brushes rattled.

Ms. Star came over and knelt so her eyes met Ellie’s. “I see a big feeling,” she said softly. “Let’s help your body first. Ready to breathe with me?”

Ellie nodded. Ms. Star held up a painted card with a big star. “Trace the star with your trunk. Breathe in on the long side, and out on the short side.”

Ellie traced slowly. In… out… in… out… Her heartbeat slowed. The room felt safe again. She noticed the cool paint on her toes and the tickle of a paper scrap on her trunk. “I feel better,” she said.

“Wonderful,” said Ms. Star. “Now, let’s solve the art problem together.”

Ellie blinked. “Art problem?”

“Yes,” said Ms. Star. “Problems are chances to think. We can use our steps: Pause, Plan, Try.”

Ellie repeated, “Pause. Plan. Try.”

They looked at the puddle. The blue paint was thick and shiny. Ellie touched it gently with the tip of her trunk. It felt smooth and slippery. “It looks like a pond,” she said.

“That’s a smart idea,” said Nia the newt. “I love ponds!”

Ellie smiled. “Pause. Plan. Try.” She paused to take one more breath. Then she planned: “I will make a calm pond picture. I will use soft colors. I will choose three things at a time so my pile is not too big.”

“Good plan,” said Ms. Star.

Ellie tried. She chose three pieces: a soft green paper, a round smooth button, and a small piece of yellow felt. She pressed the green paper around the blue puddle. She placed the button like a little pond stone. She tapped the yellow felt like a tiny sun.

She looked. “Do I feel calm?” She checked her body. Her shoulders were low. Her tummy was light. “Yes,” Ellie said. “I do.”

Bella chirped, “Maybe some reeds?” She handed Ellie thin, stripey paper. Shhh, shhh, it sounded as Ellie snipped with little safety scissors. She added the reeds in gentle lines.

Ellie worked slowly. She chose three things, then stopped to pause and check. She added a soft cloud with puffy cotton. She added a smooth leaf that smelled like minty paper. The blue pond shone under the sunny window.

“Ellie,” Ms. Star said, “tell us your calm steps.”

Ellie stood tall, her ears flopping. “First, I felt a big feeling. I paused and breathed. Then I made a plan: three things at a time. Then I tried my idea. If I felt wiggly, I paused again. Now my picture helps my body feel good.”

Everyone clapped. Milo squeaked, “I want to try your steps!” Bella chirped, “Me too!”

Ellie smiled. “We can all try. Pause. Plan. Try.”

When it was time to share, Ellie held up her collage. The pond was blue and smooth. The reeds made soft shhh sounds when she touched them. The button stone was cool. “This is my calm pond,” she said. “When I look at it, I remember to breathe.”

Ms. Star nodded. “Beautiful art. Beautiful wellness.”

On the way out, Ellie whispered to her picture, “Thank you for helping me.” She took one more flower breath in, and one bubble breath out. Her body felt peaceful. Her mind felt strong.

Lesson: When big feelings come, we can care for our bodies with slow breaths, use our brains to make a plan, and try one small step at a time. Art can help us feel calm, think clearly, and be kind to ourselves and others.

The End.
