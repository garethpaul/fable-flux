# Fawn's Calm-Down Plan

In a sunny garage workshop, a gentle deer fawn named Fawn stepped carefully on the cool concrete floor. The air smelled like pine wood and orange soap. Sunbeams slid through the small window and made dust dots dance. Tools hung on the wall—hammers, wrenches, and a bright red toolbox that clicked when it closed.

“Tap-tap,” went the little nails in a jar when Fawn nudged them with her nose. She loved helping her friend, Mr. Owl, build small birdhouses. Today they were making one shaped like a little star.

“I am ready!” Fawn said softly. Her hooves made tiny clack sounds. “Can I hold the wood?”

“Of course,” hooted Mr. Owl kindly. “We will measure, mark, and tap, tap, tap. Slow and steady.”

Fawn tried to hold the smooth wood blocks. But the tape measure curled like a funny snake. It tickled her leg and sprang away. “Oh!” Fawn giggled, then wriggled. Her body felt bouncy. She wanted to hop and race.

“Let’s take a wiggle break,” Mr. Owl suggested. “Wiggle, wiggle, shake, shake, deep breath.”

Fawn smiled. “Wiggle break!” She shook her tail, swished her ears, and took a big, slow breath in through her nose. She felt a little calmer, like warm tea inside.

They tried again. Mr. Owl drew a line with a blue pencil. “Right here,” he said. “We will cut along the line.”

Fawn wanted to do it all at once. Her hooves moved fast. The pencil rolled off the table. Clatter! The red toolbox slid. Clack! A jar of screws tipped. Tink-tink-tink! The sounds bounced around the garage.

Fawn’s heart bounced too. “I can’t do it!” she burst out. Her cheeks felt hot. Her chest felt tight. She stamped one hoof. The stamp echoed, boom, in the quiet space.

Mr. Owl blinked his big round eyes. “Sounds like big feelings,” he said gently. “What does your body need?”

Fawn looked down. Her small body felt full of wiggles and worry. “I feel fast,” she whispered. “I need to slow down.”

“That is smart thinking,” Mr. Owl said. “Would you like a calm plan?”

“Yes, please,” said Fawn.

Mr. Owl pointed to a poster on the wall. It showed pictures: a blue cloud, a yellow star, a green leaf, and a red heart.

“This is the Calm-Down Plan,” said Mr. Owl. “Step one: Cloud Breath. Step two: Star Stretches. Step three: Leaf Look. Step four: Heart Words.”

Fawn touched the blue cloud picture. “Cloud Breath,” she repeated. “How?”

“Smell the flower,” Mr. Owl said, holding a tiny wooden flower they had carved. “In through the nose.” Fawn sniffed. The flower smelled like wood and a little like lemon oil. “Blow the cloud,” Mr. Owl said. “Out through the mouth.” Fawn puffed gently. She did it again. In, out. In, out. Her shoulders felt softer.

She reached for the yellow star picture. “Star Stretches.”

“Stretch tall like a star,” said Mr. Owl. Fawn stood with legs wide and lifted her head high. She stretched her neck and legs. “Now wiggle your hooves, then freeze,” Mr. Owl said. Fawn wiggled and froze. Her wiggles felt friendly now.

Fawn tapped the green leaf. “Leaf Look.”

“Find something green and look close,” said Mr. Owl. Fawn found a leaf stuck to the door. She looked and saw tiny lines like little roads. She listened. The garage hummed softly, and a far bird sang, peep-peep. Her breathing slowed. In, out.

Lastly, she touched the red heart. “Heart Words,” she said.

“What do you want to tell me?” asked Mr. Owl.

Fawn took a breath. “I feel rushed,” she said slowly. “I want to go slow. Can you help me?”

“I hear you,” Mr. Owl hooted. “We will go slow together.”

They made a plan. Mr. Owl would hold the wood. Fawn would count and move. “You can be the counter and the mover,” he said. “Counting helps our bodies go steady.”

Fawn nodded. “One… two… three…” Her voice was soft and even. On three, she slid the wood gently to Mr. Owl. He marked the line. “One… two… three…” She passed the blue pencil. “One… two… three…” She pressed the wood blocks together while Mr. Owl tapped, tap, tap. The taps were small and careful. They sounded like rain on leaves.

When the tape measure curled again, Fawn smiled. “Hello, silly snake,” she said. She took a Cloud Breath and waited for “three” before she pulled it straight. Her body felt calm and strong.

Soon the little star birdhouse was ready. It smelled like fresh wood. It felt smooth and warm under Fawn’s nose. Mr. Owl tied a green ribbon on top. “You did it,” he said. “You used movement, breath, and words.”

Fawn’s eyes shone. “I felt big feelings,” she said. “I used my Calm-Down Plan. I asked for help. I went slow.”

Mr. Owl nodded. “That is self-regulation. You noticed your body, you moved to calm, you spoke your needs, and you worked your plan.”

Fawn lifted the birdhouse carefully. Outside the door, the light was golden. A tiny bird chirped, peep-peep, as if saying thank you.

Fawn smiled. “When I feel fast,” she whispered, “I can slow with Cloud Breath, Star Stretches, Leaf Look, and Heart Words.”

“And I can listen,” Mr. Owl said. “We can be a team.”

They hung the star birdhouse by the window. Fawn took one more deep breath. The garage felt cozy and bright. Her heart felt steady, like a gentle drum.

The End.
