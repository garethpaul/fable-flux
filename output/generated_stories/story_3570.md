# Momo’s Big Helper Day

Momo was a small, courageous monkey with bright eyes and a warm smile. He lived in a playful living room full of soft pillows, bouncy rugs, and colorful blocks that went click-clack when they snapped together. Sunshine slid across the floor like golden ribbons, and the air smelled like fresh bananas and clean soap.

One morning, Momo stretched his furry arms and said, “Today, I will try new things by myself. I can be brave. I can be kind. I can help!”

Mama Monkey smiled from the doorway. “You can try, Momo,” she said gently. “I am here if you need me. Ask for help if something is not safe.”

Momo nodded. His heart went thump-thump with courage. He wanted to be independent. He wanted to do things all by himself.

First, Momo looked at a tall stack of storybooks on a shelf. “I will pick a book by myself,” he said. He dragged over a soft stool that made a little squeak on the floor. He climbed slowly. His toes curled on the edge. “Step, step,” he whispered. His tail swished for balance. He reached up, grabbed his favorite red book, and hugged it. “I did it!”

Mama clapped. “Great job, Momo! You were careful and brave.”

Momo grinned. “I can do more.”

Then he saw a basket of clean socks on the couch. The socks smelled warm and cozy, like sunshine and cotton. “I can help. I can fold,” Momo said. He pressed the socks together—squish!—and rolled them into little balls. He made a tiny mountain of socks. “I am a helper,” he whispered. It felt good to help.

Next, Momo found his art box. It rattled with crayons, safety scissors, and glue stick caps that went pop-pop when opened. “I will make a kindness card,” Momo said. “I will volunteer to cheer someone up.” He folded a paper in half. Snip, snip went the safety scissors as he cut a big heart. He colored it with sunny yellows and sky blues. He glued a smiley face in the middle. It smelled sweet and sticky. “For Grandma Monkey,” he said. “She will feel loved.”

As Momo worked, he heard a small sound. “Plink… plink… plink.” A cup of water on the table had tipped over. The water dripped down the table leg and made a little puddle on the rug.

“Oh no,” Momo said. His tummy felt wiggly. “I made a mess.”

Mama looked up from her knitting but did not rush over. She gave Momo a calm smile. “What can you do, Momo?”

Momo took a deep breath. He put one hand on his belly. In… and out. He looked around. He saw a clean towel hanging by the sink. He remembered Mama’s words: “Ask for help if something is not safe.” The cup was safe. The water was safe. He could try.

“I can fix it,” Momo said. He trotted to the kitchen. His feet pattered on the floor. Pat-pat-pat. He tugged the towel down. It felt soft and a little heavy. He carried it back. He laid it on the puddle. “Sip, sip,” the towel drank the water.

Momo wrung the towel in the sink. Twist, twist. Cold water splashed his fingers. It tickled. He giggled. He came back and wiped the table, going round and round in slow circles. The table shone again. “All clean!” he said proudly.

Mama clapped softly. “You solved the problem, Momo. You were independent and careful.”

Momo’s chest felt warm. “I did it by myself! I was brave. I was kind to our home.”

Just then, there came a gentle knock at the door. Tap-tap. It was Mr. Turtle from next door. He wore a tiny hat and looked tired. “Hello, friends,” he said in a soft, slow voice. “I dropped my mail on the step. I can’t bend very well today. Can someone help?”

Momo’s eyes sparkled. “I can volunteer!” he chirped. He skipped to the door. The step felt rough under his feet. He picked up the mail, neat and careful, and brought it inside. “Here you go, Mr. Turtle,” Momo said, handing over the letters.

“Thank you,” Mr. Turtle smiled. “You are very kind.”

Momo felt his courage grow, like a balloon filling with air. He realized something important. “I can try new things by myself,” he said, “and I can help others too.”

To celebrate, Momo finished his kindness card. He added little star stickers that went peel… press! Then he cleaned up his art box, putting each crayon back—red, blue, green—click, click, click. He even put the stool away. Scrape, scrape, then quiet.

Mama gave Momo a gentle hug. “I see a brave and independent monkey,” she said. “You chose safe ways to try things. You cleaned up your spill. You helped Mr. Turtle. You used creativity to make a card. You were kind with your time.”

Momo smiled a big monkey smile. “When I try by myself, I learn. When I need help, I ask. Helping is a good feeling.”

Mama nodded. “That is the lesson, Momo.”

Momo held his red book, his card for Grandma, and his happy heart. The playful living room felt extra bright, and the rugs felt extra bouncy under his feet. The room smelled like crayons and bananas and clean water. Momo whispered, “I am brave. I am kind. I can do it.”

And he could.

The End.
