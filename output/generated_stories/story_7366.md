# Benny Beaver’s Little Plan

In a warm, comfortable nursery with soft rugs and sunny windows, a busy beaver named Benny sat on a cushion. The room smelled like clean wood blocks and fresh crayons. Outside the window, the trees waved in the breeze. Inside, toys made gentle clinks and clacks as friends played.

Benny’s tail thumped when he was excited. Today, he was very excited. Ms. Maple, the kind teacher, clapped her hands and said, “Friends, we have three little jobs before outdoor play: put blocks in the bin, draw a picture, and water the tiny garden on the porch.”

Benny looked at the tall tower he had built. He looked at the crayons. He looked at the little watering can by the door. His tail went thump-thump-thump. “I want to do it all!” he said.

Benny started with the blocks. He scooped some up, but his eyes drifted to the crayons. “Oh! I want to draw a blue river,” he said. He dropped the blocks and reached for a crayon. Then he glanced at the watering can. “And water the garden!” He set the crayon down and hurried to the door.

Blocks on the floor. Crayons on the table. Watering can at the door. Benny’s tail made a wiggly swoosh. His tummy felt twisty.

Ms. Maple knelt beside him. “Benny, your body looks busy. How do you feel?”

“I feel jumbly,” Benny said softly. “I want to do everything, but I’m not finishing anything.”

Ms. Maple smiled. “Thank you for telling me. That is good self-advocacy. Let’s make a little plan.”

“A plan?” Benny repeated. He liked the word. It sounded like a gentle tap: plan.

Ms. Maple took a small paper and drew three simple boxes. She drew a block in the first box, a crayon in the second box, and a tiny flower in the third box. “We will do one job at a time,” she said. “Blocks, then drawing, then watering. When you finish a job, you can put a sticker on the box.”

Benny took a deep breath. He could smell the crayon wax and the sweet scent of the wood blocks. “Okay,” he said. “Blocks first.”

He scooped the red blocks. Clink, clack, clunk into the bin. He scooped the blue blocks. Clink, clack, clunk. His tail thumped a happy beat. His paws moved slowly and carefully. “One block, two blocks,” he counted softly. “Three blocks, four.”

Tessa Turtle rolled by in a toy car. “Play?” she asked.

“Soon,” Benny said kindly. “I’m finishing my blocks.” He picked up the last little block and dropped it in. Clunk!

Ms. Maple handed him a star sticker. It was shiny and gold. Benny pressed it into the first box. “I did it,” he said. His chest felt warm and proud.

“Great completing!” Ms. Maple cheered.

“Now drawing,” Benny said. He sat at the table. He chose a blue crayon. Scritch-scratch went the tip on the paper. He drew a wide river. He added a brown log and a tiny beaver with a big smile. He sniffed the waxy smell and felt the smooth paper under his paw. “Almost done,” he whispered.

Ollie Owl flapped over. “Want to use my glitter?” he hooted.

Benny looked at the glitter. It sparkled like stars. He wanted to sprinkle it, but he looked at his plan. “I will finish my drawing first,” he said. “Then I can add one sprinkle.”

“Okay,” Ollie said, and waited.

Benny colored the last blue wave. Scritch-scratch. He wrote his name: BENNY. He smiled. “Finished!” He added one tiny sparkle on the sun. Just one. Then Benny pressed a sticker into the second box. Two shiny stars now.

“Last job,” Benny said. He picked up the little watering can. It felt cool in his paws. Ms. Maple filled it with a tiny swoosh. “Careful feet,” she reminded.

Benny walked slowly to the porch. The air felt fresh, and he could hear leaves whispering. He tipped the watering can. Splish, splish. Drops kissed the soil. The flowers smelled sweet and earthy.

A gust of wind tugged at Benny’s fur. He hugged the can close and finished each pot. “All done,” he said. His tail made a happy thump.

Back inside, Benny placed the third sticker on the paper. Three shiny stars in a row. He looked at his plan and beamed. “I finished all my jobs!”

Ms. Maple clapped. “You planned your steps, you spoke up when you needed help, and you completed each task. Now it’s time for outdoor play.”

Outside, the sun felt warm on Benny’s back. The grass was tickly under his feet. Tessa Turtle rolled her car to him. “Play now?” she asked.

“Yes!” Benny laughed. “I finished my jobs. Now I can play.”

They ran and rolled and giggled. Benny felt light and calm. He liked how finishing made his heart feel steady.

When the bell chimed, Benny tucked his plan paper in his cubby. He traced the stars with his paw. “A plan helps me finish,” he said. “When I do one thing at a time, I can do many things.”

Ms. Maple nodded. “That’s the lesson, Benny. Plan, speak up, and complete your tasks. Then fun feels even more fun.”

Benny’s tail thumped a happy rhythm. “I can do it,” he said.

And he did.

The End.
