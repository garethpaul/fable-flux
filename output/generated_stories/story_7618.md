# Rooftop Cleanup with Rusty the Robot Dog

On a sunny morning, a helpful robot dog named Rusty blinked on. His blue eyes glowed softly. Beep-beep! His metal tail wagged with a happy ting-ting sound. Rusty lived on a rooftop garden. The rooftop was warm under his paws, and the air smelled like mint and little tomatoes.

Rusty loved to help. He loved to sort, tidy, and keep things in the right place. Today, Rusty heard a worried voice. “Oh no! Where is everything?” It was Mia, the rooftop gardener. She wore a red hat and had a bright smile—except today. Today her smile was hiding.

Mia looked around the rooftop. The watering cans, seeds, and little tools were all mixed up. A soft wind swished. A pot rolled clink-clink. Birds chirped as they flew by. It was a big mess.

Rusty sat and lifted one paw. “Woof-beep! How can I help?” he asked in his gentle robot voice.

Mia sighed. “I cannot find the sunflower seeds. I cannot find the small shovel. I cannot find the blue spray bottle. I need them to water, plant, and spray. But everything is everywhere!”

Rusty nodded. “We have a problem,” he said. “We will find a solution.” His ears clicked as he listened. His nose light blinked as he looked. He took a deep robot breath. “Organization helps. We can make a plan.”

Mia knelt beside him. “A plan?”

“Yes,” Rusty said. “Step one: Talk. We will share what we need.” He wagged. “Communication helps us know.”

Mia smiled a little. “I need the sunflower seeds, the small shovel, and the blue spray bottle.”

“Good,” said Rusty. “Step two: Sort. We will make groups.” He beeped kindly. “Tools here. Seeds here. Water things here.”

They put out three baskets. Rusty read the labels aloud in his soft, steady voice. “Tools. Seeds. Water!” He tapped the baskets so they made a friendly tap-tap. “Step three: Put things in the right basket.”

Mia giggled. “Okay!”

They began. Rusty used his gentle magnet paw to lift metal tools. Click! He placed the big spoon-shaped shovel in the Tools basket. “Tools go with tools,” he said.

Mia found little brown packets. “Seeds!” she said. They made a soft papery swish as she put them in the Seeds basket.

Rusty scooted to a corner and sniffed with his sensor nose. “I smell water,” he said. Drip, drip. He found the blue spray bottle hiding under a towel. “Aha!” He placed it in the Water basket. “Water things go with water things.”

A breeze blew. “Rusty,” Mia said, “what about the rolling pots?”

Rusty thought. His head made a tiny whirr. “We need a home for pots.” He looked around the rooftop. He saw a flat shelf. “Step four: Give everything a home. Shelves for pots. Hooks for tools. A box for seeds. A tray for water things.”

Mia clapped her hands. “Yes!”

Together, they worked. Rusty hung tools on hooks. Clink, clink. Mia lined up the pots on the shelf. Tap, tap. Rusty put seed packets in a clear box. Swish. Mia placed the spray bottle and watering can on a tray. Plunk.

“Step five,” Rusty said with a happy wag, “Make a map!”

“A map?” Mia asked.

Rusty nodded. “A simple map helps us remember.” He brought a small board. He drew with a big blue marker. “Tools—by the tall pole,” he said. “Seeds—in the clear box.” He drew a little picture of seeds. “Water things—on the tray near the mint.”

Mia smiled again. “The map is easy to see,” she said. The marker smelled a little sweet. The board felt smooth under her finger.

Rusty looked around. The rooftop garden now looked neat and calm. The wind hummed. The plants rustled softly. Everything had a home.

Mia’s face was bright. “But Rusty, where are the sunflower seeds?” she asked. “I still need them.”

Rusty checked the Seeds box. He used his knowledge chip. Beep-beep. “Sunflower seeds are small, striped, and crunchy,” he said. “They like to hide.” He listened. He looked. He peeked behind a pot on the shelf. “Aha!” He held up a striped packet. “Found you!”

Mia laughed. “You did it!”

Rusty wagged. “We did it. We used communication to share needs. We used organization to make groups and homes. We used knowledge to search and find.”

Mia opened the packet and poured the seeds into her hand. They made a soft rattle. Together, they planted them in a long, sunny box. Rusty pressed the soil gently with his paw. Pat, pat. Mia used the blue spray bottle. Spritz, spritz. The soil smelled fresh and earthy. Rusty’s tail ting-tinged with joy.

Mia looked at the neat baskets, the hooks, the shelf, and the map. “Rusty,” she said, “I learned something.”

Rusty tilted his head. “What did you learn?”

“When we talk, sort, and give things a home, we can find what we need. Organization makes work easier. It makes me feel calm and proud.”

Rusty nodded. “Yes. A place for everything, and everything in its place.” He tapped the map. “And we can tell others where things go.”

Mia hugged Rusty. His metal fur felt cool and smooth, and his heart light glowed warm. “Thank you, helpful robot dog.”

“Woof-beep! Anytime,” said Rusty.

The sun was bright. The rooftop was tidy. Birds sang. Rusty lay down by the mint and listened to the quiet, happy garden. He felt peaceful. He felt proud, too.

And every day after, Mia and Rusty put things back in their homes. They used their map. They talked to each other. They remembered what they knew. The garden grew tall and strong—just like their good habits.

The lesson: Talk about what you need, sort your things, and give everything a home. Organization helps you find, learn, and feel calm.

The End.
