# Bobo and the Busy Laundry Room

Bobo was a courageous monkey with bright eyes and soft, curly fur. One rainy afternoon, he tiptoed into the laundry room. The room hummed and whirred. “Whirr, whirr,” sang the washing machine. The dryer spun with a warm, toasty smell, like fresh toast and sunshine.

Bobo wiggled his tail. “I will help!” he said. “Helping is brave!”

He saw a big blue basket full of clothes. Socks peeked out like little noses. Shirts hung on a line like colorful flags from around the world. One shirt had a tiny map. Another had stars and moons. “Wow,” Bobo whispered. “These clothes go everywhere!”

Bobo reached for the red soap bottle. “Glug, glug?” he asked. The bottle was heavy and slippery.

Just then, Maya walked in. Maya was Bobo’s friend. She wore green rain boots and smiled kindly. “Hi, Bobo,” she said. “It’s laundry day. We wash, we dry, and we fold.”

Bobo puffed up his chest. “I am brave. I can wash everything fast!”

Maya nodded. “Being brave is good,” she said. “But we must be careful and respectful too. These clothes belong to our family. We take care of them.”

Bobo blinked. “Respect? What does that mean?”

“It means we think about others,” Maya said softly. “We ask before we use. We share the space. We keep things clean for everyone.”

Bobo looked around. He saw a small basket of tiny baby socks. He saw a careful pile of Daddy’s clean shirts. He saw Grandma’s soft scarf with flowers from faraway places. The scarf smelled like lavender. Bobo touched it gently. “So soft,” he whispered.

He wanted to help, but he also wanted to go very fast. He grabbed the red soap bottle again. “Glug, glug, gl—oops!” A big bubble mountain popped out. “Pop! Pop! Pop!” Bubbles bounced on his nose.

“Oh, Bobo,” Maya said. She was not mad. She handed him a small cup. “We only use this much soap. That keeps the water safe and the clothes clean. People all over the world need clean water. We should not waste it.”

Bobo’s ears perked up. “All over the world?”

“Yes,” said Maya. “In many places, washing is hard work. Some people wash by hand. Some carry water far. When we use just enough, we show respect.”

Bobo looked at the bubble mountain. He felt a little wobbly inside. “I didn’t think about that,” he said. “I want to respect others. How do I fix it?”

Maya smiled. “We can scoop out extra bubbles with this bowl,” she said. “We can also wipe the floor so no one slips.”

Bobo took a deep breath. “I can do that!” He scooped and scooped. “Swoosh, swoosh.” He wiped the floor with a soft towel. “Swipe, swipe.” He felt proud and calm.

The machine hummed. “Whirr, whirr.” Bobo listened to the steady sound. He felt the warm air from the dryer on his cheeks. It smelled like lemon and clean rain.

“Now we wait,” Maya said. “Then we share the folding.” She pointed to a little sign on the wall. It had pictures: a hand asking, a hand sharing, a broom, and a smile. “Our laundry room rules,” Maya explained. “Ask. Share. Clean. Be kind.”

Bobo pointed to each picture. “Ask. Share. Clean. Be kind,” he repeated. He liked the way the words felt. They were like steps on a ladder.

When the washer beeped, “Beep! Beep!” Bobo jumped. “It’s time!” he said.

Maya opened the door. Steam puffed out, warm and cuddly. “Careful,” she said. “It’s hot.” Bobo nodded. He waited, then took out a small towel. It was soft and damp. He squeezed it gently. “Squish,” he said with a giggle.

They folded together. Bobo matched socks like friends holding hands. “One, two,” he counted. He lined up tiny baby socks and big fuzzy socks. He folded Daddy’s shirts like neat squares. He patted Grandma’s scarf and set it on top. “Safe and soft,” he said.

The door creaked. Grandma peeked in. “Oh my,” she said. “Look at this tidy place!”

Bobo stood tall. “We asked. We shared. We cleaned. We were kind,” he said proudly.

Grandma smiled. “And you respected everyone’s things,” she said. “Thank you, Bobo. That is brave and wise.”

Bobo’s tail swished. “I learned to think first,” he said. “How much soap? Who needs the space? Whose clothes are these? I can ask and help.”

Maya clapped softly. “That’s critical thinking,” she said. “And good hygiene too. Clean clothes help us feel comfy and healthy.”

Bobo sniffed the lemony air and hugged the clean towel. “Mmm,” he said. “Clean feels happy.”

Grandma handed Bobo a small sticker shaped like a little earth. “For your brave, respectful heart,” she said.

Bobo pressed the sticker on his chest. “I will remember,” he whispered. “Ask. Share. Clean. Be kind. Respect others, everywhere.”

He looked at the laundry room. It wasn’t just a room. It was a place where helping could be careful and kind. It was a place where the whole world mattered, one little scoop of soap at a time.

The End.
