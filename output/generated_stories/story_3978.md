# Tiko the Tortoise and the Treehouse Checklist

High in a friendly old tree, a cozy treehouse creaked and whispered in the breeze. The walls smelled sweet like fresh wood. Sunlight made warm squares on the floor. A wise tortoise named Tiko lived there. His shell was smooth and green like a little hill, and his eyes were kind and sparkly.

Every morning, Tiko stretched his legs slowly. He breathed in and out. “Hello, day,” he said in a soft, steady voice. “Let’s feel good and do good.”

Today, Tiko had three jobs. He tapped them on his little chalkboard list with a tiny twig pointer.
“Job one,” he read. “Drink water and do morning stretches.”
“Job two,” he read. “Water the tree and plant new seeds.”
“Job three,” he read. “Tidy the treehouse and make a calm corner.”

Tiko smiled. “Wellness first,” he said. “When I feel well, I can help well.”

He poured cool water into a blue cup. The cup made a soft clink on the table. “Sip, sip, sip,” he said, and the water felt fresh and smooth on his tongue. He wiggled his toes. He rolled his neck gently. “Stretch, stretch,” he hummed. His shell felt light. His heart felt calm.

Just then, a tiny squirrel named Pip popped up at the window. “Tiko! Let’s play! Let’s bounce and race and hop!” Pip chattered. His tail flick-flick-flicked.

Tiko smiled. “Hello, Pip. I want to play too. First, wellness. Would you like a sip of water?”

Pip tilted his head. “Water before play?”

“Yes,” Tiko said. “Water helps our bodies. Calm helps our hearts.”

Pip nodded and took a sip. “Mmm! Cold and nice!” He giggled.

They went to the little balcony that hugged the treehouse. Leaves whispered sh-sh-sh in the wind. The bark felt rough like a gentle scrub brush. Tiko patted the tree trunk. “Thank you, tree,” he said. “You hold our house.”

He lifted a small watering can. It was green with a painted leaf on the side. “Job two,” he said. “Water the tree and plant new seeds.” He tipped the can. “Pour, pour.” The water made a happy trickle sound. The tree’s roots drank, and the leaves shivered with joy.

Pip leaned close. “Why do we give water to the tree?”

Tiko blinked slowly. “The tree gives us shade and a home. We give it care. That is conservation. We take care of the earth, and the earth takes care of us.”

Pip’s eyes shined. “I want to help!”

Tiko handed him a tiny scoop and a little pouch of seeds. The seeds smelled earthy, like rain and mud pies. “We plant,” Tiko said. “We wait. We care. Good things grow.”

Together, they tucked the seeds into soft, crumbly soil in a wooden planter. Pip patted the dirt with his paws. “Night-night, seeds,” he whispered.

A bluebird landed on the rail and sang a bright, twinkly song. “Tweet-tee-tee!” The sound tickled the air. Tiko listened. “Thank you, bird,” he said. “Music for our hearts.”

Back inside, sunbeams warmed the floor. A tiny broom waited by the door. A basket waited for toys and art. Tiko looked at his chalkboard. “Job three,” he said. “Tidy the treehouse and make a calm corner.”

Pip frowned a little. “Cleaning is not fun,” he said.

Tiko nodded. “Sometimes it feels big. We can make it small. We can make it a game.” He tapped the chalkboard and drew three boxes. “We check, check, check!”

He handed Pip a soft cloth. “You wipe the low places. I’ll sweep the high places.” Pip giggled. “I am small, but quick!”

They sang a tidy song. “Sweep, sweep, sweep, happy feet on the floor. Wipe, wipe, wipe, now the window shines more!” Dust floated and danced in the light, then puffed away like tiny clouds. The room smelled clean and a little like lemons.

They sorted gently. Blocks in the basket. Shells on the shelf. Crayons in a tin with a friendly clink. Pip’s tail swished. “We are fast!”

Tiko took a deep breath. He made a calm corner with a soft blanket, a little plant, and a basket of quiet books. He set a smooth stone there. It felt cool in his hand. “This stone is for slow breaths,” he said. “In and out.”

Pip plopped on the blanket. “Can I try?”

“Of course,” Tiko said. They held the stone together. “In…” They breathed in. “Out…” They breathed out. Their bellies rose and fell like tiny waves. The treehouse seemed to sigh with them.

A little later, the sky turned a brighter blue. “Now we can play,” Tiko said. He drew a check mark for each job. Check, check, check! His chalk made a happy squeak.

Pip beamed. “We did it! My body feels good. My heart feels calm. And the tree is happy!”

Tiko nodded. “Wellness helps us finish our tasks. Finishing our tasks helps our home and our earth.”

They went outside to the balcony. The leaves brushed their cheeks. The bluebird sang again. The seeds slept under the soil, safe and snug. Tiko and Pip played a gentle leaf-catcher game, hopping softly so they wouldn’t bump the planter.

As the sun slid a little across the sky, Tiko smiled. “Today we drank water, stretched, cared for our tree, and cleaned our space. We were kind to our bodies, our hearts, and our home.”

Pip hugged Tiko. “I learned a big thing,” he said. “Wellness first, and then I can do good things.”

Tiko patted Pip’s paw. “Yes,” he said. “Slow and steady, kind and ready.”

The wind sang sh-sh-sh. The tree held them safe. The treehouse felt bright, clean, and calm. And inside, Tiko’s chalkboard shone with three happy checks.

The End.
