# Ruby Rabbit Fixes It Right

Ruby was a small, determined rabbit with bright eyes and soft gray fur. She loved her grandpa’s garage workshop. It smelled like fresh wood and warm oil. Sunlight made long stripes across the floor. Tools hung on hooks like shiny silver fish. Tick, tick, tick went the big clock on the wall.

“Today, I will fix a squeaky wheel,” Ruby said, puffing out her cheeks with a brave little grin. She wore tiny red gloves and a blue apron with a carrot on the pocket.

Grandpa Rabbit smiled. “Take your time, Ruby. Be calm. Do it right.”

Ruby hopped to the corner where a little red wagon rested. The wagon’s wheel squeaked, eee-eee, every time it turned. Ruby wiggled her nose. “I can fix this!”

She brought the wagon to the workbench. Clink, clink, went the wrenches. Tap, tap, went the small hammer. Ruby watched carefully, her ears twitching. “First, I look. Then, I think. Then, I try.”

She turned the wheel. Eee-eee! “Hmm,” she hummed. “It needs new grease.” She reached for the grease can. It felt slippery. The lid smelled like pine and soap.

“I can make it quiet,” Ruby whispered. “I want to do it the right way.”

She dabbed a tiny bit of grease and turned the wheel again. The squeak got softer, eee-ee. “Better,” she said, “but not done.”

Just then, her friend Max Mouse scooted in under the door. “Hi, Ruby! Can we play?” he squeaked.

“Soon,” Ruby said kindly. “I’m fixing Grandpa’s wagon.”

Max’s eyes sparkled. “I know a fast trick! We can paint the wheel. Then no one will hear it.”

Ruby’s ears stood up. She felt a little tug in her tummy. She looked at the paint shelf. The colors were bright and pretty. But Ruby remembered Grandpa’s words: “Take your time. Be calm. Do it right.”

Ruby took a slow breath. In…and out. She felt her shoulders relax. “Max,” she said softly, “painting over a squeak won’t make it truly better. I want to be honest and fix the real problem.”

Max shuffled his tiny feet. “But fast is fun.”

“Fast can be fun,” Ruby agreed. “But being true is best. Integrity means doing the right thing, even when no one is watching, and even when it’s hard.”

Max tilted his head. “In-teg-ri-ty?”

Ruby smiled. “Integrity is telling the truth and fixing things the right way.”

Max nodded slowly. “Okay. I can wait.”

Ruby looked at the wagon again. She loosened the nut. Click. She peeked at the axle. It had a tiny crack. “Ah,” she said. “I found it.”

She hopped to the parts bin. The metal pieces were cool and smooth. She chose a new axle and held it like treasure. “This is the right fix.”

Max whispered, “Do you need help?”

“Yes, please,” Ruby said. “Hold the wheel steady.”

Max pressed his tiny paws on the wheel. Ruby slid out the old axle. Scritch. She put in the new one. Snug. She wiped off the grease with a clean cloth that smelled like lemon. Then she tightened the nut. Click, click.

“Now we test,” she said. Ruby stood tall. She turned the wheel. Silence! No eee-eee. Only a soft whoosh.

Max’s whiskers quivered. “You did it!”

“We did it,” Ruby said. Her heart felt warm and proud. It wasn’t a loud pride. It was a calm, gentle glow that filled her chest.

Grandpa Rabbit came in, wiping sawdust from his fur. “How goes the work?”

Ruby met his eyes. “I found a crack. I changed the axle. I did not paint over it. I fixed it right.”

Grandpa’s smile was big and kind. “That is integrity,” he said. “You were honest, even when a quick trick looked easy. You stayed calm. You accepted help. And you accepted that fixing things takes time.”

Max raised his paw. “Ruby taught me a new word!”

Grandpa laughed softly. “Good. Let’s take the wagon for a ride.”

They rolled the wagon across the floor. Whoosh, whoosh. Smooth as a river stone. The garage felt bright and quiet. Ruby breathed in the warm, woodsy air and let it out slowly. Her whiskers tingled with happy peace.

Max climbed into the wagon, and Ruby pulled. The wheels turned like a song with no squeak. Grandpa cheered, “Hooray for doing it right!”

Ruby stopped and looked at Max. “Thank you for waiting,” she said.

Max smiled. “I accept that good work can be slow. I like slow and true.”

Ruby nodded. “Me too.”

Grandpa patted Ruby’s shoulder. “When we choose integrity, we feel calm inside. We can accept hard jobs and do them with care.”

Ruby’s eyes shone. “I will remember. Look, listen, think, try—and tell the truth.”

They put the wagon back with a soft thunk. The tools hung neatly. The clock went tick, tick, tick. Ruby took off her red gloves and tucked them in her apron.

“Grandpa,” she said, “tomorrow I can fix the loose handle on the toolbox.”

Grandpa winked. “With integrity?”

Ruby giggled. “Always.”

The End.
