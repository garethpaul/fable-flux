# Nibby’s Spin-Cycle Surprise

Nibby was a small, fluffy squirrel with bright eyes and a busy tail. One rainy morning, Nibby scampered into the laundry room to stay dry. The room smelled like warm soap. The floor felt cool under her tiny paws. Machines hummed and buzzed. One washer went whirr-whirr-whirr, like a sleepy song.

Nibby liked to explore. “Hello, big round window,” she said to the washing machine. She tapped the glass. Tap-tap. Inside, socks danced in soapy water. Swish, swish, swish.

On a low shelf, a basket brimmed with clothes. A tiny blue sock peeked out and drooped like a tired ear. “Don’t worry,” said Nibby. “I’m a helper squirrel!”

Nibby tugged the blue sock. Tug, tug. The sock slipped and—plop!—fell under the shelf. “Oh no,” said Nibby. “You are lost.”

She crouched to look. Under the shelf it was dark and dusty. It smelled like lint and a little like lemons. The blue sock lay far back, just out of reach.

Nibby tried to squeeze under. Scritch, scritch. But her fluffy tail got stuck. She wriggled back out with a little sneeze. “Achoo! Dust tickles,” she said.

Nibby took a deep breath. “I can do this. I will try again. Slow and steady.” She flattened her tail and tried another way. She stretched one paw under the shelf. Stretch… stretch… too far. The sock did not move.

Nibby sat down to think. “When one way is hard, we try a new way,” she said softly. She looked around the laundry room. She saw a wooden spoon by a folded towel. She saw a clothespin. She saw a long dryer sheet floating down like a cloud.

Nibby smiled. “I can make a tool!” She tied the dryer sheet to the handle of the spoon. She clipped the clothespin to the end like a little mouth. “Nibby’s Sock Grabber,” she said. “I am a resourceful squirrel!”

She slid her Sock Grabber under the shelf. The clothespin clicked. Click! It caught the blue sock. Nibby pulled. Slow… steady… almost there—snap! The clothespin slipped. The sock flopped deeper into the dark.

Nibby’s whiskers drooped. “Oh,” she whispered. “That didn’t work.” Her heart felt heavy for a moment. She pressed her paws together. “It’s okay to feel sad. I can rest. I can try again.”

Nibby closed her eyes and listened to the washer hum. Whirr, whirr. She took three slow breaths. In… out. In… out. In… out. Her chest felt calm again.

“Patience,” she told herself. “Try with care.” She opened her eyes and saw a mop leaning by the door. The handle was longer. The hook at the end was curvy.

“Hello, Mop,” said Nibby. “Can you help?” She wrapped the dryer sheet around the hook. She made two tight knots. “We will be gentle,” she said to the mop, “and we will not give up.”

Nibby slid the hook under the shelf. She could not see the sock. She had to feel it. She moved slow. She listened for tiny sounds. Scritch… scritch… soft fabric. “I feel you,” she said. “I am here.” She curled the hook, lifted, and pulled so slowly it was like a snail walking.

The blue sock peeked out. “I did it!” cheered Nibby. She pulled more—and the sock popped free into her paws. “You’re safe!” she said, hugging the soft, damp sock. It smelled like lemons and rain.

Just then, a quiet “meow” came from behind the laundry basket. Nibby’s ears perked. A small gray kitten stepped out. Its whiskers trembled. “Meow?”

Nibby tilted her head. “Hello, little one. Are you lost?”

The kitten blinked big green eyes. It pawed at the damp floor. “Me-ow,” it said softly.

Nibby felt warm in her heart. “I will help you too,” she said. “I remember feeling stuck. I can use patience. I can be kind.”

She looked for the kitten’s family. She saw a collar with a tag. The tag made a tiny jingle. Nibby read the letters on the tag. “Dot,” she said. “Your name is Dot.”

Nibby listened. Outside the door, she heard a gentle whistle. “Dot! Dot!” A voice called. It was the neighbor, Mrs. Lee.

Nibby puffed up her tail and tapped the door with the mop handle. Tap-tap-tap. The door opened a little. Mrs. Lee peeked in. “Dot! There you are!” she cried. She scooped up the kitten, who purred like a tiny engine. “Oh my, who helped you?”

Nibby waved her paw. “We did it,” she said shyly.

Mrs. Lee smiled. “Thank you, little helper,” she said. She set a dish of water on the floor. “For your brave work.”

Nibby sipped the cool water. It tasted fresh. She placed the blue sock on top of the basket and patted it. “Back where you belong,” she said.

The washer finished with a happy beep. The room felt warm and clean. Nibby’s heart felt warm too. She had tried, and tried again. She had been gentle with herself. She had helped a friend.

Nibby looked at her mop, her spoon, and the clothespin. “I made tools. I used my brain. I waited and did not give up,” she said. Her tail swished with pride.

She waved goodbye to Dot and Mrs. Lee and scampered to the window. The rain had stopped. Sunlight slipped in and made the bubbles on the washer sparkle like tiny stars.

Nibby whispered, “When things are hard, I can be patient. I can try new ideas. I can help others, like they might help me. That is resilience.”

The End.
