# Otter Ollie Says “I’m Sorry”

In a busy basement full of whirring fans and humming pipes, a playful otter named Ollie lived with a kind family. The basement smelled like warm laundry and fresh wood glue. Sunlight slid in through a small window, making dust sparkle like tiny stars. Shelves held boxes of buttons and ribbon. A big craft table stood in the middle, covered in paper, glue sticks, and sparkly stickers.

Ollie loved to splash in a little tub by the sink. He also loved to craft. Today, the family was making a poster for Grandma’s party. It said, “We Love You!” in bright letters. The poster lay flat on the table. Markers clicked. Scissors snipped. Music played soft and bouncy.

“I want to help!” Ollie chirped, clapping his wet paws.

“Great!” said Mia, the big sister. “Dry your paws first, please.”

Ollie wiggled. He saw shiny buttons in a bowl. He saw glitter that looked like rainbow sand. He saw a roll of tape that went shhh-shhh when pulled. Ollie bounced on his toes. “I can do it!” he said.

“Remember,” said Dad, with a gentle smile, “ask first, then help.”

Ollie nodded fast. But the music felt so fun, and the glitter looked so bright. He forgot. He reached out with damp paws and grabbed the glitter. Fwsssh! Glitter flew into the air like fairy snow. It landed on the poster, the table, and all over the floor. The letters smudged. The paper got soggy. Plop, plop, went the wet drops from Ollie’s paws.

Mia’s eyes grew wide. “Ollie!” she said. “You didn’t ask.”

Dad stopped the music. The basement was quiet, except for the soft hum of the dryer. Ollie’s tummy felt twisty. His cheeks got hot. He looked at the messy poster and felt small.

“I…I just wanted to help,” Ollie whispered.

Mia took a deep breath. “We like your help,” she said, calm but firm. “But you need to ask, and you need to have dry paws.”

Her voice was strong, not mean. Ollie blinked. He could hear his heart thump. He remembered Dad’s words. Ask first, then help. He also remembered something he had learned: When you make a mess, you can say sorry and fix it.

Ollie stood up tall. He put his paw on his chest. “Mia, Dad,” he said, “I’m sorry. I didn’t ask. I made a mess. How can I fix it?”

Dad’s face softened. “Thank you for your clear words, Ollie. That’s a good apology.”

Mia nodded. “Yes. Thank you for saying sorry. Let’s fix this together.”

Ollie took a towel and dried his paws until they felt warm and fuzzy. Swish, swish. He swept glitter into a little pile with a tiny brush. Swish, swish. The sound was soft and sandy. He dabbed the wet spots with paper towels. Dab, dab. He threw away soggy scraps. Soon, the table looked better.

“We can make a new poster,” said Dad. “This time, we will share jobs.”

Mia pointed to the craft boxes. “I will draw the big letters,” she said. “Dad will cut shapes. Ollie, you can place buttons and pass the tape. You can also say ‘Stop, please,’ if someone reaches without asking. That is called being assertive.”

“Assertive,” Ollie repeated. He liked how the word rolled in his mouth. “That means I use strong, kind words.”

“That’s right,” said Dad.

They began. The markers squeaked happily. Snip, snip went the scissors. Tape went shhh-shhh, and the dryer hummed along. Ollie held the bowl of buttons. He felt proud and careful. When his paws itched to grab, he took a breath and asked, “May I place a blue button now?”

“Yes, please,” said Mia, smiling. “Right by the heart.”

Ollie pressed the button down gently. Click. He grinned. He was helping the right way.

Soon, Dad reached for the glitter. Ollie saw his paw hover over the bottle. Ollie’s whiskers twitched. He stood up straight and used his strong, kind voice. “Stop, please. Can we ask first and use a little glitter?”

Dad paused and laughed softly. “Good assertive words, Ollie. Yes, we will use a little.” He asked, “Mia, may I add glitter to the stars?”

“Yes,” Mia said. “A little sprinkle.”

They shook just a tiny bit. Twinkle, twinkle, went the glitter. The letters stayed bright. The hearts looked shiny. The room smelled like glue and crayons. The poster looked beautiful.

When it was done, everyone clapped. Ollie’s chest felt warm and light. He looked at Mia. “I am glad I said sorry,” he said. “I am glad I asked first.”

“I’m glad, too,” Mia said. She gave him a gentle hug. “Saying sorry helps hearts. Asking first keeps things safe. And using strong, kind words helps everyone.”

Ollie nodded. “Today I learned: I can say, ‘I’m sorry,’ I can ask before I help, and I can use my strong, kind voice.”

They carried the poster upstairs together. The basement hummed behind them, tidy and bright with just a little sparkle.

The End.
