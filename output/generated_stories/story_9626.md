# Panda Bao Says "I Can"

The cozy bedroom was warm and soft. A small lamp glowed like a tiny moon. The patchwork quilt felt fluffy, like clouds. A gentle rain tapped the window, tap-tap-tap. It smelled like clean sheets and cinnamon tea.

Panda Bao sat on the rug with big curious eyes. Bao was a patient panda. Bao waited well, listened well, and breathed slow and calm. Today, Bao had a new idea.

“I want to make my bedtime just right,” whispered Bao. “I can ask for what I need.”

Bao’s cozy bedroom had a bookshelf, a box of blocks, a sleepy chair, and a bed with striped pillows. A little clock ticked, tick-tick, tick-tick. Bao’s bamboo toy, Bibi, smiled from the pillow.

Mama Panda peeked in. “Bedtime soon, Bao. Five more minutes,” she said softly.

Bao took a deep breath in, and out. “Mama, I have a small ask,” Bao said, using a steady, kind voice. “I would like a quiet story and the green night-light, please.”

Mama Panda nodded. “Thank you for telling me clearly,” she said. “That was very brave and helpful.”

Bao’s heart felt warm. It was nice to say what was needed. But then the rain went tap-TAP-tap, and the shadows on the wall looked like big tall trees.

Bao felt a little wiggly. The shadows were new and strange. “I can handle this,” Bao told Bibi. “I can try, and if I need help, I can ask.”

Bao had an idea. Initiative! Bao went to the shelf and picked a gentle book with bright stars on the cover. Bao patted the chair. “Mama, can we read here?” Bao asked.

“Yes,” said Mama, sitting down. Bao climbed onto Mama’s lap with Bibi in a hug. The chair felt soft and warm. The pages went swish-swish. The pictures showed shining stars and sleepy animals. Mama read in a low, calm voice. Bao listened and breathed slow. In... out... in... out...

After the story, Mama Panda stood up. “Time for lights out,” she said.

“Wait, Mama,” Bao said, holding up a paw. “I would like the green night-light on. The bright lamp is too bright for my eyes.”

“Good asking,” Mama said, and clicked. The room turned softly green, like a quiet forest. The shadows looked smaller now.

Tick-tick, tick-tick, went the clock. Tap, tap, tap, went the rain.

Bao climbed into bed. The quilt felt snuggly. But the tag on the pillow kept tickling Bao’s cheek. It scratched like a tiny leaf. Bao wriggled, then stopped.

Persistence!

“I will try again,” Bao said to Bibi. “I can fix this.”

Bao turned the pillow. Scratch-scratch on the other side. Bao sat up. “Mama?” Bao called, calm and clear. “The pillow tag is scratchy. May I have a smooth pillowcase, please?”

Mama smiled. “That is a smart request,” she said. She brought a soft pillowcase, smooth like a river stone. Swish, swish. The new pillow felt just right.

Bao lay down again. Breath in... out... in... out...

But the rain went tap-TAP-tap again, and Bao’s ears perked up. A worry bubbled. What if the rain got louder? What if the shadows grew tall again? Bao felt a tiny tight feeling in the belly.

Bao put a paw on the belly. “Hello, feeling,” Bao whispered. “You can be here. I am safe.” Bao looked at the door. It was open just a little, and the hall light made a friendly stripe on the floor.

Bao had one more idea. Initiative again! “Mama,” Bao said, steady and kind, “may I have the door open a little more? I sleep better when I can see the hall.”

“Yes,” Mama said, “and thank you for telling me exactly what helps.” She opened the door a bit wider. The room felt cozy and safe.

Bao’s eyes felt heavy now. The clock sang a soft tick-tick lullaby. The green light glowed like a leaf. The rain was a gentle shhhhh. Bao snuggled Bibi close.

Then, from the shelf, a block wobbled and fell—clack! Bao’s eyes popped open. Bao waited. One breath. Two breaths. Three breaths.

“I can handle this,” Bao said, patient and brave. “I will check with my words.” Bao called, “Mama? I heard a block. I am okay, but can you please help me put it back?”

Mama came in quietly. “You used your words so well,” she said. “You asked for help in a calm, strong way.” She picked up the block and set it gently in the box. Clack, then silence.

Bao felt proud. Bao had been assertive—asking for needs with calm words. Bao had used persistence—trying again and again. Bao had used initiative—making good choices to feel safe.

“I did it,” Bao whispered to Bibi. “I can say what I need.”

Mama kissed Bao’s head. “Yes, you can,” she said. “When you speak up kindly, you help yourself and others.”

Bao smiled a sleepy smile. The bed felt perfect now. The night felt friendly. The cozy bedroom held the quiet like a soft blanket. Bao closed those bright panda eyes.

“I can,” Bao whispered, and drifted into sweet dreams.

The End.
