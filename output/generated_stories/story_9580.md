# Sprig the Squirrel Helps in the Guest Room

Sprig was a small, resourceful squirrel with bright eyes and a fluffy tail. One sunny morning, Sprig peeked into the guest room at the edge of the garden. The window was open, and a soft breeze made the curtains sway like slow, white waves.

“Hello?” Sprig chittered, stepping onto the thick, blue rug. The rug felt soft under his paws, like clouds. He sniffed the air. It smelled like clean sheets and a little bit like lavender.

On the bed sat a tiny field mouse named Millie. Millie rubbed her eyes and sniffled. “I’m scared,” she whispered. “This room is big, and I feel little.”

Sprig tilted his head. “I hear you,” he said gently. “You feel scared because the room is new. New things can feel big.”

Millie nodded. “I’m visiting my aunt. She is not home yet. I have to wait in the guest room. Everything is different.”

Sprig sat beside her and listened. The quilt was cool and smooth. He could hear the clock—tick, tock, tick, tock. He could see dust dancing in the sunlight. “Thank you for telling me, Millie,” Sprig said. “When we share feelings, it helps. I want to help you feel safe.”

“How?” Millie asked, rubbing her little paws together.

Sprig took a deep breath. He liked solving problems. He liked helping others too. “Let’s make a plan,” he said. “Three small goals! Goal one: make the room feel friendly. Goal two: make waiting feel fun. Goal three: help someone else while we wait.”

Millie’s ears perked up. “That sounds good.”

Sprig clapped his paws. “Goal one! Friendly room.” He looked around. “What helps you feel cozy?”

Millie sniffed. “Soft things. Warm lights. A kind voice.”

“I can do that,” Sprig said. He hopped to the bedside lamp and clicked it on. The light glowed warm and golden. He pulled a small, striped pillow close to Millie. “Here’s a cuddle pillow.”

Millie hugged the pillow. “It’s soft.”

Sprig found a tiny pinecone in his satchel. “This is Piney. He is very brave,” he whispered. “He can sit with you.”

Millie smiled a little. “Hello, Piney.”

Sprig watched her face and listened to her breaths. They were slower now. “How do you feel?” he asked.

“A bit better,” Millie said.

“Good,” Sprig said. “Goal two! Fun waiting.” He looked at the desk. There were crayons and paper. He sniffed the crayons. They smelled like sweet wax. “Let’s draw our feelings,” he suggested. “I will draw my brave tail. You can draw your brave heart.”

Millie’s eyes lit up. “Okay.” She chose a red crayon. Scritch, scritch, went the crayons across the paper. Sprig drew a big tail with sparkly lines. Millie drew a heart with a smile in the middle.

“Tell me about your picture,” Sprig said.

“My heart is small, but it tries,” Millie said softly. “It likes kind words.”

Sprig nodded. “That is beautiful. Your heart is strong.” He felt warm inside. He could see Millie’s shoulders relax.

They laughed when Sprig’s tail drawing got extra fluffy. “Oops! My tail is a cloud!” Sprig giggled. Millie giggled too. The room did not feel so big now.

“Goal three,” Sprig said. “Help someone else while we wait. Volunteering means we help with no reward, just kindness.” He looked around the guest room. There were books on a shelf, some leaning. The quilt had a wrinkle. A plant on the windowsill looked thirsty.

“We can help the room,” Millie said, sitting up taller.

“Yes!” said Sprig. “I will fix the books. You can smooth the quilt. Then we’ll give the plant a little sip.”

Together, they worked. Sprig straightened the books—tap, tap, tap—so they stood tall like a tiny forest. Millie pressed her paws across the quilt—swish, swish—until it was smooth and neat. Sprig poured a tiny bit of water into the plant’s soil. Glug, glug. The leaves perked up as if they said thank you.

“How do you feel now?” Sprig asked.

“I feel helpful,” Millie said. “Helping made me feel brave.”

Sprig’s heart felt big and warm. “When we help others, we also help ourselves,” he said. “That is empathy—listening, caring, and doing kind things.”

Footsteps sounded in the hall. Tap, tap. Millie’s ears twitched. “Is that my aunt?” she asked.

The door opened, and Auntie Marn the mouse peeked in. “Millie! There you are.” Her voice was soft as a blanket. “Thank you for waiting.”

Millie ran into her aunt’s arms. “Sprig helped me,” she said. “We made a plan. We made the room friendly. We made waiting fun. We helped the room, too.”

Auntie Marn smiled at Sprig. “Thank you for being so kind.”

Sprig felt shy and happy. “We did it together,” he said. “Millie was very brave.”

Auntie Marn looked around. “The room looks lovely,” she said. “I can feel the kindness in here.”

Sprig looked at Millie. “Remember our goals? You did all three,” he said.

Millie nodded. “And I learned empathy,” she said proudly. “I listened to my feelings, and you listened to me. We helped each other.”

Sprig touched his paw to his chest. “That is the lesson,” he said. “When someone feels scared, we listen. We make a plan. We help. That is empathy. That is being a good friend.”

The breeze moved the curtains again. The room was warm and bright. Millie waved Piney the pinecone in the air. “Thank you, Sprig,” she said.

“You’re welcome,” Sprig chittered. “If you ever need me, I’ll be right here—ready to help.”

The End.
