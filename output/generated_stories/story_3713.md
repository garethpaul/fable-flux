# Ozzie Otter’s Splattery Day

Ozzie the playful otter loved the creative art room. It smelled like fresh paper and lemony soap. Sunlight made bright squares on the floor. Paints in little cups lined the table like a rainbow: red, orange, yellow, green, blue, purple. Brushes stood up like tiny trees. Soft music hummed from a speaker, like a gentle wave.

“Today we paint shapes!” said Ms. Lark, the art teacher with a smile. She wore a blue apron with white dots. “First, we put on aprons. Then we listen. Then we paint. One step at a time.”

Ozzie wiggled his whiskers. He tugged on a green apron and tied it in a big loopy bow. His paws felt tingly with excitement. He bounced on his toes. “I want to make a big blue circle!” he chirped.

“Me too!” said Tia, a tiny turtle with a purple shell.

“I like stars,” said Benji, a fuzzy bunny with long ears.

Ms. Lark held up a big paper with three shapes. “We will paint a circle, a square, and a triangle. First, we trace. Then we paint. Remember: slow and gentle strokes.”

Ozzie grabbed a brush. Swish, swish! He dipped it into the blue cup. Plop! Blue drops splashed onto his paw. “Oops,” Ozzie giggled.

He pressed the brush to the paper. The brush slid too fast. The circle went lumpy. It looked like a blueberry cloud. Ozzie’s smile wobbled. “It’s not a circle,” he whispered.

He tried again. Swish! The brush made a big splat. Blue dots sprinkled his nose. “It’s messy,” he said. His chest felt tight like a knot. His whiskers drooped. He scrunched his little paws. “I can’t do it!”

Ms. Lark came closer, her voice calm and warm. “I see you’re feeling frustrated, Ozzie.”

Ozzie crossed his paws and looked down. “My circle keeps getting bumpy. I want it to be round, but it won’t listen.”

Tia the turtle scooted next to him. “Sometimes I press too hard,” she said softly. “Then I get bumpy lines too.”

Benji wiggled an ear. “When I feel grumpy, I take a tiny break. Like a tiny hop back.”

Ms. Lark nodded. “Great ideas. Ozzie, let’s try our calm plan. Step one: pause. Step two: deep breath. Step three: ask for help or try again slowly.”

Ozzie blinked. He set his brush down. He put a paw on his belly. In… and out. In… and out. The music hummed. The room smelled like paper and paint. The knot in his chest got smaller.

“Can you show me how to make it round?” he asked.

“Of course,” said Ms. Lark. She placed a round lid on the paper. “First, trace with a finger.” She guided Ozzie’s paw around the lid. “Feel the curve. Slow and steady.”

Ozzie traced again by himself. The lid felt cool and smooth under his paw. “Round,” he whispered. His whiskers lifted.

“Now, hold the brush like this,” Ms. Lark said, moving his paw gently. “Soft strokes. Follow the line.”

Ozzie dipped the brush. Swish… smooth. The blue line hugged the circle. No bumps! He grinned. “I did it!”

Tia smiled. “Nice circle, Ozzie.”

Benji clapped his paws quietly. “Round and neat!”

Ms. Lark pointed to the next shape. “Square time. Four sides. Remember our steps.”

Ozzie nodded. “Pause. Breathe. Slow.” He whispered the words like a song. He traced with his finger. Then he painted. The square looked sturdy, like a little window.

For the triangle, Ozzie felt his paw wobble again. His heart fluttered fast. “Oh no,” he said. A tiny line wiggled out of place.

He stopped. He took two slow breaths. “Ms. Lark, can I try again with a lighter touch?” he asked.

“Yes,” she said. “That is excellent communication.”

Tia scooted closer. “Want to use my triangle ruler?” she asked. “We can share.”

“Thank you,” said Ozzie. He guided his brush along the ruler’s edge. The line came out straight. “We did it together!”

Soon each child had three bright shapes. Ms. Lark brought out stickers and cotton balls. “You can turn your shapes into anything,” she said. “Follow the directions for glue: dot, dot, not a lot.”

Ozzie laughed. “Dot, dot, not a lot!” He tapped the glue bottle gently. He added cotton clouds around his circle sun and put tiny star stickers near his triangle mountain.

When they finished, they placed their art on the drying rack. The rack smelled like wood. The papers rustled softly. Ozzie’s picture glowed with blues and yellows.

Ms. Lark gathered everyone. “How did we handle tricky moments today?”

Ozzie raised his paw. “I felt frustrated. My circle was bumpy. I paused, took deep breaths, asked for help, and went slow. I used tools, and I shared with Tia. That helped me.”

Tia smiled. “We followed directions, and it worked.”

Benji nodded. “And we included each other.”

Ms. Lark beamed. “Yes! When we feel big feelings, we can pause, breathe, and use our words. We can follow steps. We can ask friends to help. That is how artists—and friends—grow.”

Ozzie looked at his clean paws and his bright painting. He felt calm and proud. He whispered to his brush, “Thank you for going slow with me.”

He turned to Tia and Benji. “Tomorrow, want to make rainbow fish shapes together?”

“Yes!” they cheered.

Ozzie’s whiskers wiggled happily. He knew that even on splattery days, he could handle his feelings, follow directions, and make space for friends.

The End.
