# Benny Bird’s Big Choice in the Attic

Benny was a small singing bird with bright blue feathers and a warm, round belly. He lived in a quiet attic at the top of a friendly old house. The attic smelled like clean wood and soft dust. Sunlight made golden lines on the floor. Boxes sat in tidy rows. Strings, buttons, and balls were kept in little baskets. Benny liked it that way. He liked order. He liked being able to find his favorite shiny marble when he wanted it.

“Good morning, attic!” Benny sang, trilling a happy tune. “Tweet-a-dee, tweet-a-doo!”

He stretched his wings and hopped to his sports basket. Inside were small things he used for games: a tiny feather-light ball, a ribbon he used as a finish line, and a whistle that went peep. Today, he wanted to play a sport. But which one? And with whom?

Benny peered out the attic window. Outside, the yard was quiet. Inside, the attic was even quieter. He liked the quiet. But he also liked playing with friends. Hmmm.

“I could play kick-and-flit,” he said to himself, tapping the tiny ball with his toe. “Or I could practice flying laps around the lantern. Or I could teach new friends how to play a fair game.”

Benny fluttered to a little mirror. He looked at his bright eyes. “I can make a good decision. I can choose.” His chest felt warm and brave.

He set up the ribbon as a finish line between two boxes. He moved the baskets so there was space. “First, I will organize,” he said. “A clean space helps me think.” He dusted a spot with his wing. He lined up the tiny ball, the whistle, and a stack of soft beanbag seeds.

Peep! Benny blew the whistle. “Practice time!”

He tried flying laps around the lantern, counting softly. “One, two, three.” He landed and caught his breath. He tried kick-and-flit, tapping the little ball through a ring made of string. Tap, tap, swoosh! It felt good. But playing alone started to feel a bit too quiet.

Just then, he heard a gentle rustle. From behind a hat box came a small gray mouse with pink ears. “Hello,” said the mouse. “I’m Millie. I heard your song.”

“Hi, Millie!” Benny chirped. “Do you like sports?”

Millie smiled. “I don’t know. I have never tried. I’m a little shy.”

Benny felt a flutter in his belly. He liked his quiet. He liked his plan. But he also wanted to be kind and fair. He took a deep breath and made a choice. “I can be brave,” he told himself. “I can share. I can teach.”

“Millie,” Benny said, “let’s play a game that is simple and fair. We can take turns. We can make clear rules. And we can organize so we both feel safe and happy.”

Millie’s whiskers twitched. “Okay,” she said softly.

Benny clapped his wings. “First, we decide the game. Would you like to try Beanbag Toss or Kick-and-Flit?”

Millie looked at the beanbag seeds. “Beanbag Toss, please. They look soft.”

“Great decision,” Benny said. “Now we make rules. Rule one: We take turns. Rule two: We cheer for each other. Rule three: We keep the attic tidy. Rule four: We stop to drink water when we need it.”

Millie nodded. “That sounds fair.”

Benny set up a gentle target: a circle drawn with chalk on the floor. He put the beanbags in a neat basket. “You can go first, Millie,” he said.

Millie tossed a beanbag. Plop! It landed near the circle. “I missed,” she said, ears drooping.

Benny chirped kindly. “That’s okay. We learn by trying. Let’s do three slow breaths.” They breathed in and out together. Benny sang a tiny tune. “Try again when you are ready.”

Millie tried again. Plop! The beanbag landed right in the circle. Millie beamed. “I did it!”

Benny cheered. “Hooray! Now it’s my turn.” He tossed a beanbag and missed. He smiled anyway. “It’s fair to have turns, and it’s fair to be honest. I will try again.”

They played and laughed. The attic felt friendly and bright. The tiny ball rolled in a corner. The ribbon fluttered gently like a soft cloud. The sun warmed their feathers and whiskers. They shared water from a tiny cup. They paused to stretch. They took turns. They kept the beanbags in the basket when not in use.

Soon, a soft creak came from a trunk. A small brown spider peeked out. “Hello,” she whispered. “I’m Suri. May I play too?”

Benny looked at Millie. He felt that flutter again. More friends meant more fun, but it also meant more choices to make. He remembered his rules. He straightened his feathers and decided.

“Yes, Suri,” Benny said. “We can make a team. We will add one more rule: Rule five: Everyone gets the same number of turns.”

Suri’s eyes sparkled. “Thank you,” she said.

Benny organized the turns: Millie, Suri, Benny, then back to Millie. He used the whistle to start each round. Peep! They tossed. They cheered. They put beanbags back in the basket after each turn. If someone missed, they said, “Good try. You can learn.” If someone scored, they said, “Great job!” The attic felt full of fair play.

When they finished, Benny sang a sweet, calm song. He looked around. The attic was still tidy. The baskets were neat. His heart felt happy. Millie and Suri smiled.

“I made a good decision today,” Benny said. “I chose to share. I chose fair rules. I chose to stay organized. We played a sport, and we helped each other.”

Millie nodded. “You helped me be brave.”

Suri added, “You helped me feel included.”

Benny puffed his feathers with pride. “When we take time to think, make a plan, and choose kindly, everyone wins.”

They put the beanbags away together. They folded the ribbon. They set the whistle in its place. The attic returned to quiet, warm and peaceful.

Benny hummed one last note. “Tweet-a-dee, tweet-a-doo. Good choices make good games, and good friends, too.”

The End.
