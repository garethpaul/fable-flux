# Ollie Otter and the Cozy Closet Cave

Ollie was a playful otter with bright eyes and busy paws. He loved to splash, scribble, and sing little songs. One rainy day, the pitter-patter on the roof sounded like soft drumbeats. Ollie’s whiskers twitched with an idea.

“I want a quiet place to write my story,” he said, holding a stubby blue crayon and a small notebook. “A secret, cozy place!”

He padded down the hallway and peeked into a closet. It was warm and smelled like clean cotton. Soft sweaters made a fluffy hill. A yellow scarf hung like a sunbeam. A box of buttons rattled when he nudged it. The closet felt like a tiny cave.

“This is my closet hideaway,” Ollie whispered. “My Cozy Closet Cave!”

He scooted in and closed the door a little. A thin line of light slipped under the door, a gentle glow. He could hear the rain whisper, tip-tap, tip-tap, and the house hum quietly. Ollie smiled. “Perfect,” he said.

He opened his notebook. “I will write a story about a brave fish,” he announced. He put the blue crayon on the paper.

But the crayon made a fat scribble. It wasn’t neat. It didn’t look like letters. Ollie tried again. The crayon slipped. The line wiggled away. Ollie’s cheeks grew warm. His whiskers drooped.

“Hmph,” he huffed. “This is hard!”

He pressed the crayon harder. It broke with a tiny crack. A little blue piece rolled into a shadow. Ollie’s tummy felt tight. His paws curled. He wanted to roar, but it came out as a squeaky “Grr!”

He thumped his tail on the floor. Thump. Thump. The box of buttons rattled again. A sweater slid down with a soft flump and tickled his nose. “Ah-choo!” cried Ollie.

Ollie felt mad and stuck. His eyes felt hot. He held his breath. The closet felt smaller. He wanted to throw the broken crayon. He wanted to give up.

Then he remembered something his friend, Ms. Dot the duck, always said in class. “When you feel big mad,” Ms. Dot quacked kindly, “stop, flop, and breathe.”

Ollie whispered to himself, “Stop. Flop. Breathe.”

He put the crayon down. He flopped his body gently onto the soft sweaters. He placed a paw on his tummy and breathed slowly in and out. In… out. In… out. He counted, “One… two… three.”

His tummy felt softer. His shoulders relaxed. The closet smelled like soap and lemons. The rain made its comfy song again. Tip-tap. Tip-tap. Ollie blinked. He felt calmer.

He sat up. “I can try again,” he said. “But I need help. I need to follow the directions.”

He remembered another thing Ms. Dot had taught. “Slow strokes,” she said. “Start at the top. Small lines. Gentle paws.”

Ollie looked around his cozy cave. “Do I have a sharper crayon?” he wondered. He thought about asking for help. He wanted to respect the closet and keep it neat, too. He picked up the fallen sweater and folded it carefully. He set the button box back so it wouldn’t rattle.

Then he opened the door a little and called, “Mama?”

Mama Otter padded down the hallway. “Yes, Ollie? Are you in the closet?”

“Yes,” Ollie said. “It is my Cozy Closet Cave. It smells nice and is very quiet. May I write in here if I keep it tidy and listen to the rules?”

Mama smiled. “Yes, you may. Thank you for asking and for respecting our things. What do you need?”

“I need a pencil grip and a sharp crayon, please. And maybe… a board so my paper is firm.”

Mama brought a small clipboard and a purple pencil grip. “Remember Ms. Dot’s directions,” she said softly. “Gentle paws, slow strokes.”

Ollie nodded. “I will try.”

He slid the grip onto a new blue crayon. He clipped his paper to the board. He sat crisscross on the soft sweater hill. He took a calm breath.

“Start at the top,” he said. He made a short, straight line. “Small lines,” he said. He made a tiny curve. His paw felt steady. He smiled and wiggled his whiskers.

He wrote a big letter F. It wasn’t perfect, but it looked like an F. Ollie giggled. “F for fish!” He drew bubbles—pop, pop, pop. He added a smile to the fish. He wrote another letter. S. It took two tries. On the first try, he paused and breathed when it got tricky. On the second try, he made the curves slowly.

“This is working,” Ollie said happily. “When I feel frustrated, I can stop, flop, and breathe. Then I can try again and follow directions.”

He kept writing his small story: “A fish swims fast.” The letters were wiggly, but they showed his hard work. He felt proud.

When he finished, he carefully closed his notebook and looked around. He tucked the crayon in a little cup. He straightened the scarf and patted the sweater hill. The closet felt neat and kind.

Mama peeked in. “How did it go, my writer?”

Ollie beamed. “I felt mad. I wanted to roar. But I stopped, flopped, and breathed. Then I followed Ms. Dot’s directions. I used gentle paws and slow strokes. I wrote my story in my Cozy Closet Cave. And I kept it tidy to show respect.”

Mama hugged him. Her fur was warm and smelled like sunshine. “I am proud of you,” she said. “You used calm tools and kind choices.”

Ollie listened to the soft rain and his happy heart. He whispered to his notebook, “Thank you, cave. Thank you, calm. Thank you, directions.”

Then he splashed his tail once, just a little, and giggled. “Tomorrow,” he said, “I will write about a brave crab!”

The End.
