# Fern’s Soft Steps in the Attic

Fern was a gentle deer fawn with big, kind eyes and soft, spotted fur. One rainy afternoon, Fern tiptoed up the creaky stairs to a quiet attic. The air smelled like warm wood and old books. Dust sparkled in the thin light from a tiny round window. The rain made a patter-patter song on the roof.

“What a cozy place,” Fern whispered. Her hooves made the softest clack on the floorboards. She saw a red toy wagon, a pile of blankets, and boxes with silly hats. A moth fluttered by, and a sleepy old clock went tick-tock, tick-tock.

Fern wanted to play. She picked up a fluffy scarf and giggled. “Peek-a-boo!” she said to her reflection in a shiny tin. But then Fern heard a sound that was not rain, and not the clock.

“Sniff… sniff… sob.”

Fern’s ears tilted. “Hello?” she called softly. “Are you okay?”

From behind a stack of boxes came a tiny voice. “I am stuck.”

Fern peered around a box. There, in a nest of string and buttons, sat a small gray mouse. The mouse’s whiskers trembled. A little button was tangled in her tail.

“I am Mimi,” squeaked the mouse. “I tried to make a round bed, but the string pulled the button, and now my tail is tied.”

Fern’s heart felt warm and wiggly. “I am Fern,” she said. “I want to help.” She took a slow breath. “I will be careful.”

Fern took a step, but the floorboard squeaked loudly. The mouse flinched. Fern paused and thought. Her mama had said, “Use gentle steps, and use gentle words.” Fern nodded.

“I will move like a whisper,” said Fern. “Is this okay?”

“Yes,” Mimi sniffled. “Thank you for asking.”

Fern looked around. She saw the red wagon, the soft blankets, and a small pair of blunt safety scissors in a craft box. She also saw a tiny teddy bear missing a ear. Fern’s ears perked up with an idea.

“Teamwork,” Fern said. “We will do this together. You tell me when it feels okay. I will listen.”

Mimi’s eyes grew calm. “I can try,” she said.

Fern moved the wagon close to make a soft seat. “Here, sit here so your tail can rest.”

Mimi climbed in. “It is soft,” she said, rubbing the smooth red side. Fern took a blanket and folded it into a little pillow for Mimi’s back.

Fern looked at the string. It was tight around the button and tail. “It looks snug,” she said. “I think we need to make a tiny snip.”

Mimi’s whiskers twitched. “I am scared.”

“It is okay to feel scared,” said Fern. “I will go slow. We will stop if you say stop.”

Mimi took a breath. “Okay.”

Fern picked up the safety scissors. They felt cool in her mouth. The metal smelled like pennies. “I will count,” Fern said. “One… two… snip.” She made the smallest snip in the string. “How does that feel?”

Mimi wiggled. “A little better,” she said.

Fern smiled. “You are brave,” she said. “We can do it.”

“One… two… snip.” Another tiny cut. The string loosened, and the button rolled away with a clink.

“It’s free!” Mimi squealed. She swished her tail. “Thank you, Fern!”

Fern’s chest felt light, like a bubble. “We did it together,” she said. “You helped me by telling me how you felt.”

Mimi nodded. “You listened. That made me feel safe.”

The rain tapped softer now. The attic felt brighter. Fern and Mimi looked around. They saw the teddy bear with one ear. “Oh,” said Fern. “The bear looks a little sad.”

Mimi touched the bear. “I know that feeling,” she said. “Maybe we can help.”

Fern found some soft yarn and the button that had rolled away. “The bear needs an ear,” said Fern. “We can make one.”

They worked side by side. Fern held the bear steady with gentle hooves. Mimi looped the yarn around and around, humming a tiny tune. The yarn felt fuzzy and warm. Soon the bear had a new round ear. It was not perfect, but it was cozy.

“There,” said Fern. “Hello, Bear. Do you feel better?”

The bear did not speak, but the attic seemed to smile. The clock went tick-tock like a happy heart.

Mimi hugged Fern’s leg. “You have a kind heart,” she said. “You saw me. You listened. You helped.”

Fern’s cheeks felt sunny. “You have a clever heart,” she said. “You showed me what you needed. You were brave. That is good self-love.”

Mimi’s eyes shone. “We are a good team.”

Fern nodded. “Empathy means I try to feel what you feel. When I listen to your feelings, I can help in a kind way.”

They climbed into the red wagon and shared a blanket. They sipped warm air that smelled like cinnamon from a box of old sticks. The rain stopped. A beam of light made a golden path on the floor.

Fern whispered, “When someone is stuck, I will listen, go slow, and help.”

Mimi squeaked, “And I will say how I feel and let friends help me.”

They smiled at each other. The attic was quiet and full of care.

The End.
