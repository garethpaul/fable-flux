# Benny the Busy Beaver’s Backyard Plan

Benny was a busy beaver with bright eyes and a big, flat tail. He lived near a sunny backyard with soft grass that tickled his toes. The backyard smelled like sweet flowers and fresh dirt. Birds sang, “Tweet, tweet!” and a gentle breeze whispered through the leaves.

“Good morning, backyard!” Benny said, patting the ground with his tail. “I have a plan. I will build a tiny pond to share water with my friends. Splish, splash!”

Benny gathered sticks and small stones. He stacked them carefully. Tap, tap. Click, clack. He hummed a happy tune. “Work, work, work,” he sang.

Just then, a ladybug named Dot landed on his nose. “Hello, Benny,” Dot said. “What are you building?”

“A tiny pond,” Benny said. “It will be pretty and cool. Frogs can hop. Birds can sip. We can all rest and listen to the water.”

Dot looked around. The sunny backyard belonged to Mia, a kind child who tended the garden with her grandpa. There were neat rows of carrots, a sunflower patch, and a shiny sign that said, “Please stay on the path.”

Dot tilted her head. “Benny, see that sign? It says to stay on the path. Are you following directions?”

Benny blinked. “Oh! I did not see the sign. I was so excited.” He felt a little flutter in his tummy. “I want to do the right thing.”

Just then, Mia came outside with her watering can. Swish, swish went the water. “Good morning, friends!” Mia smiled. “The sun is warm today.”

Benny waved his tail. “Hi, Mia. I have a plan to help the backyard. May I build a tiny pond right here?”

Mia knelt down and listened. She smelled like soap and strawberries. “That sounds helpful,” she said. “But this spot is the carrot bed. The carrots need space and sunlight.”

Benny looked at the carrot tops. They were bright and green. He felt sorry. “I did not mean to bother your carrots,” he said softly. “I want to be helpful and respectful.”

Mia nodded. “Thank you for caring. Respect means we think about other people and their things. We ask first. We listen. We share space.”

Benny sat up straight. “I can follow directions. What should I do?”

Mia smiled. “Let’s make a plan. We can use strategy. A strategy is a smart way to do something.”

She pointed to a corner by a smooth rock and a patch of daisies. The ground was a little low there. “This spot is not for planting. It gets shade at noon. The birds like to visit the rock. If we build your tiny pond here, it won’t bother the garden.”

Benny’s eyes shone. “That is a good strategy! May I build here?”

“Yes,” Mia said. “Let’s also use only fallen sticks and stones. We care for the earth. We don’t break plants. We keep the water clean.”

“Environmental care,” Dot buzzed. “Good idea!”

Benny clapped his tail. “I will follow those directions.”

They worked together. Benny gathered only fallen sticks. Tap, tap. Mia carried smooth stones in a little bucket. Clink, clink. Dot flew ahead and pointed to safe spots. “Here’s a stick! Here’s a leaf!” The breeze felt cool on their faces. The dirt felt crumbly under their paws and fingers.

Benny stacked the sticks in a neat circle. He placed the stones like a little wall. Mia added a small bowl to hold the water. She poured just enough. Swish, swish. The water shone like tiny stars. Plip, plip went a drop.

Soon, a robin fluttered down. “Chirp! May I have a sip?” the robin asked.

“Yes,” Benny said. “This pond is for sharing.”

A green frog peeked from the grass. “Ribbit! Is there room for me?”

Benny smiled. “Yes. We respect each other. We take turns.”

The frog dipped a toe. The water was cool. The robin took a gentle sip. Mia clapped. Dot spun in a happy circle.

Grandpa came out with a sun hat. “What a lovely tiny pond,” he said. “I see you followed directions and kept the garden safe.”

Benny stood tall. “I asked first. I listened. I used a smart plan. I cared for the backyard.”

Grandpa patted Benny’s head. “That is respect. When we respect others, we make peace. We keep things healthy and happy.”

Benny looked at the carrots, standing proud. He looked at the clean water, shining bright. He felt warm inside. “Thank you, Mia. Thank you, Dot. Thank you, backyard.”

The sun began to drift, and shadows grew long. The air smelled like leaves and sunshine. Benny gave the pond a final pat with his tail. Tap.

“Today I learned,” Benny said, “that being busy is good, but being respectful is best. I can follow directions, make a smart plan, and care for the earth.”

Mia nodded. “And when we do those things, everyone can enjoy the sunny backyard together.”

Benny smiled and listened to the soft plip-plip of the pond. Birds sang a sleepy song. The frog hummed, “Ribbit, ribbit.” The tiny pond was perfect, because it was made with respect.

The End.
