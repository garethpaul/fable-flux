# Bunny Beats in the Little Workshop

In a cozy garage workshop, a determined rabbit named Rumi twitched his whiskers and tapped his fluffy feet. The workshop smelled like warm wood and a tiny bit like lemon soap. Light streamed in through a small window and made sparkles on the tools. There were boxes of nuts and bolts, a soft rug, and a shiny radio on a shelf.

“I want to dance today,” Rumi said, ears up and bright. “I want to learn new steps!”

Rumi’s friend, Mr. Soto, the kind neighbor who fixed bikes in the workshop, smiled. He wore blue overalls and had a gentle voice. “Dancing is wonderful, Rumi. Dancing can tell stories. Dancing can help us listen and learn.”

Rumi bounced. “I can hop, hop, hop!”

“Yes,” said Mr. Soto. “But first, we listen.” He turned the shiny radio knob. Soft music filled the air like a warm blanket. The beat went tap-tap-tap, like little raindrops on a tin roof.

Rumi tried to hop fast, but his paws slipped on a squeaky bolt. Clink! The bolt rolled. A box of screws rattled like tiny maracas. Rumi’s nose scrunched. “Oops!”

Mr. Soto put up his hand. “Let’s pause. Safe space, happy dance. We move the tools, then we move our feet.”

They made a little dancing space on the soft rug. They moved the toolbox to a low shelf, rolled up a loose cord, and put the squeaky bolt in a jar. Rumi patted the rug. “Soft and safe!”

“Good listening,” Mr. Soto said. “Now, let’s learn with our ears, our eyes, and our feet.”

He clicked the radio again. A new song drifted in, slow and smooth. “This is a dance from far away,” he said. “It comes from a country across the ocean.” He pointed at a map on the wall with colorful stickers. “We can be global friends when we listen and learn from others.”

Rumi’s eyes grew round. “Global friends?”

“Yes,” said Mr. Soto. “People around the world dance in many ways. When we listen, we show care. We say, ‘Your dance matters.’ That is being a good neighbor.”

Rumi nodded. “I will listen. I will care.”

Mr. Soto clapped a gentle pattern. Clap… clap-clap… clap. “Follow the beat. Feel it in your ears. Feel it in your belly.”

Rumi pressed his paws to his belly. “I feel it,” he giggled. He copied the claps with soft paw taps on the rug. Tap… tap-tap… tap.

“Good,” said Mr. Soto. “Step, slide, and sway.”

Rumi stepped. He slid. He swayed. His ears swished like soft brushes. The air smelled like lemon and wood, and the music felt like a hug.

“Now try a bow,” said Mr. Soto. “A bow says thank you. It is a way to show respect.”

Rumi bowed low. His whiskers tickled the rug. “Thank you,” he whispered to the music, to the map, to the world.

The song changed again. A happy drum beat popped like popcorn. Rumi wiggled his tail. “Pop-pop-pop!” He leaped with joy and — oh no! — his paw nipped a small tin can. The can tipped and spilled glittery screws. They scattered like little stars. Tink-tink-tink!

Rumi’s eyes filled with tears. “I… I made a mess.”

Mr. Soto knelt beside him. “It’s okay, Rumi. We all make mistakes. What do we do when we make a mess?”

Rumi took a deep breath. He remembered the bow. He remembered the beat. He put a paw on his heart. “I say I’m sorry. I help fix it.”

“That is a kind apology,” Mr. Soto said with a nod.

Rumi looked at the shiny screws. “I’m sorry, Mr. Soto. I will pick them up.”

They picked up the screws together, one by one. The tiny stars went back into the can with a soft tinkle. When they were done, Rumi bowed. “Thank you for helping me.”

Mr. Soto smiled. “Thank you for listening and saying sorry.”

“Can we dance again?” Rumi asked, hopeful.

“Of course,” said Mr. Soto. “Let’s try a dance from another place.” He turned the knob, and bright, quick music filled the room like sunrise. “In this dance, we count to four, then we spin.”

Rumi listened. “One, two, three, four.” He spun slowly, ears fanning out. He didn’t bump any cans. He didn’t slip on any bolts. He stayed on the soft rug. “I did it!”

“You did,” said Mr. Soto. “You listened. You learned. You danced with care.”

Rumi hopped a happy hop. “Dancing helps my body and my brain!” he squeaked. “I can learn from the world, and I can be gentle and say sorry.”

Mr. Soto clapped. “That is the lesson, little friend. We dance with our ears, our feet, and our hearts. We listen to music and to people. We respect dances from many places. When we make a mistake, we apologize and try again.”

Rumi looked at the map on the wall. He waved a paw at the stickers. “Hello, global friends!”

Then he turned to the radio. The beat went tap-tap-tap. The workshop glowed with golden light, and the lemony air felt bright and clean. Rumi swayed, slid, and bowed. He moved with joy and care, step by step, beat by beat, a little rabbit dancer in a little garage workshop.

Rumi smiled. “Today I learned to dance, to listen, and to say sorry. Tomorrow, I will dance again.”

The End.
