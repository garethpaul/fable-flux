# Tilly Tortoise Plans the Wash-Day Parade

In a sunny little house, the laundry room hummed and swished. Warm air puffed from the dryer like a gentle sigh. The floor smelled like soap and lemon. Socks made tiny mountains on a basket. In the middle of it all stood Tilly, a wise tortoise with bright eyes and a steady smile.

“Today is Wash-Day Parade!” Tilly said. Her voice was soft and sure. “We will make a banner. We will sing. But first, we prepare.”

She looked at a big calendar on the wall. The square for today was circled. A small note said, “Plan, Pack, Practice.” Tilly tapped the note with her little toe. Tap, tap. “Preparation,” she said, “is getting ready before we go.”

A tiny mouse named Pip peeked from a sock. “Can we start now?” squeaked Pip.

“Soon,” said Tilly kindly. “First, we make a list.”

Tilly pulled a small notepad from a pocket on her shell. She wrote slowly, saying each word. “Clothespins. Markers. Ribbon. Snacks. Water. Clean towels.” Scritch, scratch went her pencil. Pip watched, whiskers twitching.

“We need the big blue basket,” Tilly said. “It helps us carry.”

Pip scampered to the corner. He tugged on the basket handle. “It’s heavy!”

Tilly nodded. “You were brave to try. Let’s ask for help.” She raised her head and called, “Laundry room friends, we have a job.”

The washing machine blinked a friendly light. The dryer hummed low. A dust bunny rolled out and bumped the basket. Together, with Tilly’s steady push, they slid the basket close. Tilly smiled. “Thank you. Gratitude is saying thank you for help.”

Pip sniffed the air. “Mmm, warm towels smell like clouds!”

Tilly chuckled. “Yes. Now, preparation time.” She opened a drawer. It went squeak. Inside were markers, ribbons, and a folded white sheet.

“This sheet is from my nana,” Tilly said. Her eyes sparkled. “On wash day, she made banners. She shared stories while we worked. Tradition means we do special things again and again to remember and share love.”

Pip touched the sheet. “It feels soft.”

Tilly spread the sheet on the clean floor. She taped the corners so it would not slide. “We plan our art before we draw,” she said. “What will our banner say?”

Pip bounced. “Clean and Kind!”

Tilly nodded. “Good words. Clear and kind.” She drew big block letters. Pip dotted the i’s with little bubbles. Pop, pop went the sound in Pip’s head as he giggled.

“Colors?” Tilly asked. She held up markers. Red, blue, green, yellow.

“Rainbow!” Pip squeaked.

“Let’s prepare the space,” said Tilly. She laid old towels around the edges. “These catch drips. Preparation keeps mess small.”

They colored together. The markers smelled sweet and inky. Tilly moved slow and careful. Pip made swirls and stars. “This is creative expression,” Tilly said. “We use art to show our hearts.”

The dryer dinged. The sound was bright. Tilly looked at the clock. “Snack break soon,” she said. “We planned it. We pack our bodies with energy too.”

She opened the blue basket and checked the list. “Clothespins. Markers. Ribbon. Snacks. Water. Clean towels.” She touched each item. “We are almost ready.”

Pip tugged a shiny ribbon. “Can we tie it on now?”

Tilly gently set the ribbon down. “Not yet,” she said, calm and firm. “We finish the letters first. Then we tie the ribbon so it won’t drag. Saying ‘not yet’ is being assertive. It keeps our plan safe.”

Pip’s ears drooped. “I wanted to do ribbon.”

Tilly patted his paw. “You will. Preparation means we do steps in a good order. Order helps us succeed.”

They finished the colors. Tilly lifted the banner. It swished like a soft flag. The letters shone bright: CLEAN AND KIND. She smiled, remembering Nana’s voice. “On wash day, we sing,” Tilly said. She hummed a simple tune. “Swish, swash, swoosh, we work with care. Clean and kind, the love we share.”

Pip joined in, high and happy. The laundry room felt warm and full.

“Ribbon time,” Tilly said. She showed Pip how to tie a simple knot. “Over, under, pull it through.” Pip tried. His first knot fell out. He frowned.

“You can do it,” said Tilly. “Let’s prepare your hands. Dry them.” She handed him a towel. Pip dried. He tried again. This time, the knot held snug. He squealed with joy. “I did it!”

“You did,” said Tilly. “Practice is part of preparation.”

They clipped clothespins to the top of the banner. Click, click, click. Tilly put snacks and water into the basket. She checked the list one more time. “All set.”

They opened the laundry room door. Sunlight spilled in like warm milk. Outside, neighbors waved. Tilly and Pip held the banner between them. It fluttered gently.

“Wash-Day Parade!” called Tilly. They walked slow and steady. People smiled. A child clapped. A grandma hummed the tune. Tilly told a small group, “My nana taught me this. I am sharing our tradition.”

At the end of the walk, they came back to the laundry room. Tilly set the basket down. She breathed in the lemon soap smell and felt proud.

Pip hugged Tilly’s leg. “Thank you for showing me how to get ready.”

Tilly hugged back. “Preparation helps us feel calm and strong. When we plan, pack, and practice, our fun grows bigger.”

Pip nodded. “Clean and kind,” he said.

“Clean and kind,” Tilly agreed. “And grateful, and brave, and ready.”

The End.
