# Squeaky’s Careful Plan

Morning sun peeked through the dusty windows of the garage workshop. It smelled like warm wood, orange soap, and a little bit of paint. Tools hung on the wall, shiny and still. A tiny broom leaned in the corner. In the middle sat Squeaky, a kind mouse with soft gray fur and bright, curious eyes.

“Today I will build a tiny cart,” Squeaky said. “I want to pull my toy blocks.” He patted the small wheels he had found, two bottle caps that clinked like little bells.

Squeaky spread a drawing on the floor. It showed a rectangle for the cart, a line for a handle, and circles for wheels. “Plan first,” he whispered. “Then build.” He smiled and wiggled his whiskers.

He measured with a bit of string. He counted softly, “One, two, three.” He tapped a pencil. Tap, tap, tap. He cut a piece of cardboard. Snip! The scissors made a cheerful snipping sound.

Squeaky tried to poke a hole for the first wheel. But the cardboard bent. The bottle cap rolled away and pinged against a jar. “Oh!” he squeaked. His cheeks felt hot. His tiny fists balled up.

“I feel mad,” Squeaky said, voice tight. His tail twitched. He wanted to stomp. He wanted to toss the cap. He almost did—almost!

He stopped and put his paws on his chest. “Name it to tame it,” he told himself, like Papa Mouse had taught him. “This is anger.” He took a slow breath in, smelling the sweet sawdust. He breathed out like blowing bubbles. “Pffff.”

“Try again,” Squeaky whispered. He shook his paws loose and found the cap. “Hello, wheel. Let’s be friends.”

He made a plan. “First, I will mark the spot,” he said. Dot! He made a tiny dot with his pencil. “Next, I will ask for help from a tool.” He looked around. A small nail glinted in a bowl. “A helper!”

Squeaky gently pressed the nail on the dot. Tap, tap. He used a little hammer, the smallest in the workshop. The nail went through with a soft crunch. “I did it!” he cheered. He wiggled the wheel onto a stick. Click.

He tried the second wheel. The nail slipped. The stick tilted. The cart leaned and squeaked, “Eeeeee.” Squeaky’s whiskers drooped. The sound made his ears fold back.

“I feel mad again,” he said. He felt it like a hot bubble in his tummy. He stamped one tiny foot, and the floor felt cool. He remembered his plan. “Stop. Breathe. Count.” He held up his paws and counted to five. “One… two… three… four… five.” He breathed in slow like smelling soup. He blew out slow like blowing out candles. His shoulders relaxed.

“What else can help?” he wondered. He looked around the garage workshop. He listened. Drip, drip went a leaky tap. Tick-tick went a wall clock. He spotted a roll of tape. He spotted a stack of washers—little circles of metal like tiny donuts.

“Resourceful time,” Squeaky said. He slid a washer over the stick. “This will keep the wheel straight.” He tested it. Roll. The cart rolled a little and then stopped. “Better, but not yet.”

Squeaky smiled a small smile. “Practice makes better,” he said. He adjusted the washer. He added a bit of tape to hold it snug. He tried again. Roll… roll… roll! The cart went in a straight line.

“Ha!” Squeaky clapped. “You can do hard things, little mouse.”

He tied a string to make a handle. He pulled gently. The cart bumped into a paint can and wobbled. “Oops.” Frustration bubbled again. His tail flicked.

Squeaky patted his chest. “Hello, feeling,” he said calmly. “I see you. Let’s cool down.” He closed his eyes and imagined a quiet pond. He listened to the tick-tick clock. He opened his eyes with a soft smile. “Now, I will plan.”

He looked at the floor and saw a tiny bolt sticking up, like a little mountain in the road. “Aha!” He moved the paint can to the side. He swept a path with the tiny broom. Swish, swish, swish. The floor felt smooth.

“Try again, but slower,” he told himself. He took turtle steps and pulled gently. The wheels whispered, “whirr, whirr.” The cart glided across the clean floor like a small boat. Squeaky put two wooden blocks inside. Clack, clack. The cart held strong.

Squeaky grinned from ear to ear. “I did it! I kept calm. I used a plan. I practiced. I was resourceful.”

He rolled the cart in a circle and laughed. The bottle-cap wheels shined. The washers gleamed. The tape felt sticky but strong. The garage smelled like fresh wood and orange soap. Sunbeams made little dust sparkles in the air.

Just then, a friend poked her head in. It was Dot the ladybug. “Hi, Squeaky! Wow, a cart!”

Squeaky waved. “Hi, Dot! I felt mad many times. I wanted to throw the wheel. But I stopped, I breathed, I planned, and I tried again.”

Dot buzzed happily. “That is a big skill! What did you say to your anger?”

Squeaky stood tall. “I said, ‘Hello, feeling. I see you.’ Then I counted to five. Then I made a plan.”

Dot smiled. “Can I ride?” She hopped into the cart with a tiny thump. Squeaky pulled the handle. They rolled around the workshop, giggling, “Whirr, whirr, whee!”

When they stopped, Squeaky patted the cart. “Thank you, helpers—nail, washer, tape, broom, and breath.”

Dot nodded. “And thank you, plan!”

Squeaky wrapped his tail around himself in a hug. “When I feel mad, I can stop, breathe, and make a plan. Practice makes better. I can try again.”

The cart sat steady. The tools hung neatly. The sun felt warm on Squeaky’s fur. His heart felt calm and proud.

The End.
