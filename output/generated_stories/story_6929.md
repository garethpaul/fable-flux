# Ollie Otter Plans a Picnic

In a cozy house by the river, there was a cool, quiet pantry. It smelled like warm bread and sweet apples. Jars clicked softly on the shelves. A playful otter named Ollie poked his nose in. His whiskers wiggled. His paws patted the floor. Tap, tap, tap.

“I have a big idea!” Ollie whispered. “Let’s plan a river picnic!”

His tummy gave a happy rumble. Outside, the sun was bright. The water sang a splishy song. Ollie could almost feel the cool splash on his fur. But first, planning.

Ollie looked up at the shelves. Crackers. Fruit cups. Peanut butter. A little basket sat on the floor. He put the basket in the middle and said, “I will make a list.”

He found a crayon and a tiny notepad by the door. He wrote in big, simple lines:
- Snacks
- Water
- Blanket
- Trash bag
- Sunscreen (for friends)

Ollie smiled. “I can do this,” he said. “I can say what I need.”

Just then, his friend Tia Turtle peeked around the door. “Hi, Ollie. What’s that smell? Mmm!”

“It smells like apples,” said Ollie. “I’m planning a picnic. Do you want to help?”

“Yes, please,” Tia said, patting her shell. “Can we go outside soon?”

“Yes,” said Ollie. “But let’s plan first.” He pointed to his list. “We need water and a blanket. We need a trash bag to keep the river clean. We care for our world.”

Tia nodded. “That is kind. That is like a helper of the Earth.”

Ollie stood on his tippy toes. He stretched and reached, but the water bottles were on a high shelf. His paws slipped. Clink! Clink!

Ollie took a deep breath. He felt a small worried bubble in his chest. “I can speak up,” he told himself. “I can ask for help in a strong, kind way.”

“Tia,” Ollie said, “I need help. The water is too high for me. Will you spot me, please?”

“Yes,” Tia said. “I will be your helper. I will hold the basket steady.”

With Tia’s help, Ollie lifted the water down safely. “Thank you,” he said. “When I ask for help, I stay safe and strong.”

Next, Ollie reached for the peanut butter. It was heavy. His paws wobbled. He hugged the jar close. “Oof! This is heavy for me,” he said.

“Do you want me to carry it?” Tia asked.

Ollie thought. He checked his list. He checked his body. He checked his feelings. “Yes,” he said. “I want help. The jar is too heavy.”

Tia smiled and tucked the jar into the basket. “Good asking,” she said.

They found apples, crackers, and a tiny jar of jam. They packed spoons, napkins, and a bright red blanket that smelled like sunshine.

Ollie held up a clean, crinkly bag. “This is for our trash,” he said. “We will leave the river better than we found it.”

“I like that plan,” said Tia. “We are kind to the Earth. We are global friends.”

Ollie blinked. “Global means big world,” he said. “We help our big world by not leaving a mess.”

They carried the basket outside. The grass was cool and tickly. The river glimmered. Birds chirped. Plop! A frog jumped. The air was fresh and leafy.

They spread the blanket. Ollie set out snacks. He placed the trash bag by a rock so it would not blow away.

But then—oh no! The wind whooshed. Whoosh! A napkin flew up. It skipped across the grass and toward the water.

“My napkin!” Ollie cried. His heart went thump, thump. “I need help and a plan.”

He took a deep breath. “Tia, I will get the napkin. Can you hold the basket? Please?”

“Yes,” said Tia, steady and calm.

Ollie ran, paws soft on the grass. He spoke to himself. “I can do this. I can use my words. I can keep the river clean.” He reached the napkin just before it touched the water. He caught it! “Got you!”

He walked back, proud and tall. “I felt worried,” he said, “and I said what I needed. That helped.”

They ate crunchy crackers and sweet apple slices. The peanut butter was sticky and yummy. Water was cool and splashy on their tongues.

A duck waddled by and quacked hello. Ollie waved. “We have extra fruit,” he said. “Let’s share with friends.” They placed a piece away from the water, safe and tidy.

After lunch, they picked up every crumb and every wrapper. The trash bag went “crinkle, crinkle.” The grass looked neat. The river sang happy.

Tia smiled. “You planned well. You asked for help. You kept the river clean.”

Ollie’s whiskers twinkled. “I learned to speak up when I need help. That keeps me safe. I also learned to plan before I play. That helps the Earth and my friends.”

They lay on the blanket and watched clouds float by. “That one looks like a boat,” said Tia.

“That one looks like a big, soft otter,” Ollie giggled.

When it was time to go, Ollie checked his list again. Snacks? Done. Water? Done. Blanket? Folded. Trash? In the bin.

He hugged Tia. “Thank you for listening when I asked for help,” he said.

“Thank you for asking,” said Tia. “Your voice matters.”

Ollie smiled. “My voice matters. I can say what I need. I can plan my day. I can care for our big world.”

The End.
