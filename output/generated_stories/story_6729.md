# The Brave Bounce at Dinner

In a cozy dining room, a joyful kangaroo joey named Kiki wiggled in her chair. The table smelled like warm bread, sweet mango slices, and veggie soup. The lamps made a soft glow, like a friendly moon. Kiki’s tail thumped lightly—thump, thump, thump—on the floor.

Mama Roo set little bowls on the table. “Tonight is a taste-adventure,” she said with a smile. “We will learn about foods from different places.”

Papa Roo brought a shiny book with pictures. “We can look and learn,” he said. “This soup is from Grandma’s country. These mangoes grow where the sun is bright. This bread is from our neighbor’s recipe.”

Kiki’s ears perked up. She liked adventures. But when she saw a new bowl with tiny green leaves floating, her tummy made a flutter. “What is that?” she asked in a small voice.

“It’s mint,” Mama Roo said. “Just a little. It smells fresh.” She held the bowl close. Kiki sniffed. It smelled cool, like a tiny winter breeze. Still, Kiki felt unsure. What if she didn’t like it? What if everyone was watching?

Her tail went still. “I don’t think I can,” Kiki whispered. “I’m not brave enough.”

Mama Roo sat beside her. “Being brave can be small,” Mama said softly. “A brave step can be a tiny taste or a tiny word.”

Papa Roo nodded. “Remember your bounce?” he asked. “When you learned to hop?”

Kiki blinked. She remembered. At first, her hops were wobbly. She had fallen on a soft cushion, then stood up again. Her heart had said, Try once more. And she did. Hop, hop, hop! She smiled.

Kiki looked at the table. The bread was warm on her fingers. The mango was smooth and sticky-sweet. The soup steam tickled her nose. The mint leaves looked like tiny boats, ready to sail.

“Can I use my imagination?” Kiki asked.

“Yes,” said Mama. “Imagination helps courage grow.”

Kiki closed her eyes. In her mind, the dining room became a big, bright map. The bread became a desert dune, golden and soft. The soup became a calm lake. The mint leaves were brave little boats. The mango slices were suns smiling in the sky.

She opened her eyes. “Captain Kiki,” she said in a pretend-deep voice, “will guide the mint boats!”

Papa laughed gently. “Captain Kiki, what do you need for your journey?”

Kiki sat tall. “I need a tiny taste,” she said. Her voice shook a little, but she kept going. “I can do a tiny brave thing.”

Mama Roo clapped softly. “That is self-confidence,” she said. “Believing you can try.”

Kiki picked up one mint leaf with her small fingers. It felt cool and soft. She touched it to her tongue. Tingle! It tasted like a little snowflake with a smile. She giggled. “It’s funny!” she said. “It tastes like a cold kiss.”

Her tummy flutter faded. “I did it,” she said. “I was brave.”

Papa Roo pointed to the shiny book. “Would you like to learn where mint grows?” he asked. “People around the world use mint in tea, salads, and sweets.”

Kiki nodded. “Yes, please.” She looked at a picture of green fields. “So many places,” she said. “So many friends we have not met yet.”

Mama Roo poured warm tea into tiny cups. The steam curled like ribbons. “This tea is from our friends across the sea,” she said. “We say thank you when we try new things, because we learn from others.”

Kiki held her cup. The cup felt warm and safe in her paws. She took a careful sip. “Mmm,” she hummed. “It’s gentle.”

“See?” Mama said. “Bravery can be gentle too.”

Kiki grinned. “I want to try the soup boats,” she said. “Captain Kiki will sail the lake!” She took a small spoonful. The soup was cozy and soft on her tongue. “It tastes like a hug,” she whispered.

“Now you know more,” Papa said. “Knowledge grows when we try.”

Kiki’s chest felt bright, like a little sun. “I feel proud,” she said. “I did a brave thing. I can do more.”

She looked at the mango. “Hello, sunshine slice,” she said, giggling. She took a bite. Juice ran down her chin. “Sweet!” She licked her lips and wiped her chin with her napkin. “I’m learning.”

Mama Roo smiled. “What did you learn tonight, little joey?”

Kiki thought. She tapped the table, thump, thump. “I learned that trying is brave,” she said. “I learned that new things can be yummy. And I learned that my imagination helps me feel strong.”

“And about our big world?” Papa asked.

Kiki looked at the book again. “Our world is full of friends and foods,” she said. “When we try something from someone else’s home, we are saying, ‘I want to know you.’”

Mama and Papa hugged Kiki. The dining room felt extra warm. The lamp glow seemed to clap too.

Kiki puffed up with self-confidence. “Next time I feel wobbly,” she said, “I will take a tiny brave step. I will listen to my heart. I will say, ‘I can try.’”

Mama kissed Kiki’s head. “That is a wonderful lesson.”

Kiki held her cup and whispered to the mint leaves, “Thank you for the adventure.”

The table was quiet for a moment, except for soft sips and happy munches. Kiki’s tail thumped again—this time with joy.

The End.
