# Fawn’s Fair Share Dinner

The gentle deer fawn had soft brown eyes and tiny white spots like sugar sprinkles. One cool evening, Fawn tip-tapped into the dining room on careful hooves. The dining room smelled warm and yummy, like cinnamon toast and sweet apples. A round wooden table stood in the middle, and the chairs made little squeaks as they moved. Candles flickered like stars, and the window showed a sky turning purple.

Fawn’s friends were visiting for a special dinner. There was Bunny with floppy ears, Raccoon with bright, curious paws, and Robin with a soft red tummy. They all gathered around the table. Mama Deer brought a big plate of cornbread, a bowl of crunchy carrots, and a shiny pie that smelled like apple hugs.

“Welcome, little ones,” Mama Deer said kindly. “We will use our gentle voices and our kind paws. Everyone will get a fair share.”

Fawn felt excited. Her tummy made a tiny growl. “I’m so hungry,” she whispered.

Bunny’s nose twitched. “Me too! The pie smells like cozy!”

Raccoon tapped his paws. “I want the biggest piece!”

Robin fluttered her wings and chirped, “Cheep-cheep! I’m small, but I still want a turn.”

Mama Deer smiled. “We will pass the plates and take turns. That is fair.” She set the cornbread in the center. “Fawn, will you pass the basket?”

Fawn’s hooves wobbled a little. She wanted the biggest, butteriest piece. Her heart thumped fast—thump-thump. Her cheeks felt warm. “But I’m really, really hungry,” Fawn said, her voice squeaky.

Bunny leaned closer. “It’s hard to wait,” she said. “I feel jumpy in my legs.”

Raccoon frowned and reached for the pie first, even though it wasn’t time yet. The fork clinked on the plate. “I want it now!”

“Cheep!” Robin hopped back, startled. “That scared me.”

Mama Deer placed a gentle hoof on Raccoon’s paw. “Let’s pause.”

The room got very quiet. The candle made a soft whisper sound. Fawn’s ears listened to her own breathing. In… and out. In… and out.

Mama Deer spoke softly. “I see big feelings. Fawn, I see your hungry tummy. Raccoon, I see your grabby paws. Robin, I hear your scared chirp. Bunny, I see your wiggly legs.” She nodded to each of them. “Feelings are okay. Let’s name them.”

Fawn took a breath. “I feel hungry and a little mad,” she said. “My body feels hot.”

Bunny said, “I feel jumpy and worried.”

Raccoon looked down. “I feel excited and impatient.”

Robin tilted her head. “I feel tiny and left out.”

Mama Deer nodded. “Thank you for telling me. When we name feelings, they get softer.” She touched the cornbread basket. “Now, what is fair?”

Bunny raised a paw. “Taking turns is fair.”

Robin chirped, “Everyone gets a piece. None too big, none too small.”

Raccoon sighed. “I can wait. I can count to five.”

Fawn looked at the shiny pie. She looked at her friends. Fawn’s heart felt quiet now—thump… thump. She thought of justice—making things fair for everyone. She thought of tolerance—being patient with others, even when it’s hard.

“I can pass the cornbread,” Fawn said. “I can help make it fair.”

“Thank you,” said Mama Deer.

Fawn lifted the basket carefully. The cornbread felt warm and crumbly through the napkin. She smelled butter and honey. “Bunny first,” she said, passing the basket. “Then Robin, then Raccoon, then me.” She smiled. “That feels fair.”

Bunny took a small piece. “Thank you, Fawn.”

Robin took a tiny piece. “Cheep! Thank you!”

Raccoon took a square and tucked his paws back. “Thanks. I waited.”

Fawn took a piece last. It still felt warm and tasted sweet. Her tummy relaxed.

They crunched the carrots together. Crunch, crunch, crunch. It sounded like tiny twigs in the snow. They sipped apple tea that tasted like sunshine with cinnamon freckles. Their shoulders dropped, and the room filled with soft sounds and happy faces.

When it was time for pie, Mama Deer said, “We can cut the pie into equal slices.”

Raccoon held the knife with Mama’s help. “I will cut carefully,” he said. “Four the same.”

Fawn watched the slices. “One for Bunny, one for Robin, one for Raccoon, and one for me,” she said. “Fair shares.”

But then Fawn noticed something. Robin’s slice was a little too big, and Bunny’s slice was a little too small. Fawn felt a tiny twist in her chest. She remembered justice means fairness for all. She also remembered tolerance—being kind and patient.

“Mama,” Fawn said gently, “I think Bunny’s piece is smaller. Can we fix it?”

Bunny blinked. “Oh! I didn’t see.”

Robin fluttered. “Cheep! I can share. I want it to be fair.”

Raccoon nodded. “I’ll help move the slices.”

Together, they slid a bit from Robin’s slice to Bunny’s. The plates clinked like tiny bells.

Mama Deer smiled proudly. “You used your feelings to find a fair solution. That is wise.”

They ate pie that tasted like happy apples. Fawn felt light and calm. She looked at her friends. “When I feel mad or hungry,” she said, “I can breathe. I can use my words. I can make things fair.”

Bunny patted Fawn’s hoof. “We can help each other.”

Robin chirped, “Cheep! We can share.”

Raccoon said, “We can wait our turn.”

Mama Deer lit one more candle. “This dinner was gentle and just,” she said. “You listened to your bodies and your hearts.”

Fawn’s spots seemed to sparkle in the candle glow. “I learned that big feelings can get softer when I name them,” she said. “Fairness makes dinner taste better. And being patient shows care.”

Everyone nodded. They sat together in the warm, cozy dining room, with crumbs like golden snow and smiles like moonlight.

The End.
