# Pippa the Parrot Finds Her Voice

In a cozy garden shed, where the air smelled like fresh soil and lemony wood polish, lived Pippa the parrot. Pippa had bright green feathers, a sunny yellow chest, and a red beak that liked to click when she thought hard. She loved to make art. She painted with her wingtips, glued buttons, and made twinkly mobiles from shiny bottle caps.

The shed was busy every afternoon. Buzz the bumblebee hummed by the window. Mousy the mouse rustled in the seed sacks. Tilly the tortoise napped beside a clay pot. And Mr. Lou, the gardener, kept brushes, scissors, and ribbons in a big blue toolbox.

One warm day, Pippa set out a small canvas. “Today, I will paint a rainbow garden,” she chirped. She dipped a feather in cool blue paint. Drip! Drop! She smiled. “So smooth!”

Just then, Buzz zipped in. “Bzz! I need the blue paint for my flower signs,” Buzz buzzed. He tipped the jar.

“Wait, please,” Pippa said softly. “I’m using—”

Glug, glug! The blue paint was gone. Pippa’s smile slipped. Her chest felt tight, like a knot in a string.

“It’s okay,” she told herself. “I can try green.” She dipped her feather into leafy green and swished. Swish, swish.

Mousy hurried over, whiskers twitching. “Eek! Green glue? For my seed labels!” He grabbed the sticky jar.

“That’s not glue,” Pippa whispered. “That’s—oh…”

Plop. The jar was empty. Pippa’s wings drooped. Her beak clicked-clicked. Inside, she felt a puff of hot, fizzy frustration, like a kettle getting too steamy.

She tried red. She tried yellow. Someone needed each color. Someone needed the soft brush. Someone needed the scissors. Pippa’s rainbow garden turned into a few tiny dots.

Pippa took a slow breath. In, out. She listened. Drip from the faucet. Tap-tap from the rain starting outside. Tilly’s soft, sleepy snore.

Her tummy felt swirly. “I feel left out,” she murmured. “I feel mad.” A little tear plopped onto the table. Plip.

Mr. Lou peeked in. “Everything all right, Pippa?”

Pippa wiped her eyes with a feather. Her voice shook, but she spoke up. “I have a problem. I want to make my art. But everyone takes what I’m using. I don’t know how to say what I need.”

Mr. Lou nodded kindly. “That’s big talking. Thank you for telling me. When we feel mad or left out, we can use our words. That is called self-advocacy. Try this: Say what you need. Say it clear. Say it kind.”

Pippa practiced. Her beak clicked once. “I need time. I need the blue paint for five minutes.”

Mr. Lou smiled. “Good. Let’s make a plan.” He set a small sand timer on the table. The sand inside shimmered like sugar. “This can help take turns. And we can make a sharing list.”

Pippa puffed up, a little brave balloon in her chest. She turned to Buzz, who was buzzing in circles. “Buzz,” she said, steady and gentle, “I feel frustrated when my paint is taken. I need the blue paint for five minutes. Then you can have a turn.” She flipped the timer. Swish went the sand.

Buzz paused. “Bzz… okay. I can wait.” He landed on a peg and hummed a patient tune.

Pippa dipped, dabbed, and made a cool, calm sky. The timer whispered down. When the last grain fell—swish—she pushed the blue toward Buzz. “Your turn. I’m happy to share.”

Buzz zipped with joy. “Thank you! Bzz!”

Next, Mousy crept near the yellow jar. Pippa placed her wing on it and breathed. In, out. “Mousy,” she said, “I feel upset when my tools are taken. I need the yellow for two minutes. Then I will help you make labels.”

Mousy’s ears perked. “Help me? Really?” His eyes shone. “I can wait. And… thank you.”

Pippa painted warm sunshine. Then she handed Mousy the jar and drew tiny seed pictures for his labels. Together they giggled at the way the sticky tape squeaked. Squeee!

Soon, the shed buzzed in a new way. The sharing list hung on the wall with big, friendly letters: Take Turns, Use Timer, Ask Kindly, Help Each Other. Tilly woke up and nodded. “Good plan,” she rumbled in a soft, gravelly voice.

When the rain tapped harder—tap, tip, tap—Pippa looked at her canvas. Blue sky. Yellow sun. Red flowers. Green leaves. She had done it. Her feathers felt light and proud.

Mr. Lou clapped softly. “Pippa, you spoke up for yourself. You asked for what you needed. You were also generous. You shared and helped your friends.”

Pippa ruffled happily. “I can be kind to me and kind to others,” she chirped. “When I feel big feelings, I can breathe, use my words, and make a plan.”

Buzz buzzed a cheer. Mousy squeaked a cheer. Even the rain sounded like a cheer.

Pippa hung her rainbow garden on a nail by the door. It smelled like fresh paint and orange peel soap. She winked. “Art time tomorrow?”

“Art time tomorrow,” everyone agreed.

The End.
