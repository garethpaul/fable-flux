# Mabel Mouse and the Paint That Sang

Mabel was a kind little mouse with bright whiskers and soft gray fur. She lived in the wall of a big, sunny creative art room. The room smelled like crayons and fresh paper. Light sprinkled in through tall windows and made sparkles on the tables. Paintbrushes stood in jars like tiny trees. Paper rustled like leaves. The art room hummed, ready for a new day.

Mabel peeped out from a tiny hole near the baseboard. Her whiskers twitched. Her heart felt bouncy with curiosity. “What will I learn today?” she whispered to herself. She loved asking questions. She loved finding out how things worked. She loved being kind and careful, too.

On the big table, the teacher had left bright paints in round cups—red, blue, yellow, and a special shimmering white. There were scissors with round ends, safe glue sticks that smelled sweet, stacks of paper, and soft sponges that looked like clouds.

Mabel climbed up a chair leg, paw over paw, with a little scritch-scratch sound. Her paws felt the warm wood. Her nose wriggled. “Hello, colors,” she said. “What can you do?”

The blue paint was smooth and shiny. Mabel leaned close and whispered, “Why are you so blue?” The blue did not answer, but it shined back like a little lake. Mabel giggled. “Maybe you like the sky,” she guessed. She dipped a tiny tip of a brush into the blue and swished it on the paper. Swish, swish. The brush felt tickly in her paw.

“Good morning, brave brush,” Mabel said. “Let’s try red now.” She washed the brush in a cup of water. The water turned a cloudy purple. “Oh!” Mabel’s eyes grew round. “Blue and red make a new color?”

She smiled. “Curiosity helps me see new things,” she said softly.

Mabel tried yellow next. Swish, swish, swish. She made a sunny circle. She asked out loud, “What happens if I mix yellow and blue?” She mixed them gently, round and round. The paint turned green, like little leaves. “Wow,” said Mabel. “When I ask a question and try it, I learn.” Her ears wiggled with joy.

On the far end of the table sat a box labeled Textures. Mabel sounded it out slowly, “Tex-tures.” She opened the lid. Inside were feathers, bits of fabric, a tiny square of shiny foil, and a soft piece of felt. She rubbed the felt on her cheek. “Mmm, so soft.” She tapped the foil. Tink, tink. “So crinkly.” She brushed a feather under her nose and squeaked, “Ah-CHOO!” Then she giggled and whispered, “Feathers tickle.”

She glued the felt to her paper. Pat, pat. She listened to the sound. “Pat, pat, sticky glue,” she sang. She added the shiny foil. Crinkle, press. “My picture feels like a garden,” she said, looking at the green swirls and sunny circle. She drew little mice with a thin black marker—one mouse asking a question, one mouse listening, one mouse smiling.

On a high shelf sat a jar with sparkling white paint. The label said Glitter Glaze. It shone like snow. Mabel’s nose twitched. “What will you do?” she wondered. The jar was tall. She looked around. “I should be safe and careful.” She pulled a small stool closer and climbed up slowly. Step, step. “I am curious,” she told herself, “and I also make good choices.”

Just then, she heard soft footsteps. It was Mr. Ben, the art teacher, humming a happy tune. “Hmm, hmm, hum,” went his song. Mabel froze, then peeked. Mr. Ben smiled when he saw her tiny whiskers. “Well, hello there, little friend,” he said in a gentle voice. “I see you love art time, too.”

Mabel nodded. “I am curious,” she squeaked. “I want to learn about the shiny paint. But I also want to be safe.”

Mr. Ben crouched down so his eyes were kind and close. “Curiosity is wonderful,” he said. “Curiosity helps us learn. And asking for help keeps us safe.” He held out his hand. “Would you like me to bring the glaze down for you?”

“Yes, please,” said Mabel.

Mr. Ben set the jar on the table and opened it carefully. The glaze smelled like sweet soap and glitter. It sparkled even in the shade. “This glaze sings when it dries,” Mr. Ben whispered. “Listen.”

Mabel blinked. “Paint that sings?” She leaned in. Mr. Ben dipped a clean brush into the glaze and touched it to Mabel’s paper. The glaze made a soft, tiny crackle. It sounded like little stars popping. Pop, pop, hush. Mabel’s mouth made a round O. “It sings!” she squeaked. “Hello, singing paint!”

She added gentle swirls of glaze to her sunny circle and her green leaf shapes. The paper gleamed. Pop, pop, hush. She listened with happy ears.

Mabel sat back and felt proud. “I asked questions,” she said. “I tried new things. I asked for help when I needed it. Now I understand more.”

Mr. Ben nodded. “You used your curiosity with care and kindness. You watched, listened, and learned. That’s how artists think.”

Mabel looked at her picture. She had made a garden with a bright sun, leafy swirls, felt-soft grass, a crinkly foil pond, and three tiny mice holding tiny question marks. She wrote, with careful letters, “Ask, Try, Understand.”

She smiled up at Mr. Ben. “Curiosity helps me learn about colors, sounds, and feelings. It helps me be kind, too, because I learn what others need.”

Mr. Ben smiled back. “And when we understand, we can imagine even more.”

Mabel’s whiskers wiggled. She felt brave and gentle and bright. She slid the picture to the drying rack. Pop, pop, hush, sang the glaze.

“Thank you, art room,” Mabel whispered. “Thank you, questions.” She padded back toward her tiny hole, turning once to wave a tiny paw at Mr. Ben.

“See you next time,” he said. “Bring your curiosity!”

“I will,” said Mabel. “Curiosity helps me grow.”

The End.
