# The Brave Little Song in the Home Office

The home office was quiet and cozy. A soft chair sat by a desk. A big window let in warm, golden light. The computer hummed like a sleepy bee. Papers smelled like fresh wood, and a candle smelled like vanilla cookies.

On the windowsill sat Pip, a small blue singing bird. Pip’s feathers shone like tiny sky patches. His chest felt fluttery. Today, he needed to sing a new song for Mama’s video call. She was a language teacher, and her class loved Pip’s songs. They learned new words when Pip sang. But Pip was nervous.

“What if I squeak?” Pip whispered. “What if my song is wrong?”

Mama, wearing soft slippers, smiled at Pip. “You can do it, little friend. Your voice helps my students learn words. They listen to you. You are brave.”

Pip peeked at the computer screen. Faces were waiting—children in little squares. One child waved and said, “Hello!” Another said, “Hola!” Another said, “Bonjour!” The sounds were like colorful bubbles. Pip liked the sounds, but his tummy felt wobbly.

On the desk sat a friendly pencil named Dot. Dot had a shiny pink eraser hat. “Hi, Pip,” said Dot in a tiny voice. “We can help. Let’s plan your song. We can cooperate!”

Pip blinked. “What does that mean?”

“Cooperate means we work together,” Dot said. “We decide what to do as a team.”

Pip took a small breath. “Okay,” he said. “Let’s decide.”

Mama tapped the keyboard. “Ready, class?” she asked. “Today, Pip will sing a hello song. We will learn words for hello in different languages. Be brave and sing with him!”

Pip hopped closer to the microphone. It smelled like clean metal. The computer light blinked like a tiny star.

Dot rolled to the edge of the paper. “Step one,” Dot said. “Choose the words.”

Pip listened to the children. “Hello. Hola. Bonjour.” The words felt like soft pebbles in his beak. He tried to hum, but only a squeak came out. Pip shut his beak. His eyes watered.

“I can’t,” he whispered. “I’m scared.”

Mama leaned near. Her voice was calm and warm. “Bravery is not the same as not being scared,” she said. “Bravery is doing the right thing, even when you feel scared.”

Pip’s heart beat tap-tap-tap. He pressed his toes into the cool windowsill. He looked at the children. They were listening so carefully. They were waiting kindly. He thought, “I want to help them. I can try.”

Pip puffed his chest. “Dot,” he said, “let’s decide on a rhythm. Slow and friendly.”

Dot tapped the desk. Tap… tap… ta-tap. “Like this?” asked Dot.

“Yes,” Pip said. He felt a tiny spark of courage.

Mama nodded. “Class, let’s clap softly together.” Hands clapped like raindrops. Pip’s spark grew.

Pip took a deep breath. He smelled the sweet candle. He felt the warm sun on his back. He heard the gentle claps. He opened his beak and sang:

“Hello, hello, how do you do?
Hola, hola, nice to meet you too.
Bonjour, bonjour, we sing and play,
Words are friends we share today.”

His notes floated like blue ribbons. The children clapped louder. “Hello!” they called. “Hola!” “Bonjour!” Their voices were like bright bells. Pip smiled. His tummy felt lighter.

Dot wiggled with pride. “Great job! Want to add more words?”

Pip nodded. He looked at Mama. “Can we ask the class for ideas?”

“That is a wonderful decision,” Mama said. “Cooperation helps us learn.”

Pip chirped, “Friends, what hello words do you know?”

A child in a red sweater said, “Namaste!” Another said, “Ciao!” A tiny voice said, “Salaam.”

Pip listened carefully. He repeated the sounds, gentle and slow. He loved the shapes of the words. They felt like colorful leaves floating on air. “Namaste,” he sang. “Ciao.” “Salaam.” He practiced each word with care. The children helped, saying the sounds with him. Pip laughed when he tickled his tongue on “Ciao.” Everyone giggled too.

They made a second verse:

“Ciao, ciao, we wave with cheer,
Namaste, we’re glad you’re here.
Salaam, salaam, our hearts say hi,
Words can fly, just like I!”

Pip lifted his wings and twirled. The window showed a tiny slice of blue sky. He felt brave, strong, and kind.

But then the computer flickered. The sound crackled. Pip froze. “Oh no,” he whispered. “What if they can’t hear me?” He felt the wobble again.

Dot rolled closer. “We have a problem. What is our plan?”

Pip took another deep breath. He thought. Decision-making time. “We will sing slower and clearer,” he said. “We will use hand motions too.”

Mama smiled. “Excellent choice.” She raised her hands and showed a wave for “Hello,” hands to heart for “Namaste,” and a friendly nod for “Salaam.”

The children copied. Pip sang slowly, clearly, bravely. Even with the crackle, the message traveled: kind words, shared together. The class clapped and cheered. The computer screen glowed with happy faces.

When the call ended, Pip cuddled against Mama’s hand. It was warm and soft. Dot rested on the paper with a little sigh.

“I was scared,” Pip said, “but I still sang.”

“That is bravery,” Mama said. “You helped your friends learn new words. You made a good plan. You cooperated. You decided what to do.”

Pip chirped a tiny happy note. “Words are friends,” he said. “And friends help friends be brave.”

That evening, the home office was quiet again. The candle flickered. The computer hummed. Pip hummed too, practicing new words like “Konnichiwa” and “Jambo,” smiling at their gentle music. He knew that tomorrow, he could be brave again.

The clear lesson danced in his heart: Be brave, use kind words, make a plan, and work together.

The End.
