# Milo Monkey Helps the Guest Room

Milo was a small, courageous monkey with bright eyes and steady hands. He lived with his big, busy troop in a tree house. One day, the troop’s guest room needed help. Friends from another tree were coming to stay. But the guest room was messy, dusty, and a little squeaky.

Milo peeked in. He wrinkled his nose. “Oh,” he said softly. “It smells like old bananas and dusty pillows.” He could see socks on a lamp, crumbs on the rug, and curtains that were tangled like vines.

Grandma Monkey clapped her hands. “We want our guests to feel welcome,” she said. “Who can help?”

Milo’s heart thumped. He wanted to help his community. He stood tall. “I can,” he said. “I am brave. I can do hard things.”

He stepped into the guest room. The floor felt gritty under his toes. Tiny dust sparkles floated in the sun. Milo took a deep breath. “Slow and steady,” he whispered. “I can do this.”

First, Milo picked up the socks. “Into the basket you go,” he sang. Thump, thump. Easy. He smiled. Helping felt good.

Next, he grabbed a broom. “Sweep, sweep, sweep,” he said. The broom made a soft hush sound. But the crumbs stuck to a sticky spot. His face felt hot. “This is hard,” he frowned.

Milo paused. He remembered self-regulation. “When I feel mad, I breathe,” he told himself. He took a slow breath in. He let a slow breath out. “I am calm. I can try again.”

He found a wet cloth. Wipe, wipe, wipe. The sticky spot said goodbye. Milo giggled. “Goodbye, sticky!”

He pulled the curtains straight. Swish! Light poured in. The room felt brighter. He opened a window. A warm breeze brought the smell of sweet leaves. “Mmm,” he said. “Fresh air.”

Then he saw the big problem. The guest bed squeaked and leaned to one side. “Oh no,” said Milo. “Our guests will not sleep well.” He looked under the bed. A wooden leg was wobbly.

Milo tried to push the leg into place. Squeak! The bed shook. Milo slipped back and bumped onto the rug. “Ow,” he said. His eyes felt watery.

He wanted to quit. But he remembered why he was helping. Community-service means caring for others. He pictured their friends smiling, resting, and feeling welcome. His heart felt strong again.

Milo took three calm monkey breaths. In… out. In… out. In… out. He wiped his eyes. “I can try a new way,” he said. “I will be safe, and I will keep trying.”

He found a toolbox. It was heavy, and it smelled like metal and orange oil. “Clink, clank,” sang the tools when he opened it. Milo read a simple picture card: Tighten the screw. He found a small wrench. His little hands were careful.

“Tighten, tighten, turn,” he said. Turn, turn, turn. The wrench made tiny clicks. He checked the leg. It was better but still wobbly.

“Hmm,” said Milo. “I need help.” Asking for help is part of being brave, he remembered. He peeked out the door. “Grandpa Monkey?” he called. “Can you help me make the bed strong?”

Grandpa Monkey smiled. “I can guide you, Milo. You can do it.”

Together, they looked at the leg. “Try a wood block,” said Grandpa. Milo found a smooth block in the toolbox. He slid it under the leg. He took a breath. “Steady,” he whispered. He pressed gently. The bed stood tall and quiet.

Milo grinned. “We did it!” He felt light and proud.

He shook out clean sheets. They smelled like sunshine. Whoosh! The sheets floated down like clouds. He smoothed the blanket. Pat, pat. He fluffed two pillows. Poof! Little feathers danced in the air like snow.

He set a basket of fruit on the table: red apples, yellow bananas, purple berries. “Welcome,” he said to the empty room. He placed a small sign he made from a leaf: “We are glad you are here.”

At last, the friends arrived. Tap, tap, tap went the knock. Milo opened the door. “Welcome to the guest room!” he said.

The friends looked around. “Ooh,” said one. “It smells fresh.” “Ahh,” said another. “The bed looks cozy.” The room felt warm and safe.

Grandma Monkey hugged Milo. “You helped our community,” she said. “You worked hard. You kept calm. You asked for help. Thank you, Milo.”

Milo’s tail curled with joy. “Helping feels good,” he said. He felt brave, steady, and kind.

That night, the guest room was full of soft breathing and sleepy smiles. The bed did not squeak. The curtains swished quietly in the breeze. Milo peeked in once more. He whispered, “Good night. I am proud of my brave heart.”

Lesson: When we help our community, we make others feel welcome. We can be brave by staying calm, trying again, and asking for help. Together, we make things better.

The End.
