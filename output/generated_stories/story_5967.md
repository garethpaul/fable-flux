# Pebble the Penguin and the Brave Little Book

Pebble was a cheerful penguin with bright eyes and a wiggly belly. He lived in a cozy bedroom with a soft teal rug and a bed that squeaked a happy squeak. Fairy lights twinkled like tiny stars. His favorite pillow smelled like fresh soap. On his desk sat a little notebook with blue fish on the cover and a smooth purple pencil.

Pebble loved to hum and clap. He loved to slide on the rug. But today, Pebble wanted to write his very own story. He opened the notebook and touched the paper. It felt cool and crisp. He took a deep breath. The room was quiet. He could hear the soft whoosh of the heater and the tick-tick of the clock.

“I will write a brave story,” Pebble chirped. “I will write about a penguin hero!”

He put his pencil on the page. The pencil made a tiny scratch-scratch sound. Pebble drew one line. Then he stopped. His tummy felt fluttery, like little snowflakes inside.

“What if my story is not good?” he whispered. He looked at the door, at the lights, at the soft blanket with stars. “What if my words are too small?”

Pebble’s beak drooped. His wings felt heavy. He closed the notebook a little.

Just then, Pebble’s stuffed walrus, Wally, slid down from the pillow. Wally had a soft whiskered smile. Pebble picked him up and hugged him tight. Wally smelled like clean cotton.

Pebble said, “Wally, I feel funny. I want to write, but I feel… worried.”

He put his flipper on his chest. “My heart is going thump-thump.” He put his other flipper on his tummy. “My tummy feels wiggly. I think I feel scared.”

Pebble sat criss-cross on the rug. He took a slow breath in… and out. The air was warm. The fairy lights glowed gentle and kind.

“Let’s use our thinking steps,” Pebble told Wally. “Step one: Name the feeling. I feel scared and small.” He nodded. “Step two: Ask if it’s true. Is it true that I can’t write? Hmm.” He blinked. “I can draw. I can sing my ABCs. I can tell stories when I play. So maybe I can try.”

Pebble smiled a tiny smile. “Step three: Take a small step.”

He opened the notebook again. “I will write one word.” He wrote: PENGUIN. The pencil said scratch. Pebble’s eyes grew bright. “I did it!”

Pebble clapped softly. “I can do one more word.” He wrote: BRAVE. Scratch, scratch. The sounds felt friendly now, like little feet dancing on the page.

Pebble whispered, “I am brave when I try.” His chest felt warm. The fluttery snowflakes got smaller. He wiggled with joy.

He began to talk out loud while he wrote. “Once there was a penguin named Pip. Pip had a big heart.” He drew a tiny heart next to the word. He giggled. “Pip wanted to help a friend find a lost button.”

But then Pebble made a squiggly letter. “Oh no!” he said. “My B is bouncy.” His beak frowned. His eyes felt watery.

He hugged Wally again. “Step one: Name the feeling. I feel frustrated.” He sniffed. “Step two: Ask if it’s true. Does a bouncy B mean my story is bad? Hmm. My story is still kind. My story is still brave. A bouncy B is okay.”

Pebble took a deep breath. He felt the soft rug under his feet. He heard the clock say tick… tick… steady and calm. “Step three: Try again.” He made another B right next to it and circled the one he liked. “Both are B’s,” he said with a grin. “Both try their best.”

Pebble kept going, one small step at a time. He wrote, he drew, he whispered the words. He paused to sip warm water from his penguin cup. The cup felt smooth and cozy in his flipper. He smiled at his fairy lights, at his blanket with stars, at his friend Wally cheering silently.

Soon, Pebble finished his little book. He ran his flipper over the cover. “The Brave Little Book by Pebble,” he read. His voice trembled, but it was a happy tremble.

He toddled to the mirror on his closet door. He looked at his own bright eyes. “Hello, Pebble,” he said softly. “You tried. You kept going. That is brave.” His chest felt big and proud, like a warm balloon.

Pebble showed the book to Wally and read out loud. “Pip the penguin found the lost button by looking carefully and asking for help. Pip felt proud for trying and thinking.”

Pebble nodded. “I felt worried. I felt frustrated. I used my thinking steps. I did small steps. I asked, ‘Is it true?’ I tried again. And now I feel proud.”

He hugged Wally and did a tiny happy dance on the squeaky bed. Squeak-squeak! He giggled. “My words matter. I matter.”

Pebble put his book on the desk. The clock went tick-tick, soft and slow. The fairy lights twinkled like friendly stars.

Pebble whispered to the room, “I am Pebble. I can learn. I can try. I can write my own story.”

The End.
