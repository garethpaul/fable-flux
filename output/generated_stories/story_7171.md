# Tiko the Tortoise Tries

In a cozy home office, a wise tortoise named Tiko lived with bright plants, soft rugs, and a big sunny window. The keyboard clicked like tiny raindrops. The printer hummed like a happy bee. Tiko loved the gentle sounds. He loved the smell of warm paper and the squeak of his rolling chair. He kept a tiny pencil behind his ear and a notebook by his lamp.

One morning, Tiko watched a sports video on the computer. Ping! The screen showed children from many places playing together—soccer, dancing, tossing beanbags, and yoga. Their skin, hair, and clothes were all different and colorful. They smiled and cheered for one another. Tiko’s eyes felt bright.

“I want to try,” Tiko said in his slow, kind voice. “I may be slow, but I am steady. I can learn.”

He taped a paper to the wall: “Goal: Try a new sport today.” The tape made a soft rip. Tiko took a deep breath. His shell felt strong. His heart felt a little fluttery.

First, Tiko tried soccer. He set a soft ball on the rug. “Tap, tap,” he told his feet. He waddled and bumped the ball gently. The ball rolled under the desk. “Oh!” said Tiko. He peeked under. Dust tickled his nose. “Achoo!”

Tiko pulled the ball out and tried again. Tap. Tap-tap. The ball rolled to the door, then bonked the trash bin. “Oops.” Tiko’s cheeks warmed. “Maybe soccer is hard for me right now,” he whispered. He wrote in his notebook, “I tried. I can practice.”

Next, he tried beanbag toss. He set a basket by the window and picked up a blue beanbag. The fabric felt cool and soft. “One, two, throw,” Tiko said. The beanbag flew… and landed on his shell. “Plof.” Tiko blinked, then giggled. “My shell caught it!”

He tried again. “One, two, throw.” The beanbag whooshed, then plopped into the basket. Tiko clapped. “I did it!” His heart felt like a warm cookie. He wrote, “Small steps work.”

Just then, his tablet chimed. Friends were on a video call. A bunny named Nia waved, a penguin named Pep waddled in, and a giraffe named Sori bent down to fit on the screen. Their voices sounded happy.

“What are you doing, Tiko?” asked Nia, her ears bouncing.

“I am trying sports,” said Tiko. “I want to learn and feel proud.”

Pep nodded. “I like sliding. But I slip a lot first.”

Sori smiled. “My legs are long. Sometimes I trip. We can try together!”

Tiko felt brave. “Let’s do a tiny sports time,” he said. “We can cheer for each other.”

They picked three small sports: gentle yoga, beanbag toss, and chair soccer. The home office turned into a soft gym. The rug was the field. The chair was the goal. The lamp made a golden spotlight.

“First, yoga,” said Tiko. He showed a turtle pose. He tucked his head and breathed in slowly. The air smelled like lemon tea. “In and out,” he said. “I am calm, I am kind.” His shell pressed the rug. He felt steady.

Nia tried bunny stretch. Pep did penguin flap. Sori reached her long neck and smiled. They all moved differently, and that was okay. “We are all unique,” said Tiko. “Different bodies, same big hearts.”

“Now beanbag toss,” said Tiko. “Aim. Breathe. Throw.” Nia missed and giggled. Pep hit the basket and cheered. Sori tossed softly and made a swish. Tiko’s first toss missed. His second toss made it in. “I kept trying,” he said. “That makes me proud.”

“Last, chair soccer!” Pep tapped the ball with a little kick. Nia blocked with a book. Sori waved a scarf as a flag. Tiko took his time. Tap… tap… tap. He aimed for the chair. The ball rolled gently and stopped right at the chair leg. “Almost!” said Tiko. He smiled. “I can try again.”

He looked at his paper on the wall. Goal: Try a new sport today. He put a star next to it. “I tried,” he said. “I learned. I improved a little.”

Nia clapped. “You are wise and brave.”

Pep chirped, “Steady steps!”

Sori nodded. “We cheer for effort.”

Tiko felt his chest glow. “My speed is my own,” he said. “Slow is fine. I can grow, one little step at a time.”

They all took a sip of water. The cool water felt like rain on a hot day. The room smelled like crayons and lemon. The sun made the desk shine.

Tiko picked up his pencil and wrote a new note: “I am proud of me.” He drew a small tortoise with a big smile. He taped the note next to his goal. Rip. Press. Smooth.

He turned to his friends. “Today I learned that self-esteem means liking who I am, inside and out. I can try, I can practice, and I can improve. We may look different and move different, but we can play and help each other.”

“Team Tiko!” they cheered.

Tiko laughed. “Team Us,” he said. “We all belong.”

He closed the computer and listened to the soft hum of the home office. Click. Whirr. The sounds felt like a lullaby. Tiko curled into his chair, warm and calm.

“I am enough,” he whispered. “And I can still grow.”

The End.
