# Hoot the Owl’s Rainbow Words

In a warm kitchen, steam puffed like little clouds from a silver pot. The air smelled like cinnamon toast and orange jam. Sunlight painted yellow squares on the floor. A wise old owl named Hoot sat on a stool by the window, his soft feathers the color of tea. His round eyes blinked kindly behind little round glasses.

“Good morning, words,” Hoot hooted softly. “Today, we learn together.”

Tiny feet pattered into the kitchen. It was Mina, Hoot’s neighbor and friend. Mina wore a blue apron with a star on it. She loved to visit Hoot and help him make breakfast.

“Hi, Hoot!” Mina said, hopping up to sit by him. “What are we making?”

Hoot tapped the cookbook with his wing. “We are making toast from around the world,” he said. “And we will say hello in many ways.”

Mina’s eyes got big. “Hello in many ways?”

Hoot nodded. “Yes, little star. Words are like colors. They make our world bright.”

Mina clapped. “I want to try!”

Hoot smiled. “First, we prepare.” He pointed to four little bowls. “Preparation helps our minds feel calm. It is part of self-regulation.”

Mina took a big breath in and a slow breath out. “I’m ready.”

“Good,” said Hoot. “Bowl one has butter. Bowl two has honey. Bowl three has jam. Bowl four has avocado. We will toast bread and make tiny bites. Each bite will have a word.”

The toaster clicked. The smell of warm bread filled the room. Hoot placed slices on a wooden board. “Now we begin our word walk.”

He spread butter on one toast triangle. “In English we say hello,” he said, “like this: Hello!”

“Hello!” Mina echoed, smiling.

Hoot spread honey on the next triangle. “In Spanish, we say hola.” He said it slowly. “Oh-la.”

Mina tried. “Oh-la!” Her voice was sticky-sweet like the honey. She giggled. “It sounds sunny.”

“Sunny and friendly,” Hoot agreed.

He put jam on the third triangle. “In French, we say bonjour. Bon-jour.”

Mina sang, “Bon-jour,” soft as jam. “It feels like a song.”

“Oui,” Hoot chuckled.

He mashed avocado on the fourth triangle. “In Swahili, we say jambo. Jam-bo.”

“Jam-bo!” Mina said, bouncing a little. “It sounds like a drum.”

They tasted each bite after saying each word. Crunch. Sweet. Smooth. Tangy. The kitchen felt warm in Mina’s chest. The words felt warm, too.

Hoot poured two small cups of mint tea. “Let’s breathe and listen.” They held their cups and sat quiet. The clock ticked. A kettle hummed. The world felt calm.

Hoot spoke softly. “When we learn new words, we go slow. We prepare our lips and ears. We take turns. We show care.”

Mina nodded. “I can do that.”

They made a second round of toast. Hoot said, “In Mandarin, we say nǐ hǎo.” He shaped the sounds carefully. “Nee how.”

Mina tried. She paused. “Nee… how.” She frowned. “It is hard.”

Hoot touched the table gently with one wing. “It is new,” he said. “Let’s prepare again.” He breathed in and out. Mina copied him. Together, they said, “Nee how.”

Mina’s face lit up. “I did it!”

Hoot’s eyes shone. “Yes. We practice. We are patient with ourselves.”

Next came Arabic. “We say marhaba,” Hoot said. “Mar-ha-ba.”

“Mar-ha-ba,” Mina said slowly. The word rolled like warm bread in her mouth.

Then Japanese. “Konnichiwa.”

“Koh-nee-chee-wah!” Mina grinned. “It feels bouncy.”

Hoot nodded. “Every word is a little bridge. Bridges help us meet friends far away.”

Mina thought about that. “If I say hola, a friend in Spain might smile. If I say jambo, a friend in Kenya might smile.”

Hoot pointed to the globe cookie jar on the shelf. “Our kitchen is warm, but our hearts can be big like the whole world.”

A tiny worry tickled Mina. “What if I say it wrong? What if I make a mistake?”

Hoot tapped his glasses. “We give ourselves gentle words. We can say, ‘I can try again.’ We can ask, ‘Can you help me?’ That is self-regulation and kindness.”

Mina took a careful sip of tea. “I can try again.”

They played a game. Hoot would point to a bowl, and Mina would say a hello word to match a flavor. Sweet honey was hola. Smooth avocado was jambo. Bright jam was bonjour. Simple butter was hello. When Hoot mixed it up, Mina laughed and corrected him kindly, “No, that’s jamb… oh! Jam-bo!”

They made tiny flags from paper and toothpicks and stuck them in the toast. The colors looked like a rainbow of countries. The kitchen smelled like toast and mint and a little bit of victory.

A soft knock came at the door. It was Mrs. Kim from next door. “Annyeong!” she said with a smile.

Mina’s eyes sparkled. She looked at Hoot. Hoot nodded.

“Ann-yeong,” Mina said, carefully and kindly.

Mrs. Kim clapped. “Beautiful!”

Mina beamed. “We are learning hello words. Would you like toast?”

“Yes, please,” said Mrs. Kim. She sat with them, and they shared warm bites and warm words.

When the plates were clean, Hoot closed the cookbook. “What did we learn today?” he asked.

Mina counted on her fingers. “We learned to prepare. We breathed. We went slow. We tried new sounds. We were kind to ourselves. And we learned that words are bridges to friends.”

Hoot hooted happily. “And we learned that our warm kitchen can hold the whole world.”

Mina hugged Hoot’s soft feathers. “Hello, world,” she whispered. “I’m ready to listen.”

The End.
