# Thank You, Little Hooves

The gentle deer fawn stepped into the creative art room. The floor felt cool on her tiny hooves. Tap, tap, tap. The room smelled like crayons, fresh paper, and a little bit like sweet glue. Sunlight made warm squares on the floor.

“Hello,” she whispered. “I’m Fawn.” Her ears twitched with curiosity. She wanted to see everything.

Paints sat in neat cups—red, blue, yellow, and green. Brushes stood like little trees. A shelf held soft clay. Sparkly stickers slept in a box. In the corner, a big easel waited, clean and white.

Fawn’s heart felt happy. “I want to try it all!” she said.

A friendly voice answered. “Welcome, Fawn.” It was Ms. Lark, the art teacher. She had kind eyes and a gentle smile. “In this room, we explore. We use kind words. We ask before we touch friends. We say thank you.”

Fawn nodded. She liked those rules. They felt warm and safe.

At a big table, children made art. A boy swirled paint in circles. “Wooosh,” he said, moving his brush. A girl pressed clay. “Squish, squish.” A small rabbit in a smock stuck stars onto a card. “Twinkle!”

Fawn watched with wide eyes. Her little tail flicked. “May I look?” she asked the boy.

“Yes,” he said, smiling. “Please look.” Fawn leaned close. The paint smelled sweet and a little like fruit. The colors shone like rain.

“Thank you,” Fawn said. Gratitude tickled warm in her chest.

She turned to the clay. “May I touch?” she asked the girl.

The girl nodded. “You may touch one small piece.”

Fawn pressed the clay. It was cool and soft. “Squish,” she giggled. “Thank you.” She gave the piece back. She felt proud. She had asked first.

Ms. Lark set a tray in front of Fawn. “Here is your space,” she said. “These are your tools. You may make a picture.”

Fawn’s heart jumped. “I can make a forest!” She dipped a brush into green paint. “Drip.” A drop fell on her hoof. It felt wet and funny. She laughed. She made a tall line. “Tree,” she whispered. She added dots of red. “Berries.” She made a blue sky. “Swish, swish.” She made a soft brown shape. “Me,” she said. “I am in my forest.”

The rabbit hopped closer. “I like your berries,” he said. “May I try a dot?”

Fawn looked at her picture. She liked to share, but she also liked her space. She took a breath. “This is my picture,” she said softly. “You can make berries on your own paper. I can show you how.”

The rabbit’s ears wiggled. “Okay! Thank you for telling me.” He smiled. Ms. Lark nodded. “That was kind,” she said. “You used your words. You made a boundary.”

Fawn felt steady and strong. She showed the rabbit how to hold the brush. “Tap, tap, tap.” The dots were bright. The rabbit said, “Thank you, Fawn.”

“You’re welcome,” Fawn said. Her chest felt warm again. Gratitude felt like a hug.

Across the table, the boy spun his brush fast. “Wheee!” A splash of blue flew—splat!—onto Fawn’s picture. A big blue dot sat on her brown deer shape.

Fawn’s eyes grew big. Her ears drooped. “Oh.” She felt a little sad.

The boy’s mouth made a small O. “I’m sorry,” he said. “I was too fast. Are you okay?”

Fawn took a slow breath. She looked at her painting. “I feel sad,” she said. “Please be careful. I worked hard.”

“I will slow down,” the boy said. He held his brush still. “May I help fix it?”

Fawn thought. The blue dot looked like a flower. “Yes,” she said. “Let’s make a flower.” They dabbed green for a stem. “Tap.” They added yellow dots for pollen. “Pop, pop.”

“It’s pretty,” the boy said.

“Thank you for helping,” Fawn said.

“Thank you for the idea,” the boy said back. They smiled at each other. Fawn felt lighter. With calm words and thankful hearts, they made something new.

Ms. Lark clapped softly. “Beautiful teamwork,” she said. “You used careful hands. You used kind words. You turned a problem into art.”

Next, Ms. Lark brought a small tray with bells. “Would you like to make a sound picture?” she asked.

Fawn’s eyes sparkled. She loved gentle sounds. “Yes,” she said. She tied a tiny bell to her brush. “Jingle,” it sang as she painted. The bell tickled her ears. She moved her brush slow and steady. Jingle… jingle… swish.

“My body can move slow,” Fawn said. “I can control my hooves.” She smiled and took her time. She liked the steady rhythm. The room became a soft song—tap, swish, squish, jingle.

When Fawn finished, she held up her painting. The forest glowed with dots and lines. The blue flower smiled by her deer shape. She felt proud, and she felt thankful.

“Thank you, Ms. Lark, for the paints,” Fawn said.

“Thank you, friends, for sharing the room,” she added.

“Thank you, Fawn, for your kind words,” Ms. Lark said. The rabbit and the boy chimed, “Thank you!”

Fawn’s heart felt full and bright. She learned that asking before touching makes friends feel safe. She learned that careful, slow moves help her art. She learned that saying thank you makes everyone smile. And when mistakes happen, kind words and gratitude can make them into something beautiful.

Fawn tucked her painting close. “I am grateful,” she whispered. “I can use my words. I can try new things. I can be gentle with myself and with friends.”

They all waved goodbye. “See you tomorrow,” Ms. Lark said.

Fawn trotted out. Tap, tap, tap. The sunlight felt warm on her back. She hummed a tiny jingle as she went. Her heart carried the art room’s song—care, curiosity, and thank you.

The End.
