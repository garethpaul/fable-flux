# The Treehouse Tune

Toby was a shy turtle. He liked quiet ponds and soft moss. One sunny day, Toby climbed, step by slow step, up to a wooden treehouse. The treehouse smelled like fresh pine and warm sunshine. Birds sang, “Tweet, tweet,” and leaves made a gentle shhhh sound in the breeze.

Inside the treehouse, friends were gathering. There was Mia the mouse with tiny drums, Ben the bluebird with a shiny whistle, and Lila the lamb with a small guitar. They smiled when Toby peeked in.

“Hi, Toby!” said Lila, her voice like a song. “We are making music for the Friendly Forest Festival. Do you want to join?”

Toby tucked his head a little into his shell. His heart felt thump-thump. His cheeks felt warm. “Maybe,” he whispered. “I feel shy.”

“That’s okay,” said Mia, tapping her drum gently. “You can listen first.”

Toby sat on a soft rug and listened. Mia’s drums went tap-tap-tap. Ben’s whistle went whee-whee! Lila strummed, strum-strum. The sounds tickled Toby’s ears. The treehouse floor hummed. Sunlight sprinkled the walls like yellow confetti.

“This is fun,” said Ben. “Let’s all play louder!”

They played louder and faster. Tap-tap-tap! Wheee! Strum! The music bounced around the treehouse. Toby’s tummy felt tight. The sounds felt like big waves. He pulled into his shell a little more.

Lila noticed. She put her guitar down and sat beside Toby. “How do you feel inside, Toby?” she asked softly.

Toby took a slow breath. “I feel… too much,” he said. “My heart is fast. My tummy is twisty. I feel shy and a little scared.”

Mia nodded. “Thank you for telling us. Your feelings are important.”

Ben fluttered to the window. “We can make the music softer,” he said. “Like a feather.”

“Let’s try a calm song,” said Lila. “Toby, would you like to help make a soft, slow tune?”

Toby peeped out from his shell. “Maybe I can tap with my toe,” he said. He wiggled his small turtle foot. Tap… tap…

They started again. Mia’s drum was quiet: pat-pat. Ben’s whistle was gentle: woo-woo. Lila strummed slowly: strum… hum. Toby tapped his toe: tap… tap…

The treehouse felt cozy. The music was like a blanket. Toby’s heart slowed down. His tummy felt loose. He smiled a small smile.

“I like this feeling,” Toby said. “It is calm. I can breathe.”

Lila smiled back. “Music can change feelings. We can choose soft or loud. We can talk about it. That is how we help each other.”

Just then, a new friend arrived. Pip the squirrel burst in, tail flicking, eyes bright. “Let’s play super fast!” Pip shouted. He grabbed two spoons and clacked them: CLACK-CLACK-CLACK!

Toby jumped. His shell shook. “Oh!” he squeaked, scooting back.

Pip froze. “Did I scare you?” he asked, his voice small now.

Toby took another slow breath, in… and out… “I feel startled,” he said. “The loud clacks hurt my ears. I feel shy and tight again.”

Pip’s ears drooped. “I didn’t know. I feel sorry.”

Ben fluttered down. “We can solve this,” he said. “We can make a plan together.”

Mia tapped a gentle rhythm: pat… pat… “Let’s use our listening voices,” she said. “We can take turns. We can ask, ‘Is this okay?’”

Pip nodded. “Can I try a quieter clack?” he asked Toby.

Toby listened. “Yes,” he said. “But soft, please.”

Pip held the spoons lightly. Clack… clack… soft and slow. Toby breathed in the pine smell, felt the warm sunlight, and heard the quiet, friendly beats. He smiled wider.

“I like that,” Toby said. “Thank you for asking.”

Lila clapped softly. “We found a way that works for everyone! That is kind and smart.”

They practiced their song for the festival. They made a music map:

- Start soft to say hello.
- Grow a little louder for the happy part.
- Come back to calm to say goodbye.

Before each change, Ben would ask, “Is this okay?” Everyone would nod or share a feeling. Toby would say, “I feel ready,” or “Let’s slow down,” and the friends listened.

Soon, it was time to play for the Friendly Forest Festival. The treehouse doors were open. The breeze carried the music like a ribbon. Animals gathered below—rabbits, frogs, even a sleepy fox.

Toby stood in front with a small wooden block and a tiny mallet. He held it with steady hands. “I can start,” he said, his voice brave and clear. Tap… tap… pat… woo… strum…

The song rose and fell like waves at a gentle beach. When it grew louder, Ben asked, “Okay?” Toby smiled. “Okay!” The friends watched each other’s faces. They noticed feelings. They kept the music kind.

When the song ended, the forest cheered. The claps were soft because of the sign Mia made: a picture of an ear and a heart. Everyone smiled and wiggled quietly.

Pip hugged Toby. “You helped me learn,” he said. “Feelings matter.”

Toby felt warm and light, like a sunbeam on a leaf. “We can say our feelings,” he said. “We can listen. We can make a plan. That helps everyone feel safe.”

The friends packed up the drum, the whistle, the guitar, the spoons, and the little block. The treehouse glowed with late-day gold. Leaves whispered good job.

On the way down the steps, Toby hummed their soft hello song. He felt proud and calm.

The clear lesson: When we notice our feelings, use gentle words, and listen to each other, we can solve problems together—and make beautiful music, too.

The End.
