# Koala Kiki’s Porch Picnic Plan

Koala Kiki sat on a peaceful front porch, swinging gently on a soft squeaky chair. The morning air smelled like sweet leaves and warm sun. A tiny breeze tickled her fuzzy ears. Birds went tweet-tweet. Bees hummed hum-hum in the flowers. Kiki felt calm and happy.

“My tummy says grr-rumble,” Kiki giggled, patting her belly. “I want a picnic! A yummy, healthy picnic.”

Her friend, Pip the parrot, landed on the porch rail with a flutter. “Picnic? Squawk! What will we eat, Kiki?”

Kiki wiggled her toes. “We will eat rainbow foods. Rainbow foods help our bodies grow strong and feel good.”

“Rainbow foods?” Pip tilted his bright head.

“Yes,” Kiki said. “Red, orange, yellow, green, blue, and purple. Different colors have different good things for us. That is called nutrition. Nutrition is how food helps our bodies.”

Pip flapped happily. “Let’s find colors!”

Kiki took a deep breath and looked around. “We will be resourceful. That means we use what we have and make a plan.”

First, Kiki found a small basket. It was plain and a bit wobbly. “Hmm,” she hummed. “We can make it better.” She pulled out a roll of twine and little cloth squares from a porch drawer. “We will craft a picnic basket.”

Snip-snip went Kiki’s safe scissors, cutting tiny triangles of cloth. She asked, “Pip, please hold the twine.”

“Hold twine! Squawk!” Pip held it with his claws.

Kiki tied knots slowly and carefully. Knot, knot, pull. She glued the colorful cloth around the basket rim. It looked like a happy crown. “Ta-da!” Kiki smiled. “Our basket is strong and pretty.”

Now it was time to find rainbow foods. Kiki looked at a bowl on the porch table. Inside were apples, carrots, bananas, and a little box of blueberries. She also saw a small container of spinach leaves and a cup of yogurt in a cooler bag.

She pointed and counted on her fingers. “Red apple. Orange carrot sticks. Yellow banana. Blue blueberries. Green spinach. We need purple.”

Pip peered into the cooler. “Grapes?”

Kiki opened the cooler with a soft pop. “Yes! Purple grapes.” She washed the fruit in a bowl of clean water, swish-swish, and patted them dry with a towel. “Clean food is happy food.”

They placed the foods in the basket, one color at a time. The basket looked like a tiny rainbow garden.

Pip blinked at the yogurt. “What is that white stuff?”

“Yogurt,” Kiki said. “It is creamy and helps our tummies. We can make fruit-yogurt cups. That is a healthy snack.”

Kiki felt a little nervous. “We need small cups and little spoons.” She looked around the porch and did not see any. “Hmm. What can we use?” She breathed in, slow and calm. “We can be resourceful again. We can craft them!”

Kiki found clean paper and a roll of tape. She folded the paper into tiny cups—fold, fold, tap-tap with tape. Pip helped by holding the edges. “Teamwork!” Pip chirped.

Kiki whispered, “I will be brave with the scissors.” She remembered to cut safely, with the blades pointing down and slow little snips. Snip, snip. She made simple spoon shapes from sturdy paper and taped a second layer to make them strong. “Courage can be quiet,” Kiki said. “Being careful and trying new things is brave, too.”

The porch smelled like fresh fruit now. Kiki spooned yogurt into the cups. Plop. She added blueberries—pop-pop—and tiny pieces of apple and banana. She sprinkled chopped spinach leaves into a few cups. “Green gives us vitamins,” Kiki said. “We try a little to learn what we like.”

Pip tasted a cup. “Mmm! Creamy-cold and sweet.”

Kiki nibbled a carrot stick. Crunch! “Carrots help our eyes.” She took a bite of apple. “Apples help our hearts.” She popped a blueberry. “Blueberries help our brains.” She wiggled a spinach leaf. “Greens help our muscles.” She peeled the banana. “Bananas help our energy.” She plucked a grape. “Grapes help us feel good and hydrated.”

Pip asked, “What if I do not like the green?”

Kiki smiled. “That is okay. We try a tiny bite. We listen to our bodies. We can try again another day.”

The sun moved higher, warm on their fur and feathers. The porch wood felt smooth under Kiki’s feet. A little ant marched by, carrying a crumb. “Look,” Kiki said softly. “The ant is resourceful, too. It carries food home.”

Suddenly, the wind sent a napkin flying. Whoosh! It sailed toward the steps.

“Oh!” Pip yelped, flapping.

Kiki held her breath. “We can be brave.” She walked calmly to the step, reached out, and caught the napkin. “Slow and steady,” she said. “We did it.”

They set the napkin under the basket to keep it safe. Then they ate their rainbow picnic on the peaceful front porch. Birds sang. Bees hummed. Their tummies felt happy and full.

Kiki patted the basket crown. “We crafted with care. We used what we had. We ate rainbow foods.”

Pip nodded. “We were brave and calm. Squawk! I learned about nutrition.”

Kiki smiled. “Me too. Today’s lesson: Eat many colors, try small bites, and use kindness and courage when we make and share food.”

Pip chirped, “Rainbow food makes a rainbow you!”

Kiki giggled. “Yes. Healthy food helps us grow, play, and think.”

They cleaned up, washing cups and spoons, and put scraps in the compost. The porch looked neat again. The air felt soft, and the sun glowed like a cozy lamp.

Kiki stretched and sighed. “What a good day.”

Pip tucked his wings. “What a good snack.”

They both listened to the gentle porch sounds—creak of the chair, tweet of birds, hum of bees—and felt proud.

The End.
