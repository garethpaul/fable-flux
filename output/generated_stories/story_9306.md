# Toby's Big Wait

In the busy basement of the Miller house lived a shy little turtle named Toby. His shell was green and brown, like autumn leaves. The basement was always full of sounds - washing machines going WHOOSH, dryers tumbling THUMP-THUMP, and footsteps going CLOP-CLOP-CLOP overhead.

Toby loved to hide in his cozy corner behind the old toy box. But today was special. Today was Game Day!

"Come on, Toby!" called his friend Rosie the rabbit. "We're playing Musical Boxes! When the music stops, find an empty box to sit in!"

Toby peeked out from his shell. All around the basement, colorful cardboard boxes sat in a big circle. His friends were already running around - Benny the bear, Rosie the rabbit, and Penny the pig.

The music started. DING-A-LING-LING went the little music box. Everyone began walking around the boxes.

But Toby was slow. His little legs couldn't move as fast as the others. Step... step... step... he went, while his friends practically danced around the circle.

CLICK! The music stopped.

"Quick, find a box!" shouted Benny.

Rosie hopped into a red box. Benny squeezed into a blue one. Penny plopped into a yellow box. But poor Toby was still walking slowly toward the green box when Penny called out, "Sorry Toby, no more boxes!"

Toby felt his eyes get watery. "This game isn't fair," he whispered. "I'm too slow."

"Don't give up!" said Rosie kindly. "Let's try something different."

"What if we walk slowly like Toby?" suggested Benny. "That way everyone has the same chance."

"But that's boring," complained Penny. "I like running fast!"

Toby pulled his head into his shell. The basement suddenly felt too loud and too busy.

Then Rosie had an idea. "What if we practice patience? We can all start walking when the music plays, but we promise to be fair and give everyone time to find a box."

"How do we do that?" asked Penny.

"We count to ten slowly after the music stops," explained Benny. "One... two... three... That gives everyone time to choose."

Toby poked his head out. "Really? You'd wait for me?"

"Of course!" said his friends together.

They tried again. The music played DING-A-LING-LING. This time, everyone walked at a steady pace. When the music stopped, they all counted together: "One... two... three... four... five... six... seven... eight... nine... ten!"

Toby had plenty of time to reach the purple box. He climbed in just as they finished counting.

"I did it!" Toby smiled big. "This is much more fun!"

They played three more rounds. Sometimes Rosie was out, sometimes Benny. But everyone had a fair chance because they practiced patience together.

"You know what?" said Penny. "Waiting and being patient made the game better for everyone."

"And practicing patience helped me be brave," added Toby, no longer hiding in his shell.

From that day on, whenever they played games in the busy basement, they always remembered Toby's lesson. Taking time and being patient made everything more fair and fun for everyone.

Toby learned that being different wasn't bad - it just meant his friends needed to practice patience and kindness. And that made their friendship even stronger.

The End.
