# Koa the Calm Koala and the Busy Bathroom

Koa the calm koala loved quiet mornings. He had soft gray fur and gentle eyes. One sunny day, Koa padded into the bright, steamy bathroom. The mirror was foggy, and the air smelled like minty soap and warm towels.

Koa took a deep breath. “Ahh,” he hummed. “Time to brush my teeth and get ready for park time.”

He picked up his tiny toothbrush. “Swish, swish,” he sang softly, moving it in little circles. He liked to move slowly and follow directions. He remembered the steps on the picture chart by the sink: wet brush, tiny dot of paste, gentle circles, spit, rinse.

Just then, his friend Wally the wombat thumped in. Thump, thump! Wally’s paws were muddy. His whiskers wiggled with excitement. “Koa! Let’s go! Let’s race to the big playground!” he chirped.

Koa smiled. “I am almost ready, Wally. I need to finish brushing.”

Wally bounced on his toes. Tap, tap, tap. He grabbed the faucet and turned it on all the way. WHOOSH! Water splashed everywhere—on the counter, on the mirror, and on Koa’s soft fur.

Koa blinked. His fur felt cold and wet. “Oh,” he said gently. “That is a lot of water.”

“I like big splashes!” Wally giggled. He hopped from foot to foot, making the bath mat wiggle. “Let’s hurry! Let’s zoom!”

Koa put his paw down, calm and steady. “Wally,” he said in a soft voice, “I feel wet and chilly. I don’t like big splashes on me. Please turn the water down.”

Wally paused. His ears drooped a little. “But I want to go fast,” he said.

Koa nodded. “I want to go fast to the park too. If we follow directions, we will be ready soon. And we can move and play outside. First, bathroom time. Then, park time.”

Wally watched the water splash. He looked at Koa’s damp fur. “I didn’t know you felt chilly,” he said. “I’m sorry.”

“Thank you,” said Koa. “That is kind.” He handed Wally a soft towel. “We can clean together.”

They dabbed the counter. Dab, dab. They wiped the mirror. Swish, swish. The bathroom smelled like clean cotton now. The mirror shone bright.

Koa showed the picture chart. “Step one,” he said, pointing, “wet brush.” He turned the faucet to a small trickle. “Step two, tiny dot of paste.” A little minty blob squeezed out with a quiet squish.

Wally copied Koa. “Tiny dot,” he said, squeezing a small bit. “I can do gentle circles too.”

They brushed together. “Circle, circle,” sang Koa. “Front teeth, back teeth, little teeth.” Foam tickled their tongues. They giggled. “Ptoo!” They spit into the sink and rinsed with a small sip of water. “Sip, swish, spit.” It sounded like a little rain.

Wally wiped his mouth and stood very still. “Koa,” he whispered, “I like when you talk calm. It helps me slow down.”

Koa smiled. “Thank you. You are a good friend to listen.”

Just then, Tia the turtle peeked in. “May I have a turn?” she asked in a tiny voice. “I need to wash my hands.”

Wally started to rush to the sink again, but Koa put a gentle paw on his shoulder. “Let’s show respect,” he said. “We take turns so everyone feels good.”

Wally looked at Tia. She held her little shell close and waited. He stepped back. “Your turn, Tia,” he said. “I can wait.”

Tia smiled. “Thank you.” She turned the faucet to a small stream, just like Koa did. She rubbed soap—squish, squish—and sang, “Tops and bottoms, in between,” as she rinsed. “All done!”

“Great following directions,” Koa said. “Now we are all ready.”

They hung the towels neatly. The bathroom felt warm and peaceful again. Koa’s fur was dry. Wally’s paws were clean. Tia’s hands were shiny and fresh.

“Park time?” Wally asked, eyes bright.

“Park time,” Koa agreed.

Outside, the sun felt cozy on their faces. The grass smelled sweet and green. Birds chirped, “Cheep, cheep!” The friends moved their bodies with joy. They stretched long like tall trees. They hopped like kangaroos—boing, boing. They balanced on the low log—arms out, slow steps, careful toes.

“Let’s play a taking-turns game,” Koa said. “We can all go down the slide. We wait. We cheer. We show respect.”

Wally nodded. “I go after Tia,” he said. “Then Koa.”

Tia slid first. “Whee!” Wally clapped. Koa smiled. Wally slid next, gentle and safe. “Woo-hoo!” Tia clapped. Koa slid last, calm and happy. “What a nice breeze,” he said, feeling the wind on his fur.

They ran in the open field, then marched in a line, stepping over pretend rocks. “Lift knees, place feet,” Koa said. “Follow directions, keep space.” They laughed and moved and breathed in the fresh air.

On the walk home, Wally squeezed Koa’s paw. “I liked the bathroom better when we took turns,” he said. “It felt kind.”

Koa nodded. “Respect means listening to how others feel, sharing space, and waiting our turn.”

Wally smiled. “I can do that. I can be gentle and still have fun!”

Koa’s eyes shone. “Yes. When we respect each other, everyone has a good day—inside and outside.”

The End.
