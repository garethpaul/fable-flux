# Owl Hoots in the Guest Room

Hootley the wise old owl blinked his big golden eyes. He was visiting a cozy house with a soft, blue guest room. The guest room smelled like clean blankets and warm soap. Sunlight made tiny squares on the rug. A ticking clock said, “tick-tock, tick-tock.”

Hootley fluffed his feathers and smiled. “What a nice place,” he hooted gently. “I will rest here.”

On the bed sat a small puzzle box. It was wooden and smooth. It had a shiny button, a twisty knob, and a little picture of a moon. A paper note said, “Can you open me?”

Hootley felt curious. “Hoo-hoo! A puzzle,” he said. He loved learning. He loved solving things. He loved using a plan.

He tapped the button. Nothing. He turned the knob. Click, but still closed. He slid his wing along the moon picture. No open. Hootley’s feathers ruffled.

“I feel a little frustrated,” he said, breathing in. The guest room was quiet, and the clock kept ticking. Hootley took a slow breath in and a slow breath out. “When I feel stuck, I can use a strategy,” he said.

He said his plan out loud, in simple steps.
“Step one: Look carefully.”
“Step two: Try one thing at a time.”
“Step three: Be patient.”
“Step four: Ask for help if I need it.”
“Step five: Be honest about how I feel.”

Hootley looked carefully. He used his smart owl eyes. He saw tiny arrows near the knob. He saw a little line by the button. He saw a very small star next to the moon picture.

“Oh,” he hooted softly. “The arrows point left. The line is thin. The star is shiny.”

He tried one thing at a time. He turned the knob left. Click! He smiled. He pressed the button very gently. Tap. He slid the moon picture to the side. Scritch. The box still did not open.

Hootley felt his tummy twist. “I am feeling frustrated again,” he said honestly. He put his wing on his chest. He took another slow breath in and out. He listened to the clock. Tick-tock, tick-tock. The quiet beat helped him feel calm.

He whispered, “Be patient, Hootley. Try again. You can do this.”

He looked again and noticed a soft sound when he pressed the button and turned the knob at the same time. “Hmm,” he thought. “Maybe these parts work together.”

He made a new plan.
“New strategy: Press, then turn, then slide, slowly.”

He pressed the button. He turned the knob left. He slid the moon picture. Click-clack-scritch! The box moved a little. He felt excited.

Just then, the door squeaked. A small child peeked in. “Hi, Owl,” the child said. “That box is tricky.”

“Yes,” Hootley said kindly. “I am using a plan. I am taking my time. I am telling the truth about my feelings. I felt frustrated, but I am trying again.”

The child nodded. “Sometimes I get mad at puzzles.”

“It’s okay to feel mad,” said Hootley. “We can breathe. We can think. We can try steps. Would you like to help?”

“Yes, please,” said the child. They sat together on the soft rug. The rug felt warm and fuzzy under their toes.

Hootley said, “Let me explain the steps. First, look. What do you see?”

“I see little arrows,” said the child. “They point left.”

“Good knowledge,” said Hootley. “Second, try one thing at a time. You press the button. I will turn the knob.”

“Okay,” said the child. They pressed. Hootley turned. Click. Nothing.

“I feel a little mad,” said the child.

“Thank you for being honest,” said Hootley. “Let’s use patience. Breathe with me.” They breathed in and out together. The air smelled like clean blankets. The clock said, “tick-tock.”

“Third,” said Hootley, “change the order. Strategy means making a smart plan and changing it if needed. I will press. You turn. Then we slide together.”

They tried it. Hootley pressed. The child turned. They slid together. Click-clack-scritch… Pop! The box opened!

Inside was a tiny bell and a note. The bell was silver and shiny. The note said, “Great strategy! You used your brain and your calm.”

The child giggled and rang the bell. It made a gentle ding-ding. Hootley chuckled. “We did it,” he said. “We used knowledge. We used patience. We told the truth about our feelings. We made a plan. We changed the plan. That is strategy.”

The child smiled. “When I feel frustrated again, I will breathe, think, and try steps.”

Hootley nodded. “Yes. Look carefully. Try one thing at a time. Be patient. Ask for help. Be honest. That is a wise way.”

They put the bell back in the box and closed it softly. The guest room felt bright and happy. The sun made warm shapes on the floor. The clock ticked softly, like a tiny drum.

Hootley fluffed his feathers and gave a final hoot. “Today we learned that strategy helps us solve problems. We can use our minds and our calm hearts.”

The child waved. “Thank you, wise owl!”

“Thank you, helper friend,” Hootley said. “Together is a good strategy, too.”

The End.
