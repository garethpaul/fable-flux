# The Fawn Who Found the Fair Song

The warm kitchen smelled like cinnamon bread and sweet apples. Sunlight made golden squares on the floor. A gentle deer fawn named Dori stood on soft hooves by the big wooden table. Her ears twitched to the tiny tick-tick of the clock and the hum of the kettle.

“I love sounds,” Dori whispered. “They are like friends.”

On the counter sat a shiny bell, wooden spoons, and a little drum with a blue strap. Dori’s nose wiggled. Tap-tap, ring-ring, hum-hum—the kitchen made music.

In walked Mama Bear with a floury apron, and Little Robin with bright red feathers. “Good morning!” sang Little Robin. “Can we play music while the bread bakes?”

“Yes, please,” said Dori. Her voice was soft but excited. “I want to try the drum.”

Little Robin fluffed his wings. “I want the drum too,” he chirped.

Mama Bear smiled. “We can all make music. But we must be fair,” she said gently. “Fair means everyone gets a turn.”

Dori nodded. “Fair is kind,” she said.

They set the instruments on a cloth. There was one bell, two spoons, and the little drum. The drum was bright and beautiful. It glowed in the sun. Dori felt her heart thump like dum-dum, dum-dum. She wrapped the blue strap around her shoulder.

“I want the drum now,” Little Robin said, hopping closer. “I want it first.”

Dori looked down. She wanted it first, too. Her ears felt hot. The kitchen got very quiet, except for the kettle’s soft hiss.

Mama Bear stopped slicing apples. “We have a problem,” she said kindly. “Two friends want one drum. What can we do to make it fair?”

Dori took a breath. She listened to the kettle, the clock, and her own heart. “We can share,” she said. “We can take turns.”

Little Robin puffed. “But I want it now.”

Dori blinked. She felt a pinch in her chest. She wanted to be fair and kind, but she also wanted the drum. “Maybe we can make a plan,” she said.

Mama Bear nodded. “A plan is smart. Justice means fairness. Fairness means a plan that is kind to everyone.”

Dori brightened. “Let’s have a song circle,” she said. “We can play three short songs. Little Robin can drum for the first song. I can drum for the second song. And the third song can be for someone else.”

Little Robin tilted his head. “Who will drum the third song?” he asked.

Dori looked at Mama Bear. “Maybe Mama Bear,” she said with a shy smile. “And I can ring the bell then.”

Mama Bear laughed a warm, jolly laugh. “That sounds fair and generous,” she said. “You gave away a turn. That is kindness.”

Dori felt warm, like sunlight on fur. She gently took the drum off her shoulder and set it in front of Little Robin. “You can go first.”

The kettle went click and was quiet. “Ready?” Dori asked. She picked up the shiny bell and the wooden spoons.

They began. Little Robin tapped the drum with soft, happy beats. Dum, dum-dum, dum. Dori rang the bell like a twinkle star—ring, ring. Mama Bear kept the spoon beat on the table—tap, tap, tap. The kitchen filled with cozy sounds and the smell of baking bread. Little Robin smiled. “This sounds like rain on leaves,” he sang.

“Now my turn to drum,” Dori said when the first song ended. She took the drum gently. She held the sticks like two small branches and breathed in deep. “I will try to play steady,” she said. “I want to improve.”

She began softly. Dum… dum… dum-dum. Her hooves felt tingly. The drum skin felt smooth, and the sound rolled like a warm blanket. She missed a beat and frowned.

“It’s okay,” Mama Bear said. “Practice helps us grow.”

Dori tried again. Dum-dum, dum-dum, dum-dum. She kept the beat steady. Little Robin shook the bell and giggled. “It sounds like footsteps in snow!”

Dori smiled wide. “I did it,” she whispered. She felt proud, but not too big. She felt just right—strong and gentle.

“For the third song, it is my turn,” Mama Bear said. She put on the drum carefully. Dum-dum, dum. “Oh my,” she giggled. “I am learning too!”

Dori saw Mama Bear’s hands, a little clumsy but happy. Dori picked up the bell and shared the spoons with Little Robin. “Here,” she said, “you can have the spoons. I will ring softly so you can shine.”

Little Robin’s eyes sparkled. “Thank you, Dori. You are generous.”

They played the third song together. It sounded like apple slices falling into a bowl. It sounded like kindness. It sounded fair.

When the song ended, Mama Bear sliced the warm bread. Steam rose up and smelled like hugs. She gave each friend a slice with honey. “You made music,” she said. “You made fairness. You shared. That is justice.”

Dori licked a little honey from her hoof. “Music helps us listen,” she said. “When we listen, we can be fair.”

Little Robin nodded. “And we can get better with practice,” he chirped. “Your drumming improved!”

Dori’s ears perked happily. “Thank you. Your turn made my heart glad.”

Mama Bear poured warm milk. “What did we learn today?” she asked.

Dori thought. “We learned that music is for everyone,” she said. “We learned to share and take turns. We learned that practice helps us grow. And we learned that being fair feels good.”

Little Robin tapped his cup. “And music tastes like honey bread,” he joked.

They all laughed. The kitchen felt extra warm. The clock ticked like a gentle metronome. Dori hugged the drum with her eyes and set it back on the cloth.

“Tomorrow,” she said, “we can make a new song. We can let Squirrel have a turn too.”

“Yes,” Mama Bear said. “Fair and kind, again and again.”

Dori’s heart went dum-dum, dum-dum, in a happy beat. She looked at her friends and the bright drum. “Music makes us better,” she whispered. “Together.”

The End.
