# Trunksy Paints the Porch

On a peaceful front porch, a gentle elephant calf named Trunksy wiggled his small, soft ears. The wooden steps felt warm under his feet. The porch smelled like fresh banana bread from the kitchen, and a little breeze made the wind chimes sing, ting-ting-ting.

Trunksy had a big idea. “I want to paint a picture!” he said, his trunk swishing with joy.

Mama Elephant brought a small box outside. “Here are paints, paper, a cup of water, and a towel,” she said with a smile. “We can paint together on the porch. Let’s build your skills today.”

Trunksy nodded. “I can learn,” he said proudly.

Mama pointed to the supplies. “First, we set up. That’s step one. We put the paper on the table. We keep the water in the middle. We use the towel to clean your trunk brush.”

Trunksy touched the soft towel. “Step one: set up!” he repeated. He placed the smooth paper on the low table and set the cup of water carefully in the center. The cup made a tiny glug sound.

“Good job,” Mama said. “Step two: choose colors. Start with two.”

Trunksy looked at the bright paints. Red looked like a juicy apple. Blue looked like the sky. “I choose red and blue,” he said.

“Great choice,” said Mama. “Step three: dip, dab, and draw. Dip your trunk in paint. Dab on the towel. Then draw slow lines.”

Trunksy dipped his trunk into the red. It felt cool and tickly. He dabbed on the towel—tap, tap—and drew a soft red circle. “It looks like a sun,” he giggled.

“Lovely,” Mama said. “Now you try blue.”

Trunksy dipped into blue and dabbed. He drew a wavy line. “This is a river!” he said. He could almost hear the splash in his mind.

From the yard came a chirp. Little Bird landed on the porch rail. “Cheep! Can I see?”

“Yes!” Trunksy said. He lifted his picture. “I am painting a sun and a river.”

“Cheep! Add yellow!” Little Bird chirped.

Trunksy looked at Mama. He felt a tiny twist in his tummy. He wanted yellow too, but Mama said to start with two colors. Trunksy took a deep breath. “I am learning steps,” he said softly. “I will use yellow later.”

Mama smiled. “That is assertive, Trunksy. You used your strong, kind voice. You said what you need.”

Little Bird fluttered. “Cheep, okay! I will wait.”

A neighbor, Tortoise, slowly climbed the porch step with a gentle scrape-scrape. “Hello,” he said in a slow voice. “I paint slowly. Slow is good for skill.”

Trunksy watched Tortoise dip his brush, dab, and make short, careful lines. The lines looked neat and calm.

Trunksy tried it too. Dip. Dab. Draw. He made small dots around the sun. “They sparkle,” he said. The porch felt cozy. The paint smelled a little sweet. The wind chimes sang again.

Mama said, “Step four: check your work. Look with your eyes. Ask, ‘What is missing?’”

Trunksy looked. The sun and river were pretty, but the sky was plain. He nodded. “Now I add yellow.” He opened the yellow paint with a tiny pop.

“Cheep!” said Little Bird. “Yellow time!”

Trunksy dipped, dabbed, and drew soft rays on the sun. The yellow glowed. He added a little green by mixing blue and yellow slowly. “Look, I made a tree,” he said. He felt proud and warm inside.

Just then, a puppy friend, Dot, bounced onto the porch. Tap-tap-tap went her paws. “Can I paint too? Can I use all the colors at once?” she barked fast.

Trunksy thought. He liked Dot, but he wanted to keep things neat. He lifted his chin and used his calm voice. “Dot, we are following steps. We use two colors first. Then we add more. You can share with me if we take turns.”

Dot sat down and wagged. “Okay! I can take turns. Dip, dab, draw!” she said, copying the words.

They painted together. Trunksy guided Dot. “Dip here. Dab on the towel. Slow lines.” Dot tried her best. She made a happy, bouncy sun with polka dots. Little Bird added a tiny feather print by tapping the tip of his wing in yellow—tap, tap.

Tortoise smiled. “You are all learning. You are building skills. You are patient.”

“Step five,” Mama said softly, “clean up. We wash the brushes. We close the paints. We let the pictures dry.”

Trunksy hummed as he swished his trunk in the water. Swish, swish. The water turned purple and made little bubbles. He wiped the table and folded the towel.

They set the paintings along the porch rail to dry. The paper curled a little in the breeze. The colors shone in the warm light. Trunksy felt his heart thump-thump with happy pride.

Mama hugged him with her trunk. “What did you learn today, Trunksy?”

Trunksy smiled. “I learned steps: set up, choose colors, dip-dab-draw, check, and clean up. I learned to use my kind, strong voice. I learned to make new colors. I can build skills when I go slow and follow directions.”

Little Bird chirped, “Cheep! And you shared.”

Dot barked, “And you helped.”

Tortoise nodded. “And you practiced. Practice makes strong.”

Trunksy looked at his picture. The sun, the river, and the tree looked bright and brave. “I can paint again tomorrow,” he said. “I can keep building my art skills.”

The wind chimes sang one more time, ting-ting-ting. The porch felt peaceful and proud.

The End.
