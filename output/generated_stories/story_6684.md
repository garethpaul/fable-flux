# Etta Elephant Listens

Etta was a gentle elephant calf with soft gray ears and a tiny paint-splattered tail. One bright morning, Etta stepped into the creative art room. The room smelled like crayons and glue. Sunlight made the glitter sparkle like tiny stars. Paints from many colors—ruby red, ocean blue, sunny yellow, and forest green—waited in little cups.

“Welcome, artists!” said Ms. Maya, their teacher, with a warm smile. Ms. Maya wore a scarf with patterns from all over the world—swirls from India, dots from Australia, and bright shapes from Ghana. “Today, we will make a Big Kindness Mural for our community center.”

Etta’s heart thumped with happy thuds. “I want to help!” she said.

Around the big paper on the wall stood friends from many places. Lina wore a red ribbon from Mexico. Amir had a tiny drum from Lebanon hanging on his neck. Mei brought paper fans from China. Tayo wore bright beads from Nigeria. They all smiled at Etta.

Ms. Maya clapped softly. “First, we listen to each other’s ideas.”

Etta’s trunk wiggled. She wanted to paint a giant elephant helping a tiny bird. She bounced on her toes. “I have an idea!” she blurted.

“Me too!” said Lina.

“I do as well,” said Amir, tapping his drum.

“So many ideas,” Ms. Maya said, laughing gently. “Let’s go one by one. We listen with our eyes, our ears, and our hearts.”

Etta tried to wait, but her idea felt big, like a bubble in her belly. When Lina began, “I think we can paint flowers from my grandma’s garden—marigolds and roses—”

Etta stepped forward. “And an elephant!” she said loudly.

Lina stopped. Her smile faded. The room went quiet, except for a soft drum tap.

Ms. Maya touched Etta’s shoulder. “Etta, it’s good to be excited. Being assertive means sharing your idea. But first, we listen. Listening helps us care. Can you try again?”

Etta’s ears drooped. “I forgot to listen,” she whispered. She took a deep breath. The air smelled like oranges from someone’s snack. “I’m ready to listen now.”

Lina’s smile returned. “Thank you, Etta. My grandma grows flowers and gives them to neighbors. It makes people feel loved.”

“That is community service,” Ms. Maya said. “Helping others.”

Amir tapped his drum. “In my family, we drum to call people together. We can paint music lines dancing across the wall.”

Mei fanned her face. “We can paint hands holding fans, books, and food, all sharing.”

Tayo held up his beads. “We can paint people of many colors, all standing side by side.”

Etta listened to each voice. She watched their faces and heard their feelings. She felt their ideas like warm sun on her back. Her bubble of excitement changed into a bubble of understanding.

When it was Etta’s turn, she stood tall but gentle. “May I share my idea now?” she asked.

“Yes, Etta,” Ms. Maya said. “Thank you for waiting.”

“I want to paint a gentle elephant helping a tiny bird reach a high branch,” Etta said softly. “The elephant listens to the bird and lifts it up.”

Amir nodded. “The drum can call them to help.”

Lina clapped. “Flowers can bloom around them.”

Mei smiled. “Fans can wave the air to help the paint dry.”

Tayo grinned. “We can add people of many colors cheering for teamwork.”

Etta’s heart felt light. “We can mix our ideas!” she cheered.

They began to paint. The brushes tickled the paper with swish, swish, swish. Etta dipped her brush into ocean blue. She painted the elephant’s kind eyes. Lina dabbed sunny yellow marigolds. “They smell sweet,” she said, giggling.

Amir tapped a soft rhythm: dum, da-dum, dum. “The music lines are curvy like smiles.”

Mei glued tiny paper fans. “Crinkle, crinkle,” she said as the paper folded.

Tayo painted people holding hands—brown, tan, pink, and gold. “We all belong,” he said.

Etta listened while they worked. “Do you need space here, Lina?” she asked.

“Yes, please,” said Lina.

Etta moved her brush. “Thank you for telling me.”

“Can I add drum dots here?” Amir asked Etta.

“Yes,” Etta said. “I will wait for your turn. I’m listening.”

The mural grew bright and big. Ms. Maya stepped back. “I hear kindness in this room,” she said. “I see listening. I see strong, brave voices.”

Etta felt proud. She had used her voice, but she had also listened with her eyes, ears, and heart. The elephant on the wall lifted the tiny bird to a branch filled with marigolds. Music lines wrapped around them like hugs. People from many places smiled together. The air smelled like glue and oranges and a little like flowers.

Just then, they heard quiet footsteps. Mr. Park from the community office peeked in. “Hello, artists,” he said. “We need a picture for the front hallway to welcome everyone. Can we use your mural?”

Etta looked at her friends. She listened to their breaths and saw their hopeful eyes. She felt brave and kind. “Yes,” she said, “we made it for everyone.”

Mr. Park bowed. “Thank you. It shows love and listening.”

They carried the mural together, step by careful step. Etta led with a gentle trunk. Outside the art room, people stopped and smiled. A little girl whispered, “I feel welcome.”

Etta’s ears wiggled with joy. She looked at her friends. “When we listen, we fit everyone’s ideas,” she said. “Listening helps us help others.”

Ms. Maya nodded. “That is the lesson, artists. Listening makes room for every voice. Being assertive means sharing your idea kindly and waiting your turn.”

Etta touched the painted elephant with the tip of her trunk. “I will listen first,” she said, “with my eyes, my ears, and my heart.”

The End.
