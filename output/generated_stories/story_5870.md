# Beaver Ben Builds a Balcony Garden Helper

Beaver Ben was a busy beaver with bright eyes and a big, flat tail. He lived with his family in a cozy home. On the second floor, there was a sunny balcony garden. Pots of mint, tomatoes, and tiny carrots lined the rail. The air smelled like fresh dirt and sweet leaves. Bees buzzed softly, and the wind whispered, shhh, like a lullaby.

Every morning, Ben watered the plants with a small blue can. Splash, drip, drip, drip. “Good morning, plants!” he said. “Drink up!”

One day, the sun was extra hot. The soil looked dry and sad. Ben’s arms felt tired. “I wish I had a helper,” he sighed. He tapped his tail on the wooden floor. Tap, tap, think. Tap, tap, plan.

Mama Beaver peeked outside. “What’s on your mind, Ben?”

“I want to help the plants,” Ben said. “But the can is small, and the sun is big.”

Mama smiled. “That’s a big problem. How will you solve it?”

Ben’s eyes lit up. “I can invent something! I can make a watering helper!” He felt a little hop of joy in his chest.

He looked around the balcony. He saw:
- Two empty juice bottles
- A long piece of string
- Some wooden sticks
- A tiny bell
- A clean bowl

Ben clapped his paws. “I have a plan!” he said. “First, I will draw it.” He got paper and a stubby crayon. Scratch, scratch. He drew the pots, the bottles, and big arrows. “Water goes here,” he said, pointing.

Mama nodded. “That is good planning, Ben.”

He tied the string to a bottle. He balanced the bottle on the rail. “Careful,” he whispered. The string felt rough. The bottle felt smooth and cool. He poked tiny holes in the cap with Mama’s help. “Drip holes,” he said. “Plink, plink.”

Next, he set the clean bowl under the bottle to catch extra drops. He used the wooden sticks to hold everything still. “Stay, sticks,” he said. He tied them gently. “Tie, tie. Do not wiggle.”

Ben filled the bottle with water. Glug, glug, glug. He twisted the cap. He held his breath. “Please drip,” he said.

A tiny drop fell. Plink. Then another. Plink, plink. The soil turned dark and happy. The mint leaves looked shiny. The little bell tinkled as the string moved. Ting!

“It works!” Ben cheered. “My watering helper works!”

Mama clapped. “You used innovation,” she said. “You had a new idea. You tried it. You made it real.”

Ben puffed his chest. He felt proud. He felt brave. He felt like a helper, too.

Just then, Neighbor Bird fluttered to the rail. “Chirp! What’s that?” she asked.

“My watering helper,” Ben said. “It drips water slowly.”

Neighbor Bird tilted her head. “Chirp, smart! Could I try one for my porch plant?”

Ben smiled. “Yes! We can share.” Sharing made his heart feel warm, like sunshine.

They looked for more bottles. They only found one. Ben frowned. “We need more sticks,” he said. He tapped his tail. Tap, tap, think. Tap, tap, plan.

Mama said, “Let’s make a list. That is good planning.”

Ben wrote: sticks, string, bottle. He said the words slowly. “Sticks. String. Bottle.”

“Now we wait,” Mama said. “We can be patient.”

Waiting felt hard. The sun warmed his fur. The mint smelled minty and cool. Buzz, went a bee. Ben wiggled. “I want to build now,” he said.

Mama touched his paw. “Patience helps plans grow,” she said.

They waited until Papa came home with the mail. Papa had a bundle of rubber bands and a roll of tape. “I found these!” he said.

Ben grinned. “Perfect!” He used the rubber bands to hold the sticks tighter. Snap, snug. He used the tape to keep the string safe. Smooth, sticky. He helped Neighbor Bird tie a tiny drip bottle for her porch plant. “Chirp, thank you!” she sang.

Soon, the balcony garden was calm and happy. Drip, drip, drip. Ting, went the bell now and then. The tomatoes looked plump. The carrot tops stood tall and green.

The next morning, Ben checked the soil. He touched it with one gentle paw. Cool and damp. He sniffed the mint. Fresh and sweet. He listened. Drip, drip. “Good job, helper,” he said to his invention. “You are a good friend.”

Mama hugged him. “What did you learn, Ben?”

Ben thought. He tapped his tail, just once. “I learned to plan first,” he said. “I learned to share ideas and tools. I learned to be patient. And I learned that new ideas can help lots of friends.”

Neighbor Bird chirped from her porch, “Innovation is trying a new way!”

Ben beamed. “Yes,” he said. “Innovation is trying a new way.”

He looked at his drawing again. He made a new little sketch. Scratch, scratch. “Next, I will add a second bottle for very hot days,” he said. “That is my next plan.”

Mama smiled. “You are a busy beaver with a bright brain.”

Ben patted the mint, smelled the sweet leaves, and waved to the bees. The wind whispered, shhh, and the bell tinkled, ting. The balcony garden felt safe and cared for, drop by drop.

And Ben felt glad. He had used innovation, sharing, planning, and patience. He had helped the plants and his friends. He had turned a big, hot problem into a cool, gentle drip.

The End.
