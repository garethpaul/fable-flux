# Roo the Rabbit Finds the Right Note

In a quiet attic, under slanted wooden beams, a little rabbit named Roo wiggled his whiskers. Dust danced in the sunlight like tiny stars. The air smelled like old books and cedar. Roo’s ears perked up at a shiny thing in the corner.

“A drum!” Roo whispered. He tapped it with one paw. Thump. Thump. The sound was soft and full, like a heartbeat.

Roo was a determined rabbit. When he wanted to learn something, he kept trying. Today, Roo wanted to make music.

He looked around the attic. There was a tin pan. There was a glass bottle. There was a silver bell with a ribbon. “I can make a band!” Roo said, tail twitching.

Roo set them in a row on a blanket. “Tap. Ting. Ding,” he said, practicing the sounds.

Then Roo picked up two wooden spoons. Tap-tap-tap on the tin pan. Ting on the glass bottle. Ding on the bell. The attic filled with bright, bouncy sounds. Roo smiled so big his cheeks felt warm.

Just then, a soft voice floated up the stairs. It was Aunt Owl, who liked to nap in the late afternoon. “Hoo-hoo,” she called. “Is someone tapping?”

Roo stopped and listened. He heard the gentle wind outside and the quiet creak of the attic floor. He also heard his own fast breathing. He wanted to play more. He wanted to learn a song.

“I will play softly,” Roo told himself. “Soft like a cloud.”

He tried again. Tap… ting… ding. The sounds were quiet, like raindrops on leaves. Roo swayed side to side. “This feels nice,” he said.

He closed his eyes and made a little tune. Tap, tap, ting, ding-ding. He imagined butterflies dancing. He imagined carrots growing. He imagined stars singing twinkle songs.

Then Roo got excited. His paws moved faster. Tap-TAP, TING-TING, DING-DING-DING!

“Whoa!” cried Roo as the glass bottle wobbled. It fell with a clink and rolled. The bell slid and the ribbon brushed Roo’s nose. He giggled and kept playing, louder and louder.

Aunt Owl fluttered up the stairs. “Hoo! Little Roo,” she said, blinking her round eyes. “That is a big sound for a small attic.”

Roo froze. His ears drooped. “Oh,” he said. “I was making music. I forgot to be soft.”

Aunt Owl folded her wings. “I like music,” she said kindly. “But I also like rest. My head feels rumbly.”

Roo’s tummy felt twisty. He put down the spoons. “I’m sorry,” he said in a small voice. “I didn’t mean to disturb you. I was excited. I wanted to learn a song.”

Aunt Owl nodded. “Thank you for your apology,” she said. “That was thoughtful. Music is wonderful. It can be loud like thunder. It can be quiet like snow. Do you want to learn about both?”

Roo’s ears perked up again. “Yes, please!”

Aunt Owl smiled and pointed to the drum. “Touch it with one finger,” she said.

Roo tapped with one finger. Thip. The sound was tiny and soft.

“Now your whole paw,” said Aunt Owl.

Thump. The sound was round and big.

“Music has dynamics,” she explained. “That means loud and soft. We choose what the moment needs.”

Roo took a deep breath. “The attic needs soft,” he said. He looked at the tin pan. “Tap.” He looked at the bell. “Ding.” He looked at the drum. “Thump.” He made a gentle pattern: tap… ding… thump… tap… ding… thump.

Aunt Owl hummed. “Hoo-hoo,” she sang along, low and cozy. Roo listened and matched her hum. The attic felt warm. The dust sparkles seemed to slow down and dance.

Roo tried something new. “Would you like to use a spoon?” he asked. “We can share. We can play together.” He held out a wooden spoon with both paws. His nose wiggled with hope.

“That is generous,” Aunt Owl said, taking the spoon carefully. “I would like that.”

They played a quiet duet. Tap… hoo… ding… hoo… thump… hoo… The sounds wrapped around them like a soft blanket. Roo watched Aunt Owl’s eyelids grow heavy. He smiled and kept the beat soft and steady.

After the song, Aunt Owl yawned. “What understanding you have, Roo,” she said. “You listened to the room. You listened to me. You listened to the music.”

Roo’s chest felt light and proud. “Listening helps me make better music,” he said. “And saying sorry helps hearts feel calm.”

Aunt Owl patted Roo’s head with a wing. “Yes,” she said. “Music is not just notes. Music is care. Music is sharing.”

Roo cleaned up the bottle and the bell. He tied the ribbon in a neat bow. He set the spoons side by side. He whispered, “Thank you, instruments.” The attic seemed to smile.

Before Aunt Owl went back to her nap, she placed a tiny feather by Roo’s drum. “For your brave, kind heart,” she said.

Roo tucked the feather in the ribbon. He made one last soft pattern: tap… ding… thump.

He whispered, “I can play with my ears, my paws, and my heart.” Roo felt peaceful. He had learned to make music that fit the moment. He had learned to say sorry. He had learned to share.

The attic was quiet again, but inside Roo’s heart, a gentle song played on.

The End.
