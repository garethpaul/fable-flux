# Otto Otter Speaks Up

In a bright, warm laundry room, Otto the playful otter slid across a pile of soft towels. The washing machine hummed like a sleepy bee, and the dryer clicked and clacked like tiny tap shoes. Otto giggled. “Swoosh!” he said, making a towel wave like a flag.

Mama Mei folded shirts into neat stacks. She wore a bright apron with red and gold flowers. The room smelled like lemon soap and fresh cotton. On a small table sat a colorful snack box. Inside were crunchy carrot sticks, a little rice ball with a cute seaweed smile, and sweet apple slices sprinkled with cinnamon.

Otto peeked. “Yum!” he whispered.

“Snack time after we finish,” Mama Mei said kindly. “First, we follow directions. Fold, stack, then snack.”

Otto wiggled his whiskers. He liked to play more than to fold. Just then, cousins Sami and Priya came to visit. Sami wore a blue cap with a moon on it. Priya had jingling bangles that chimed softly. They brought a basket of laundry too.

“Hi, Otto!” Sami said. “Let’s make a towel fort!”

Priya clapped. “I can show you how to tie a scarf like my grandma taught me!”

Otto bounced. “Yes! Fort time!”

Mama Mei smiled. “Friends are welcome. But we must follow directions first. Fold, stack, then snack. And please ask before taking food.”

Everyone nodded, but the towels looked so fluffy, and the machines sounded so fun. Hum, whirr, click-click!

Sami scooped towels into a wiggly wall. Priya draped a bright scarf with tiny elephants. It felt smooth and silky. Otto slid inside and chirped, “Comfy cave!”

Mama Mei called, “Remember the plan.”

“Okay!” Otto said, but he didn’t stop. He felt a flutter in his tummy. He knew he should speak up and say, “We need to fold first.” But he didn’t want to sound bossy. His mouth felt small.

Then Otto heard a rustle. Sami had opened the snack box. “I’m so hungry,” Sami said. “Just one apple slice.”

Priya reached too. “These look like the ones my aunt makes, with cinnamon from her home.”

Otto’s tummy flipped. The apple smell was sweet and warm. He wanted some, but he remembered Mama Mei’s words. Follow directions. Ask first.

He took a breath. In, out. The dryer clicked like a clock. In, out.

Otto stood tall on a folded towel. “Friends,” he said, voice gentle but strong, “please stop. Mama Mei said to ask first and fold first.”

Sami blinked. “We’re just hungry.”

“I get hungry too,” said Otto. “Let’s ask. And let’s help. Then we can eat together.”

Priya paused, her bangles quiet. “You’re right. Thank you for telling us, Otto.”

Mama Mei looked over, proud. “That was assertive, Otto. You used your strong, kind voice.”

Otto smiled. “I felt nervous, but I said it anyway.”

Sami pulled his hand back. “Sorry, Mama Mei. I didn’t ask.”

Priya nodded. “I’m sorry too. We will follow directions.”

“Thank you,” Mama Mei said. “Your apology is kind. Now, let’s fold as a team.”

They made a folding game. Otto folded little socks—soft and tiny like clouds. Sami folded shirts—smooth and warm from the dryer. Priya paired colorful scarves—silky and bright. Mama Mei showed them a new way to fold towels, rolling them into cozy tubes.

As they worked, they talked about food from their families. Priya said, “At my house, we eat chapati with lentils. It is warm and soft.”

Sami said, “My grandma makes moon-shaped dumplings with spinach inside.”

Otto said, “I love rice balls. The seaweed is salty and tickly.”

Mama Mei nodded. “Different foods help our bodies grow strong. Carrots help our eyes. Spinach helps our muscles. Apples help our tummies.”

Otto’s tummy rumbled. “After folding, can we share?”

“Yes,” Mama Mei said. “And we will make a rainbow plate. Many colors, many tastes.”

Soon the baskets were empty. The fort was gone, but the room looked neat. The washing machine hummed to a happy stop. Click! The dryer door opened with a warm whoosh.

Mama Mei washed everyone’s hands. The soap smelled like oranges. They sat on a puff of towels and made rainbow plates: orange carrots, red apples with cinnamon, green spinach in tiny dumplings, and white rice balls with smiley faces.

Sami crunched a carrot. “Crunch, crunch! This helps my eyes?”

“Yes,” Mama Mei said.

Priya bit an apple slice. “Sweet and warm! I’m glad we asked.”

Otto held his rice ball. “I was scared to speak up. But I did it.”

Mama Mei hugged him. “That is being assertive—using your words kindly to keep the rules and keep everyone safe.”

Sami said, “Thank you, Otto. You helped me remember.”

Priya chimed, “Me too. I’m sorry I almost took the snack. Next time, I will ask first and follow directions.”

Otto’s whiskers twitched. “We did it together!”

After snacks, they cleaned up, putting peels in the bin and wiping the table. The laundry room smelled fresh and happy. The towel stacks stood tall like little mountains.

Otto patted his tummy. “I feel strong. My voice is strong too.”

Mama Mei smiled. “When we follow directions, ask before taking, and use a kind, strong voice, everyone feels safe and cared for.”

Otto wiggled. “Next time, we can play fort after folding!”

“Deal!” said Sami.

“Deal!” said Priya.

And the dryer clicked one last friendly click, like a tiny cheer.

The End.
