# Panda Pao’s Polite Words in the Treehouse

Panda Pao lived in a cozy treehouse that smelled like fresh bamboo and warm tea. The treehouse was high in a friendly fig tree, with soft leaves that tickled the windows. Each morning, Pao opened the round door and said, “Good morning, world!” in a gentle, patient voice.

One sunny day, Pao’s friends climbed up the ladder to visit. There was Mimi the mouse, who wore a tiny blue scarf. There was Tito the turtle, slow and steady, with a shiny shell. And there was Lala the ladybug, bright red with seven black dots that looked like little buttons.

“Hello!” Pao said with a warm smile. “Welcome to my treehouse.”

“It smells sweet,” Mimi squeaked, sniffing the air. “Is that figs?”

“Yes,” Pao said. “Figs and tea. And today, we will have a word picnic! We will taste kind words and share them.”

“A word picnic?” Lala buzzed, her wings humming softly. “Do we eat words?”

Pao laughed. “We listen to them. We say them. We share them. Words can be chewy like ‘please,’ or smooth like ‘thank you.’ They make our hearts feel full.”

Tito nodded slowly. “Words… help us talk.”

Pao clapped gently. “Yes! Words help us communicate. That means we tell our thoughts and feelings. We can use words from many languages too.”

Pao showed a small basket. Inside were cards with words and pictures. The cards smelled like new paper and lemon soap. Pao lifted one card with a smiling sun. “Good morning,” Pao said. “In Mandarin, we can say ‘Zǎo an.’ Can you try?”

“Zow an!” Mimi giggled.

Pao nodded kindly. “Good trying. Zǎo an.”

Tito said, “Za… an,” slow and proud.

Lala buzzed, “Zah an!”

Pao gave each friend a sip of warm water. “We drink water to keep our bodies happy. Hydration is healthy.”

They sat on a round rug, soft like moss. Pao picked another card with two hands sharing a toy. “Please,” Pao said. “In Spanish, we can say ‘por favor.’”

“Por fa…vor,” Mimi squeaked carefully.

“Por favooor,” Lala sang like a little violin.

Tito smiled. “Por favor.”

Pao’s eyes sparkled. “Wonderful! When we say ‘please,’ we show fairness. We do not push. We ask. We wait our turn.”

Mimi looked at a shiny red ball in the corner. Her nose twitched. “Ball, please. Por favor,” she said, hands folded.

Pao gently rolled the ball to Mimi. “Thank you for asking with kind words.”

“Gracias,” Mimi said.

Pao held up a third card with a big heart. “Thank you. In Swahili, we say ‘asante.’”

“Asante,” they all repeated, soft and sweet, like honey on toast.

The friends took turns with the ball. Pao counted with rhythm. “One, two, three, pass. One, two, three, pass.” The ball made a soft thump on the rug and a quiet whoosh through the air. Everyone had a turn. It felt fair. It felt calm.

After a while, they had a snack. Pao washed little paws, wings, and a shell with warm, soapy water. “We wash before we eat,” Pao said. “Clean hands keep us strong.”

They crunched on crisp apple slices and nibbled chewy fig bars. The apples were juicy and bright. The fig bars were sweet and soft. “Mmm,” said Tito. “Yummy. Asante.”

“De nada,” Pao replied with a wink. “That means ‘you’re welcome’ in Spanish.”

Lala looked shy. “Sometimes my words get mixed.”

Pao nodded kindly. “That is okay. We can be patient with words. We can ask, ‘Can you help me?’ In French, we say ‘Aide-moi, s’il te plaît.’”

“Aid… moi,” Lala tried. “Sil teh play?”

“Very good,” Pao said. “We try. We breathe. We learn.”

Mimi tugged on the ball too fast. The ball bumped Tito’s shell with a soft bonk. Tito blinked. “Ouch… a little.”

Mimi’s ears drooped. “I’m sorry,” she whispered, eyes shiny.

Pao picked a card with two hands touching gently. “I’m sorry. In Japanese, we can say ‘gomen.’”

Mimi said it softly. “Gomen.”

Tito smiled. “I forgive you,” he said. “Thank you for telling me.”

Pao added, “When we use caring words, our hearts feel better. That is fairness. We listen. We share.”

The wind drifted through the window, smelling like leaves and sky. Pao stood, straight and calm. “Let’s make our own word song,” Pao said. “A song of kindness.”

They tapped the floor, tap-tap-tap. Pao hummed. “Please, por favor,” Pao sang.

“Thank you, asante,” Lala buzzed.

“Good morning, zǎo an,” Tito said softly.

“I’m sorry, gomen,” Mimi squeaked.

They sang again, a little louder, smiling. Tap-tap-tap. The word song filled the treehouse like warm light.

When it was time to go, Pao gave each friend a tiny card with a star. On each card was a word they liked best.

Mimi’s card said “please / por favor.”
Tito’s card said “thank you / asante.”
Lala’s card said “help, please / aide-moi, s’il te plaît.”

Pao kept one that said “good morning / zǎo an.”

“Today,” Pao said, “we learned to love words from many places. We used words to be fair, to be kind, and to be healthy. We drank water, we washed hands, and we took turns.”

Mimi hugged Pao’s fluffy arm. “Words can be hugs.”

Tito tapped his shell. “Words can be bridges.”

Lala twirled in the air. “Words can be songs.”

Pao smiled, eyes soft and bright. “Yes. Words are gifts. When we speak with care, we feel close. When we listen, we learn.”

They said their goodbyes, each in their new way.

“Zǎo an for tomorrow,” Tito said.

“Asante for today,” Lala buzzed.

“Por favor, can we sing again next time?” Mimi asked.

Pao nodded. “Yes. We will share more words, with patient hearts.”

The treehouse glowed in the fading sun. The leaves whispered like gentle voices. Pao sipped a last cup of warm tea and whispered to the sky, “Thank you. Asante.”

The End.
