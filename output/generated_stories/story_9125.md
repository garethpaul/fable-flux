# Ollie Otter’s Car-Seat Story Show

Click! went the seat belt. Swish! went the doors. The family car smelled like oranges from a snack and felt cozy and warm in the afternoon sun.

Ollie was a playful otter with bright eyes and whiskers that tickled when he giggled. He sat in his car seat, feet wiggling like little fish tails. “Are we there yet?” Ollie asked.

“Not yet,” said Mama, starting the car. “We’ll be home soon.”

Ollie sighed. “I feel squirmy,” he said. “My body wants to wiggle!”

Mama smiled at him in the mirror. “Let’s help your body and your mind feel good. How about a story? You can tell it.”

“Me?” Ollie’s whiskers twitched. “I can tell a story?”

“Yes,” said Mama. “Stories can help us pass time. They help our feelings too.”

Ollie took a deep breath. He put his paw on his tummy. “In and out,” he said softly. “I’m ready.”

His tail thumped the seat in a tiny happy dance. “Once upon a splash,” he began, “there was an otter named… Ollie! He lived by a bright blue river that smelled like fresh rain.”

Mama nodded. “What did Ollie do?”

“He went on a river dance!” Ollie said. He swayed his shoulders and made a quiet beat with his paws: pat-pat, pat-pat. “Ollie danced with the water. Swish, swish!”

“Swish, swish,” Mama echoed, keeping her eyes on the road. The car hummed: mmmmmm.

Ollie reached into his little bag and pulled out a crayon and a tiny notepad. “And Ollie drew the river,” he said, making gentle lines that curved like waves. “He drew the sun, all warm and yellow.” He sniffed the crayon. “It smells waxy.”

“That sounds beautiful,” Mama said. “How did Ollie feel?”

Ollie thought. “He felt calm in his body. He took slow breaths.” Ollie breathed in and out again. “Like this. He felt brave too.”

A red light made the car stop. Mama stretched her shoulders. “What happened next in your story?”

Ollie smiled. “Ollie made a new friend. A tiny turtle named Toot.” He giggled. “Toot liked to tap-tap dance on a rock.”

He tapped the notepad softly: tap-tap, tap-tap.

“In the story,” Ollie said, “Ollie wanted to dance fast-faster-fastest. But Toot wanted to go slow. Ollie’s feet felt fizzy like soda. He didn’t know what to do.”

Mama asked, “What did Ollie say to Toot?”

Ollie used a gentle voice. “He said, ‘Let’s dance together. Slow first, then fast.’”

“How did that go?” Mama asked.

Ollie raised his paws and wiggled his whiskers. “They made a dance plan! Slow like a turtle. Then fast like a fish. Then still like a stone. Then swish like the river!”

He showed Mama the dance with tiny car-seat moves: slow sway, quick wiggle, quiet freeze, and a soft arm swirl. “My body feels good when I dance like that,” he said. “It’s a wellness dance.”

“I love that,” Mama said. “Your story is helping your body.”

Ollie colored a little turtle next to the river on his notepad. “Toot said, ‘I feel seen!’” Ollie said in a squeaky turtle voice. He giggled at his own sound. “Ollie said, ‘I feel calm!’”

The car rolled by tall trees. The leaves made whisper sounds: hush, hush. Sunlight blinked through like tiny stars. Ollie watched and added dots to his drawing. “The sky had sparkles,” he said softly.

“Did Ollie and Toot share their dance?” Mama asked.

“Yes!” Ollie clapped once, very quiet. “They told the frogs a story with their bodies. They danced the river. They used art too. Ollie drew the ending while Toot tap-tap-tapped the beat. The frogs clapped with their wet hands: splish-splash-clap!”

Mama laughed. “That’s a great ending.”

Ollie thought. His eyes got thoughtful and kind. “But wait,” he said. “A cloud came. It was gray and grumpy. It said, ‘I am too big. I don’t fit in your story.’”

“What did Ollie say to the cloud?” Mama asked.

Ollie put a soft paw on his chest. “He said, ‘You can fit. All feelings can fit in a story.’”

“And then?” Mama whispered.

“Then the cloud took a deep breath.” Ollie breathed with it. “The cloud danced slow like a turtle, fast like a fish, still like a stone, and swish like the river. The cloud felt better. It turned light and fluffy. It made soft rain that tickled everyone’s noses.”

Ollie wiggled his nose. “Tee-hee! It smelled like clean air.”

Mama turned onto their street. “Ollie,” she said, “your story helped you wait, helped your body, and helped a cloud.”

Ollie looked at his drawing. He had made blue lines, yellow sun, a little green turtle, and a gray cloud with a smile. “My art shows my story,” he said. “My dance shows my feelings. My words help my heart.”

The car stopped at home. Click! went the belts. Ollie held up his notepad. “Mama, can we have another Car-Seat Story Show tomorrow?”

“Yes,” Mama said, hugging him. “Stories help us move, breathe, and share. They help us be kind to ourselves and others.”

Ollie grinned. His whiskers tickled her cheek. “Today I learned: I can tell a story to calm my body, share my feelings, and have fun. I can dance the story, draw the story, and speak the story.”

He took one more slow breath. “In and out,” he said. “I feel good.”

The End.
