# Gilly the Giraffe Says "I'm Sorry"

Gilly was a thoughtful giraffe with long, gentle eyelashes and a warm, careful heart. Today was Bath Day. The bathroom was bright and echoey. The tiles felt cool on her hooves. The mirror was shiny, and the air smelled like bubbles and minty soap.

“Splish, splash!” Gilly hummed, swishing her tail. She liked to help tidy the bathroom after her bath. Helping made her chest feel light and sunny, like morning.

Mama set out a big blue towel. “All clean, Gilly,” Mama said. “Please hang your towel and put away the soap. We share this bathroom, so we take care of it together.”

“I can do that,” Gilly said proudly. She nibbled a smile. Gilly cared about everyone who used the bathroom—her family, her neighbors who visited, and even the tiny spiders who lived near the window. She knew a clean space helps everyone.

Gilly reached her long neck for the soap. Her hoof bumped the bottle. “Uh-oh!” The bottle tipped. Squirty soap flew out—plop, plop, plop—and made the floor slippery like a banana peel.

Gilly slipped. “Whoooa!” Her knees wobbled, her tail flipped, and her towel fell into the sink with a squish. Water splashed. The mirror got dotted with tiny water stars.

Gilly’s belly felt tight. The bathroom was messy now. The floor was slick, the towel was soggy, and a roll of toilet paper had tumbled and unrolled like a long white snake.

Mama peeked in. “Oh my,” she said gently. “What happened, Gilly?”

Gilly’s cheeks felt warm. She looked down at her hooves. “I made a mess,” she whispered. “I bumped the soap. I slipped. I feel sorry.”

Mama nodded and knelt so her eyes met Gilly’s. “Thank you for telling me the truth,” Mama said softly. “When we make a mess, we make amends. That means we fix what we can. We care for our shared space. That’s being kind and responsible.”

Gilly breathed in. The air smelled lemony where the cleaner sat on the shelf. She breathed out. “I want to make amends,” she said. Her voice was small but brave.

“Good plan,” Mama said. “What is your first step?”

Gilly looked around. She listened to the drip, drip, drip of the faucet. She felt the cool tile. She used her thoughtful giraffe brain.

“Step one,” Gilly said, “turn off the drippy faucet.” She reached with her careful hoof and turned it tight. The dripping stopped. The bathroom grew quiet and calm.

“Step two,” Gilly said, “wipe up the slippery soap.” She took a dry towel from the shelf. It felt fluffy and soft. She pressed it to the floor. Swish, swash, swish! The soap bubbles made tiny rainbow circles that popped with a happy pfft.

“Step three,” Gilly said, “pick up the roll.” She wound the toilet paper back onto the tube. It made a gentle whisper sound—ffrrrp—like leaves in the wind.

“Step four,” Gilly said, “hang up the towel right.” She chose a fresh towel and looped it neatly on the hook. The towel smelled like sunshine and clean cotton.

Mama smiled. “You are doing it,” she said. “How does it feel?”

Gilly paused. Her belly didn’t feel tight now. It felt steady, like standing tall under a big blue sky. “It feels… good,” she said. “I made a mess, and I am making it right.”

There was one more splash on the mirror. Gilly stretched her long neck and gently wiped the dots until the mirror shone. She could see her own eyes looking back—brave and kind.

Someone knocked. It was Mr. Beaver from next door. “Hello!” he called. “May I wash my paws? I brought you some carrots from the garden.”

Gilly opened the door a tiny bit. “One moment, please,” she said. “We’re tidying the shared bathroom.”

Mr. Beaver smiled. “That’s very thoughtful,” he said. “A clean bathroom helps everyone. Thank you for caring.”

Gilly’s heart felt warm like toast. “I had an accident,” she told him. “I said I’m sorry, and I’m fixing it.”

“That is making amends,” Mr. Beaver said kindly. “That’s good neighbor care.”

Gilly finished wiping the last bubble. She set the soap upright and checked the floor with her hoof. Not slippery. Safe.

“All done,” Gilly said. She opened the door wide. Mr. Beaver came in, washed his paws with a soft splash, and dried them on the fresh towel. He sniffed the lemony air. “Lovely,” he said.

When he left, Gilly turned to Mama. “Today I learned something big,” she said.

“What did you learn?” Mama asked.

Gilly stood tall, her spots glowing in the bright light. “When I make a mess, I tell the truth. I say, ‘I’m sorry.’ I fix what I can. That helps me, and it helps everyone. That is kindness. That is taking care of our shared place.”

Mama hugged her long neck. “Yes,” Mama said. “That is making amends. That is civic responsibility. And that is you, Gilly—thoughtful and brave.”

Gilly smiled. The bathroom sparkled. Her heart felt light and sunny again. She hung a tiny sign on the hook. It said, “Please leave it clean.” She liked helping others remember too.

Then Gilly washed her hooves, turned off the tap, and clicked off the light.

“Good job, me,” she whispered. “Good job, us.”

The End.
