# Miko’s Kitchen Mural

In a warm kitchen, a kind mouse named Miko lived behind a tiny crack in the wall. The kitchen smelled like cinnamon toast and apples. The sunlight made golden squares on the floor. Pots and pans hung like shiny moons. The stove hummed softly, like a cat purring.

Miko loved to make art. He used crumbs and seeds to make little pictures on the floor by his crack. He arranged rice like stars and black beans like buttons. “Ta-da!” he said each morning. “Art makes hearts feel bright!”

One day, Miko had a big idea. “I want to paint a mural!” he squeaked. “A big picture for everyone to see.” His whiskers twitched with excitement. He looked around the warm kitchen. The biggest, blankest spot was the cabinet door near the floor. It was smooth and light and perfect.

But there was a problem. The cabinet door belonged to Benny the Cat. Benny liked to nap right there on a soft mat. His tail flicked like a metronome. His fur was orange like toast, and his paws were white like cream.

Miko tiptoed closer. “Hi, Benny,” he said, holding a tiny cornflake like a flower. “I want to paint a mural on your cabinet. May I?”

Benny blinked his slow cat blink. “Hmm,” he rumbled. “Paint is messy. I like my naps neat. Also, I am a cat. I like to look at cat things, like fish and string.” His voice was deep and sleepy.

Miko’s ears drooped. “I understand,” he said softly. He looked at the cabinet door again. He looked at Benny’s whiskers. He took a big breath. “Maybe we can make a plan together.”

Benny opened one eye. “A plan?” he asked.

“Yes,” Miko said. “We can use things that are safe and clean. No drippy paint. We can use food art. We can use old paper. We can use ribbons. We can make a mural that you like and I like.”

Benny’s tail slowed. The kitchen smelled like warm bread. The clock ticked, steady and calm. “Tell me more,” said Benny.

Miko’s eyes shone. “We can use stale bread for clouds. We can use dry pasta for fish scales. We can use carrot peels for orange stripes. We can use a bit of string for a kite tail. We can make it on a big piece of cardboard first. Then we can hang it low, only on the bottom half of the cabinet door, where my friends can see. We will not use sticky goo. We can tie it with ribbon to the handles, so the door is safe and clean.”

Benny lifted his head. His whiskers twitched. “And what will be on this mural?”

Miko smiled. “Half can be your favorite things: fish, string, sunshine. And half can be mine: seeds, stars, and a tiny mouse house. We can share.”

Benny thought about naps. He thought about neatness. He thought about fish with pasta scales. He sniffed. The ribbon smelled like lavender. The cardboard smelled like paper and a little like cookies. “This sounds tidy,” he said. “And it sounds fair. We can try. But I have one rule.”

“What is your rule?” asked Miko.

“Quiet paws,” said Benny. “No clatter while I nap.”

Miko nodded fast. “Quiet paws. I promise.”

Miko got to work. He was resourceful. He found a big flat box and cut a neat rectangle. He smoothed it with his tiny paws. He made glue from a little bit of flour and warm water, and he used only a tiny bit on the cardboard, not on the cabinet.

He sorted supplies: pasta bows, carrot peels, rice, a dried blueberry, a shiny button he once found under the stove, and ribbons from an old tea tin. He hummed a little kitchen song. “Tap-tap,” went his tiny paws. “Swish-swish,” went the ribbon.

Benny watched with calm eyes. “That fish needs a smile,” he murmured.

Miko added a pasta smile to the fish. “Good idea,” he said.

A little draft came from the window. The curtains moved like soft waves. “It looks like the sea,” Benny said.

Miko made a kite with red ribbon and a button. He glued seeds into star shapes and placed rice as twinkly dots. He used carrot peels to make orange stripes for a sun cat. He made a tiny mouse house with a bean door and a grain path.

“Is it ready?” asked Benny.

“Almost,” said Miko. He held up the mural. It was bright and friendly. It smelled faintly like soup and carrots. The pasta fish sparkled in the sun. The ribbon kite looked ready to fly.

Miko tied the mural to the cabinet handles with neat bows. It hung low and light. No sticky mess. No drips. “Ta-da!” he whispered.

Benny stood and stretched, long and tall. He looked at the mural. He purred, low and happy. “This is good,” he said. “It shows your mouse things and my cat things. It is tidy. It makes nap time feel warm.”

Miko’s heart felt big. “We did it together,” he said.

Just then, the door opened. A warm breeze came in, smelling like apples. The mural fluttered, but the ribbons held it safe. Miko and Benny smiled.

“I learned something,” said Miko.

“What did you learn?” asked Benny, curling his tail.

“When you have a problem, you can use what you have,” said Miko. “You can make a new plan. You can talk and share. That is called resourceful. That is called compromise. And art can help hearts feel bright.”

Benny nodded. “And quiet paws make good naps,” he added.

They both laughed. The kitchen hummed. The sunlight made golden squares again. Miko curled up by his tiny crack. Benny curled up on his soft mat. The mural glowed softly on the cabinet door, full of fish and stars and hope.

The End.
