# The Little Song in the Attic

In a quiet attic, where dust sparkled like tiny stars, a small singing bird named Lila lived near a round window. The attic smelled like old wood and warm sunshine. The floor felt smooth and cool under Lila’s tiny feet. Soft light came in, and the air was calm.

Every morning, Lila sang. “Tweet-tweet-tee!” Her voice was bright and sweet. She loved to sing songs she learned from Grandma Bird: gentle songs, happy songs, and songs from faraway places. The attic held boxes, ribbons, buttons, and little bells that chimed when the wind whispered through the window.

One sunny day, Lila had a big idea. “I want to make a special song all by myself,” she said. “I want to choose the notes. I want to choose the words. I want to be independent.”

Lila hopped to a box. She lifted the lid with her beak—creak! Inside were colorful scarves and tiny drums. “These can help me,” she said. She pulled out a red scarf. It felt soft and warm. She tapped a drum—tap tap tap! The sound was round and friendly.

But Lila had a problem. There were too many things. There were bells, buttons, ribbons, and little shakers. There were old music sheets from Grandma Bird, with twisty lines and notes. Lila’s head felt busy. “Oh no,” she said. “I don’t know where to start. How do I make my own song?”

She sat by the window. Her tummy fluttered. She wanted to be independent, but she felt stuck.

Just then, a small mouse peeked out from a wool mitten. “Hello, Lila,” squeaked the mouse. “I’m Mo. I like your singing.”

“Hi, Mo,” Lila said softly. “I want to make a song by myself, but there are too many sounds.”

Mo twitched his whiskers. “Maybe start small,” he said. “Pick two things.”

Lila took a deep breath. The attic smelled like cinnamon from an old candle. “Okay,” she said. “Two things.” She chose the red scarf and one tiny bell.

She spread the scarf on the floor. “This is my song mat,” she said. “I can keep things here.” She placed the bell on the corner. Ding! The sound was bright and shiny. Lila smiled. “First note: ding!”

A gentle moth floated by, wings like pale paper. “Hello,” she whispered. “I am Mina. I flutter to music.”

Lila fluffed her feathers. “Hi, Mina. I’m making a song on my own.”

Mina nodded. “You can make a plan. First, choose the sounds. Then, choose the order,” she said.

“Plan,” Lila repeated. “Good idea.” She looked around. “I choose bell, drum, and voice.” She put the bell on the scarf, the drum next to it, and she sat in front. She made three spots in a line. “First bell, then drum, then sing,” Lila said.

Lila tried it. “Ding, tap, tweet!” The bell sang, the drum hummed, and Lila’s voice floated. It sounded nice, but she wanted more. She wanted her song to say something.

At that moment, the wind tickled the wind chimes by the window—ting-ting-ting! Lila remembered Grandma Bird’s stories about songs from different places. She remembered a gentle “la-la” from a village by the sea, a sweet “do-re” from a festival, and a lively “ba-da” from a street parade.

Lila smiled. “I can add sounds from many places,” she said. “That is cultural appreciation. I can include many voices.”

She lined up colorful buttons like little music steps—red, blue, green. She decided that each color meant a sound:
- Red: ding
- Blue: tap
- Green: la-la

Mo and Mina watched. Lila put the buttons in order: red, blue, green, red, blue, green. She followed the steps. “Ding, tap, la-la, ding, tap, la-la!” Her wings tingled. The attic felt warm and happy.

Mo clapped his tiny paws. “It’s your song!”

Mina swirled in the air. “It’s clear and lovely.”

Lila said, “I did it by myself. I used a plan. I made choices. I also included sounds from many places.”

But then, a box slipped—thump! Buttons rolled all over the floor. The neat line broke. Lila’s heart thumped. “Oh no! My song steps!”

Mo squeaked, “We can help clean.”

Lila took a slow breath. “I can do this. I want to be independent. I will organize.” She made a new rule: buttons in a tray, drum by the scarf, bell by the window. She picked up each button and said, “Red ding, blue tap, green la-la.” She put them in the tray. It took time, but it felt good.

“Look,” Lila said. “Now I can find everything. I made a system.”

She tried again. She chose her order all by herself, mixing in a soft “do-re” and a happy “ba-da.” She sang, “Ding, tap, la-la, do-re, ba-da, tweet!” The attic glowed with sound. It smelled like sunshine. Her chest felt strong and proud.

Mo and Mina swayed together. “We feel included in your music,” said Mina.

“You are,” Lila said. “Everyone can listen. Everyone belongs.”

Lila looked at her tidy tray, her song mat, and the bright window. “I solved my problem,” she said. “I made choices. I made a plan. I stayed calm. I was independent.”

Then Lila sang her new song to the sky. The wind carried it far, like a kiss.

The End.
