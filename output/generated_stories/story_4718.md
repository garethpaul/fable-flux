# Peanut the Parrot Finds His Voice

In a quiet attic, with soft dust like tiny snow and a window that whispered with the wind, lived Peanut the Parrot. Peanut had bright green feathers, a sunny yellow chest, and a beak that clicked when he thought. He loved to make art. He painted with little brushes, he hummed gentle songs, and he used fallen buttons to make pictures that glittered like stars.

One afternoon, the attic felt extra still. The wooden floor felt warm under Peanut’s feet. Sunlight made golden squares on the floor. Peanut took a deep breath. He smelled old books, dry wood, and a sweet hint of lavender from a tiny sachet. “Today,” Peanut said, “I will make a big, beautiful mural! I will use scraps and bits and pieces. I will make something kind, and bright, and new.”

Peanut looked in his box of art things. “Oh!” he chirped. “I am out of blue paint. And I need more paper.” His feathers ruffled. He felt a tiny twist in his tummy. “I want to make my art. I also want to be careful and not waste,” he said softly. Peanut remembered his friend, Mabel the Mouse, who lived near the old hat boxes. Mabel was kind and loved to share. But Peanut was shy to ask. He fluffed his feathers and practiced. “I can use my voice. I can ask with kind words.”

He waddled over to Mabel’s nook. “Tap-tap,” Peanut said with his beak on a little tin lid. “Hi, Mabel.”

Mabel peeked out, her whiskers twitching. “Hi, Peanut! It smells like sunshine today. What do you need?”

Peanut took a slow breath. “I want to make a mural. I am out of blue paint and paper. May I please borrow some? I will use only what I need. I will share my art with you. I will also help you tidy later.”

Mabel smiled. “That was brave to ask. Yes, I can share. I have extra paper from the old calendar, and a little blue paint. Let’s not waste. Let’s use both sides of the paper.”

Peanut’s chest felt light. “Thank you,” he said. “I spoke up! I asked for help!”

They walked back to the sunny square. Peanut dipped his brush. Drip, drip. The paint smelled like blueberries. He painted a river, smooth and shiny. He glued buttons that clicked like tiny rain. He used old ribbons that felt soft and silky. While he worked, he sang, “Color flows, sharing grows,” in a gentle, bouncy tune.

Soon, footsteps thumped on the stairs. Thump, thump. It was Rowan the Rabbit and Pip the Puppy. “What are you making?” Pip wagged his tail so fast it made a soft whoosh.

“A mural,” Peanut said, “about caring for our home. We can make new things from old things. We can use just enough, not too much.”

Rowan twitched his ears. “Can we help?”

Peanut felt that small twist again. He looked at his little pile of supplies. He wanted his mural just so. He also wanted friends to join. He put a wing over his heart and said to himself, “I can say what I need.” Then he spoke out loud. “Yes, you can help. I need careful hands. Please use small dabs of glue. Please ask before cutting. We will share, and we will save.”

Rowan and Pip nodded. “We can do that,” said Rowan. “Small dabs!”

As they worked, the attic filled with sounds: snip-snip of scissors, pat-pat of paws, click-click of buttons, and soft humming. The mural grew. There was a blue river, tall green trees made from old magazine pages, and a bright sun cut from the yellow lid of a cookie tin. It looked cheerful and cozy.

Then Pip reached for the last big piece of paper. Peanut’s tummy flipped. He needed that piece for the sky. He took a breath. “Pip,” Peanut said kindly, “I need that piece for the sky. Can you please use a smaller piece? We are saving our supplies.”

Pip paused. His ears drooped. “Oh. I didn’t know,” he said quietly.

Peanut stepped closer. “Thank you for listening. I feel worried when the big piece is gone. I feel calm when we plan. Let’s use small pieces together. We can make a patchwork sky.”

Pip brightened. “Patchwork sky! Woof! That’s fun!”

They tore tiny bits from the edges. Rip-rip, like soft paper rain. They glued the pieces in a gentle line. The sky became a mosaic of blues and whites. It looked like fluffy clouds drifting.

Mabel clapped her tiny paws. “You did it! You asked for what you needed. You shared. You saved!”

Peanut’s feathers felt warm with pride. “We did it together,” he said. He added a sign made from a tea box. It read, “Please care for our world. Use just enough. Share and repair.”

They all stood back. The mural shone in the sunlight. It smelled like paper and a hint of blueberry paint. It sounded like quiet breaths and happy hearts.

Rowan said, “What did we learn?”

Peanut smiled. “We learned to use our voices. We can ask for help. We can say what we need with kind words. We can share and not waste. When we care for things, the world smiles.”

Pip wagged. “And patchwork skies are awesome!”

Mabel nodded. “And friends who listen are special.”

Peanut puffed his chest. “Color flows, sharing grows,” he sang softly. The attic seemed to hum along.

The friends placed the mural by the window. The wind whispered, yes, yes, yes. The quiet attic felt bright and brave.

The End.
