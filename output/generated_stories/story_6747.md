# Koala Kori’s Calm Dinner

Kori the koala had soft gray fur and the gentlest eyes. Kori loved quiet places and slow chewing. Tonight was special. Kori had been invited to dinner in the big, bright dining room at Cousin Kipa’s house.

The dining room smelled warm and yummy, like fresh bread and sweet carrots. The long table glowed under a soft lamp. The chairs were smooth and cool. The window was open, and a kind breeze whispered, “Whoosh, whoosh,” making the curtains swish like soft clouds.

“Welcome, Kori!” said Cousin Kipa, a cheerful koala with a wide smile. “Come sit here.”

Kori climbed onto a chair. The seat felt firm but comfy. Plates clinked softly, spoons tapped, and everyone’s voices hummed like gentle bees. Kori liked the calm sounds.

On the table sat a rainbow of bowls. Green peas, orange slices, fluffy rice, crunchy cucumber, and a new dish that shimmered golden. Kori sniffed. “Mmm,” Kori said, “I smell honey. And… ginger?” The new dish smelled sweet and tickly at the same time.

Aunt Kiki set a small plate in front of Kori. “This is golden pumpkin stew,” she said. “It is sweet and soft. We made it together.”

Kori blinked. Pumpkin stew was new. New could feel big and wobbly inside the tummy. Kori held the spoon, feeling cool metal against warm paw. “Thank you,” Kori said in a calm, soft voice.

Cousin Kipa scooped a big spoonful and blew gently. “I love pumpkin stew!” he said. “It tastes like sunshine.”

Kori watched the steam curl up like tiny ghosts. Kori took a deep breath. In, out. Smell the sweet. Feel the warm. Sit up soft and steady.

Across the table, Little Nori the joey wrinkled her nose. “I don’t like orange food,” she said loudly. “No, no, no!”

Kori looked at Nori with kind eyes. “It’s okay,” said Kori. “Sometimes new food feels new. We can be patient.”

Aunt Kiki nodded. “We can try one tiny taste. A baby taste. Or we can wait and smell first.”

Kori smiled. “I like to listen to my tummy,” Kori said. “I ask, ‘Are you ready, tummy?’”

Kori closed eyes and listened. The dining room was peaceful. Chairs whispered along the floor. Someone poured water. “Glug, glug.” The stew smelled warm and friendly.

Kori dipped the spoon and scooped just a baby taste. Kori blew. “Hoo… hoo…” Then Kori touched the spoon to the tongue.

The taste was soft and sweet, like a hug. There was a small zing from the ginger. It made Kori’s tongue tingle in a nice way. “Oh,” said Kori. “It is new. It is different. I think I can try another tiny taste.”

Nori peeped over her bowl. “Is it yucky?”

Kori shook their head slowly. “It is not yucky. It is just new. And new can be okay.” Kori took another small taste. “I am giving it time. I am being patient with my mouth.”

Kipa grinned. “I take two tiny tastes when I am not sure,” he said. “Then I decide.”

Nori dipped her spoon. “Baby taste,” she whispered. She touched the tip of her tongue. “Hmm.” She frowned a little, then smiled a little. “It’s… warm.”

Aunt Kiki clapped softly. “Good trying.”

Kori felt proud. Not because of liking or not liking, but because of trying. Trying felt like growing. “We can accept new things,” Kori said. “Even if we don’t love them right away. We can say, ‘It’s okay to be new.’”

Nori nodded. “It’s okay to be new.”

They all ate together. The rice felt fluffy and cozy. The cucumbers crunched like tiny drums. The orange slices were juicy and bright, like sunshine drops. The pumpkin stew warmed Kori’s belly, like a small fire on a cool night.

Then Uncle Koa brought a big bowl of green peas. “Oops,” he said as a few peas tumbled. “Little green balls on the run!” The peas rolled across the table, clicking and bouncing. Everyone laughed. Kori gently placed a small napkin wall so the peas would not fall. “Slow and steady,” Kori said.

When the peas were safe again, Kori said, “Sometimes things roll away. We can be calm and make a peaceful plan.”

After dinner, Aunt Kiki gave each of them a tiny sticker star. “For patience,” she said, sticking one on Kori’s fur. It felt papery and light.

Kori’s heart felt warm. “Thank you. I tried new food. I waited. I listened to my tummy. And I stayed calm.”

Nori held up her sticker. “I tried too! I still don’t love the stew,” she said. “But I like the warm feeling. Maybe I will try again another day.”

Kori smiled big. “That is wonderful. Acceptance is saying, ‘It’s okay to be me. It’s okay for you to be you. It’s okay for food to be new.’ We can improve a little each time.”

Kipa nodded. “We can add a tiny squeeze of lemon next time!”

Everyone cleaned up together. The spoons chimed. The water ran soft. The window breeze said, “Whoosh,” again. The dining room felt extra cozy now, full of try-brave feelings.

At the door, Kori hugged Nori. “You were patient,” Kori said. “I am proud of you.”

Nori hugged back. “You were calm. I learned from you.”

Walking home under the twinkly night, Kori smiled. The stars looked like tiny sticker stars in the sky. Kori whispered, “Accept the new. Be patient. Keep trying. Find peaceful ways.” Kori’s belly felt happy and full, and Kori’s heart felt even fuller.

The End.
