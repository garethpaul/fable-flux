# Leo and the Little Balcony Jungle

Leo was a brave little lion cub with soft golden fur and bright curious eyes. He lived with his family in a cozy home. Outside the living room was a small balcony garden. It was a tiny jungle! There were pots of mint and basil, a tomato vine with shiny red tomatoes, a small lemon tree, and a patch of soft, sweet-smelling lavender. The sun felt warm on the leaves, and a breeze made them whisper, swish-swish.

One sunny morning, Mama Lion said, “Leo, today you may care for the balcony garden. I will be nearby if you need me, but try your best first.”

Leo’s tail twitched. “I can do it!” he said. He wanted to be self-reliant. That means trying to do things on his own, step by step.

Leo took a deep breath. He smelled mint—fresh and cool. He felt the wooden floor under his paws—smooth and warm. He listened to a buzzing bee. “Bzz, bzz,” it sang as it visited a flower. Leo smiled. “Hello, Bee,” he whispered. “I will be careful.”

On a small table sat his tools: a tiny watering can, a soft brush, a little trowel, and a cloth. There was also a picture chart that Mama had made. It showed four steps with simple drawings.

Leo read the pictures aloud:
“Step 1: Check the leaves.
Step 2: Give water if the soil is dry.
Step 3: Pick ripe food.
Step 4: Clean up and rest.”

“I can follow the steps,” Leo said. “I will try.”

He went to Step 1. He touched the leaves gently with his paw. “Hello, leaves,” he said. The mint leaves were bouncy. The basil leaves smelled peppery and sweet. But the lemon tree had three droopy leaves. Leo frowned. “You look tired,” he said softly.

He remembered: when leaves droop, the soil might be dry. He poked the soil with one claw. It felt dry like sand. “Okay,” Leo said. “Step 2.”

He filled the tiny watering can. The water tinkled like a little bell. “Tinkle, tinkle.” He carried it carefully. His paws felt strong, and his heart felt brave.

“Slow and steady,” Leo told himself. He poured a little water around the lemon tree. He looked at the soil. It grew dark and shiny. He checked the basil and mint. Their soil felt a bit damp, so he only gave them a sip. “Just a sip for you,” he said. “I am learning to listen.”

Next was the tomato vine. Red, round tomatoes hung like little suns. Leo looked at his chart. “Step 3: Pick ripe food.” He touched the tomatoes. One was soft and ready. “Twist and pull,” he said. He twisted gently and pulled. Plop! The tomato dropped into his paw. It smelled sweet and warm. “I did it!” Leo cheered.

But then he saw a tiny snail on a leaf. The snail had a shiny shell. It was eating a little hole. “Oh!” said Leo. He felt worried. He wanted to run to Mama. He paused. He looked at the chart again. He looked at his tools. There was a soft brush.

“I can try,” Leo whispered. He spoke to the snail. “Hello, little snail. The leaves are for everyone, but we need to share.” He used the soft brush to move the snail to a small saucer with wet leaves Mama kept nearby. The snail curled happily. “Safe place for you,” Leo said, smiling. “We can both be okay.”

He checked the lavender next. He brushed off dry petals. They smelled calm, like a sleepy hug. He hummed, “Mmm, mmm,” and felt proud.

Then, a small lemon rolled off the tree and bumped his paw. “Ouch,” Leo said. He felt a little sting and a little sad. He looked at his paw. No cut, just a bump. He remembered self-care. He sat down and took a deep breath. “In… and out…” He pressed the cool cloth to his paw. The coolness felt nice. “I can care for me,” he said. He waited until the sting went away.

When he felt better, he looked around. “Step 4: Clean up and rest.” He put the tools back on the table. He swept tiny leaves into a bucket. Swish, swish. He wiped a bit of water from the floor. He sighed a happy sigh.

Mama Lion came to the door and peeked out. “How did it go, brave cub?”

Leo’s eyes shone. “I followed the steps! I gave water. I picked a tomato. I helped a snail. I cared for my paw. I cleaned up. I tried by myself.”

Mama smiled and nuzzled his head. “You were careful. You were kind. You learned and took care of yourself. That is self-reliance.”

Leo placed the tomato in a little bowl. “Can we share this?” he asked.

“Yes,” Mama said. She sliced the tomato. It tasted juicy and sweet. “Mmm,” said Leo. “I like learning. I like trying. It’s okay to ask for help, but I try first.”

Mama nodded. “We all learn at our own pace. I accept your brave tries, and you accept your own feelings. That is growing.”

Leo looked at the balcony garden. The leaves no longer drooped. The mint and basil waved in the breeze. The lemon tree stood a little taller. The bee buzzed, “Bzz, bzz,” and the air smelled like sunshine and green things.

Leo stretched and yawned. He felt calm, proud, and strong. “Tomorrow,” he said, “I will try again.”

The End.
