# Momo’s Calm-Down Plan

Momo was a courageous monkey with bright, curious eyes and soft, fuzzy fur. One sunny morning, Momo’s family had guests. The guest room smelled like clean soap and lavender. The curtains were light and floaty, and the blanket was smooth and cool like a leaf in shade. Momo liked to peek in and see the neat pillows stacked like little clouds.

“Remember,” Mama Monkey said gently, holding Momo’s small hand, “this is the guest room. We use quiet feet and careful hands. Our guests are resting.”

Momo nodded. “Quiet feet. Careful hands. I can do that.”

Auntie Bee and Baby Pip were the guests. They smiled and waved. “Thank you for sharing your home,” Auntie Bee said.

Momo loved to share. But Momo also loved to play. On the guest bed sat Momo’s favorite red ball. It was shiny like a ripe apple. Momo’s tail swished with happy wiggles. “My ball!” Momo cheered, bouncing up.

“Slow and soft,” Mama reminded. “We can get your ball after we ask.”

Momo took a deep breath, just like Mama taught. “Auntie Bee, may I get my ball, please?”

“Of course, dear,” Auntie Bee smiled. “Go gently.”

Momo tiptoed across the soft rug. The rug tickled Momo’s toes. But when Momo reached for the ball, Baby Pip grabbed it first. Pip laughed, “Mine!” and hugged the ball tight.

Momo’s tummy got hot. Momo’s cheeks felt prickly. Momo’s hands curled into little fists. “Hey! That’s my ball!” Momo squeaked. The words came out loud and sharp, like a snap.

Baby Pip’s smile faded. Auntie Bee looked worried. Mama’s eyes were kind but firm. “Pause, Momo,” Mama said softly. “What is your plan when big anger shows up?”

Momo remembered the plan they practiced every day. It was called Momo’s Calm-Down Plan.

Step 1: Stop and Freeze.
Step 2: Take three deep breaths.
Step 3: Use words to name the feeling.
Step 4: Choose a peaceful solution.

Momo stood very still, like a tree in the wind. “Stop and freeze,” Momo whispered. The hot feeling wiggled, but Momo stayed planted.

Momo placed a hand on the round belly and breathed in through the nose. In… 1. Out… 1. In… 2. Out… 2. In… 3. Out… 3. The air smelled like lavender and clean sheets. The prickly feeling got softer.

“My feeling is anger,” Momo said in a small voice. “I feel mad because I want my ball.”

Mama nodded. “Thank you for telling us.”

Momo looked at Baby Pip. Pip was small, with tiny hands and big eyes. Pip wasn’t trying to be mean; Pip just didn’t know. Momo touched the soft blanket and thought, What is a peaceful solution?

Momo’s mind remembered more ideas from the routine chart by the door:
- Ask to take turns.
- Find a timer.
- Offer a trade.
- Play together.

Momo knelt down so Pip could see Momo’s face. “Baby Pip,” Momo said, using a calm voice like warm tea, “I feel mad. I want my ball. Can we take turns?”

Pip blinked. “Turn?”

Auntie Bee smiled. “That is a kind idea.”

Mama brought the kitchen timer shaped like a lemon. It clicked-clicked softly in Mama’s hand. “How about two minutes for Pip, then two minutes for Momo?” Mama suggested.

Momo nodded. “Okay.” The lemon timer went tick-tick-tick. Momo watched and waited. Waiting was hard, but Momo rubbed the smooth edge of the blanket and hummed a quiet tune. The soft fabric and the gentle tick-tick helped.

“Would you like to play roll-and-catch with the pillow?” Mama asked.

“Yes,” Momo said. They rolled a plush pillow back and forth on the floor. Whoosh. Thump. Giggle. The room felt lighter, like sunshine inside.

Ding! The timer chimed. Pip looked up. Momo smiled and held out open hands. “My turn?”

Pip hugged the ball once, then placed it in Momo’s palms. “Mo-mo turn,” Pip said.

“Thank you,” Momo replied. “After my turn, it’s your turn again.” Momo bounced the ball gently so it wouldn’t shake the bed. Boing. Boing. Boing. The red ball flashed like a little cherry each time it rose.

Auntie Bee sighed with relief. “That was very respectful, Momo.”

Momo felt proud and calm, like sitting under a big, shady tree. “My plan worked,” Momo said. “Stop and freeze. Breathe three times. Name the feeling. Choose a peaceful solution.”

The guests clapped. Mama gave Momo a soft hug that smelled like bananas and soap. “You were courageous,” Mama said. “Courage is not only big jumps. Courage can be calming a big feeling.”

Momo grinned. “I can be brave on the inside.”

For the rest of the morning, Momo remembered the guest room rules: quiet feet, careful hands, gentle voice. Momo and Pip took turns with the ball and played roll-and-catch on the soft rug. The lemon timer ticked and dinged, making the game fair. When someone felt grumpy, they used the Calm-Down Plan. Soon the guest room felt warm and friendly, like a cozy nest.

At nap time, Auntie Bee whispered, “Thank you for sharing, and thank you for peace.” Pip yawned and waved. “Bye-bye, ball,” Pip said, smiling.

Momo placed the ball on a shelf and patted it. “See you after rest,” Momo told it, keeping the routine steady. Then Momo and Mama tiptoed out on quiet feet, the lavender air soft behind them.

That night, Mama asked, “What did you learn today, Momo?”

Momo thought. “I learned my anger is a feeling that can grow big. But I can help it get small. I can stop, breathe, say my feeling, and find a peaceful way. That keeps the room calm and our hearts kind.”

Mama kissed Momo’s forehead. “That is a wonderful lesson.”

Momo smiled, snug in bed, heart as light as a feather. “Tomorrow,” Momo whispered, “I will use my plan again.”

The End.
