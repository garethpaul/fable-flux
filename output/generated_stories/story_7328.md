# Polly’s Thank-You Tool

In a sunny garage workshop, tools hung like shiny stars on pegboards. The air smelled like warm wood and orange soap. Sunlight made little dust sparkles dance. In the corner sat a bright green parrot named Polly. Polly was a creative parrot. She loved to make, fix, and imagine.

“Squawk! Idea time!” Polly chirped, ruffling her rainbow feathers. She hopped from the workbench to a stool. Her claws clicked on the metal. She looked at a small broken wagon wheel on the table. It belonged to her friend, Max.

Max was a gentle boy who visited the garage with his grandma. He always said, “Please” and “Thank you.” Today, Max had asked Polly for help. But Max wasn’t here now. He had gone home to rest. Polly wanted to surprise him with a fix.

Polly picked up a tiny screwdriver. “Twist-twist,” she sang. The screw rolled away and went ting! across the floor.

“Oh no!” Polly puffed her feathers. She hopped down, brushing her wing on cool concrete. It felt smooth under her feet. She found the screw by the door, but a soft sign hung there: “Ask before borrowing tools. This is my special set. —Grandpa Joe.”

Polly tilted her head. “Boundaries,” she said softly. “Ask first.” She remembered how Grandpa Joe smiled when people respected his tools. Polly took a deep breath. “I can still fix the wheel. I will use my own tools.”

Polly opened her little blue toolbox. Inside were safe, small tools just for her: a tiny wrench, some wooden pegs, a kid-safe glue, and a bright yellow tape measure. The tools smelled like pine and lemon. Polly felt brave.

She tried the tiny wrench. It slipped. She tried the pegs. They didn’t fit. Her wings drooped. “This is hard,” she whispered.

Polly closed her eyes and listened. The clock tick-tocked like a slow drum. A soft breeze whooshed under the garage door. Somewhere, a drip-drop sang from the sink.

“I can try a new way,” Polly told herself. “I can learn. I can grow.” She hopped back on the stool. “Growth mindset,” she said, nodding.

Polly looked around. She saw a cardboard tube, a rubber band, and a bright red bottle cap. “Innovation!” she chirped. “I can make a helper tool.”

She slid the bottle cap onto the end of her tiny wrench, padding it with folded tape so it fit snug. She wrapped the rubber band around for grip. “Grip-grip,” Polly giggled. She placed the new tool on the wheel’s tiny nut. With a careful twist-twist, it turned. Click!

“Squawk! It works!” Her heart fluttered happily.

Polly gently sanded a rough edge on the wheel with a small emery board from her own kit. It made a soft shhh sound. She dabbed a tiny bit of kid-safe glue and pressed a peg in place. She waited. She hummed while it dried. “Hum-hum-hum.”

Just then, Max peeked in with his grandma. He wore bright blue shoes that squeaked. “Hi, Polly!” Max waved. “Grandpa Joe said we could come say thank you.”

Polly puffed with pride. “Hello, Max! I fixed your wheel with my own tools,” she said. “I saw Grandpa Joe’s sign. I did not take his special tools. I made a new helper tool instead.”

Max’s eyes went wide. “Wow,” he whispered. “You are so smart, Polly.”

Grandpa Joe stepped in too, wiping his hands on a clean rag. His gray mustache twitched with a smile. “I heard some happy chirps,” he said. He looked at the wheel. He looked at the bottle-cap tool. “Now that is clever.”

Polly bowed her head. “Thank you for keeping your tools safe, Grandpa Joe. Your sign helped me remember to ask. I am grateful.”

Grandpa Joe nodded. “And I am grateful you respected my tools. You made something new. You learned. That makes my heart warm.” He placed his hand over his chest. “Thank you.”

Max hugged his wagon. The wheels rolled smooth and made a soft rumble. “Thank you, Polly,” he said. “You fixed it! You kept my wagon safe. I feel happy inside.”

Polly’s feathers felt like sunshine. “I am thankful for my toolbox,” she said. “I am thankful for my brain and my friends. Thank you for trusting me.”

They all stood for a moment, listening to the gentle tick-tock and the quiet rumble of the wagon. The garage felt cozy and bright.

Grandpa Joe pointed to a small hook on the wall. “Polly, would you like a special spot to hang your new tool?” he asked.

Polly’s eyes sparkled. “Yes, please!” She hung the bottle-cap tool on the hook. A little label read: “Polly’s Helper. Made with gratitude.”

Max clapped. “Can we celebrate?” he asked.

“Let’s have orange slices,” Grandma said, coming in with a plate. The oranges smelled sweet and juicy. Polly tasted a tiny drop on her beak. “Mmm! Zing!” she giggled. Everyone laughed.

Polly looked around the warm, busy garage. “Today I learned,” she said. “When things are hard, I can try new ideas. I can respect other people’s things. And I can say thank you.”

Max and Grandpa Joe said together, “We are grateful for you, Polly.”

Polly fluffed her feathers and smiled. “And I am grateful for you.”

The End.
