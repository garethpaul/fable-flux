# Otter Ollie and the Balcony Garden Chores

Otter Ollie loved to splash, play, and wiggle his whiskers. He lived with Nana on a small home with a sunny balcony. On the balcony was a little garden with pots of mint, strawberries, and tiny tomatoes. The air smelled like sweet leaves and warm soil. Bees buzzed softly. The wind tickled Ollie’s fur.

“Good morning, garden!” Ollie chirped. He tapped a pot—plink, plink. “Let’s play!”

Nana smiled and opened the sliding door. “Good morning, Ollie. It’s chore time first,” she said kindly. “Then we play.”

“Chores?” Ollie made a funny face. “But I want to splash in my water tub!”

Nana chuckled. “Chores help our garden grow. When we help, we learn. When we learn, we can solve little problems.” She handed Ollie a small watering can. It was light and blue, with a gentle spout. “You can be the Water Helper.”

Ollie held the can. It felt cool in his paws. “Water Helper!” he cheered. “Okay!”

He tipped the can. Sprinkle, sprinkle, sprinkle. The mint leaves shivered. The soil smelled like rain. “This is fun!” Ollie said.

After a while, Nana pointed to a droopy tomato plant. “Hmm,” she said. “I see a problem.”

Ollie’s whiskers twitched. “A problem? What is it?”

“The tomato plant looks tired,” said Nana. “The stem is bent, and the leaves need a lift. What can we do?”

Ollie looked closely. The leaves were soft and floppy. The red tomato was heavy. He touched the soil. It was damp, squish-squish.

“More water?” he asked.

Nana shook her head. “Not now. It already had a drink. Let’s think.” She tapped her chin. “What could help hold it up?”

Ollie blinked. He looked around. There were wooden sticks in a basket, and soft string like a shoelace. He wiggled his tail. “A stick! And a tie!”

“That’s a smart plan,” Nana said. “Let’s try.”

Ollie held the stick. It felt smooth. He pushed it into the soil—push, push—right next to the plant. “Like this?”

“Yes,” said Nana. “Now we tie the stem gently. Not too tight.”

Ollie poked out his tongue as he focused. He tied the string in a soft bow. The plant stood taller. The tomato stopped pulling it down.

“It worked!” Ollie clapped. “We solved the problem!”

Nana nodded. “You did. You used your eyes. You thought. You tried.”

Ollie felt warm and proud.

They kept going with their chores. Ollie wiped the leaves with a soft cloth. Swish, swish. Dust came off like tiny clouds. He picked dry, yellow leaves and put them in a small bin. Plop, plop. He brushed spilled soil back into the pots. Brush, brush.

Then Ollie saw another problem. Ants were walking in a line—one, two, three, four—march, march, march. They were near the strawberries.

“Oh no!” Ollie squeaked. “They want our berries!”

Nana crouched down. “Ants are little helpers in nature, but we don’t want them in the berries. What can we do?”

Ollie thought. He looked at the watering can. He looked at the broom. He sniffed the mint. It smelled cool and fresh. His nose wiggled. “Mint smells strong. Maybe ants don’t like strong smell?”

“Great idea,” Nana said. “Let’s try mint water around the pots.”

They picked a few mint leaves and put them in a jar. Nana added warm water. Ollie stirred with a spoon—clink, clink, clink. The water smelled bright and tingly.

Ollie poured a little mint water in a circle around the pots. The ants turned around. They marched away. “Bye, ants!” Ollie waved.

“You solved another problem,” said Nana. “You used kind, safe ideas.”

Ollie’s chest puffed with pride. “I like chores,” he said. “Chores are like puzzles.”

“They are,” Nana agreed. “Chores help us learn patience, care, and smart thinking.”

They finished the chores. The balcony looked neat and happy. The plants stood tall. The strawberries and tomatoes glowed in the sun. The air smelled like mint and sunshine.

“Now,” Nana said, “it’s play time.”

Ollie filled his little tub. Splash! The water sparkled on his fur. He giggled and swished. But after a moment, he peeked at the garden. “Nana? Can I have one strawberry?”

Nana smiled. “Yes. You earned it.”

Ollie picked a red berry. It felt warm from the sun. He took a bite. Juicy! Sweet! “Mmm,” he hummed. “Helping tastes good.”

Nana laughed. “Helping feels good, too.”

Ollie nodded. “Today I learned: Chores help things grow. When there is a problem, I can look, think, and try. I can be a helper.”

Nana hugged him. “That’s the lesson, Ollie. Little helpers make big differences.”

Ollie wiggled his whiskers and looked at the bright, tidy balcony garden. “Tomorrow,” he said, “I will be the Leaf Checker. And the Ant Watcher. And the Water Helper.”

Nana winked. “We’ll do our chores together.”

The wind whispered through the mint. The bees buzzed softly. Ollie splashed one more time and smiled.

“I love our garden,” he said.

“And our chores,” Nana added.

Ollie grinned. “Chores, learning, and problem-solving!”

The End.
