# Ollie Otter and the Balcony Garden Fair Share

Ollie was a playful otter with bright eyes and a wiggly tail. He lived with Nana on the fourth floor, where a small balcony garden grew in the sunshine. The garden smelled like sweet basil and wet dirt. Bees hummed softly. The leaves felt cool and velvety when Ollie touched them with his paws.

“Today is Garden Day!” Nana said, tying on her sun hat. “We will water, we will weed, and we will pick tomatoes.”

Ollie bounced. “I love tomatoes! I can pick them all!”

Nana smiled. “We will share the jobs. We will be fair. We follow directions so the plants stay happy.”

Ollie nodded quickly, but his tail wiggled extra fast. He wanted to do everything.

Nana set out three small baskets. “One for you, one for me, and one for our neighbor, Mr. Blue. He gave us the tomato seeds. We will share the harvest.”

Ollie wrinkled his nose. “But I want more tomatoes for me.”

Nana touched Ollie’s paw. “We are fair. We take a turn, we share, and we follow the plan.”

Ollie looked at the shiny red tomatoes. They looked like tiny suns. He could almost taste their pop and sweetness. His tummy made a happy sound. “I will try,” he said.

First, Nana showed Ollie how to water. “Slow and low,” she said. “Water the roots, not the leaves.”

Ollie held the watering can. The water made a soft whoosh. It sparkled in the light. “Slow and low,” he whispered. He tried to go slow, but he splashed too hard. Water sprayed everywhere. “Oops!” Ollie giggled. His whiskers dripped.

Nana laughed softly. “It’s okay. Try again. Follow the directions.” She guided his paws. The water made a quiet glug-glug. The soil turned dark and smelled like rain.

“Like that?” Ollie asked.

“Just like that,” said Nana.

Next was weeding. “Only the little weeds,” Nana said. “Not the tomato plants.”

Ollie leaned close. He sniffed the green smells. He felt the warm sun on his fur. He found a tiny weed and plucked it. It made a tiny pop in the dirt. “This is fun!” he said.

Then came picking. “We will pick only the red ones,” Nana said. “Not the green ones. Green ones need more time.”

Ollie saw a big red tomato. He reached out. “I pick this one!”

“Gently,” Nana said. “Twist and lift.”

Ollie twisted and lifted. The tomato came free. It was smooth and warm in his paw. “I did it!” he cheered.

He put it in his basket. He saw another, and another. His basket filled up fast. He felt proud. He felt excited. He felt… tempted.

“What if,” Ollie thought, “I put some of Nana’s tomatoes in my basket too? Then I would have the most.”

He looked at Nana. She was humming and counting. “One for me, one for you, one for Mr. Blue,” she said, placing each tomato in its basket. Her voice was calm, like the bees.

Ollie glanced at Mr. Blue’s basket. It only had two. Ollie’s basket had five. His tummy didn’t feel happy now. It felt squirmy. His whiskers drooped.

Nana looked over. “Ollie, are we being fair?” she asked gently.

Ollie’s paws felt hot. “I… I wanted more,” he whispered. “I didn’t follow the plan.”

Nana knelt. Her eyes were kind. “Thank you for telling the truth. Fair means everyone gets a turn and a share. We follow directions so it works for all of us.”

Ollie looked at the baskets. He took a deep breath. The basil smelled sweet. The breeze felt cool on his fur. “I want to be fair,” he said. “How do I fix it?”

“We can sort again,” Nana said. “We can count together.”

Ollie nodded. “Let’s count!”

They poured the tomatoes onto a soft towel. The tomatoes rolled and made little thumps. Together they counted, tapping each with a gentle paw.

“One for me,” Ollie said, plink.

“One for Nana,” Nana said, plink.

“One for Mr. Blue,” they said together, plink.

They went around and around, sharing one by one, like a quiet tomato parade. Ollie smiled. His tummy felt better now. His whiskers lifted.

When they finished, each basket was the same. Ollie peeked into Mr. Blue’s basket. It looked bright and happy.

Nana clapped softly. “That was fair. You followed directions and you learned.”

Ollie puffed his cheeks and blew out a happy sigh. “Fair feels good,” he said. “I like fair.”

Just then, Mr. Blue stepped onto the balcony with his blue hat. “I smelled tomatoes!” he said with a big grin. “Oh my! What a harvest.”

Ollie held up the neighbor’s basket. “We shared. We made it fair.”

Mr. Blue’s eyes twinkled. “Thank you, Ollie. You followed the plan. That helps everyone.”

Ollie’s tail swished. “I learned to water slow and low. I learned to pick the red ones. And I learned to share, one by one.”

Nana cut a tomato into slices. The juice was sweet and warm. They tasted together. “Mmm,” Ollie said. “Fair tastes yummy!”

Nana smiled. “Fairness is a choice. When we share and follow directions, we all grow.”

Ollie looked at the plants, at the baskets, and at his friends. The bees hummed. The leaves rustled. The sun felt soft and golden.

“I will be fair tomorrow, too,” he said.

“And I will help you remember,” Nana said, tapping his nose.

Ollie giggled. He felt proud, calm, and kind. The garden felt happy, too.

The End.
