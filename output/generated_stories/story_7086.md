# Brushes of Many Colors

In a bright, sunny creative art room, a determined rabbit named Rumi twitched his whiskers and took a deep breath. The room smelled like fresh paper and sweet, safe glue. Paint jars sat in a rainbow line. Soft music hummed like a gentle bee.

Today was mural day. Children from many places would make one big picture together. Rumi wanted the mural to show everyone’s stories. He wanted it to be kind and fair.

“I can do this,” Rumi whispered, patting his chest. His paws felt shaky, but his heart felt brave.

Rumi hopped to the big table. The table was cool and smooth. He pressed his paw on the paper. “Let’s make a Happy Earth mural!” he said. “We can share our cultures and help our planet too.”

A little girl named Aiko smiled. “I can paint cherry blossoms like my grandma’s garden in Japan.”

A boy named Mateo grinned. “I’ll paint a bright sun, like in Mexico.”

A child named Zuri clapped. “I’ll paint a drum from Ghana. Boom-boom!”

Rumi’s ears perked up. “Yes! We can show many places. Many hearts in one picture.”

He rolled out recycled paper. It felt a little bumpy. “This paper is saved from the scrap bin,” Rumi said. “We are conserving, so we don’t waste.”

“Good idea!” said Aiko.

Rumi opened the paint jars. “We will share paints and water,” he added. “That’s a healthy habit for the Earth. We can use just what we need.”

He filled one big cup with clean water. “We rinse brushes together,” he said. “We’ll keep the water clean as long as we can.”

The brushes were soft and tickly. The water went swish-swish. The room filled with tiny sounds: tap-tap of brushes on the cup, rustle-rustle of paper, and happy hums.

Then a problem popped up. Mateo frowned. “My sun is too big. It covers Aiko’s blossoms.”

Aiko’s face fell. “Oh no. My pink petals are hiding.”

Zuri looked worried. “My drum has no space. I only have a small corner.”

Rumi’s paws paused. His tummy did a small flip. His ears drooped a little. “This is tricky,” he said softly.

He took a slow breath in. He let a slow breath out. “Healthy breath,” he said. “In… out… We can think clearly.”

Rumi stood tall. “We have a problem. But we can solve it. We can make room for every culture. We can be brave and kind.”

He hopped to the paint. “We can layer and share space. The sun can peek, the blossoms can dance around it, and the drum can sing in the middle.”

“How?” asked Mateo.

Rumi dipped his brush and pointed gently. “Mateo, make your sun a warm hug around the blossoms. Aiko, let some petals float in front of the sun. Zuri, paint the drum with patterns from your home. We can place it where the petals and sun meet. They can be friends.”

Aiko’s eyes brightened. “I can add tiny petals. They can float like snow.”

Zuri smiled wide. “My drum can have triangles and dots. I learned them from my auntie.”

Mateo nodded. “My sun can share its light.”

They began again. Swish-swish. Tap-tap. The sun became a golden circle that hugged the page. Cherry blossoms dotted the air with soft pink. The drum sat proudly, red and brown, with bright patterns that told a story.

Rumi added a little river at the bottom. “This river is for all of us,” he said. “It brings water to drink and to wash. We take care of it.”

He dipped his brush lightly. “We use just a little paint,” he reminded them. “We can save some for tomorrow.”

A tiny child named Noor came close. “Can I add dates? We eat them at home.”

“Please do,” said Rumi. “Food from many places keeps us healthy. We can also paint a bowl to share.”

Noor painted sweet brown ovals on a small blue plate. “Yum,” Noor giggled.

Aiko added green tea leaves. Mateo painted corn. Zuri painted okra. Rumi drew a carrot. The air smelled like paint and a little like oranges from a snack bowl in the corner.

Suddenly, the water cup turned muddy. “Uh-oh,” said Zuri.

Rumi nodded. “Time for a water break. We can pour this old water into the plant pot. It will not hurt the plant. Then we will use fresh water and not waste.”

They carefully fed the thirsty classroom fern. The soil made a soft crinkle. The plant stood a little taller, like it said, “Thank you.”

Back at the mural, they wrote small words in many languages: Hello, Hola, Konnichiwa, Jambo, Salaam. The letters looked like a happy dance.

Rumi stepped back. His heart thumped with joy. “We did it,” he said. “Everyone is seen. Everyone belongs. We cared for the Earth and our bodies, and we were brave.”

The teacher smiled. “What do we call this mural?”

Rumi’s whiskers twitched. “Brushes of Many Colors,” he said.

They all clapped. The room filled with warm light and friendly smells: paint, paper, and orange peels. Rumi felt calm and strong.

He turned to his friends. “We learned that our cultures are different and special. When we listen and share, we make one big, beautiful picture. We use healthy habits for our bodies and our Earth. That is courage.”

Everyone nodded. The mural glowed.

The End.
