# Tully the Turtle Takes a Slow Breath

Tully was a shy turtle who loved the quiet garden shed. The shed smelled like fresh soil and wood. Sunlight slid through tiny windows and made warm stripes on the floor. Soft dust floated in the light, like little stars.

One morning, Tully peeked from his shell and whispered, “Hello, paints. Hello, pots. Hello, broom.” The brushes in a jar made a little clink-clink sound. Tully liked to paint tiny dots on smooth stones. He called them his calm stones.

“Today I will paint a rainbow,” he said in a small, hopeful voice.

Just then, the shed door creaked. Creak… eeeek! In came friends from all around the garden.

Mina the mouse scurried in with a colorful scarf. “Hola, Tully!” she squeaked. “I brought music!” She tapped two little buttons like drums. Tap-tap, tap-tap.

Benji the beetle rolled a shiny marble. “I want to play rolling games!” he buzzed.

Pip the puppy padded in with soft paws and a wagging tail. Thump-thump, thump. “Let’s dance!” he barked.

The shed got busy and noisy. Tap-tap, buzz-buzz, thump-thump! Tully’s heart went thump too. The brushes clinked louder. His shell felt tight. He wanted quiet. He wanted space. His rainbow paints quivered.

“I… I need room,” Tully whispered, but the sounds swirled all around. Pip’s happy tail knocked Tully’s paint water. Splash! It dripped down, cold on Tully’s toes.

Tully’s cheeks felt hot. His tummy felt twisty. His head felt full, like a balloon. He felt a bubble of mad coming up, up, up.

“I DON’T LIKE THIS!” he yelled. His shout made the windows ring. Everyone froze. Pip’s tail drooped. Mina’s scarf stopped swishing. Benji held his marble still.

Tully stared at the drip on his toes. He felt scared and sorry. He was shy and didn’t like shouting. A little tear rolled down his nose. “I’m mad,” he said in a tiny voice. “I feel too noisy and too close.”

Mina took a slow breath. “We hear you, Tully,” she said gently. “Tu espacio—your space—matters.”

Benji nodded. “Let’s help. What do you need?”

Tully remembered something Mama Turtle had taught him. “When I feel mad, I can stop, I can breathe, and I can use my words.” He put his hand on his shell. In… two… three… Out… two… three… He watched the dust stars float. He smelled the wood and soil. His balloon feeling got softer.

“I need quiet,” Tully said. “And I need this corner for painting. My body needs slow. Please ask before touching my things.”

Pip’s ears perked up. “I’m sorry for the splash,” he said. “Can we make a plan?”

Tully blinked. A plan sounded safe. “Yes,” he said. “We can share the shed if we make zones.”

Mina smiled and tied her bright scarf on a broom. “This scarf can mark the music zone,” she said. “Soft music only.”

Benji rolled his marble to a sunny patch. “This is the rolling zone,” he buzzed. “Roll slow.”

Pip sniffed and wagged softly. “I will dance outside the door,” he said. “Then come in with quiet paws.”

“And this corner,” Tully said, pointing to the warm striped light, “is the painting zone. Please ask before you come close.”

Everyone nodded. “We agree,” they said together.

Tully set out a little sign he painted: Paint Zone. He made another sign for Benji: Roll Zone. And one more for Mina: Music Zone. He painted them with dots and little flags from many places—red, green, gold, blue—like tiny hello hugs from around the world.

Mina tapped a gentle beat. Tap… tap… soft like raindrops. Benji rolled slow. Pip tiptoed in and curled up by the door like a fluffy rug. The shed hummed in a kind way. Tully’s shoulders dropped. He dipped his brush in blue. Swish… swish… The smell of paint mixed with the woody air. He felt calm.

“Can I see?” Pip whispered.

“Ask with words,” Mina reminded.

Pip sat and asked, “Tully, may I come to your painting zone?”

“Yes,” Tully said. “Please come slow.” Pip stepped softly. He watched with big eyes.

Benji rolled his marble to Tully. “May I add a tiny dot?” he asked.

Tully thought and touched his shell. He remembered his boundary. “You may add one dot here,” he said, pointing to a little empty spot. Benji dipped a brush and made a gold dot that sparkled like sun on water.

Mina sang a soft song she learned from her abuela. “Canta, canta,” she hummed. The notes floated like butterflies.

Tully smiled. “Your song feels like a hug,” he said.

They all smiled back.

Later, the shed door creaked again. Creak… eeeek! The day was almost done. Tully looked at his rainbow stone. It had blue, green, red, and gold dots from friends near and far. It told a story of sharing and care.

“I learned something,” Tully said. “When I feel mad, I can stop, breathe, and use my words. I can say what I need. We can make a plan together.”

Pip wagged. “I learned to ask and go slow.”

Benji buzzed. “I learned to listen and take turns.”

Mina twirled her scarf. “I learned that everyone’s space and feelings matter.”

Tully put the rainbow stone by the window. The warm light kissed it. The shed was calm and kind. Tully felt brave inside his shell and outside it too.

“Thank you,” he said. “This is a good day.”

The End.
