# Tilly Turtle Finds Her Beat

Tilly was a shy turtle with a gentle smile. She liked quiet corners and soft sounds. One day, Tilly peeked into the musical practice room. The room smelled like polished wood and lemon cleaner. Bright lights sparkled on shiny drums, jingling bells, and big round tambourines. In the corner sat a speaker that hummed like a friendly bee.

Tilly took a tiny step inside. Tap. Tap. Her shell made a soft sound against a chair. On the wall, a poster showed smiling children dancing. Tilly’s heart felt fluttery, like a small bird in her chest.

“I want to dance,” Tilly whispered, “but I feel shy.” She touched a scarf from a basket. It was smooth and swishy. She rubbed it on her cheek. It felt cool and safe.

Just then, Ms. Melody, the music teacher, walked in. She wore bright blue shoes that squeaked on the floor. “Hello, Tilly,” she said with a warm smile. “Welcome to the musical practice room.”

Tilly pulled the scarf close. “I want to dance,” she said softly, “but my tummy feels wiggly.”

“That feeling is called nervous,” said Ms. Melody. “It’s okay to feel nervous. We can use our feelings to make a plan.”

Tilly blinked. “A plan?”

“Yes,” said Ms. Melody. “Being resourceful means using what we have to solve a problem. Let’s look around.”

Tilly looked around the room. She saw drums, bells, scarves, tape, a soft rug, and a basket of beanbags. She took a deep breath and listened. The room was quiet except for the gentle hum of the speaker.

“What do you need to feel brave?” asked Ms. Melody.

Tilly thought. “I need a quiet beat. I need a cozy space. And I need a way to practice without everyone watching.”

“Great ideas,” said Ms. Melody. “Let’s be resourceful.”

Tilly felt a tiny spark of hope. She walked to the beanbag basket. “These are soft,” she said, pressing one to her cheek. “They make me feel calm.”

She put four beanbags in a square on the rug. “This is my dance spot,” Tilly said. She placed the scarf inside the square. “This is my safe scarf.”

Ms. Melody clapped softly. “Smart thinking.”

Tilly looked at the drums. The big drum was too loud. The small hand drum sounded softer. She tapped it with one finger. Dum… dum. “This is a gentle beat,” she said. She put the drum next to her square.

She looked at the speaker and pressed a button. The music started, but it was too fast. “Oh!” Tilly’s heart fluttered again. She pressed another button to make the music slow. The beat became slow and steady, like the tick of a sleepy clock.

Tick… tock… tick… tock.

Tilly smiled. “This is my speed.”

She still felt shy, so she looked around again. On a shelf, she found a little mirror with a crack in the corner. She set the mirror near the rug, tilted away from the door. “I can watch myself,” she said, “but no one can see me.”

Ms. Melody nodded. “You used the room to help your feelings. That is resourceful.”

Tilly stood in her beanbag square, scarf in hand, soft drum at her side. She put one foot out. Slide. She put the other foot out. Tap. She swung the scarf in a slow rainbow arc. Swish… swish. She tapped the drum. Dum… dum. Tick… tock.

“I’m dancing!” Tilly giggled. Her shell felt warm now, not heavy.

The door creaked. Two friends peeked in—Pip the rabbit and Sunny the squirrel. “Hi, Tilly,” said Pip. “Can we watch?”

Tilly’s tummy wiggled. She looked at her scarf. She looked at her square. She took a breath in… and out. “I feel shy,” she said, “but I have a plan. You can sit on the soft rug and use your quiet paws.”

Pip and Sunny nodded and sat. They held their paws still and smiled.

Tilly started again. Slide. Tap. Swish. Dum… dum. The scarf whispered in the air. The beanbags felt safe like tiny fences. The slow music hugged her steps.

Sunny whispered, “I like your beat.”

Pip said, “Your dance is gentle and brave.”

Tilly felt proud. Her cheeks felt warm, and her heart felt steady. She took one more look at the mirror and saw a small, strong turtle.

When the song ended, Ms. Melody clapped. Pip clapped. Sunny clapped. Clap, clap, clap!

Tilly bowed. “Thank you,” she said. “I used my feelings to make a plan. I used the things in the room. I made a cozy place. I picked a gentle drum. I slowed the music. I practiced with a scarf. That helped me dance.”

Ms. Melody smiled. “That is resourcefulness. You solved a problem with what you had.”

Pip hopped. “Can you teach us the beanbag square?”

“Sure,” said Tilly. She showed them how to make a square, how to slow the music, and how to tap a gentle beat. They all danced together. Slide. Tap. Swish. Dum… dum.

Tilly still felt shy sometimes, but now she knew what to do. She knew she could listen to her feelings, make a plan, and use what was around her.

That day, the musical practice room smelled like lemon, sounded like soft drums, and felt like brave, happy feet.

The End.
