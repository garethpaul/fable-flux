# Leo Learns to Take Turns

In a sunny backyard, a brave little lion cub named Leo wagged his tufted tail. The grass felt cool under his paws. The air smelled like sweet flowers and fresh soil. Birds sang, “Chirp-chirp!” and the sun warmed Leo’s golden fur.

“Let’s play!” Leo roared, but it was a small, happy roar. His friends gathered at the sandbox: Mia the monkey, Ben the bunny, and Tori the turtle. A shiny red bucket and a yellow shovel sat in the sand, waiting.

“I want the shovel!” Leo said, reaching with a paw.

“I was about to use it,” Mia said softly, her tail curling.

Leo grabbed it fast. “I’m brave and fast! I’ll build the biggest sand castle!” he cheered.

Ben’s long ears drooped. “Can we take turns?” he asked.

Leo didn’t hear. Scrape, scrape, scrape went the shovel. Sand swished and sprinkled. The castle grew tall, with turrets and tiny windows. Leo smiled big. He felt proud.

Then Tori pointed with a careful claw. “Look. A little ant trail. We should be safe and gentle.”

Leo leaned closer and saw the tiny ants marching. “Oh! Hi, ants.” He moved the bucket away from them. “Safety first,” Tori said. Leo nodded. He wanted to keep everyone safe.

Mia tapped Leo’s shoulder. “My turn with the shovel now?”

“Just one more scoop,” Leo said. Scoop, scoop, scoop. The shovel flashed in the sun. But as Leo scooped, the castle wall wobbled—wibble, wobble—then splat! It fell.

Ben hopped back. “Whoa!”

Leo’s heart thumped. “Oh no.” He looked at his friends’ faces. Mia looked sad. Ben looked worried. Tori blinked slowly.

“I think we need a plan,” Tori said. “And turns.”

Leo’s ears lowered. He took a deep breath and listened to the birds for a calm moment. Chirp-chirp. The wind was soft. He remembered something Mama Lion always said: “Brave means kind. Brave means fair.”

Leo set the shovel down. “I’m sorry,” he said, his voice gentle. “I did not take turns. That was not fair. I will make it right.”

Mia smiled a little. “Thank you for the apology.”

Ben nodded. “We can try a turn chart!”

“What is a turn chart?” Leo asked.

“It means we take turns,” Ben said. “We each get the shovel for a little time.”

Tori added, “And we keep the ants safe. And we clean the yard after. That is community service. We help our backyard home.”

Leo’s eyes shone. “Yes! Let’s do it.” He felt a warm, brave feeling in his chest. It felt like sunshine inside.

They picked a smooth stone to be the “turn rock.” Whoever held the turn rock got the shovel. Leo said, “Mia goes first. Then Ben. Then me. Then Tori.”

Mia grinned. “Thank you, Leo.” She held the turn rock and the shovel. Scrape, pat, pat. She made a round tower. “All done,” she said, passing the rock to Ben.

Ben dug a moat. Swish, swish. Water from his little cup trickled around the castle. “All done,” he said, and he passed the rock to Leo.

Leo took a breath. “My turn!” He pressed soft paw-prints along the path. Pad, pad, pad. He smiled and passed the rock to Tori.

Tori, slow and steady, placed tiny leaves like flags. “All done,” she said. The castle was beautiful. It gleamed in the sunlight, with leaf flags fluttering and a shiny moat.

The friends cheered. “We did it—together!”

Then Mia pointed to the fence. “Look at the fallen leaves. They cover the path. Someone might slip.”

“That is not safe,” Tori said.

“Let’s help,” Leo said. “We can do community service and clean the backyard.”

They found little rakes and a small dustpan. “We take turns with tools,” Leo added. He felt strong and honest. That is integrity—doing what is right.

Mia took the rake. Swish, swish. “All done,” she said, and passed it to Ben.

Ben raked. Swish, swish. “All done,” he said, and passed it to Leo.

Leo raked with careful strokes, keeping away from the ant trail. “Safety first,” he said. “All done.” He passed it to Tori, who swept the last leaves into the bin. Rustle, rustle. The path was clear.

When they finished, everyone washed paws at the outdoor sink. The cool water splashed and felt tickly. “Clean and safe,” Ben said.

Leo looked at his friends. “I am glad we took turns,” he said. “I am sorry I grabbed before. Next time, I will ask and wait.”

Mia hugged him. “You learned and you cared.”

Tori nodded. “That is brave.”

Ben’s ears perked up. “Let’s have a snack. We can take turns choosing.”

They sat in the shade. The grass smelled fresh. The sky looked bright and blue. A soft breeze brushed their cheeks. One by one, they took turns choosing apple slices, carrot sticks, and little cups of water.

Leo licked his whiskers. “Taking turns feels good,” he said. “It keeps us safe, it helps us help others, and it makes playtime fair.”

Everyone smiled. The castle stood strong. The yard was clean. The ants marched safely on their tiny trail.

Leo’s tail swished with happy pride. “Brave means kind. Brave means fair,” he said softly.

And all his friends agreed.

The End.
