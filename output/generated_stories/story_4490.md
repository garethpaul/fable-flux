# Leo Plans His Art Day

In a comfortable nursery with sunny windows and soft rugs, a brave little lion cub named Leo flicked his tufted tail. The air smelled like crayons and warm toast. Gentle music hummed. Colorful paper sat in tidy stacks, and little jars of paint sparkled like tiny rainbows.

“I want to make a big art poster today,” Leo said, his whiskers twitching with excitement. “It will be brave and bright!”

Miss Nia, the kind teacher, smiled. “That sounds wonderful, Leo. What do brave artists do first?”

Leo paused. His paws tingled. “They… roar?” he guessed.

Miss Nia chuckled. “They prepare. They plan what they need, and they set things out. Preparation helps ideas come to life.”

Leo nodded. “Prepare. Yes!”

He looked at the art shelf. There were brushes, crayons, glue sticks, and scissors with round tips. He could also smell the fruity markers—cherry, grape, and lemon.

Leo took a deep breath. “I will make a sun, a big tree, and a smiling moon,” he said. He picked paper: yellow for the sun, green for the tree, silver for the moon. He put them in a neat pile. He counted softly, “One, two, three brushes. One cup of water. One smock.” He slipped on a tiny blue smock that felt cool and smooth. He even tied the waist ribbon in a bow.

“Good preparation,” Miss Nia said. “What else do artists do?”

Leo tapped his chin. “They protect the table!” He laid out a crunchy paper mat that crackled like leaves. He set a napkin to wipe drips. He filled his water cup halfway, not too full.

His friend Maya, a small panda with bright eyes, waddled over. “Can I paint too?” she asked.

“Yes,” Leo said, puffing with pride. “I am preparing. We can share the table.”

Maya smiled. “I want to paint a big blue ocean!”

Leo blinked. “But I want to use the big brush for my huge sun.”

Maya held the big brush gently. “I want the big brush for waves.”

Leo’s ears twitched. He felt a warm worry in his belly. He looked at his plan. He had chosen three brushes, but only one was big and soft.

Miss Nia came closer. “What can brave artists do when they both want the same tool?”

Leo remembered another word. “Compromise,” he said slowly. “We can take turns.”

Maya nodded. “Yes! You can use it first for the sun. Then I can use it for waves.”

Leo’s tail swished happily. “Deal.” They bumped paws. Integrity mattered to Leo. He wanted to be fair and keep his word.

He set a timer shaped like a little bee. It buzzed softly. “When it goes beep, my turn is done,” Leo said. “I promise.”

Leo dipped the big brush into warm yellow paint. Swish—swirl—spread! The sun bloomed on the paper like a bright pancake. He wiped the brush carefully on the napkin. The timer went beep-beep. Leo placed the brush on the table. “Your turn, Maya,” he said.

Maya’s eyes sparkled. “Thank you!” She painted the ocean in flowing lines—shhhh, shhhh—like gentle waves. “It looks so blue,” she giggled, sniffing the grape marker and then giggling again. “Oops! That’s grape, not ocean!”

Leo laughed. “Grape ocean!” He picked up a smaller brush for his tree. Tap-tap dots for leaves. A strong brown trunk. He glued tiny scraps for apples. They looked like little buttons.

Suddenly, a drip of blue water slipped from Maya’s brush. Plop! It landed near Leo’s tree. His heart thumped. “Oh no! My tree!”

Maya’s eyes widened. “I’m sorry, Leo.” Her ears drooped. “I didn’t mean to drip.”

Leo took a calm breath. He had prepared a napkin. He dabbed the drip gently. Dab, dab. The blue smear turned into a little pond by his tree. He grinned. “It’s okay. Look! Now the tree has a pond.”

Maya smiled with relief. “Your plan saved the day.”

Leo nodded. “Preparation helped me fix it.” He added a tiny duck with a yellow crayon. Quack!

They kept painting and talking. The room felt cozy and busy. Brushes whispered. Paper rustled. Crayons made warm, waxy lines. Other friends peeked and said, “Oooo!” and “Ahhh!”

When their pictures dried, Leo set them side by side. A bright sun near a strong tree with a pond. Next to it, a deep blue ocean with a happy moon in the sky. Miss Nia clapped softly. “Beautiful art, both of you. I saw good preparation, kind compromise, and honest words.”

Leo stood tall. “I planned my tools. I shared the big brush. I kept my promise.”

Maya nodded. “And I said sorry for the drip. We turned it into art.”

Miss Nia placed a little gold star sticker on their smocks. “That’s integrity,” she said. “Doing the right thing, even when it’s hard. And planning ahead so you can do your best.”

Leo felt warm and light, like sunshine in his chest. He looked at the table: brushes cleaned, lids closed, paper scraps in the recycle bin. He and Maya had prepared for cleanup, too.

“Tomorrow,” Leo said, “let’s prepare a color chart first. Then we can choose colors faster.”

Maya giggled. “Good plan!”

Leo smiled. “Preparation helps brave artists make brave art.”

They waved at their pictures, now hanging by the window where the light made them glow. The nursery smelled sweet and safe. Leo’s tail curled with joy.

The lesson was clear in his mind: Plan first, share kindly, and tell the truth. With preparation, compromise, and art, little problems can become something beautiful.

The End.
