# Miko’s Gentle Fix-It Day

In a cozy garage workshop, a courageous monkey named Miko woke up from his little hammock. The garage smelled like pine wood and orange oil. Sunlight made golden squares on the floor. Tools hung neatly on the wall: wrenches, screwdrivers, and a shiny blue hammer. A small fan hummed a soft, steady sound.

“Good morning, tools,” Miko said. He smiled and patted his chest. “Today I will be calm and brave.”

Every morning, Miko had a routine. First, he stretched his arms wide. He took a slow breath in. He let a slow breath out. He tapped a tiny bell that went ting! Then he checked his list on a chalkboard.

“Sweep the floor. Sort the screws. Fix the squeaky tricycle,” he read. “I can do this, one step at a time.”

Miko held a little broom. Swish, swish. Dust danced in the sunbeams like glitter. “I like this sound,” Miko said softly. “Swish, swish.” He felt calm.

Next, he sorted screws into jars. Big screws. Little screws. Silver ones. Gold ones. The glass jars clinked, clinked. Miko hummed. His tail curled happily.

Then, a door creaked. “Eee-eee?” Miko turned. It was Dottie, a mouse friend with big round ears. She pushed a red tricycle with a wobbly wheel. The tricycle squeaked eeeeee with every push.

“Hi, Miko,” Dottie said. “My trike is squeaky and shaky. Can you help?”

Miko stood tall. “Yes,” he said. “I will help.” He put his hand on his chest. “I will stay calm.”

He set the tricycle on the work table. He listened. Squeak, squeak. Click, click. He touched the wheel. It wiggled. “I think this nut is loose,” Miko said. “I can fix it.”

Miko picked up a shiny wrench. He turned it left. He turned it right. The wrench made a soft metal song: tnk, tnk. Miko smiled. “I like that sound,” he said. “It helps me be calm.”

Suddenly, the small fan got louder. Whirr-whirr-WHIRR! A gust of wind knocked a box of washers off the shelf. Clatter! The washers rolled like little moons across the floor.

Miko jumped. “Oh!” His hand slipped. The wrench bumped the bell. TING! The sound was sharp and bright. Miko’s heart beat fast. His tail puffed. He felt hot in his cheeks.

Dottie’s eyes grew wide. “Are you okay?”

Miko held still. He remembered his routine. “When I feel upset, I stop. I breathe,” he whispered.

He closed his eyes. He took a slow breath in. He let a slow breath out. One… two… three. He relaxed his shoulders. He listened to the soft whirr of the fan and the tiny tings of washers settling.

“I am okay,” said Miko. “I will choose calm.” He smiled a little smile.

Then he looked at the floor. The washers were everywhere. Some had rolled under the toolbox. Some had hidden by the door. “I made a mess,” he said. He looked at Dottie. “I’m sorry I got startled and dropped the box.”

Dottie nodded. “It’s okay, Miko. Accidents happen.”

“Thank you for accepting my apology,” said Miko. “Let’s clean up together.” He took initiative. He grabbed a magnet-on-a-stick. Click-click! Washers hopped onto the magnet. Dottie scooped them into a jar. Plink, plink, plink. “This is almost like a game,” Dottie giggled.

Soon the floor was tidy again. Miko wiped his hands. “Thank you for helping,” he said.

“You’re welcome,” said Dottie. “You were very calm.”

Miko’s cheeks cooled. He felt proud. “Calm helps me think,” he said. He tapped the bell gently. Ting. A soft, friendly sound.

He turned back to the tricycle. He checked the nut. He added a tiny drop of oil. Drip. He turned the wrench: tnk, tnk, tnk. The wheel spun. Whirr. No squeak!

“Try it,” said Miko.

Dottie pushed the trike across the smooth floor. The wheel was steady. No squeak. “It works!” she squealed. “Thank you!”

Miko clapped softly. “We did it. Calm, apology, and teamwork.”

Dottie smiled. “I accept your apology. And I like your calm.”

Miko nodded. “I have a routine to help me: stop, breathe, listen, and then fix. Even when things are noisy, I can choose calm.”

Dottie put on her helmet. “You’re a courageous monkey, Miko.”

Miko grinned. “Courage can be quiet,” he said. “Courage can be calm.”

The fan hummed. The sun felt warm on the workbench. The garage smelled like orange oil again. Miko checked his chalkboard and drew a little star next to “Fix the squeaky tricycle.”

“Next, I will water the plant,” he said. He poured a small cup. Glug, glug. The plant’s leaves looked happy. Miko took one more slow breath. In. Out.

Dottie rang the trike bell. Ding-ding! “Bye, Miko!”

“Bye, Dottie!” Miko waved. His tail swished in a gentle S. He felt peaceful and strong.

That day, Miko learned that calm breath and kind words can fix big feelings and small squeaks. Saying “I’m sorry,” accepting help, and following a simple routine made the day bright.

The End.
