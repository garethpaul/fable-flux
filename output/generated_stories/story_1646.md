# Miko’s Closet Concert

Miko was a small, courageous monkey with bright eyes and careful hands. He loved to swing, to clap, and to hum. His favorite place was the closet hideaway. It was a cozy nook behind soft coats and long scarves. It smelled like clean cotton and a little bit like bananas, because Miko always carried a snack.

One rainy afternoon, the wind tapped the window, tap-tap-tap. The raindrops whispered, shhhhhh. Miko wiggled into the closet hideaway with his treasure basket. Inside the basket were safe, quiet things: wooden spoons, a ribbon, a paper fan, and a tiny drum with a soft top.

“I want to make a show,” Miko said. His tail curled with excitement. “I want to sing and play!”

He took a deep breath. “Laaa—” But the sound squeaked out. He frowned. His tummy felt twisty.

“What if I mess up?” he whispered to the coats. “What if I can’t keep the beat?”

He tapped the drum with one finger. Thump. Thump. Thump-thump. The beats wobbled and bumped into each other. Miko’s shoulders drooped. “It’s not right,” he said. “Maybe I should stop.”

A scarf slid down and tickled his ear. Miko giggled in spite of himself. The tickle felt silly. The closet was warm and safe. He could smell the cotton and feel the soft carpet under his toes. He took another breath. In. Out. Slow and calm.

“I can try again,” Miko told himself. “I am brave. I can practice.”

He set a plan to solve his problem:
- First, be calm.
- Next, move slowly and steady.
- Then, use my own ideas.

“Calm,” he whispered. He closed his eyes and put his hand on his chest. In… and out… like the quiet rain. His tummy felt softer. His heart slowed down.

“Steady,” he said, opening his eyes. He held the ribbon with both hands. He stretched it wide like a rainbow. He counted, “One… two… three… four,” swinging the ribbon side to side. Swish. Swish. Swish. Swish. His hands took turns. Left. Right. Left. Right. The ribbon made soft shapes in the air—loops and waves. Miko smiled. “That feels good.”

“Now my own ideas,” he said. He picked up the wooden spoons and tapped them together. Click. Click. Click-click. He matched the clicks with the swish of the ribbon. Click… swish… click… swish. His feet joined in, heel-toe, heel-toe, on the soft carpet. Pat. Pat. Pat. Pat. Miko felt steady, like a little train.

“Hello, coats! Hello, scarves!” he announced. “Welcome to Miko’s Closet Concert!”

He peeked out of the closet and waved to his friend, a plush giraffe named Dot, sitting on the bed. “Dot, you can watch.” Dot leaned against a pillow and listened.

Miko began. He tapped the drum softly with one finger. Thump… thump… thump… thump. He counted in a gentle voice, “One, two, three, four.” Then he sang a tiny song:

“I can try, I can play,
Slow and steady, I find my way.
Click and swish, clap and hum,
I feel brave—here I come!”

He clapped his hands—clap, clap—and clicked the spoons—click, click. He swished the ribbon—whoosh! The paper fan made a breeze on his face—fuff, fuff. The cool air felt nice. The beat stayed steady. His body felt strong and smooth. His tail swayed with the rhythm.

But then a bump! The drum rolled against his foot. “Oh!” Miko wobbled. The rhythm fell apart. Click—swish—clunk. He stopped. His cheeks got hot. He felt the old twist in his tummy.

Miko remembered his plan. He put the spoons down. He closed his eyes. In… and out… like the rain. Calm. He set the drum in place with both hands. Steady. He smiled and tried again, using his own idea: “I will say the beat as I play.”

“Tap, tap, tap, tap,” he said softly, tapping the drum. His words helped his hands. “Swish, swish,” he whispered, moving the ribbon on two and four. “Click, click,” he said, touching the spoons together on one and three. His feet went heel-toe on every beat. Pat, pat, pat, pat. The closet felt like a tiny stage. The coats were the curtains. The scarves were the lights.

Miko sang again, louder this time:

“I make music my own way,
I feel calm, and that’s okay.
If I wobble, I won’t stop,
Breathe and try till I reach the top!”

He finished with a long hum, “Mmmmmm,” like a warm cup of cocoa. He bowed to the coats and to Dot. The closet seemed to clap back with soft rustles.

Dot tipped over, as if bowing too. Miko giggled and clapped for himself. Clap-clap-clap!

Just then, the rain outside slowed to little plips and plops. The world felt peaceful. Miko’s body felt relaxed and proud.

He peeked out again and saw Mom smiling from the doorway. “I heard your concert,” she said softly. “It was beautiful.”

Miko grinned. “I had a problem,” he said. “My beat got bumpy. But I was calm. I moved steady. I used my own ideas. I made my own music!”

Mom nodded. “That’s creative expression,” she said. “You listened to your heart and your hands. You solved your problem with calm and coordination.”

Miko hugged his tiny drum. It felt smooth and friendly. “Tomorrow,” he said, “I will make a scarf dance and a drum song for Dot.”

Mom winked. “I can’t wait.”

Miko curled back into the closet hideaway. He whispered to the coats, “Thank you for watching.” He whispered to the rain, “Thank you for the beat.” He whispered to himself, “I am brave. I can try. I can make my own art.”

The End.
