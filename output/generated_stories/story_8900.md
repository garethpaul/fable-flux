# Fawn's Feelings at the Dinner Table

In the bright dining room, morning sun made golden squares on the floor. A gentle deer fawn named Fawn stood on soft hooves beside a wooden chair. The table smelled like warm oats and sweet apples. A tiny fan hummed. The room felt cozy and safe.

“Breakfast time,” called Mama Deer, carrying a bowl. The oats were creamy and dotted with red apple hearts. Papa Deer brought a pitcher of cool water that gurgled softly.

Fawn’s tummy rumbled, but his chest felt tight. His little tail twitched. He wanted to run and stretch his legs. Outside, the trees called, “Whoosh, whoosh.” He could almost feel the wind on his fur.

“Please sit, sweet Fawn,” said Mama Deer gently.

Fawn climbed onto his chair. He watched the spoon. He watched the steam. His hooves wiggled and tapped, tap, tap.

Papa Deer smiled. “What do you hear?” he asked.

Fawn listened. “I hear the fan,” he said. “I hear the water. I hear my hooves being tap-tappy.” He giggled, but then his face crumpled. “I want to run,” he blurted. “My legs feel jumpy.”

Mama Deer nodded. “Thank you for telling us your feeling,” she said softly. “That feeling has a name. It is called restless.”

Fawn whispered, “Restless.” The word felt new and smooth in his mouth.

He picked up his spoon, but a tiny drip fell on the table. Fawn’s eyes went wide. “Oh no!” he cried. His cheeks grew hot. His ears drooped. “I made a mess.”

Papa Deer patted Fawn’s shoulder. “It’s okay. We can fix small messes.”

Fawn sniffed. “I feel… I feel tight and wiggly,” he said, pressing his hoof to his chest. “And I feel sad about the drip.”

Mama Deer took a slow breath. “Let’s do a little exercise to help your body and feelings,” she said. “Would you like to try?”

Fawn nodded. “Yes, please.”

Mama Deer stood and showed Fawn. “We can do three gentle chair-stretches. It’s calm-moving for the dining room.”

“Calm-moving?” Fawn asked.

“Calm-moving means moving in a quiet, careful way,” Papa Deer explained. “It helps our bodies settle.”

Fawn stood beside his chair. The wood felt smooth under his hoof. He could smell cinnamon on the oats. He tried to listen to his breath.

“First,” Mama said, “reach your front legs up like tree branches.” Fawn reached up, up. His back felt long. He breathed in. The air tasted cool.

“Second,” said Papa, “give your shoulders a tiny roll, slow like a turtle.” Fawn rolled one, two, three. His tight feeling got smaller.

“Third,” Mama whispered, “take a belly breath. Hand to belly. In and out.” Fawn’s belly moved his hoof up and down like a little boat. Whoosh. Ahh.

Fawn smiled. “My legs feel softer now,” he said. “I feel calm.”

He sat again. Mama Deer gave him a small cloth. “Would you like to help with the drip?” she asked.

“Yes,” said Fawn. He dabbed the drip. “I am sorry for the mess,” he said, looking at Mama and Papa.

“Thank you for your apology,” Papa said kindly. “That is a strong, kind choice.”

Fawn tasted a spoon of oats. Warm. Soft. Sweet apple. A hint of cinnamon tickled his tongue. “Mmm,” he hummed. His ears perked up.

Just then, Cousin Squirrel scampered in. “Is there jumping time?” she chattered. “I feel bouncy!”

Fawn looked at the table. He looked at Cousin Squirrel. He remembered the calm-moving. “In the dining room, we use quiet bodies,” he said gently. “We can jump outside later.”

Cousin Squirrel blinked. “I feel disappointed,” she whispered, tail drooping.

Fawn felt that word in his belly. “I know disappointed,” he said. “I felt restless a minute ago.” He pointed to his chest. “When I feel big feelings, I use calm-moving. Want to try?”

Cousin Squirrel nodded. “Okay.”

Together they did tree-branch reaches, slow turtle shoulder rolls, and belly breaths. Cousin Squirrel’s tail made a soft swish-swish. “I feel better,” she said. “Thank you.”

Fawn grinned. “We can run after breakfast. Maybe three big hops in the yard!”

Mama Deer smiled. “That sounds like a good plan. First we eat. Then we hop.”

Fawn took another bite. He listened to his belly boat breath. He felt proud. He had used words for his feelings. He had helped a friend. He had used exercise in a calm way. The dining room felt peaceful.

When breakfast was done, Fawn carried his bowl to the sink. The water splashed with a happy plip-plop. He turned to Cousin Squirrel. “Ready to hop outside?” he asked.

“Ready!” she chirped.

At the door, Fawn paused and looked back at Mama and Papa. “Thank you for helping me not be restless,” he said. “I learned that I can say my feeling, move in a calm way, and make a plan.”

Papa Deer nodded. “That is smart, logical thinking,” he said.

Mama Deer added, “And you showed tolerance and kindness to Cousin Squirrel. You waited, and you helped.”

Fawn’s heart felt warm. He took a deep breath. “I can do it again next time,” he said.

Then Fawn and Cousin Squirrel ran into the sunny day, ready for three big, happy hops.

The End.
