# Buttons, Baskets, and Bunny Hops

Benny was a small, determined rabbit with soft gray fur and big, listening ears. He loved to help. He loved to try. He loved to say, “I can do it!”

One rainy afternoon, Benny hopped into the laundry room. The room smelled like warm soap and clean cotton. The dryer hummed, “hmmmmm.” The washer swished, “swish-swish.” A window tapped with raindrops, “tip-tip-tip.”

Mama Rabbit looked tired. A big basket of clothes sat on the floor. Socks peeked out like little ears. Tiny buttons clinked in a jar. A red towel lay in a lumpy heap.

“I want to help,” Benny said, standing tall on his tip-toes.

Mama smiled. “Thank you, Benny. That shows good initiative. Initiative means you see a job and start helping. Can you sort the laundry? Whites in this basket. Colors in that basket.”

“I can do it!” Benny said. His whiskers twitched with excitement.

Benny grabbed a white shirt. “White,” he said, plopping it in the white basket. He picked up a blue sock. “Color,” he said, dropping it in the color basket. He made little piles. He hummed with the machines. “Swish-swish, hmmmmm.”

He found two tiny socks, one green, one purple. “You are friends,” he said kindly. “But you go in colors.”

Then Benny saw Mama’s fluffy red scarf on top of the whites. “Uh-oh,” he said. He remembered a story Grandma told about a red sock that turned everything pink.

He tugged the scarf. It was stuck under a white towel. Benny pulled. “Uff!” The scarf slipped free. The towel fell off the basket and landed on his foot.

“Ow!” Benny’s cheeks felt hot. “This towel is mean!” He stomped his foot and made a little growly sound. His tail puffed.

Mama looked over. “Are you okay, Benny?”

“I’m mad!” Benny said, folding his arms. “The towel bonked me!”

Mama nodded. “I see you’re angry. That’s a big feeling. Let’s use our calm tools.”

Benny wiggled his nose. He remembered. “Smell the flower. Blow the candle.” He closed his eyes. He pretended to smell a sweet flower. Then he blew out a pretend candle. In… and out. In… and out.

His cheeks cooled. His tail un-puffed. “I feel better,” he whispered.

“That’s anger-management,” Mama said gently. “You respected your feelings. You calmed your body.”

Benny looked at the towel. “I’m sorry, towel. You are not mean. You just fell.” He picked it up carefully. “I will be gentle. I will respect the things we share.”

Benny hopped back to sorting. He found Papa’s shirt with shiny buttons. “Clink, clink,” the buttons sang. He put it in the white basket. He found his own carrot pajamas. “Hee hee,” Benny giggled, rubbing the soft cloth on his cheek. It felt cozy like a hug.

Then Benny had an idea. He wanted to do more. He wanted to show initiative again. He looked at the empty dryer. He looked at the clean clothes in the washer. He looked at Mama, who was scrubbing a muddy boot.

“I can move the clothes!” Benny said. “I can do it all by myself.”

Mama opened her mouth. “Benny—”

But Benny was already pulling the washer door. He reached in with both paws. The clothes were heavy. “Oof!” He tried to drag them into the dryer. A sleeve flopped on his face. A sock landed on his ear. He giggled and kept going.

Then he saw the jar of buttons on the shelf. “Buttons go with shirts,” Benny thought. “I’ll help more.” He lifted the jar with both paws. His paws were damp. The jar slipped. “Clink—”

Crash!

Buttons danced across the floor. “Clink-clink! Tink-tink!” They rolled under the dryer like falling rain.

Benny’s tummy felt twisty. His ears drooped. “Oh no,” he whispered. “I made a mess.”

Mama set down the boot and hurried over. She didn’t shout. She crouched low to look at Benny’s eyes. “Are you safe?” she asked softly.

Benny nodded. “I am safe. But I spilled. I wanted to help. I did too much.”

Mama took a deep breath. “Thank you for wanting to help. That is wonderful initiative. Next time, ask before lifting glass jars. That shows respect for the things we use and the rules we follow.”

Benny looked at the shiny buttons everywhere. He felt a little mad at himself. His chest felt tight. He put his paws on his belly. “Smell the flower. Blow the candle.” In… and out. In… and out. His shoulders relaxed.

“I’m ready to fix it,” he said. “How can I help?”

“That’s the spirit,” Mama said. “We can solve this together. You hold the dustpan. I will sweep gently. We will keep our paws safe.”

They worked as a team. Swish-swish went the little broom. Tap-tap went the dustpan. Benny listened. He watched. He held steady. He felt proud.

When they finished, Mama showed Benny a small, safe tin. “Let’s use this for buttons now,” she said. “Would you like to label it?”

Benny’s eyes lit up. He took a sticky note and a crayon. He drew a big circle and inside he wrote, with Mama’s help, “Buttons.”

Benny looked at the washer and dryer. “May I move the clean clothes now?” he asked.

“Yes,” Mama said. “Thank you for asking. I will stand with you.”

Together they moved the clothes. Plop, plop, plop. Benny checked the pockets. He found a leaf. He found a paper clip. He put them on the shelf. He closed the dryer gently. He waited for Mama. “Can I press the button?” he asked.

“Go ahead,” Mama smiled.

Benny pressed. Beep! The dryer started. “Hmmmmm,” it sang again.

Benny stood tall. “I helped,” he said. “I started. I asked. I fixed my mistake. I calmed my mad.”

Mama hugged him. Benny smelled her warm fur and the soapy air. “You showed initiative, anger-management, and respect,” she said. “That makes our home happy.”

Benny’s ears perked up. He felt light and bouncy. He hummed with the machines. “Swish-swish, hmmmmm.”

He hopped to the basket and folded his carrot pajamas, small and neat. “I can do it,” he whispered, “and I can do it the safe, kind way.”

The End.
