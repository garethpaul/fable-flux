# Bubbles, Brave Bear!

Baby Bramble was a friendly bear cub with soft, fuzzy ears and a shiny black nose. One sunny morning, Bramble padded into the bathroom. The room smelled like fresh soap and warm towels. Sunlight made tiny sparkles on the mirror. Drip, drip, went the faucet. Plink, plink, sang the water into the tub.

Today was the day of the Big Cub Race at the forest field. Bramble loved to run and jump, but he felt a flutter in his tummy. He whispered, “Can I do it?” His whiskers tickled with worry.

Mama Bear popped her head in. “Good morning, Bramble! Time to get ready.”

Bramble looked at the tub. He saw his running shorts folded on the stool and his little sneakers lined up like two neat paws. He felt nervous. “Mama,” he said softly, “what if I am not fast? What if I trip?”

Mama Bear knelt down and hugged him. Her fur was warm and cozy. “Every race starts with getting ready,” she said. “We can use the bathroom like our practice place. We will wash, think, and plan. We will use our bear brains.”

“My bear brain?” Bramble asked, curious.

“Yes,” said Mama. “Logical-thinking means making a simple plan. Creative-expression means using your imagination. And self-confidence is the brave voice inside you that says, ‘I can try.’”

Bramble’s ears perked up. “I can try,” he repeated.

“First,” said Mama, turning the water on, “we wash to wake up our bodies.” Swish, swish, the warm water ran. Bubbles bloomed like tiny white clouds. They smelled like sweet pine. Bramble giggled when a bubble kissed his nose. “It tickles!”

“Let’s make a plan,” Mama said. “Three steps. One: warm up. Two: focus. Three: have fun. Can you say it?”

Bramble held up his little claws. “One: warm up. Two: focus. Three: have fun!”

“Good,” said Mama. “Now make a sports warm-up in the tub. Can you step high like a marching bear?”

Bramble stood carefully on the bath mat. “March, march,” he said, lifting his knees. The mat felt soft under his paws.

“Can you jump like a frog?” Mama asked.

“Boing!” Bramble bent and bounced. His belly wobbled. He laughed. “This is fun!”

“Now stretch,” Mama said. “Reach for the soap-star!”

Bramble stretched his arms to the bar of soap. “I can reach!” He felt tall. His chest puffed with pride. “I am strong.”

Mama clapped. “You are.”

Bramble looked in the mirror. He saw a small bear with bright eyes. “Hello, Bramble,” he told his reflection. “You can try.”

“Time for focus,” Mama said softly. “Let’s use logical-thinking. How do we run a race?”

Bramble thought. He tapped his chin. “Start feet ready. Arms go back and forth. Look ahead. Breathe in and out.”

Mama smiled. “Great plan. Can you show me?”

Bramble stood by the bath rug. He put one paw forward. “Ready,” he whispered. He swung his little arms. “Steady.” He took a big breath. “Go!” He did tiny running steps in place. Pat-pat-pat went his paws.

“Very good,” said Mama. “Now, creative-expression time. Let’s make a song to help you remember.”

Bramble loved songs. He hummed, then sang, “Warm up, focus, have some fun. I can try, and then I run!” His voice bounced around the bathroom tiles like happy birds.

“I like it!” Mama sang along. “Warm up, focus, have some fun…”

Bramble giggled. He made a dance with his paws. Swish went the towel cape he tied around his neck. He imagined he was Brave Bear, the Forest Runner. The bathroom became a tiny stadium in his mind. The toothbrushes were cheer sticks. The shower curtain was a waving flag. “Go, Bramble, go!” he cheered to himself.

Then Bramble looked at his sneakers. He felt a little worry again. “What if I come in last?” he asked, voice small.

Mama sat beside him on the rug. “Coming in last is still finishing,” she said. “Finishing is brave. Self-confidence means you believe you can try again and again.”

Bramble nodded. He felt the brave voice inside him grow louder. “I can try. I can finish.”

“Let’s practice one more time,” Mama said. “Ready, steady, go!”

Bramble ran in place again. Pat-pat-pat! He counted, “One, two, three, four!” He breathed in and out. He looked ahead at the doorknob like it was the finish line. He smiled big. He felt warm and bright, like the sunlight on the mirror.

“All set,” Mama said. She helped Bramble into his shorts and tied his sneakers. The laces made neat bunny ears. “How do you feel?”

Bramble stood tall. “I feel brave. I feel ready. I can try.”

At the forest field, the other cubs lined up. Bramble remembered the bathroom plan. He wiggled his paws to warm up. He sang softly, “Warm up, focus, have some fun…” He looked ahead. He breathed in. He breathed out.

“Go!” called the squirrel coach.

Bramble ran. The ground felt bouncy. His arms moved back and forth. He looked ahead. He smiled. He listened to his brave voice. “I can try,” it said. “I can finish.”

He did finish! He was not first. He was not last. He was Bramble, who tried and who smiled at the end.

Back home, Bramble washed his paws in the bathroom. The soap smelled sweet. The mirror shone. He looked at his reflection and said, “Hello, Bramble. You did it. You tried. You finished.”

Mama hugged him. “That is self-confidence,” she said. “You used your bear brain, your plan, and your song. You were brave.”

Bramble’s heart felt big and warm. “I can try again tomorrow,” he said.

“Yes,” Mama smiled. “And you can have fun, too.”

The End.
