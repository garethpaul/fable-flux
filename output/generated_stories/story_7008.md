# Tilly Turtle on the Rooftop

Tilly was a shy turtle with a smooth, green shell. One sunny morning, she climbed a little ramp and found herself on the rooftop garden. The air felt warm on her face. Soft wind tickled her cheeks. She could smell fresh mint and sweet strawberries growing in pots. Birds chirped, “Cheep-cheep!” somewhere above.

Tilly looked around. The rooftop was bright and big. There were planters, a tiny blue watering can, and a comfy bench. But when she turned to go back down, the ramp had slipped to the side. It was too far away.

“Oh,” whispered Tilly. “How do I get down?” Her tummy felt fluttery. She tucked her head partway into her shell.

A friendly butterfly landed on a flower. “Hello, Tilly! I’m Belle. You look worried.”

Tilly took a deep breath. She smelled the mint again. It made her brave. “Hi, Belle. The ramp moved. I don’t know how to get down.”

Belle flapped her gold wings. “I can fly. I could try to get someone.”

Tilly blinked. “Thank you. That’s kind. But… maybe I can try first.” Her voice was small, but steady.

Belle smiled. “I’ll stay with you. You can do it.”

Tilly looked around carefully. She listened. Far below, she heard a soft city hum: cars like “vroom,” and a dog bark, “woof!” Near her, a watering can sat by the strawberries. A short crate was beside the bench. The ramp was nearby, just out of reach.

Tilly said, “I can make a plan.” She wiggled her toes and felt the warm roof under her feet. “First, I will move the crate. Then, I will push the watering can. Then, I will scoot the ramp.” She nodded. “I can try. I can try by myself.”

Belle cheered, “Yes, Tilly! Try one small step.”

Tilly went to the crate. It was wooden and smelled like pine. “Oof,” she said, pushing with her strong legs. It scraped with a little “skrrt.” She took a slow breath. “In… out.” The crate moved a tiny bit. “Skrrt.” She moved it again. “Skrrt.” Soon it was closer to the ramp.

“Good job,” said Belle. “You are strong.”

Tilly’s cheeks felt warm. She smiled. “Thank you.” She looked at the little blue watering can. “Now this.” She wrapped her short arms around the handle. “It sloshes!” Water swished inside with a “shh-shh.” She giggled. “Hello, water.” She pushed the can to the ramp. “Shh-shh, shh-shh.”

Tilly looked at the ramp. It was long and flat, with tiny heels to stop it from slipping. “I need to scoot it.” She stood close, set her feet, and pushed. “Mmmph.” The ramp didn’t move. Her tummy fluttered again.

“I can’t,” she whispered, and tucked her head a little.

Belle’s voice was soft. “You did two steps by yourself. You can rest and try again. Small steps are brave.”

Tilly listened to the wind. It made a gentle “whooo.” The sun warmed her shell. She thought, I already moved the crate. I already moved the can. I can try one more push.

She peeked out and said to herself, “I can do hard things, one step at a time.” She pressed her feet, pressed her hands, and pushed. “Mmmph!” The ramp nudged. “Clunk.” It moved a tiny bit.

Tilly’s eyes grew wide. “It moved!”

Belle clapped her wings. “Yes! Again, Tilly!”

Tilly pushed again. “Mmmph, clunk.” The ramp slid closer to the crate. She wiped her brow. It felt a little damp. “Almost,” she said.

One more time. “Mmmph!” The ramp rested right against the crate and the edge of the roof. It looked steady.

Tilly smiled big. “I did it. I made a way.”

Belle hovered close. “You were kind to yourself. You kept trying.”

Tilly nodded. “I felt scared. But I tried small steps. I used what I had. I can help myself.”

She placed her front feet on the ramp. She tapped it to test. “Tonk, tonk.” It was firm. Slowly, she walked down. Her shell made a soft, happy “tok-tok” rhythm. Down she went, steady and slow, until she reached the safe balcony.

At the bottom, Tilly looked up. Belle fluttered down to her and gave a gentle wing-hug. “I knew you could.”

“Thank you for staying,” said Tilly. “Your kind words helped. I did the work myself, and that feels good.”

“How do you feel now?” asked Belle.

Tilly took a deep, minty breath. She stood a little taller. “I feel brave. I feel strong. I can make a plan. I can try again when things are hard.”

Belle smiled. “That is self-reliance.”

Tilly waved. “Next time I see a problem, I’ll look, listen, and try small steps.”

That evening, the sky turned pink and gold. The rooftop garden rustled softly. Tilly set the ramp in a better place and put a small stone to keep it steady. “This will help me and others,” she said. Kindness felt warm in her chest.

She gave the strawberries a tiny sip from the blue can. “Thank you for the sweet smell,” she whispered.

Belle twirled in the air. “Good night, brave Tilly.”

“Good night,” said Tilly, curling in her cozy shell with a happy sigh. “I can help myself. I can be kind. I can keep trying.”

The End.
