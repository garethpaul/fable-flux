# Spark the Robot Dog Tries Again

The basement lab hummed and glowed. Little lights blinked red, blue, and green. It smelled like warm metal and lemon soap. Fans whirred softly, and tiny gears clicked like raindrops.

Spark, a helpful robot dog, wagged his shiny tail. His tail made a happy clink-clink sound. He had bright button eyes, soft rubber paws, and a small speaker voice. “Today I help!” he beeped. “Today I try, and try again!”

Dr. Dot, a kind scientist with big round glasses, smiled. “Good morning, Spark,” she said. “We have three jobs today. One, fix the bubble machine. Two, wash the sticky tools. Three, learn calm breathing for big feelings.”

Spark barked a cheerful “Beep!” His ears—little silver triangles—wiggled.

First, they looked at the bubble machine. It was big and round with a clear tube. It had a button that said POP. Dr. Dot said, “The bubble machine makes cleaning fun. But it is broken.”

Spark pressed his nose to the tube. “I see soap bubbles stuck!” he said. He reached in with a soft paw. His paw slipped. Squeeeak! The bubbles squealed and popped on his nose.

“Oops,” Spark said. “I made a mess.” Soap slid down his face. It tasted like lemons. “Ptoo!” He shook his head.

Dr. Dot chuckled. “It’s okay. We can clean it. Remember, persistence means we keep trying, little by little.”

Spark nodded. “I will persist!” He tried again. He pushed a small brush into the tube. The brush got stuck. “Oh no,” Spark said. His tail drooped. His chest felt hot, like a tiny engine getting warm.

“I feel mad,” Spark said honestly. “I want to bark loud.”

Dr. Dot knelt and touched his paw. “Thank you for telling the truth about your feelings,” she said. “When we feel mad, we can pause. Try our calm breathing.”

Spark sat. His paws felt sticky with soap. “Breathe in,” Dr. Dot said softly. “Smell the flower.” Spark sniffed. The lab smelled like lemons and warm wires. “Breathe out. Blow the bubble.” Spark let out a slow, long “Whoooo.” A tiny bubble floated up. It glowed rainbow in the light.

“I feel softer,” Spark said. His tail made a small wag. “I will try again.”

He used a smaller brush this time. “Slow, slow,” he whispered. He pushed, twist, twist, pull. Pop! The brush came out, and the soap cluster loosened. “I did it!” Spark cheered.

“Now, let’s run the test,” said Dr. Dot. Spark pressed POP. The machine hummed, and shiny bubbles drifted around them. They smelled clean and sweet. “Hooray for persistence,” said Dr. Dot.

Next job: the sticky tools. A tray of wrenches and screwdrivers sat by the sink. They were gooey with purple glue. Spark sniffed them. “Yuck,” he said, but he smiled. “Clean tools make healthy hands.”

He turned on the warm water. It splashed like a tiny river. “I will scrub,” he said. He sang a scrub song: “Scrub, scrub, scrub-a-dub, bubbles on my paws!” He rubbed each tool and rinsed. The water turned cloudy, then clear.

One big wrench was extra sticky. Spark scrubbed and scrubbed. His paws got tired. The wrench slipped—clank!—and bonked the sink. Spark jumped. “I made a loud noise,” he said honestly. “I didn’t mean to.”

Dr. Dot looked over. “Thank you for telling me,” she said. “Are you okay?”

“My ears are okay,” Spark said. “But I feel frustrated.”

“Let’s try calm again,” Dr. Dot said. “Smell the flower, blow the bubble.”

Spark breathed in and out. “Whoooo.” He felt his engine cool down. He tried a new way. “I will soak the wrench,” he decided. He put the wrench in warm, soapy water. He waited. Drip, drip, drip. He counted. “One, two, three, four, five.” Then he scrubbed again. The sticky glue slid away.

“Clean!” Spark said proudly. He lined up the tools in a neat row. They shined like little silver fish.

“Excellent hygiene,” said Dr. Dot. “Clean tools keep us safe.”

Last job: learning calm for big feelings. Dr. Dot placed a small red button on the table. It said, BIG FEELINGS PRACTICE. “We will pretend the button is hard to press,” she said. “If it doesn’t work, we practice honesty, calm breaths, and trying again.”

Spark pressed the button. Nothing happened. He pressed harder. Still nothing. He felt a grumble deep inside. “I want to kick the table,” he said. “I feel angry.” His voice buzzed.

“Thank you for telling the truth,” Dr. Dot said. “Anger is okay. Hurting the table is not okay. What can we do?”

Spark sat. He held his paws together. “Smell the flower,” he whispered. “Blow the bubble.” He breathed slow. The grumble went quiet. “I will try a different way.” He looked closely. “Oh! The button is dusty.” He got a soft cloth. Wipe, wipe, wipe. He tried again. Click! A gentle bell rang: ding-ding-ding!

“You did it!” Dr. Dot clapped. “You were honest, you kept clean, and you persisted.”

Spark wagged fast. The lab lights seemed brighter. The air smelled fresh. “Today I learned,” Spark said. “When I feel mad, I can breathe. When things are sticky, I can clean. When a job is hard, I can try again and again.”

Dr. Dot gave Spark a warm pat. “That is the heart of persistence,” she said. “Little steps make big wins.”

Spark looked at the shiny tools, the happy bubble machine, and the quiet red button. “I am a helpful robot dog,” he said. “I will keep trying. I will keep telling the truth. I will keep clean.”

He wagged one more time. Clink-clink. “Tomorrow,” Spark said, “I will try again.”

The End.
