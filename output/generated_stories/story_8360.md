# Pippa the Parrot and the Puzzle Pop

In a cozy house, down the creaky steps, there was a basement lab. It smelled like warm toast and lemon soap. Little lights blinked—click, click, click. Buttons glowed red and green. Beakers made gentle bubbles, blub-blub-blub.

Pippa the parrot lived there with her family. She had bright green feathers and a rainbow tail. She loved to draw, sing, and build things from boxes and tape.

“Good morning, lab!” Pippa chirped. “Let’s make a snack machine!”

She flapped to the workbench. She taped a paper cup, a long tube, a blue button, and a tiny fan together. She drew happy faces on the cup. “This is the Puzzle Pop,” she said. “It will pop fruit pieces into a bowl.”

Pippa pressed the blue button. Whirr! The fan spun. The tube rattled. Pop! Pop! Pop! Fruit shot out—whee!—and bounced off the table onto the floor.

“Oh no,” Pippa said, eyes wide. Plop. A grape rolled under a shelf. “I feel frustrated.” She placed a wing over her chest. “My tummy feels twisty. I need help.”

Her big brother Max came down the stairs. Thump, thump. He wore orange socks and goggles. “Hey, Pippa. What’s up?”

“My Puzzle Pop is popping wrong,” Pippa said. “I want fruit in the bowl, not on the floor.” She stood tall. “Can you help me, please?”

“That’s great self-advocacy,” Max said with a smile. “You told me what you need.” He knelt. “Let’s use logic. We’ll think step by step.”

Pippa nodded. “Step by step,” she echoed.

They looked closely. The fan hummed softly. The tube shook. The bowl sat too far away. Pippa sniffed. The fruit smelled sweet. She heard a tiny hiss of air.

“What do we know?” Max asked.

Pippa tapped her beak. “We know the fan blows. We know the tube points sideways. We know the bowl is here.” She moved the bowl. “Maybe the fruit needs a path.”

“Good thinking,” said Max. “What’s our plan?”

Pippa took a deep breath. “Plan: 1) Move the bowl. 2) Point the tube down. 3) Test.”

They tried. Pippa pointed the tube down with some tape. She slid the bowl under the end. “Ready,” she said, shaking her feathers.

She pressed the blue button. Whirr! Pop! A strawberry flew out and hit the edge of the bowl. Boing! It bounced onto the floor.

Pippa’s cheeks felt hot. “I feel disappointed,” she said softly. “But I can keep going.” She wiggled her claws. “What do we know now?”

Max nodded. “We know the fruit is still bouncing.”

Pippa blinked. “Maybe the fruit needs something soft to land on.”

She looked around. She found a piece of soft sponge and a strip of felt. She smiled. “New plan: 1) Put soft sponge in the bowl. 2) Add felt ramp.”

She set the sponge in the bowl. She made a gentle felt ramp from the tube to the sponge. “Test,” she said.

Button press. Whirr! Pop! A blueberry rolled down the felt—shhh—then sank into the sponge—plop. It stayed!

Pippa clapped her wings. “It worked!”

“Test again,” said Max. “Good logic checks twice.”

Pippa grinned. She pressed the button. Whirr! Pop-pop! Two grapes rolled, shhh, and plop, plop, into the bowl. They did not bounce away.

But then, pop! A slice of banana got stuck in the tube. It wiggled and stopped. The fan buzzed louder. Bzzzz.

Pippa frowned. “I feel worried. The tube is clogged.”

She paused and took a slow breath. “What do we know? Big fruit gets stuck. Small fruit slides.”

Max smiled. “That’s a clue.”

Pippa nodded. “New plan: 1) Cut big fruit smaller. 2) Add a little gate to slow the pops.”

She found child-safe scissors and a piece of cardboard. Snip, snip. She made banana pieces small. She made a tiny cardboard gate that opened and closed with a string.

She tied the string to the blue button. “When I press, the gate opens. When I let go, the gate closes,” she explained. “That will slow the pops.”

“Logical and creative,” said Max.

Pippa pressed the button. The gate lifted—click. Whirr! Pop-pop-pop! Small fruit rolled down the felt—shhh—and nestled into the sponge—plop, plop, plop. Nothing flew away. Nothing got stuck.

Pippa’s eyes shone. “I feel proud!” She looked at Max. “Thank you for listening and helping.”

Max patted her wing. “You did the thinking. You asked for help. You used your plan.”

They shared a bowl of fruit. The strawberry was sweet and soft. The blueberry was juicy. The basement lab felt cozy and bright.

Pippa turned to the Puzzle Pop. “Hello, friend,” she chirped. “You were a puzzle, and I used my brain.”

She fluffed her feathers and said, “Here is my lesson: When a problem pops, I can pause, say my feelings, ask for help, and think step by step. I can test, change, and try again.”

She pressed the button one more time. Pop-pop! The fruit rolled, shhh, and plopped into the bowl with a gentle sound like rain.

Pippa smiled. “That’s logical-thinking,” she said. “And it feels good.”

The End.
