# Ollie Otter and the Garden Shed Clock

Ollie was a playful otter with bright eyes and a bouncy tail. One sunny morning, he padded to Grandma Dot’s garden shed. The shed smelled like fresh soil and pine. Sunlight made little dust sparkles in the air. Tools hung on hooks. A big round clock tick-tocked on the wall. Tick. Tock. Tick. Tock.

“Ollie, will you help me today?” Grandma Dot asked, smiling. She wore a blue apron and soft green gloves. “We have three jobs. First, sweep the floor. Second, sort the seed packets. Third, water the potted flowers. We must finish before the clock’s big hand gets to the twelve.”

Ollie clapped his paws. “I want to help! I can do all the jobs!” His whiskers wiggled with excitement.

Grandma Dot nodded. “Good. Let’s make a plan. We will do the jobs in order. We will listen to the clock. We will take our time and do it right.”

Ollie looked at the shiny shovel and the soft broom. He heard the clock. Tick. Tock. His tummy felt fizzy with joy. “I can go fast!” he said.

“Fast is fun,” said Grandma Dot, “but careful is wise. Let’s follow the plan.”

Ollie picked up the broom. Whoosh, whoosh. He swept little leaves into a neat pile. The dust smelled like dry straw. “This is easy!” he sang. But then he saw a bright red ball on a shelf.

“Ooo! A ball!” Ollie bounced it. Boing! Boing! It rolled under a table.

Tick. Tock.

“Ollie,” said Grandma Dot gently, “what is job number one?”

“Sweep the floor,” Ollie said, with a small blush. He put the ball back. Whoosh, whoosh. The pile got bigger. His paws made little swish sounds. He hummed. He decided, “I will finish the sweep before I do anything else.”

Soon, the floor looked clean. “Done!” Ollie cheered.

“Great job,” said Grandma Dot. “Now, job number two: sort the seed packets. Flowers here, veggies there.”

Ollie opened a box. It smelled like paper and peppery seeds. He held up a packet with a yellow flower. “Sunflower!” he giggled. He put it in the flower pile. He held up a packet with a carrot. “Crunchy carrot!” In the veggie pile it went.

Then he saw shiny stickers in the box. “Stickers!” He wanted to stick them on the wall and on his nose.

Tick. Tock.

He paused. He looked at the clock. The big hand was moving. “Hmm,” Ollie said. “I can play with stickers later. I will do my job now.” He made a little choice in his heart. That choice felt strong and warm.

He sorted. Rustle, rustle went the packets. He checked each picture and followed the directions on the labels. Flowers here. Veggies there. Herbs in a small pile. He even made a sign that said “Seeds” with a big S.

Grandma Dot peeked in. “Nice sorting, Ollie. You followed directions.”

Ollie grinned. “I did! And I decided to wait on the stickers.”

“Smart choice,” Grandma Dot said.

Now came job number three: water the potted flowers. The watering can was green and cool to the touch. The water gurgled. Glug, glug. “Only a little water,” Grandma Dot reminded. “Just until the soil feels damp, not soggy.”

Ollie nodded. He touched the soil with his paw. It felt dry like toast. He poured slowly. Drip, drip, drip. The flowers smelled sweet, like honey and lemon. A small bee buzzed past the window. Bzzzz.

Then Ollie heard a chirp outside. “Birds!” He wanted to run and chase the shadows.

Tick. Tock.

Ollie looked at the clock. The big hand was almost to the eleven. He took a deep breath. “I will finish watering first,” he told himself. “Then I can go outside.” He poured just a little more on the last pot. He checked the soil. Damp, not soggy. “Done!”

Grandma Dot clapped softly. “You did it! All three jobs, and the big hand is not at the twelve yet.”

Ollie’s tail swished. His heart felt proud. “I followed the plan. I listened to the clock. I made good choices.”

Grandma Dot hugged him. Her apron smelled like mint. “That is time management,” she said slowly. “You used your time well. You did one job at a time. You followed directions. You made decisions that helped you finish.”

Ollie smiled. “Time management,” he repeated. The words felt big, but friendly. “Can I put one sticker on my paw now?”

“Yes,” Grandma Dot laughed. “Now is a good time.”

They stepped outside. The air was warm and soft. The birds chirped. Chirp, chirp. Ollie bounced the red ball once. Boing! Then he stopped, looked back at the tidy shed, and the neat seed piles, and the happy flowers.

He said, “When I plan my time, I have time for fun too!”

Grandma Dot nodded. “That is the lesson, dear otter. Plan, follow directions, and make good choices. Then work gets done, and there is time to play.”

Ollie held up his paw with the shiny sticker. It gleamed in the sun. He felt calm and glad. He had learned something important today.

“Tick. Tock. Play o’clock!” Ollie giggled, and they both laughed.

The End.
