# Peanut’s Kitchen Clock

The warm kitchen smelled like cinnamon and toast. Sunlight made the yellow curtains glow. A gentle elephant calf named Peanut stood on a stool by the wooden table. Peanut’s soft gray ears flopped as he wiggled with excitement.

“Today I will make a birthday banner!” Peanut trumpeted softly. “I want to paint, and glue, and sprinkle glitter, and bake cookies too!”

Mama Elephant stirred a pot of warm oatmeal. It made tiny bubble pops. “That is a lot for one morning, Peanut,” Mama said with a smile. “How much time do we have?”

Peanut looked at the round clock on the wall. The hands looked like little arrows. “The party is after lunch,” he said. “I want everything perfect.”

Just then, Peanut’s friend, Tiny Mouse, peeked from under a bright dish towel. “Squeak! I want to help,” Tiny Mouse said. “Let’s make the banner super long!”

Peanut’s eyes grew wide. “Yes! A super long banner. And cookies shaped like stars. And glitter everywhere!”

Mama Elephant set three bowls on the table. Clink, clink, clink. “Let’s think,” she said gently. “We have two hours before lunch. That is not a lot. If we try to do everything, we might not finish anything.”

Peanut’s ears drooped. “But I want it all…”

Tiny Mouse’s whiskers twitched. “Squeak. I want it all too.”

Mama nodded. “We need a plan. A plan is a strategy. A strategy helps us use our time well.”

Peanut took a deep breath. He smelled vanilla and warm toast. “What is our plan?”

Mama pointed to the clock. “Each thing takes time. Painting the banner takes one big song. Adding glitter takes one big song. Baking cookies takes two big songs. That’s four big songs.”

Peanut blinked. “Four big songs is a lot. We only have time for three big songs before lunch.”

Tiny Mouse clicked his tiny claws on the table. “We need to choose. But I want glitter,” he squeaked.

Peanut felt a lump in his tummy. He wanted painting and cookies and glitter, all at once. His eyes felt shiny, like he might cry.

Mama put her warm trunk around Peanut. “This is a good time to practice compromise,” she said. “Compromise means we each give a little, and we all get something we like.”

Peanut sniffed. “Compromise?” he asked.

Tiny Mouse nodded. “We pick what is most important now, and we save the rest for later,” he said softly.

Peanut looked at the bowls. One had soft blue paint. One had sparkly glitter. One had cookie dough that smelled like sugar and butter. The dough felt cool when he poked it with the tip of his trunk.

“I love painting,” Peanut said. “Painting makes my heart feel big.”

“I love glitter,” Tiny Mouse squeaked. “Glitter makes me giggle.”

“I love cookies,” Mama laughed. “But we only have time for two things before lunch.”

Peanut thought and thought. He listened to the clock tick-tock. He tapped his toes to the rhythm. Then he smiled. “I have an idea! We can paint the banner first. While the paint dries, we can bake the cookies. After lunch, we can add glitter. That is our compromise. We do two things now and one thing later.”

Tiny Mouse’s eyes sparkled. “Squeak! I can wait for glitter if we know when it comes,” he said.

Mama clapped her big hands softly. “That is a smart strategy and good time-management,” she said. “We have a plan.”

They set a timer that went ding! when a big song ended. Peanut painted the banner with swishy blue lines and sunny yellow circles. The brush felt tickly in his trunk. Tiny Mouse held the paper steady. “Good job,” he squeaked.

Ding! The paint needed to dry. Peanut and Tiny Mouse washed their hands in warm, soapy water. Bubbles floated up like shiny moons. “Pop! Pop!” Peanut giggled.

They rolled cookie dough with a smooth wooden roller. The dough was cool and soft. Tiny Mouse pressed star cutters into it. Peanut lifted the stars carefully. Onto the tray they went—plop, plop, plop.

Mama slid the tray into the warm oven. A sweet smell filled the kitchen. Tiny Mouse wiggled his nose. “Mmm,” he said.

Ding! The cookies came out golden and crunchy on the edges. Peanut’s tummy rumbled. “After lunch,” Mama said, smiling. “We can taste them then.”

They ate oatmeal with cinnamon and apple slices. The banner paint dried in the sunny window. After lunch, Peanut checked the clock. “Now it is glitter time!” he cheered.

Tiny Mouse sprinkled gently. “Sssssssparkle,” he whispered. Peanut tapped the glue with his trunk, dot, dot, dot. The banner shone like stars in the night.

They hung the banner across the warm kitchen. It said, “Happy Day!” in big curvy letters. The cookies cooled on a plate. Everything was ready, and everyone felt calm and proud.

Mama hugged Peanut. “You used compromise to make a fair plan,” she said. “You used strategy to decide the order. You used time-management to finish on time.”

Peanut’s ears lifted high. “We did it together,” he said.

Tiny Mouse squeaked, “And we saved glitter for the perfect time!”

Peanut smiled. His heart felt big again. He learned that when there is not enough time to do everything, he can talk, listen, and make a plan. He can choose what to do now and what to do later. That is compromise.

The kitchen felt warm and bright. The clock ticked softly. The cookies smelled sweet. The banner sparkled. Peanut and Tiny Mouse shared a cookie and a happy look.

The End.
