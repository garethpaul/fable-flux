# Barkley Beeps in the Attic

Click. Whirr. Beep-beep!

Barkley, the helpful robot dog, woke up in the quiet attic. The attic smelled like dry wood and old paper. Dust danced in sunbeams. Boxes sat like sleepy mountains. A small round window showed a patch of blue sky.

Barkley’s metal ears twitched. His tail light blinked happy yellow. “Good morning,” Barkley beeped softly. He moved on little wheels that hummed. Hummmm. His paws were smooth and kind.

He was helping Lily and Max. Lily was five. Max was four. They were looking for Grandma’s memory box. It held special things from long ago. The box was for a family show-and-share. They needed it today.

Lily whispered, “We have to find the memory box.”

Max whispered back, “I think it is in this big box. I am fast!”

Barkley blinked his gentle blue eyes. “Let’s use words. Let’s take turns,” he said in a warm robot voice. “Good talking helps us work together.”

Max puffed his cheeks. “I want to open first!”

Lily hugged her elbows. “I was the first to plan. I should open first.”

Barkley sat and wagged his light. “We can be fair,” he said. “Fair means justice. Justice means we share and do what is right. Let’s talk and listen.”

They could hear the wind tickle the window. Tickle-tickle. The attic was quiet, but their feelings were loud.

Barkley pressed his paw to a small speaker on his chest. It glowed green. “Here is our talking light. When it is green, one person talks. When it turns blue, we listen and the next person talks.”

Lily nodded. “Okay.”

Max blinked. “Okay.”

The green light shone on Lily. “I feel excited and worried,” she said. “I want to open a box because I want to help Grandma.”

The light turned blue, then green for Max. He said, “I feel excited too. I want to open a box because I like to move and be fast.”

Barkley wagged. “Thank you for telling your feelings. That is good communication.”

He looked at the boxes. The big box was taped shut. A medium box had a picture of a bird. A small box had a ribbon.

Barkley sniffed, beep-beep. “I hear something soft in the medium box,” he said. “Rustle, rustle.”

Max bounced on his toes. “Can I open the medium box? Please?”

Lily took a breath. “Yes,” she said. “You can open that one. I will open the small box.”

Barkley’s tail light turned warm orange. “That is kind,” he said. “Kindness is generosity. Generosity means we share and give.”

Max carefully peeled the tape. Rip…rip…rip. He lifted the lid. Inside was a nest of soft tissue paper and a tiny bell. Ting-a-ling! The sound was bright and sweet.

Lily untied the small ribbon. Inside were old buttons and a little photo. The photo showed Grandma as a girl, smiling with a kite.

Barkley cocked his head. “We are close,” he said. “The memory box may be near.”

He rolled toward the big box. His wheels went hummm. He stopped and listened. He tapped gently. Tap-tap. “This box is heavy,” he said. “Let’s move it together. We can count our steps and talk while we move.”

“Okay,” said Lily.

“Okay,” said Max.

Barkley said, “Let’s say, ‘One, two, lift!’”

They placed their small hands on the box. Barkley placed his soft paw too. “One, two, lift!”

They lifted and scooted. Scrape-scoot. Scrape-scoot. The floor felt cool under their socks. They giggled when a puff of dust tickled their noses. Achoo!

Under the big box was a wooden chest with a heart carved on top. It was smooth and warm to touch. It smelled like cinnamon and time.

“The memory box!” Lily gasped.

Max clapped. “We found it!”

Barkley blinked happy. “Before we open, let’s ask a fairness question,” he said. “Who opens the chest?”

Max looked at Lily. Lily looked at Max. They both smiled.

Lily said, “We can open it together.”

Max nodded. “We can share.”

Barkley’s tail flashed rainbow. “That is justice and generosity. You talked. You listened. You chose what is fair.”

They put their hands on the heart. Barkley placed his paw beside them. Together they lifted the lid. Gentle creak.

Inside were letters tied with blue string, a tiny seashell, a paper star, and a scarf that smelled like roses. There was also a little book that said, “Grandma’s Memories.”

Lily held the scarf. “It feels soft,” she said.

Max shook the paper star. Rustle. “It sounds like wishes.”

Barkley read the title on the little book. “Would you like me to read the first page?”

“Yes!” said Lily and Max.

Barkley spoke softly. “My name is Grandma. When I was a girl, I flew a kite. The wind sang. I learned that talking kindly helps kites fly and hearts feel light.”

Lily put a hand on Max’s shoulder. “Thank you for listening to me,” she said.

Max smiled. “Thank you for sharing with me.”

Barkley wagged. “You used your words. You took turns. You were fair. You were generous with each other. That is strong communication.”

They placed the items back with care. They closed the chest gently. Lily and Max lifted together. “One, two, lift!” They moved it toward the attic door. Scrape-scoot. Barkley guided with soft beeps. Beep…beep…beep.

At the top of the stairs, Barkley paused. “Let’s tell Grandma how we found it,” he said. “Let’s tell her how we talked, listened, and shared.”

“Yes,” said Lily.

“Yes,” said Max.

Downstairs, Grandma hugged them. Her hug felt warm like a blanket. “I am proud,” she said. “You worked together.”

Barkley wagged and beeped. “We used good communication,” he said. “We were fair and generous. We moved together as a team.”

Grandma smiled. “That is the best treasure of all.”

The End.
