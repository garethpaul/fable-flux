# Panda Poppy Practices Patience

Poppy was a patient panda who loved to dance. She had soft black-and-white fur and warm, gentle eyes. One sunny morning, Poppy woke up in the guest room at Grandma Mei’s house. The guest room smelled like clean soap and sweet jasmine. A small fan hummed softly. Sunbeams made bright squares on the floor.

Today was the park parade. Poppy and her friends would dance to help raise coins for a new swingset. “Community service helps everyone,” Grandma Mei said with a smile. “Your dancing can help children play outside.”

Poppy stretched her paws. “I will practice,” she whispered. She turned on the music. Tap, tap, swish! Her paws slid on the rug. She tried a twirl. Oops! Her tail knocked a pillow. The pillow flumped to the floor.

“It’s okay,” Poppy said to herself. “I can try again.” She took a deep breath in through her nose. In… and out. The air smelled like jasmine. She felt calm.

Her friend Tiko the turtle peeked in. “Ready, Poppy?” he asked.

“Almost,” said Poppy. “I want my jumps to be strong.”

She tried a jump. Boing! But her foot bumped the bedside table. A lamp wobbled. “Oh no!” Poppy’s cheeks felt hot. Her tummy felt tight. She didn’t like that feeling.

Grandma Mei walked in with a kind face. “Little panda, what do you feel?”

“I feel mad,” Poppy said, frowning. “I can’t do my jump!”

Grandma nodded. “Mad is a big feeling. Let’s let it pass through, like a cloud. Try my calm steps.”

“What are calm steps?” asked Poppy.

“Step one,” Grandma said softly, “Put your paws on your belly. Step two, take three slow breaths. In… one. Out… one. In… two. Out… two. In… three. Out… three. Step three, say, ‘I can try again.’”

Poppy followed. She felt her belly rise and fall. In… and out. Her shoulders relaxed. “I can try again,” she whispered.

Tiko clapped his little flippers. “Good job!”

Poppy tried her jump once more. This time, she bent her knees. Up, swish, land. “Better!” she said, smiling.

Soon it was time to go outdoors. The sun felt warm on Poppy’s fur. The air smelled like fresh grass. She heard birds chirp, “Cheep, cheep!” At the park, neighbors gathered with bright signs. “Dance for a Swing!” the signs said. A big jar waited for coins. Children laughed and parents waved.

Poppy’s friends lined up: Tiko the turtle, Lina the ladybug, and Benji the bunny. The music began. Drums went boom-boom. The crowd clapped. Poppy’s heart pitter-pattered with happy beats.

She twirled, she tapped, she jumped. The grass tickled her paws. Coins clinked into the jar. Clink, clink, clink! Poppy felt proud. “We are helping!” she whispered.

Then something happened. A breeze puffed. Poppy’s ribbon slipped over her eyes. She couldn’t see! She stumbled. Her paw slid on a leaf. Plop! She fell on her bottom.

“Oh!” Poppy’s face got hot again. Her tummy felt tight. Tears wiggled in her eyes. “I can’t dance,” she said in a small voice.

The music kept playing, but Poppy sat still. Lina fluttered close. “It’s okay to feel mad,” Lina said.

Benji nodded. “We can help.”

Grandma Mei called gently from the bench, “Calm steps, Poppy.”

Poppy remembered. She put her paws on her belly. The ribbon was soft and silky. She took three slow breaths. In… one. Out… one. In… two. Out… two. In… three. Out… three. She felt the sun on her face. She heard the leaves whisper. She smelled the fresh grass. “I can try again,” she said.

Tiko lifted the ribbon from her eyes. “Here you go,” he said.

“Thank you,” said Poppy. She stood up and wiped her tears. “I will practice the jump slowly first.”

She showed her friends. “Bend, up, land.” They copied her. Bend, up, land. The crowd cheered. “Go, Poppy!”

Poppy smiled and tried the jump with the music. Bend, up, swish! Land. She did it! The coins clinked even more. Clink, clink, clink!

After the dance, everyone shared cold water and crunchy carrot sticks. The water felt cool on Poppy’s tongue. The carrots went crunch, crunch. The jar was full of shiny coins.

A little fox cub tugged Poppy’s paw. “I liked your calm steps,” the cub said. “When I get mad, I will breathe too.”

Poppy hugged the cub gently. “Big feelings can pass like clouds,” she said. “Breathe, practice, and try again.”

Grandma Mei wrapped an arm around Poppy. “You were patient and brave,” she said. “Your calm helped your dance. Your dance helped the park.”

The park leader rang a little bell. “Thank you, dancers! We have enough coins for the new swingset!”

Everyone cheered. Birds sang. The breeze smelled like flowers and fresh cut grass. Poppy looked at the guest room key in her pocket and smiled, thinking of where she began.

Poppy waved to her friends. “Let’s play on the new swings when they arrive,” she said.

“Yes!” they all shouted.

Poppy took one last slow breath. In… and out. She felt peaceful and proud.

The End.
