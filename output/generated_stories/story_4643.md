# Hazel Squirrel’s Toasty Plan

Hazel the squirrel peeked through the window and wiggled her whiskers. Inside the warm kitchen, golden light glowed. The air smelled like cinnamon and toast. The kettle hummed a soft song. Hazel’s small heart thumped with an idea.

“I can learn to make my own snack,” Hazel whispered. Her tail flicked with excitement. “I can do it step by step.”

Hazel pushed open the little pet door and padded inside. The floor felt warm on her paws. She could hear the soft tick-tock of the clock. On the table sat a loaf of bread, a jar of peanut butter, and a shiny butter knife. There was also a small, friendly toaster. It looked like it smiled with its little slots.

From the hallway, Grandma Dot hummed. “I’m folding towels,” she called. “Breakfast is in the kitchen if you need it!”

Hazel stood tall. “I can make toast,” she said. “I want to learn.” Her voice was small but strong.

She took a deep breath. “First, I decide what to make.” She looked around and nodded. “Toast with a banana smile.” Hazel liked this plan. It felt clear and cozy.

She washed her paws with warm, soapy water. The bubbles smelled like oranges. “Clean paws first,” she said. “That’s a good skill.”

Hazel pulled a slice of bread from the bag. The bread felt soft like a pillow. She went to the toaster and paused. The toaster had buttons and a dial. Hazel frowned a little. “Hmm,” she murmured, “I do not know which button to push.”

She remembered a rule: Ask for help when you are not sure. That is a skill too.

“Grandma Dot?” Hazel called, standing straight. “I want to make toast by myself, but I need help with the safe parts.”

Grandma Dot came to the doorway, holding a fluffy towel. Her eyes were kind. “What a strong, smart choice,” she said. “You decided. You asked. That is being safe and brave.”

Hazel pointed to the dial. “Which number makes light toast?”

“Number two is light,” Grandma Dot said. “And I will plug it in. Only grown-ups plug things in. You can push the lever.”

Hazel nodded. “Thank you. I can push the lever.” She felt proud. She was learning the steps.

She slid the bread into the slot. She wrapped her tail around her waist so it would not bump the toaster. Click. The lever went down. The toaster hummed again. Hazel waited. She smelled warm bread and smiled.

Pop! The toast jumped up with a happy sound.

Hazel giggled. “Hello, toast!” She used tongs to lift it out. “Tongs keep paws safe,” she said. “I can do this.”

She placed the toast on a small plate. She opened the peanut butter jar and sniffed. It smelled nutty and sweet. She picked up the butter knife and spread carefully. “Slow and smooth,” she whispered. “Corner to corner.” The peanut butter made soft sticky sounds. Smick. Smack. Smear.

She peeled a banana and sliced soft circles. “Little wheels,” she said. She placed two circles for eyes and a curved slice for a smile. The toast looked back at her with a happy face.

Hazel clapped her paws. “I did it!”

Just then, her friend Pip the mouse peeked from a tiny hole near the baseboard. “Hi, Hazel,” Pip squeaked. “Can I have your toast?”

Hazel took a breath. Her tummy felt a wiggle. She liked Pip, but she also worked hard. She wanted to eat the snack she made.

Hazel stood tall again. “I made this toast for me,” she said clearly and kindly. “I am hungry. I will share some, but not all.”

Pip’s ears twitched. “Okay! Can I have a banana circle?”

Hazel smiled. “Yes! I can share two.” She put two slices on a napkin and added a tiny dot of peanut butter. “Here you go.”

Pip nibbled. “Yum! You make good choices.”

Hazel’s cheeks felt warm with pride. “Thank you. I planned the steps. I asked for help. I said what I needed.” She took a bite of her toast. It was crunchy, creamy, sweet, and safe. Her tail swished with joy.

Grandma Dot poured warm milk into a little cup. “Would you like a sip, Hazel?” The milk smelled like vanilla.

“Yes, please,” said Hazel. She held the cup with both paws and took a careful sip. “Mmm.”

Pip looked at the toaster. “Can I make toast too?”

Hazel thought. She remembered the safety rule. “We can make toast together,” she said. “Grandma will do the plug. I can show you the steps.”

Grandma Dot nodded. “That is teamwork. Teaching is a wonderful skill.”

Hazel and Pip washed their tiny paws. They named each step as they worked:
- Decide the snack.
- Clean paws.
- Ask for help with tricky parts.
- Use tools safely.
- Take turns and speak up.
- Share a little if you can.

Soon Pip had a tiny toast with blueberry buttons. “I did it!” Pip cheered.

Hazel felt calm and strong. The warm kitchen glowed. The clock ticked softly, and the kettle sang. Hazel had used her head, her heart, and her voice. She had built skills, made choices, and said her needs.

She licked a bit of peanut butter from her whiskers and giggled. “Today, I learned I can do hard things, one step at a time.”

Grandma Dot hugged them both. “And you did it safely and kindly.”

Hazel nodded. “Plan. Practice. Ask. Speak up. Share.” She tapped each word on the table. “That is my toasty plan.”

The End.
