# Tully the Turtle’s Balcony Garden Parade

Tully was a shy turtle who lived with Nana on the tenth floor. Their home had a sunny balcony garden. Pots lined the rail, filled with mint, tomatoes, basil, and tiny flowers. Bees hummed softly. The leaves smelled fresh and green. Tully liked to sit under the mint and listen.

One bright morning, Nana said, “Today is Balcony Garden Day! Our neighbors are sharing tastes and songs from their homes around the world.”

Tully’s heart thumped. “People?” he whispered, pulling his head into his shell just a little.

Nana patted his shell. “Yes, little one. We will meet friends. We will share. We will learn. We belong to one big world.”

Tully peeked out. “I am shy.”

“That’s okay,” said Nana. “We can go slow. We can listen first.”

The hallway buzzed with friendly voices. Tully and Nana stepped onto the long balcony that connected the apartments. Colorful cloths fluttered. Pots of herbs lined the rail. The air was warm and smelled like bread, lemons, and soil. A small banner read, “Welcome, Neighbors!”

Mrs. Amina waved gently. “Hello, Tully,” she said. “I am from Egypt. Would you like to smell my mint tea?” Steam curled from a little cup. Tully leaned close.

“Mmm,” he said softly. “It smells sweet.”

Mr. Kenji bowed with a smile. “Konnichiwa. I am from Japan. See my tiny tomatoes? We call them momotaro. Would you like to taste one?” He held out a small, shiny tomato.

Tully reached out slowly. He took a little bite. “Pop!” The tomato burst with juice. Tully smiled.

Luna and Mateo, the twins from next door, waved. “Hi, Tully! We made lemon water like our abuela in Mexico,” said Luna. “And we decorated our pot with paper flowers,” said Mateo.

Tully looked at the bright paper petals. “They are pretty,” he said.

Nana brought out a small dish. “This is our family’s bread with olive oil,” she said. “My mother taught me. Would you like to try?”

Neighbors nodded and tasted. “Delicious!” said Mr. Kenji.

A soft drum began to tap. Mrs. Amina tapped a rhythm on a little drum from home. Luna shook tiny maracas. The sounds were gentle and happy. Tully’s shell felt less tight. He swayed just a little.

Then a breeze blew. Whoosh! The banner wiggled. A big pot wobbled near the edge. “Oh!” cried Luna. The pot tipped, and soil spilled in a soft brown puff.

Tully froze. His heart thumped fast. He pulled his head in. The pot leaned more. A small bee buzzed in circles, confused by the spill.

Nana whispered, “We can help together.”

Mr. Kenji held the railing. “Let’s make a safe space,” he said.

Mrs. Amina put down her drum. “We can move the pot away from the edge,” she said.

Luna and Mateo looked at Tully. “Will you help too?” asked Mateo.

Tully peeked out. He saw worried faces. He saw the little plant in the pot, its leaves shaking. He took a deep breath. He remembered Nana’s words. We belong to one big world.

“I can,” Tully said quietly. “I am small, but I am steady.”

Nana smiled. “Yes, you are.”

Together they made a plan. Mr. Kenji held the big pot. Mrs. Amina scooped soil back in. Nana brushed soil off the leaves. Luna and Mateo brought a small cup of water. Tully used his careful turtle feet to tap the soil down, pat-pat-pat, making it firm.

The pot stood straight again. The little plant looked calm. The bee circled once and settled on a flower.

“We did it!” cheered the twins.

Tully felt warm inside. He stood a little taller. “We helped as a team,” he said. “From different homes. Together.”

Mrs. Amina clapped softly. “This is cooperation,” she said. “When we work together, we make things better.”

Mr. Kenji nodded. “And we each brought a piece of ourselves,” he said. “That is how neighbors become friends.”

Nana brushed a bit of soil from Tully’s shell. “Would you like to share our family bread story, Tully?” she asked.

Tully took a breath. He looked at the friendly faces. He smelled mint and lemon and tomatoes. He heard the soft drum and the gentle buzz of the bee.

“Yes,” he said. “My great-grandma made this bread when she moved to a new place. She shared it with new neighbors. They shared their foods too. She said sharing makes a new place feel like home.”

“That is a beautiful tradition,” said Mrs. Amina.

Luna held out the maracas. “Would you like to try?” she asked.

Tully shook them softly. Chika-chika. His shy tummy felt less twisty. He smiled. “I like the sound,” he said.

Mateo tapped the drum. Mr. Kenji hummed a tune. Nana poured tiny cups of tea and lemon water. They all took turns telling small stories: a moon festival, a lantern night, a song for planting, a game with paper fish. The balcony felt like a little world garden.

At the end of the day, the sky turned pink. The air smelled like warm bread and mint. Tully looked around. “I was shy,” he said, “but I learned. We are different, and we belong together. When we share, we are one big team.”

Nana hugged him. “That is global-citizenship,” she said softly. “We care for each other and our world.”

Tully nodded. “I can do that,” he said. “I can listen, help, and share.”

The neighbors waved goodnight. The bee buzzed sleepily. Tully tucked into his shell, snug and happy on the balcony garden. He dreamed of drums, tomatoes, paper flowers, and new friends around the world.

The End.
