# Momo's Gentle Words

Momo was a courageous monkey with bright eyes and soft, fuzzy fur. One sunny afternoon, Momo tiptoed into the laundry room. It smelled like warm soap and clean cotton. The washing machine hummed, “Whirr, whirr,” and the dryer made a cozy, “Hummm.”

Momo loved the laundry room. It felt warm and safe. Socks hung like flags, and shirts danced on the line. A basket full of clean towels looked like a fluffy mountain.

“Today,” Momo said, puffing up with brave pride, “I will help! I will sort the clothes. I am a helpful monkey!”

Just then, Momo saw a little mouse named Pippa peeking from behind the laundry basket. Pippa held a tiny pencil and a small notepad made from folded paper. Her whiskers twitched.

“Hi, Momo,” squeaked Pippa. “I’m writing a list so we can remember where everything goes.”

Momo smiled. “A list is smart! I can write too,” he said, tapping his chest. “I can write faster. I am very brave and very fast.”

Pippa shyly held out her notepad. “We can write it together,” she said. “We can take turns.”

Momo grabbed the pencil. The pencil smelled like fresh wood. Momo pressed hard and scribbled big letters: SOCKS, SHIRTS, TOWELS. He wrote fast. He wrote so fast that the pencil squeaked on the paper.

Pippa’s ears drooped. “Oh,” she whispered softly. “I wanted to write too.”

“I will do it all,” said Momo, standing tall. “It is faster if I do everything!”

The washing machine gurgled, “Glug, glug,” like it was thinking. The dryer gave a warm puff of air, and a sock fell to the floor. Pippa picked up the sock and hugged it. “We can share,” she said, voice tiny.

Momo didn’t hear the tiny voice. He was too busy being fast. He started moving baskets and stacking towels. He made a big tower. The tower wobbled.

“Be mindful, Momo,” Pippa squeaked. “Slow and careful.”

“I am brave!” Momo said. “I can do it!”

The towel tower tilted, tilted, and then—plop!—fell over. Soft towels landed all around. A small sock landed on Momo’s head. Momo blinked. He felt a hot, prickly feeling in his chest. His cheeks grew warm. He looked at Pippa.

Pippa’s whiskers drooped again. “It’s okay,” she said quietly. “We can try again.”

Momo’s ears lowered. He took a slow breath in, like the dryer’s warm breeze. He let the breath out, slow and calm. He put his hand on his chest. “I feel wobbly inside,” he said. “I wanted to help, but I forgot to listen.”

Pippa nodded. “We can listen to each other. That is respect,” she said gently. “Respect means we use kind words and take turns. We notice feelings.”

Momo looked at the notepad. He saw the big, scratchy letters he made. He saw Pippa’s tiny pencil marks. “I was not mindful,” Momo said softly. “I hurried. I did not share. I did not show respect.”

He held out the pencil. “Pippa, will you write with me? We can take turns. You first.”

Pippa’s eyes brightened. “Thank you, Momo.” She wrote carefully: 1. Socks in the small basket. 2. Shirts on the line. 3. Towels folded gentle.

Momo watched. He listened. He nodded. “That is a good plan,” he said. He added, in neat letters, 4. Use kind words.

They both smiled.

“Let’s practice,” said Pippa. “I will sort socks. You can fold towels.”

Momo sat on the warm floor and folded a towel slowly. He used his gentle hands. The towel felt soft and fluffy, like a cloud. “Pippa, you are good at sorting,” he said. “Thank you for teaching me.”

Pippa’s whiskers perked up. “Thank you for folding so nicely,” she said. “Your folds are straight like lines.”

Momo giggled. “Lines! That’s like writing.” He picked up the notepad again. “Can we write kind words we can say?”

Pippa nodded. Together they wrote:
- Please.
- Thank you.
- May I take a turn?
- You did a great job.
- I am listening.

They read the list out loud, slow and clear. The laundry room felt extra warm now. The dryer hummed happily. The washing machine swished softly.

When a shirt slipped off the line, Momo said, “Oops! I’ll fix it. Pippa, may I have a clothespin, please?”

“Yes,” said Pippa, smiling. “Here you go.”

When Pippa dropped a sock, she said, “Oops! Thank you for waiting, Momo.”

Momo nodded. “I am listening,” he said. He took another slow breath. He felt calm and strong.

Soon the laundry was neat and clean. Socks were together. Shirts were lined up. Towels were folded gentle. The notepad list was taped to the wall with a tiny piece of friendly tape.

Momo turned to Pippa. “I am sorry I did not listen before,” he said. “I was brave, but not kind.”

Pippa gave him a tiny high-five. “Now you are brave and kind,” she said. “That is respect.”

Momo’s heart felt bright, like sunshine. “Respect means we listen, we share, and we use gentle words,” he said. “We are a team.”

Pippa nodded. “Yes. And when we feel wobbly, we breathe and try again.”

They both took one more slow breath. The laundry room was quiet and cozy. The air smelled clean. The floor felt warm. Momo and Pippa smiled at each other.

“Ready for story time?” asked Momo. “We can write our own story.”

“Let’s write about respect,” said Pippa.

They sat together, side by side, and began to write in careful, kind words.

The End.
