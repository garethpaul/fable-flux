# Milo Mouse and the Calm-Down Clock

In a busy basement full of soft whirrs and gentle clanks, a kind mouse named Milo lived behind a warm cardboard box. The basement smelled like dusty books and sweet soap. Little lights blinked on a humming washing machine. Pipes went drip, drip, drip. The floor was cool and smooth, and tiny bits of lint tickled Milo’s paws.

Milo loved to help. He shared crumbs with the beetles, he held the door for the spiders, and he told bedtime stories to the sleepy moths. Every morning, he squeaked, “Good morning, friends!” and every friend squeaked back.

One day, Milo set out on his daily adventure. He carried a small string backpack with a shiny button, a paper clip, and a roll of tape. “Today I will build something new,” he said. “Something that helps.”

Just then, he heard a loud clatter. “Clunk! Clang!” A wooden spool rolled across the floor and bumped Milo’s foot. “Ouch,” he said softly, rubbing his paw. “That scared me.”

From behind a paint can popped Tilly the Tiny Toad. “I’m sorry!” croaked Tilly. “I got mad. My marble got stuck under the shelf, and I kicked the spool. I should not have done that.”

Milo saw Tilly’s face. Her cheeks puffed, and her eyes looked wet. “You feel mad,” said Milo. “Mad is a strong feeling.”

Tilly nodded. “I tried to pull the marble. It would not move. I felt hot. I stomped. I threw the spool. Now I feel worse.”

Milo took a slow breath. He smelled the soft soap. He listened to the drip, drip, drip. “When I feel mad,” he said, “I stop, I drop my shoulders, I breathe in and out. I tell a story to my tummy. Would you like to try with me?”

Tilly blinked. “Tell a story… to your tummy?”

Milo smiled. “Yes. My tummy likes calm stories. Stories help my feelings move and not get stuck.”

Tilly sniffed. “Okay.”

Milo sat on a warm dryer and patted the spot beside him. The hum felt like a purr. “Let’s make our bodies comfy,” he said. “Feet on the floor. Hands on bellies. Inhale through your nose—smell the soap. Exhale through your mouth—blow like you blow a dandelion.”

They breathed together: in, out… in, out. The hum of the dryer soothed them. The cool air brushed their whiskers and skin.

Milo began in a calm, soft voice. “Once there was a tiny boat on a quiet pond. The boat felt wobbly. A small wind blew. The boat said, ‘I am safe. I can float.’ The wind slowed down. The boat smiled. The end.”

Tilly smiled a little. “My tummy liked that,” she said. “I feel less hot.”

Milo nodded. “Stories can be keys. They open calm.”

Tilly peered under the shelf. “But my marble is still stuck. I might get mad again.”

Milo’s whiskers twitched. “We can be smart and kind to our feelings. We can try a new way. Let’s invent a helper!”

“Invent?” Tilly asked.

“Yes,” said Milo. “We will make a Calm-Down Clock. It will tick-tock slow to help us breathe. And it can be a gentle tool to help us wait before we act.”

Milo opened his string backpack. “I have a shiny button, a paper clip, and tape.” He looked around the busy basement. “And there is a plastic cup. And some yarn. And… ooh! A sticky note.”

They gathered the little things. The beetles brought a tiny screw. A moth fluttered down with a bit of ribbon. Everyone wanted to help.

Milo told the plan. “We stick the button in the center of the cup lid. We bend the paper clip to make a hand. We draw numbers 1 to 5 on the sticky note and tape it to the lid like a circle. Then we tie the ribbon to the paper clip. When we pull the ribbon slow, the clip hand moves. We count five slow breaths. Tick… tock… calm.”

Tilly clapped. “Let’s build!”

They worked with gentle hands. Tape went squeak. Ribbon went swish. The paper clip clicked as it bent. The washer hummed like a song. Soon the little clock was ready.

Milo held it up. “Now, when we feel big mad, we touch the Calm-Down Clock. We pull the ribbon slow and breathe five times. After five, we can try a new idea.”

Tilly’s eyes sparkled. “Can we try it now? I feel a little mad about my marble.”

“Let’s do it together,” said Milo.

They placed the clock between them. Milo said, “Pull,” and Tilly pulled the ribbon, slow, slow. “One,” they breathed in. “Blow,” they breathed out. “Two… three… four… five.”

Tilly’s shoulders dropped. “I feel ready,” she said.

Milo looked at the shelf. “Now we try a new plan. Not kicking. Not throwing. Sharing ideas.”

The spider twins crawled over. “We can make a lever,” they chirped. “We have a flat stick!”

Milo smiled. “Great idea.” He slid the flat stick under the shelf edge. Tilly placed a small block as a fulcrum, just like in one of Milo’s stories. Together, with a gentle push, the shelf lifted a tiny bit. The marble rolled free—plink!

“My marble!” Tilly cheered. “We did it!”

Milo grinned. “You did it with calm, story, and a smart plan.”

Tilly hopped in a happy circle. Then she stopped and looked at the wooden spool. She picked it up and set it back by the paint can. “I want to fix my mess,” she said. She turned to the beetles and moths. “I am sorry I scared you when I kicked. Next time, I will use our Calm-Down Clock and tell my tummy a story.”

The beetles clicked kindly. The moths fluttered like tiny hugs. Milo’s heart felt warm.

Tilly said, “Let’s share our clock with everyone. We can keep it on the shelf for all friends.”

Milo nodded. “Sharing helps the whole basement feel safe.”

That night, Milo told a new bedtime story to the moths. “Once there was a tiny boat who learned to breathe and wait,” he began. “The boat found help from friends and from a small clock. The boat floated strong.”

The basement hummed, soft and steady. The air smelled sweet and clean. Tilly tucked her marble in a little box and whispered, “Thank you.”

Milo whispered back, “When feelings get big, we can breathe, tell a story, and try a new idea. We can be kind.”

The End.
