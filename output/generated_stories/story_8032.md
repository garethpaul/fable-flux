# Tiko’s Big Drum Day

In the musical practice room, the air smelled like polished wood and sweet orange oil. Soft light fell on shiny drums, jingly bells, and bright ribbons. A gentle elephant calf named Tiko stood by a big round drum. His ears fluttered. His small tail swished. His heart thumped, thump-thump, like a tiny drum inside him.

Today was sharing day. Everyone would share a song from their family. The room was quiet except for the creak of chairs and a little hum from the air vent. Tiko’s trunk trembled. He loved his family’s drum song, but he felt shy.

“Good morning, friends,” said Miss Lila, the music teacher. Her voice was warm like a soft blanket. “We will take turns. We will be brave. We will share our traditions. Traditions are special songs and ways we do things in our families.”

Tiko touched the smooth drum skin with his trunk. Tap. It felt cool and stretchy. He whispered, “Can I be brave?” The drum answered with a tiny echo, tap-tap, like it was saying, “Yes.”

First, Maya shook little bells from her auntie’s dance. “Tinkle-tinkle!” She smiled. “My family rings bells when we welcome guests.” Everyone clapped. Clap, clap, clap.

Then, Ben strummed a small ukulele. “Plink-plonk,” it sang. “My grandpa plays this on sunny mornings,” Ben said. The notes hopped like little frogs. Everyone clapped again.

Tiko watched and listened. He smelled warm dust, orange oil, and a hint of chalk. He felt the floor’s smooth wood under his round feet. He wanted to share. He also wanted to hide behind the drum.

Miss Lila looked at Tiko with kind eyes. “Tiko, would you like a turn?”

Tiko took a deep breath. He felt the breath flow cool in his trunk. “I… I think yes,” he said, very softly.

“You can do it,” Maya whispered. “We will listen.”

“We will take turns,” Ben added. “Now it is your turn.”

Tiko stepped forward. His ears wiggled. “My family has a drum song,” he said. “My grandma taught me. We play it at the first moon of the month. We say, ‘Boom for the heart, boom for the home.’”

Miss Lila nodded. “That is a beautiful tradition, Tiko. Thank you for sharing.”

Tiko lifted two soft drum sticks. The wood felt smooth in his hands. He tapped the drum gently. Boom. The sound was deep and warm, like a hug. He tapped again. Boom-boom. He felt it in his tummy.

He began the pattern Grandma had shown him. “Boom… pause… boom-boom… clap!” Tiko clapped his hands once. Clap! The room echoed. He spoke softly, “Boom for the heart, boom for the home.” His voice wobbled, but the drum stayed steady.

Maya smiled. Ben swayed. Miss Lila closed her eyes and listened. Tiko felt braver. He added a soft rub with his trunk along the drum’s rim. Rrrrr. It sounded like a purr.

“I will teach you the words,” Tiko said. “Please say them with me when it is your turn.” He looked around. “We will take turns leading the line.”

Miss Lila said, “What a kind idea. Thank you for sharing your tradition and for inviting us.”

Tiko pointed to Maya. “Maya, your turn to say the words.” Maya said, “Boom for the heart, boom for the home,” and Tiko played the drum. Boom… boom-boom… clap! Then Tiko pointed to Ben. Ben said the words. Tiko played the beat. Boom… boom-boom… clap!

Soon, the whole class took turns. Each child led the words once. Their voices were soft, then strong, then giggly. The drum kept them together. Boom… pause… boom-boom… clap! The room felt full of music and smiles. Tiko’s cheeks felt warm and proud.

“May I try the drum?” Ben asked.

“Yes,” Tiko said. “We will take turns with the drum too. I will help.” He handed the sticks to Ben. The wood clicked. Tiko stood close and whispered, “Slow and steady. Listen to your heart.”

Ben played. Boom… boom-boom… clap! It was not perfect, but it was happy. Maya tried next. Boom… boom—oops! She giggled. “I slipped!”

“That’s okay,” Tiko said. “Traditions can be gentle. We try again.” He showed her how to tap soft, then strong. They both smiled. The beat came back. Boom… boom-boom… clap!

Miss Lila said, “I hear bravery. I hear sharing. I hear friends taking turns.”

Tiko felt big inside, like a bright balloon. He had been brave. He had shared his family’s song. He had helped his friends take turns. The drum was not just his now. It was part of the class.

At the end, Tiko touched the drum and whispered, “Thank you, Grandma.” He could almost smell Grandma’s spice tea and hear her laugh. He looked at his friends and said, “When we share our traditions, we share our hearts.”

Miss Lila nodded. “Yes, Tiko. Today we learned that sharing traditions helps us understand each other. Being brave helps us speak. Taking turns helps us listen.”

Everyone breathed in and out together. The room felt warm and safe. Tiko’s trunk curled in a happy hug around the drum. His heart went boom in a calm, steady way.

The End.
