# Ellie Tells the Turn-Taking Tale

Drip, drip, drip went the rain on the roof of the little garden shed. The air smelled like wet earth and sweet grass. Inside, a gentle elephant calf named Ellie stood on soft straw. Her ears were big and floppy. Her trunk curled like a question mark.

Ellie loved stories. When the rain came, she liked to tell stories to her friends. Today, her friends were here in the cozy shed: Pip the mouse, Dot the duckling, and Buzz the bumblebee. A lantern glowed warm and yellow. A wooden box held crayons. A neat sign on the wall read, “Safe and Calm.” Ellie had painted it with her trunk.

“Let’s tell stories,” Ellie said softly. Her voice sounded like a friendly hum. “We can take turns.”

“I want to go first!” squeaked Pip, hopping on the box.

“Quack-quick! I want to go first!” peeped Dot, flapping tiny wings.

“Bzz-bzz-now!” buzzed Buzz, zooming in a loop.

Ellie’s ears drooped a little. All her friends wanted the same thing at the same time. The shed felt noisy and wiggly. The lantern flickered.

Ellie took a slow, deep breath. In through her trunk. Out with a soft whoosh. She remembered the “Safe and Calm” sign. She remembered to be gentle and also brave.

“We can take turns,” Ellie said, a bit louder but still kind. “I will tell the first story. Then Pip. Then Dot. Then Buzz. I will make a turn circle.” She dipped her trunk into a jar of chalk dust and drew a big circle on the floor. She added little pictures: an elephant, a mouse, a duckling, and a bee.

Pip’s whiskers twitched. “Okay,” he squeaked. Dot waddled closer. “Quack, okay.” Buzz landed on the circle and wiggled.

Ellie smiled. “Thank you for listening. I will start.” She sat on a soft mat and felt the straw tickle her feet. Rain pattered like tiny drums above them.

“My story is called ‘The Brave Little Seed,’” Ellie said. “Once, a little seed lived under the soil. It was dark. It was quiet. The seed felt small.” Ellie made her voice small. “The seed heard the rain. Drip, drip. The seed felt the sun. Warm. The seed said, ‘I will grow,’ in a tiny, brave voice.”

Pip leaned in. Dot tucked her head close. Buzz hummed softly.

“The seed grew into a sprout,” Ellie continued. “It poked up to see the sky. Hello, blue! Hello, clouds! The sprout stood tall. When wind came, whoosh, the sprout said, ‘I can bend and not break.’ When a big foot came near”—Ellie paused and glanced at the door—“the sprout said, ‘Please step around me.’ The foot listened. The sprout grew into a flower. It was yellow like our lantern. It smiled with petals. The end.”

Pip clapped his tiny paws. Dot quacked. Buzz buzzed a happy buzz.

Ellie bowed her head. “Thank you. Now it is Pip’s turn.” She pointed her trunk to the mouse picture in the chalk circle.

Pip stood up on the wooden box. “My story is ‘The Cheese Moon.’ Once, the moon was cheese. It smelled… like cheese.” He sniffed the air with a silly face. Everyone giggled. “I built a ladder out of twigs. Clack, clack. I climbed up. My paws went pat, pat. A breeze said, shhh. I took one tiny nibble—just a nibble, not too much—and I came back down to share.” Pip smiled proudly. “The end.”

Ellie nodded. “Great sharing,” she said. “Now it is Dot’s turn.” She tapped the duckling picture.

Dot waddled to the circle. “My story is ‘Puddle Princess.’ I found a big puddle. Splash!” She hopped and splashed a little on the straw. “I asked my friends, ‘Do you want to splash too?’ They said, ‘Yes!’ We took turns. Splash, splash, splash. We stayed near the shed, away from the deep ditch. Safe and fun. The end.”

“Bzzzt! My turn!” said Buzz, zipping up to the bee picture.

Buzz buzzed softly. “My story is ‘The Little Light.’ The sun hid. The world felt gray. I made a warm hum. Mmm. I found a flower like a tiny lamp. It glowed gold. I showed my friends. We stayed together. We watched the light and felt safe. The end.”

Ellie’s heart felt full. “We did it! We told our stories and took turns.”

Just then, the shed door wiggled in the wind with a creak. The lantern flickered again. Dot startled. Pip squeaked. Buzz zipped.

Ellie took another deep breath. “We are safe inside,” she said calmly. “The door is closed. The lantern is high. We can sit together.” She pointed to the “Safe and Calm” sign. “When we feel worried, we can say our plan.”

“What is our plan?” Pip asked, ears up.

Ellie spoke clearly, a little braver. “We stay together. We use gentle voices. We take turns to talk. If we need help, we call for the gardener.”

Dot nodded. “Quack-okay.”

Buzz hummed, “Bzz-okay.”

Ellie added, “And if you want to tell a story, you can say, ‘I have a turn, please.’ That is being assertive. It is using your strong, kind voice.”

Pip stood tall. “I have a turn, please,” he practiced.

Dot said, “I have a turn, please.”

Buzz buzzed, “I have a turn, please.”

Ellie beamed. “Wonderful! We can listen. We can speak up kindly. We can be safe.”

The rain slowly stopped. Sunlight slid through a window and warmed the straw. The shed smelled like sunshine and rain mixed together—fresh and happy.

Ellie rolled up the chalk circle with her trunk like a ribbon. “Let’s go see the puddles,” she said, “and we will take turns jumping.”

They stepped outside together, one by one. Pip, then Dot, then Buzz, then Ellie. They each had a turn. Splash, splash, splash! Laughter rang like bells.

Ellie whispered, “Stories help our hearts feel brave. Taking turns helps our friends feel seen. Using our kind, strong voices keeps us safe.”

Everyone smiled.

The End.
