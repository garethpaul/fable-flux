# Rooftop Rhymes with Roo

On a sunny morning, a joyful kangaroo joey named Roo hopped up the stairs to the rooftop. “Boing, boing!” went Roo’s feet. The rooftop garden smelled like fresh mint and warm bricks. A soft breeze tickled Roo’s ears.

Roo carried a little blue notebook. “Today I will write a poem,” Roo said, patting the notebook. “I will write about this beautiful sky.” The sky was big and blue, and a few puffy clouds floated by like marshmallows.

Roo sat on a cozy mat next to a small water sink and a box of chalks. Roo’s tail swished happily. “Tap, tap,” went the pencil as Roo thought. Roo whispered, “What rhymes with Roo? Roo, blue, chew—”

Just then, Roo heard tiny footsteps. A shiny bird with rainbow feathers landed on the rooftop rail. “Hello!” chirped the bird. “I’m Pip.”

“Hi, Pip! I’m Roo,” said Roo. “Do you like poems?”

“I love poems,” chirped Pip. “Can I help?”

“Yes!” Roo grinned. “We can write together. But first, I should wash my paws.” Roo looked at the sink and the soap but felt too excited to start writing. “Maybe later,” Roo said, opening the notebook.

Roo wrote, “Blue sky for Roo.” The pencil felt smooth, and the page smelled like paper and sunshine. Roo smiled and licked the tip of the pencil—just a tiny lick—to make the words darker. “Mmm,” Roo said, “now it writes better.”

Pip tilted his head. “Uh-oh, Roo. Licking the pencil is not clean.”

Roo blinked. “Not clean?”

“Germs can hide on things,” Pip chirped. “We can’t see them, but they can make tummies hurt. Washing paws keeps you healthy.”

Roo looked at Roo’s paws. They had chalk dust and a little dirt from hopping. “Oh,” Roo said softly. Roo’s tummy made a tiny grumble, like a little drum.

Pip pointed with a wing. “There’s soap and a towel! We can make washing fun.”

Roo nodded. “Okay. Let’s learn.” Roo stood at the sink. The water shushed like a tiny waterfall. Roo wet their paws, added a small blob of soap, and rubbed. “Rub, rub, rub,” sang Roo. “Rub the palms. Rub the backs. Rub between the fingers. Rub the thumbs. Rub the wrists.”

Pip chirped a beat. “Scrub for a bubble song! Twenty bubbles long.”

Roo laughed. Bubbles smelled like lemons and felt slippery. “Pop, pop,” went the bubbles. Roo rinsed with warm water and dried with the soft towel. Roo’s paws felt clean and fresh.

Roo looked proud. “Clean paws are happy paws,” Roo said.

Pip clapped his wings. “Now we can write. And remember, no licking the pencil.”

Roo giggled. “No licking. Got it.”

They went back to the mat. Roo touched the clean pencil to the clean page. It slid just right. “Dear Sky,” Roo said aloud. “Your blue is true.”

Pip added, “And clouds float like stew—oops! Clouds don’t taste like stew.” They both laughed.

Roo wrote their poem slowly. The rooftop smelled like mint again, and the breeze brushed Roo’s fur. Roo’s ears wiggled with joy. “Listen, Pip,” Roo read. “Blue sky for Roo. White cloud or two. Clean paws help me write my poem true.”

Pip chirped, “That’s lovely!”

A small door opened, and two neighbors peeked out. One was Mia, who wore purple glasses and loved crayons. The other was Tiko, who used a shiny walker with tiny bells. “Hi, Roo!” Mia waved. “We heard your poem.”

Tiko’s bells jingled. “Can we write too?”

“Of course,” Roo said. “Everyone can write. We can share the mat.”

Mia sat and smiled. “I bring crayons,” she said. “But first, clean hands.” She went to the sink and washed, singing, “Rub, rub, rub!”

Tiko rolled to the sink and rinsed too, using a pump soap. “I do it my way,” Tiko said. “Different ways are okay.”

Roo nodded. “We all do it our way, and we all keep clean.”

Soon, the rooftop was full of gentle sounds: “Scratch, scratch,” went the pencils. “Swish, swish,” went the crayons. Pip chirped a soft tune. The mint leaves rustled. A cloud made a cool shade.

Mia wrote a silly line. “My cat says mew. He likes to chew—” She stopped and giggled. “But not pencils!”

“Not pencils,” Roo said. “Chew carrot sticks, not pencils. It’s clean and safe.”

Tiko wrote about his walker bells. “They ring when I move. Ding-ding for me, ding-ding for you.” He smiled. “We’re all different, and that’s great.”

Roo smiled wide. “Let’s make a Rooftop Rhyme Party.” Roo hopped to a little table and set out a bowl of clean carrot sticks and a pitcher of cool water. “First, we wash. Then, we write. Then, we snack!”

Everyone washed their hands again before the snack. The water felt cool, the towel felt fluffy, and the carrots crunched like tiny drums. “Crunch, crunch,” went the carrots. “Ahh,” went everyone after sips of water.

Roo stood tall and read the poem: “Blue sky for Roo. White cloud or two. Clean paws help me write my poem true.” Pip and friends clapped. The bells jingled.

Roo beamed. “Today I learned that washing hands keeps me healthy and helps me write better. Clean hands, happy hearts!”

Pip nodded. “And we discovered that different ways of moving and seeing are wonderful.”

Mia cheered. “We celebrate our differences!”

Tiko grinned. “And our clean hands!”

Roo hugged the little blue notebook. The sun warmed Roo’s fur, and the breeze felt like a soft hug. “Let’s keep writing,” Roo said. “And let’s keep washing, too.”

Everyone smiled and began a new rhyme, with clean hands and joyful hearts on the rooftop.

The End.
