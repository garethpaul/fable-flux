# Ollie Owl and the Sudsy Surprise

In a small, bright laundry room, a wise old owl named Ollie lived on a wooden shelf above the washing machine. His feathers were soft and speckled like snowflakes on wood, and his eyes were big and kind. The room smelled like clean soap and warm cotton. The washing machine hummed, “Whirr-whirr,” and the dryer clicked, “Tap-tap.”

One sunny morning, Ollie blinked and stretched his wings. “Hoo-hoo! Time to help,” he said. Ollie loved to help the neighborhood at night by hooting softly if a door was left open or if lights were still on. He cared about keeping things safe and clean. That was his civic duty, and it made him happy.

On the floor sat a small basket of tiny shirts and socks. A little sign on the basket read, “Please wash me.” But there was no one else in the room. The family was busy outside, planting flowers. Ollie tilted his head. “Hoo, a problem,” he murmured. “Dirty clothes, no helpers. Can I be self-reliant? Can I do it myself, safely?”

He hopped down to the counter and looked at the washer. The buttons were big and shiny: Start, Stop, Cold, Warm. The soap bottle smelled like lemons. He read the picture chart on the wall. It showed four steps with drawings: put clothes in, add soap, close door, press start. The pictures had little smiles on them. “This is helpful,” Ollie said. “I can follow steps.”

He spread his wings and flapped gently. “First, think. Then, do,” he reminded himself. “Hoo-hoo. That’s good problem-solving.”

Ollie listened to the laundry room: drip, drip from a faucet; buzz, buzz from a light. He looked at the small basket again. “I want to help. I will try. If I get stuck, I will stop and think.”

He opened the washer door with his beak. Click! The round door swung open. Cool, metal air touched his feathers. “Brrr,” he said with a tiny shiver. He picked up a tiny sock with his claws. “In you go!” Plop. He added shirts and more socks. “Plop, plip, plop.” He giggled. “Laundry sounds like raindrops.”

Next was the soap. The bottle said, “One small cap.” Ollie nodded. “Not too much. Too much will make too many bubbles.” He carefully tipped the bottle and filled the cap halfway. “That is small for a small load,” he said, proud and careful. He poured the soap. “Glug-glug.” The lemon smell floated up. “Mmm, fresh!”

He closed the door. Click! He pecked the “Cold” button. “Cold is kind for colors,” he said. He tapped “Start.” The washer began to swirl. “Whirr, whirr, swish!” Water moved like a little river. Ollie smiled. “I did it!”

But then—uh-oh! A drip slid down the side of the soap bottle. Drip… drop. It made a tiny, slippery puddle on the floor. “Oh dear,” said Ollie, seeing the shiny spot. He stepped closer and slid a little. “Whoops!” His wings fluffed. He did not fall, but his heart thumped. “I must keep the room safe and clean, for me and for others.”

He took a deep breath. “When there’s a problem, I don’t run. I think.” He looked around. He saw a small towel hanging by the sink. It had little stars on it. “Perfect.”

Ollie spoke softly to himself. “Step one: get the towel. Step two: wipe the spill. Step three: dry the floor. Step four: put the towel in the wash later.” He smiled. “Hoo-hoo, I can do this.”

He grabbed the towel with his beak. “Mmf!” He pulled it down and laid it over the puddle. “Pat, pat, pat,” he said, pressing gently with his feathery toes. The puddle soaked into the towel. He felt the floor. “Not slippery now.”

He hung the towel on the edge of the basket to remind himself to wash it next. “That’s good hygiene,” Ollie said. “Clean and safe for everyone.” He checked the soap bottle and wiped the drips from the cap. He set it upright. “There. No more drips.”

The washer hummed a happy song. The window fogged a little with warm mist. “Swish-swish, round and round,” Ollie sang softly. He watched the clothes dance and felt proud. He had started the wash. He had fixed a spill. He had kept the room safe.

Soon, the washer beeped, “Beep-beep!” The door unlocked. “All done,” Ollie said. He opened it, and steam puffed out like a tiny cloud. “Ahh, warm and lemony.” He pulled out a small shirt and shook it gently. “Drippy!” He giggled as drops tickled his toes.

He placed the wet clothes into the dryer. “Plop, plip.” He cleaned the lint screen, just like the picture chart showed. “Clean the lint keeps air happy,” he said. He closed the dryer door. Click! He chose “Low” and pressed “Start.” The dryer went, “Hum-hum, tum-tum.” The room filled with a soft, warm sound.

While he waited, Ollie tidied the space. He put the soap bottle back straight, folded the empty basket liner, and wiped a tiny bit of dust. “When we care for shared spaces, we help our whole home,” he said. “That is civic responsibility. We all share the laundry room.”

The dryer beeped. Ollie opened it. “Foof!” Warm air puffed out. He hugged a tiny sock. “So cozy!” He folded the shirts the best he could—one, two, three folds. They were a little wiggly, but neat. He placed everything in the basket and smiled.

Just then, the family came in. “It smells so clean!” said the child. “Who helped?”

Ollie dipped his head. “Hoo-hoo. I tried my best,” he said.

“Thank you, Ollie!” said the parent. “You worked carefully. You solved problems. You kept the room safe and clean.”

Ollie felt his heart glow warm. “When we try by ourselves, step by step, we can do big things,” he said. “Think first, then do. Clean up spills. Care for our space. That helps everyone.”

He perched back on his shelf. The laundry room was tidy, the basket was full of clean clothes, and the air smelled like lemon and warm cotton. Ollie closed his eyes, happy and proud.

The End.
