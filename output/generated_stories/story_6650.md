# Benny Beaver’s Balcony Plan

Benny was a busy beaver with bright teeth and a big heart. He didn’t live by a river. He lived in a small apartment with a sunny balcony garden. The garden had mint, basil, cherry tomatoes, and a tiny lemon tree in a pot. Bees hummed, leaves rustled, and the air smelled like fresh soil and sweet tomatoes.

Every morning, Benny put on his little yellow safety vest. It had shiny strips that went shhh-shhh when he moved. He clipped on his soft hat. He liked to say, “Safety first, smart beavers think!” It made him smile.

One warm day, Benny had a big plan. “I will build a watering path,” he said. “Water will go from the rain barrel to the plants. No spills. No slips.” He patted the rain barrel. Drip, drip went the tiny tap. He liked to save water. “Saving water is kind,” he whispered to the lemon tree.

Benny rolled out his hose. He placed small stones to make a gentle path. He lined up little cups to measure. “Measure, count, and check,” he sang. The mint leaves smelled cool and happy.

But when Benny tried the tap, the hose went wiggle-waggle. Water splashed his paws. The stones slipped. One cup tipped off the edge. “Clatter!” It bounced toward the balcony rail.

Benny felt a hot bubble of anger rise in his tummy. His tail thumped once. Thump! His cheeks felt warm. He wanted to stomp and shout.

He stopped. He put both paws on his vest. “Pause,” he told himself. “Breathe in. One… two… three.” He breathed out slowly, like a gentle wind. “I can use a calm plan.”

Benny looked at the rail. The balcony was high above the busy street. He thought about safety. “No leaning over,” he said out loud. “No standing on wobbly things. Ask for help if I need it.” He called, “Grandma Hazel?”

Grandma Hazel was a wise, gray beaver. She wore round glasses and soft slippers. She opened the sliding door with a swish. “Yes, dear?”

“My cup fell near the edge,” Benny said. “I feel mad. But I remembered to breathe. Can you spot me while I reach safely?”

Grandma Hazel smiled. “What a careful choice. I will hold your shoulders. You will keep both feet flat. No reaching too far.”

“Safety first, smart beavers think,” Benny said again.

Together, they moved slowly. Benny bent his knees, not his toes. He kept his tummy far from the rail. He used his long grabber tool, not his paws. “Snick!” The grabber picked up the cup. Benny set it in a safe bin. He and Grandma shared a happy nod.

Now Benny needed a new plan. He used his calm brain. “The hose wiggled. The stones slipped. What can I change?”

Grandma Hazel said, “We can make it steady. Strategy is a plan that keeps you safe and helps you win.”

Benny liked that word. “Stra-te-gy,” he said, tapping the syllables on his vest. Tap-tap-tap.

He looked around. He saw soft clips, a short board, and a little sign that said, “No running on the balcony.” He remembered the sun could make the floor warm and slippery when wet.

Benny set rules out loud, like a song:
- “Feet stay on the floor.”
- “No leaning over the rail.”
- “Tools go back in the bin.”
- “Wipe up spills right away.”

He clipped the hose to the rail post, not the rail top. He put the short board under the stones to make a flat path. He made small walls with extra stones to keep cups from rolling. He placed a towel to catch drips. He tested the tap just a tiny bit. Drip… drip… not splish-splash.

“Good strategy,” Grandma Hazel said. “Small steps are safe steps.”

Benny turned the tap a little more. The hose stayed still. The water went along the stone path. “Hello, basil,” Benny said, and the basil leaves looked shiny and green. “Hello, mint,” he said, and the mint smelled like a cool morning.

A bee zipped by. Bzz-bzz. Benny watched with calm eyes. “I will give the bee space,” he told himself. “Bees are helpers.” He took one step back and waited. The bee flew to a tiny flower, then away. Benny felt proud. He had used patience and space to stay safe.

Then a sudden wind came. Whoooosh! The lemon tree wobbled. The little lemons clinked like bells. Benny felt that hot bubble again. “Oh no!” he said. His tail twitched. He wanted to rush.

He paused. “Breathe in. One… two… three.” He held the pot with both paws. “Grandma, please help me tie the tree.”

Grandma Hazel brought soft ties. Together, they tied the trunk to a small stick. Not too tight, not too loose. “There,” Benny said. “Safe and snug.”

At last, the watering path worked. The plants drank just enough. No puddles. No slips. The balcony smelled fresh, like rain and mint tea. The sun felt warm on Benny’s fur, and his vest felt smooth under his paws.

Grandma Hazel poured two cups of cool water. “To safety, strategy, and calm hearts,” she said.

Benny clinked cups with her. “To saving water and helping plants grow,” he added. He looked at the tidy hose, the dry floor, and the happy leaves. He felt peaceful.

He made a little sign with a crayon: “Safety First: Pause, Plan, and Proceed.” He taped it near the door so he could see it every day.

Benny waved to the plants. “See you tomorrow,” he said. “We will use our calm plan again.”

The clear lesson warmed his heart: When things go wrong, pause and breathe. Make a safe plan. Ask for help. Take small steps. That is how busy beavers grow strong and kind.

The End.
