# Dot's Little Fix-It Day

Dot was a gentle deer fawn with soft, speckled fur and bright, curious eyes. One sunny morning, Dot visited Grandpa Pine’s garage workshop. The air smelled like fresh wood and orange soap. Sunbeams made dust sparkle like tiny stars.

Grandpa Pine, a wise old deer with kind eyes, smiled. “Welcome, Dot. Today we will fix a wobbly little wagon,” he said, patting a small red wagon that squeaked when it moved. Squeak, squeak.

Dot’s ears twitched. “I want to help,” she said, standing tall on her small hooves. Her heart felt fluttery and excited.

Grandpa Pine nodded. “We will use tools safely. First, we look and listen.”

Dot took a deep breath. In. Out. She felt the cool floor under her hooves. She heard the soft hum of a fan and the click of a clock. Click, click. “I am calm and ready,” she whispered.

Grandpa Pine put a small blue apron on Dot. “Safety first,” he said. He gently tied her apron and gave her soft ear covers. “These will keep our ears safe from loud sounds.”

Dot smiled. “Thank you.” The ear covers felt snug and cozy.

They looked at the wagon. One wheel wobbled. Wiggle, wobble. The handle squeaked. Squeak, squeak.

Grandpa Pine asked, “What do you notice, Dot?”

Dot leaned close and looked with careful eyes. She touched the handle gently. “The wheel is loose. The handle is squeaky,” she said.

“That is good thinking,” Grandpa Pine said. “What should we do first?”

Dot’s tummy fluttered again. “I’m not sure,” she said in a small voice.

Grandpa Pine nodded. “When we are not sure, we can pause. We breathe. We think.” He breathed in and out with Dot. In. Out.

Dot felt steadier. “First, we stop the wobble,” she decided. “Then we help the squeak.”

“Great plan,” Grandpa Pine said. “Let’s get the right tool.”

Dot stepped to the tool bench. There were shiny tools lined up like a silver parade. She saw a big wrench, a tiny wrench, and a medium one. “Hmm,” Dot hummed. “Which one fits?”

She tried the big wrench. It clanked and slipped. Clank. “Too big,” she said. She tried the tiny wrench. It wiggled and would not grab. Wiggle. “Too small.” She picked the medium wrench and set it on the nut of the wheel. It fit! “Just right,” she said with a happy grin.

“You used smart thinking,” Grandpa Pine said.

Dot turned the wrench slowly. “Lefty loosey, righty tighty,” she whispered, remembering. The nut turned. Twist, twist. The wheel felt steady. Dot gave it a gentle spin. Whirr. “It works!” she cheered.

Her chest felt warm and proud.

“Now the squeak,” Grandpa Pine said. He gave Dot a small oil can. It smelled a little like lemons. “What is our safety rule?”

“Just a drop, and wipe the extra,” Dot said.

She placed one shiny drop on the handle joint. Plip. Then she wiped the extra with a soft cloth. She pulled the handle. No squeak. Smooth and quiet.

Dot’s eyes sparkled. “Grandpa, I did it!”

“You did it,” he agreed. “You noticed. You planned. You were careful. You finished the task.”

Dot looked around the workshop. She heard birds chirping at the open door. She felt brave and steady inside. Then she saw something else. The wagon’s front sticker was peeling. The red star was half off.

“I want to fix the sticker, too,” Dot said.

“That is a little detail,” Grandpa Pine said. “Details matter.”

Dot smoothed the sticker with her hoof. It did not stick. She thought for a moment. “Maybe we clean the spot.” She took a damp cloth and gently wiped. Wipe, wipe. She pressed the sticker again. Press, press. It stayed!

Dot giggled. “Now the wagon looks happy.”

They rolled the wagon across the floor. The wheels went round and round with a happy whirr. The handle felt strong. The star shone bright.

Grandpa Pine clapped softly. “Dot, how do you feel?”

Dot stood tall. She felt the floor, the air, and her own steady breath. “I feel proud,” she said. “When I don’t know, I can stop and think. I can try. I can finish.”

Grandpa Pine nodded. “That is self-esteem. It is the warm feeling when you know you can learn and do.”

Dot’s tail gave a tiny wag. “I am small, but I am strong inside.”

“You are,” said Grandpa Pine. He placed a tiny wooden button in Dot’s hoof. It had a little heart carved on it. “A helper’s token, for safe hands and a brave mind.”

Dot touched the smooth wood. She liked how it felt. “Thank you.”

They put the wagon by the door for the little ones to use later. Dot hung up her apron and ear covers. She checked the floor for loose tools and wiped the bench. “We finish by cleaning up,” she said.

“That keeps us safe,” Grandpa Pine replied.

As they stepped outside, warm sun kissed Dot’s nose. She took one more deep breath. In. Out. The day smelled like pine and lemon and happy work.

“I can do hard things,” Dot said.

“Yes,” Grandpa Pine said with a smile. “One calm step at a time.”

The End.
