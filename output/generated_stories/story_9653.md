# Tiko the Tortoise Plans a Ride

Tiko was a wise little tortoise with a smooth, shiny shell and bright, curious eyes. One sunny morning, Tiko climbed into the family car with Mama, Papa, and their giggly baby, Luli. The car seats were soft and warm. The seat belt clicked like a tiny drum. The air smelled like oranges from Mama’s snack.

“Where are we going?” asked Tiko, peeking out the window with a happy wiggle.

“We’re going to the big blue lake,” said Papa, turning the key. The engine made a purr-purr sound.

Tiko’s eyes grew wide. “What will we see at the lake? Will we see ducks? Will we see boats? How far is it? How long will it take?” He had a hundred little questions bubbling like soup.

Mama smiled. “You are very curious today, Tiko.”

“I feel full of wondering,” said Tiko. “It tickles my tummy.”

The car hummed down the road. Trees swooshed by like green paint strokes. The sun made sparkles on the windows. Luli clapped and said, “Duck! Quack-quack!”

Tiko leaned back. “I want to learn more about going on trips. How do we make a good trip?” he asked.

Papa nodded. “That is a smart question.”

Mama held up a small notebook. “Let’s make a plan together! Planning helps curious minds learn.”

Tiko’s shell wiggled with pride. “I can help! I am small, but I am wise.”

“First,” said Mama, “we think about what we need.” She opened the glove box. It made a soft click. “We need water, snacks, and a map.”

Tiko sniffed the air and pointed. “I smell oranges. I hear the map paper crinkle. I feel my seat belt, snug and safe.”

“Good noticing,” said Papa. “Curiosity plus careful senses is how we learn.”

Tiko held the notebook with his little feet. “Let’s make a list,” he said in a brave, steady voice. “Step one: Buckle up. Step two: Check snacks. Step three: Look at the map. Step four: Ask questions. Step five: Stay calm and try.”

Mama wrote the steps, her pen going scratch-scratch. “What a helpful plan.”

Luli squealed, “Plan! Plan!”

As the car rolled on, a road sign popped up. It was big and green. Tiko leaned forward. “What does that sign say?”

Papa read, “Lake Road, next right.”

Tiko frowned a little. “What is right? What is left?” He felt a tiny worried bubble.

Mama touched his shell. “Right is the way your writing hand points. Left is the other way.” She held up both hands. “See? This hand is right.”

Tiko copied her. He looked at his small feet and laughed. “This foot is right! I get it now.”

“Curiosity helps us find out,” said Papa. “Asking is brave.”

The car turned right. The tires made a soft crunch on tiny stones. Suddenly, Papa slowed down. “Hmm,” he said. “There is a detour.”

“What is a detour?” asked Tiko, tilting his head.

“It means the usual way is closed,” said Mama gently. “We need a new way.”

Tiko felt the worried bubble again. “Oh.” He took a deep breath. “We can use our plan.” He held up the notebook. “Step three: Look at the map.”

Papa passed the folded map back. It felt smooth and cool in Tiko’s hands. He opened it carefully. “I see the lake,” Tiko said. “I see a road that goes around the hill. It is longer, but it still goes to the blue water.”

“Can you guide us?” asked Papa.

Tiko’s heart thumped-thump with excitement and a bit of fear. “I can try.”

“Step five,” Mama whispered. “Stay calm and try.”

Tiko nodded. “Turn left at the big oak tree,” he said. They passed a tree with a wide, friendly trunk. The leaves made a whispery shhh sound.

“Left now,” Tiko said. His voice grew strong. They turned. Luli clapped again. “Quack-quack!”

“Now go straight past the red barn with the moo-cows,” Tiko added. He could smell hay and hear a far-away moo. The car hummed steady. The sun warmed Tiko’s shell. He felt brave and smart.

“Then turn right at the little bridge,” he said, pointing with a tiny foot. Water sparkled under the bridge, and the air felt cool and fresh.

“We did it!” cried Mama as the blue lake came into view, shining like a big glass bowl.

Tiko beamed. “Our plan worked. My questions helped.”

Papa parked. The engine went quiet. The world was full of soft lake sounds—lap, lap, lap. Ducks quacked. A gentle breeze smelled like water and grass.

“I was scared for a second,” said Tiko, “but I asked, I learned, and I planned.”

Mama hugged him. “That is the wise way.”

Papa added, “Curiosity shows the path. Planning makes it clear. Trying makes you strong.”

Luli touched Tiko’s shell and said, “Brave!”

Tiko smiled, full of warm, bright pride. “Next time we go somewhere new, I will ask more questions. I will use my senses. I will make a plan. I can do it.”

They shared oranges and watched the ducks make ripples on the shiny water. Tiko listened to the soft splash and felt the sun on his shell. He felt calm. He felt ready to learn more, tomorrow and the next day too.

The clear lesson of the ride was in Tiko’s heart: Be curious. Make a plan. Try your best. That is how little steps make big journeys.

The End.
