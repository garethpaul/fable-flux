# Dori the Dolphin’s Balcony Garden

Dori was a playful dolphin who loved to splash, sing, and smile. She lived with her family in a tall building by the sea. On their sunny balcony, they had a little garden with pots of mint, tomatoes, and tiny strawberries. The balcony smelled like fresh leaves and sweet fruit. The wind felt soft on Dori’s smooth skin, and the waves below went shhh-shhh like a lullaby.

Every morning, Mama would water the plants. Papa would sweep the floor. Dori would swish her tail and watch. She wanted to help. She wanted to be a helper, not just a watcher.

One day, Dori said, “I want to help with the garden. Can I please have a job?” Her voice was bright and brave. She used her kind words and looked at Mama with hopeful eyes.

Mama smiled. “That’s wonderful, Dori. Thank you for telling us what you need. What job do you think you can do?”

Dori thought. She listened to the leaves rustle. She sniffed the mint. She felt proud for speaking up. “I can water. I can pick ripe berries. I can wipe the pots,” she said.

Papa nodded. “Those are good ideas. Let’s make a plan.”

They looked at the pots together. The tomato plant had yellow flowers. The strawberries were small and green. The mint was bushy and bright. Next to the pots was a small blue watering can with a star on it.

Mama said, “Watering needs patience. Too much water makes roots sad. Too little water makes leaves droop. Can you be patient?”

“I can,” said Dori. She wiggled her fins. “I will go slow.”

Dori tried to lift the little blue watering can. She could lift it, but when she poured, the water came out too fast. Splash! Water spilled onto her nose. The soil turned muddy. The pot looked soggy and heavy.

“Oh no,” Dori said. Her heart thumped. “I made a mess.”

Papa touched her fin. “It’s okay. We learn by trying. What can we do to solve this?”

Dori took a deep breath. The air smelled like ocean and mint. “I need help. I need a way to pour slowly,” she said. She spoke up clearly, like a bell. “Can we make the water come out in little drops?”

Mama’s eyes sparkled. “That is smart self-advocacy, Dori. You told us what you need.”

Papa said, “Let’s be creative.” He found a clean sponge and a small cup. He also found a big spoon with little holes. “Which tool do you think will help you pour slowly?” he asked.

Dori tapped each thing with her fin. The spoon with holes looked fun. “I choose the spoon,” she said.

Dori held the cup and scooped some water. She poured it over the spoon with holes. Drip, drip, drip. The water fell like tiny rain. The soil drank quietly. No splash. No mud.

“I did it!” Dori cheered. “I found a way that works for me.”

All day, Dori practiced patience. She counted slowly. “One, two, three,” she whispered as the water dripped. She checked the leaves. She touched the soil with the tip of her fin. “Just right,” she said. The mint leaves shivered with happiness.

Then Dori saw a tiny snail on the tomato pot. The snail moved very slowly. Its shell was shiny and light brown. “Hello, little friend,” Dori said. “You are patient too.”

The sun was warm. The breeze was gentle. Dori hummed a bubbly song. She wiped the pots with a soft cloth. She picked one red strawberry. It smelled sweet and juicy. She wanted to eat it right away, but she remembered their plan.

“May I taste this?” she asked.

“Yes,” Mama said. “You checked first. That’s good self-advocacy.”

Dori took a tiny bite. The strawberry popped with flavor. “Mmm,” she said. “Thank you, garden.”

The next morning, the soil looked dry again. Dori was excited to help. But the watering can was too far back on the shelf. Dori reached. She could not get it. She felt a little frustrated. Her tail flicked.

She paused. She took a deep breath. “I can speak up,” she told herself. “I can say what I need.”

“Mama,” Dori called. “I want to do my watering job, but the can is too high for me. Can we please put it on a lower shelf?”

Mama and Papa shared a happy look. “You are taking care of yourself and the garden,” Papa said. “Let’s move it.”

They set the watering can on a low stool. Papa drew a simple picture chart with three boxes: a sun for morning water, a hand for wiping pots, and a berry for picking only ripe fruit. Dori helped color it with blue and green crayons.

“I can follow this,” Dori said. “And if I need help, I will ask.”

Each day, Dori did her chores: water with patience, wipe with care, pick with kindness. She felt strong and proud. The plants grew taller. Tiny tomatoes turned red. More strawberries ripened. The balcony smelled like summer.

One evening, Dori looked at the garden and smiled. “I spoke up. I made a plan. I did my jobs,” she said. Her heart felt warm, like sunshine on her fins.

Mama hugged her. “You practiced self-advocacy, self-reliance, patience, and creativity.”

Papa added, “And you helped our home.”

Dori twirled and laughed. “When something is hard, I can use my words. I can ask for what I need. I can try new ideas. I can help!”

The wind went shhh-shhh. The leaves whispered back. The little snail waved its feelers, or maybe it just wiggled. Dori giggled and sang her bubbly song to the growing garden.

The End.
