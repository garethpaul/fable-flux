# Benny Beaver’s Study-Corner Story Plan

Benny was a busy beaver with bright eyes and a warm, wiggly nose. He had a cozy study corner in his den. The corner had a small desk made of smooth sticks, a soft chair with a squishy cushion, and a neat basket of crayons. A little lamp made a gentle yellow glow, like sunshine in a teacup. It smelled like fresh wood and clean paper. It was Benny’s happy place.

“Today,” Benny said, patting his desk with his flat tail, “I will tell a story! But first, I will plan. Then I will practice.” He smiled. “Plan, then practice!”

Benny took out his big paper and his special planning pencil. He made three boxes on the page. “Box one: beginning,” he said softly. “Box two: middle. Box three: end.” His pencil made a soft scratch-scratch sound. It felt good.

Mama Beaver peeked in and whispered, “What are you doing, Benny?”

“I am planning a story,” Benny said. “I want to share it at snack time.”

Mama smiled. “Planning helps your story,” she said. “Practice makes your voice brave.”

Benny nodded. “Plan, then practice,” he repeated.

He closed his eyes and listened. Drip, drop went the little water clock. Rustle, rustle went the paper. Benny’s heart felt steady and calm. He drew a small picture in each box.

In the beginning box, Benny drew a tiny beaver building a little bridge. In the middle box, he drew a wobbly log and a splash. In the end box, he drew a safe bridge with a big, happy sun.

“Now I will tell it with words,” Benny said. He sat up tall in his soft chair.

He cleared his throat. “Once there was a beaver. He wanted to build a bridge. He planned. He practiced. The end.”

Benny stopped. He scrunched his nose. “Hmm. That is too short,” he said. “I need more words. I can practice again.”

His friend Dot the ladybug landed on the lamp. “Hi, Benny! What are you doing?”

“I am practicing my story,” Benny said. “I planned the parts. Now I will tell it better.”

Dot clapped her tiny wings. “I will listen,” she chirped.

Benny looked at his boxes. “Beginning,” he whispered, and started again. “Once there was a little beaver named Ben. He had a study corner. He liked sticks and stories.”

Dot smiled. “I like it!”

“Middle,” Benny said. “Ben tried to put a big log on the bridge. The log was heavy. The log rolled. Splash! Ben got wet. He felt sad.”

Dot gasped. “Oh no!”

Benny put his paws on his chest. He felt his heart thump-thump. “End,” he said. “Ben made a plan. He asked for help. He moved the log slowly. He practiced slow steps. Step-step. Push-push. The bridge was safe! Ben felt proud.”

Dot twirled in the air. “That was better!”

Benny wiggled his tail. “I will practice again. I will add feelings and sounds.”

He told it again, a little slower. “Once there was a beaver named Ben,” he said in a warm voice. “He smelled clean wood. He heard the soft lamp hum. He felt brave.”

He made a deep splash sound. “SPLASH!” He made a slow step sound. “Step. Step.” He made a happy sigh. “Ahhh.”

Mama Beaver came back with a bowl of crunchy apple sticks. “Snack time,” she said. “Are you ready to share your story?”

Benny took a breath. He looked at his plan. He held his paper. “Yes,” he said. “I practiced.”

Mama and Dot sat close. The study corner felt cozy and bright. Benny began, clear and steady.

“Once there was a little beaver named Ben,” he told them. “Ben wanted to build a bridge. He made a plan in three parts. Beginning, middle, end.”

Benny pointed to his boxes. “In the beginning, he felt excited. In the middle, the log went splash, and he felt sad and wet. In the end, he slowed down. He asked for help. He practiced each step. Step. Step. Push. Push. The bridge was strong. Ben felt proud.”

Benny added a gentle lesson. “Plans help us know what to do. Practice helps us do it well.”

Mama Beaver clapped. “I love your story,” she said. “I love your plan, and I love how you practiced.”

Dot buzzed happily. “You told it with sounds and feelings! I could see it in my mind.”

Benny’s cheeks felt warm. He tasted the sweet apple sticks and crunched, crunch, crunch. “Thank you,” he said. “I will keep planning and practicing. Then I will keep telling stories.”

He put his pencil back in the basket and smoothed his paper. The lamp glowed. The study corner looked neat and ready for next time.

Benny whispered, “Plan, then practice, then share.” He smiled a big, beavery smile. “I can do it.”

And from that day on, every afternoon, Benny sat in his study corner. He drew three boxes. He planned his story. He practiced with sounds, feelings, and slow steps. Then he shared with Mama and Dot. Each day, his voice grew braver, his stories grew fuller, and his heart felt proud.

The clear lesson was simple: Planning shows the way, and practice makes us strong. With both, we can tell our stories well.

The End.
