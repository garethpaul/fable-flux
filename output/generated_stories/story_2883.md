# Miko’s Music Words

Miko was a courageous monkey with bright eyes and nimble feet. One sunny morning, Miko stepped into the musical practice room. The room smelled like polished wood and oranges from a little spray. Sunlight made warm squares on the floor. Drums stood in a row. A piano waited with shiny black and white keys. Colorful scarves hung like a rainbow curtain.

Miko’s tail swished. “Good morning, music!” he said.

“Good morning, Miko,” said Miss Lila, the music teacher. She wore jingling bracelets that sang softly when she moved. “Today we will practice new songs and new words. Music is a language too.”

Miko blinked. “Music is a language?”

“Yes,” Miss Lila said with a smile. “Music speaks with sounds and feelings. And words help us share our songs.” She tapped a small sign on the wall. It showed big letters: DO, RE, MI.

Miko liked those letters. They looked friendly. He liked how they sounded, too. “Do, re, mi,” he sang. The piano answered with gentle notes. Ding, ding, ding!

A soft breeze tickled Miko’s ears from the open window. He heard birds outside chirp, “Cheep, cheep!” He whispered, “That is bird language.” He listened to the drum when he tapped it. Boom, boom! “That is drum language.”

Miss Lila clapped a simple beat. “Today, you are my helper, Miko,” she said. “You will open the song books, put the scarves in a neat basket, and say the words on the board.”

Miko stood tall. “I can do it! I am responsible.”

He pushed a small cart with song books. The wheels went squeak, squeak. He lined the books on a low shelf. He smoothed each cover with his furry hand. “Neat and ready,” he said proudly.

Then he gathered the scarves. They felt silky and cool. “Blue scarf, green scarf, red scarf,” he said. He folded them gently and placed them in the basket. Swish, swish. The colors made him smile.

Miss Lila wrote new words on the board: hello, please, thank you. “These are kind words,” she said. “We use them in every language.”

Miko traced the words with his finger in the air. “Hello. Please. Thank you.” He liked how his mouth moved. He liked how the words felt warm and polite.

Soon, children came into the room. There was Lina with quick feet. There was Omar with a big laugh. There was quiet Kiko, who held her hands close.

“Hello,” Miko said, waving. “Welcome to music!”

“Hello, Miko!” the children said. The room buzzed with happy voices.

Miss Lila lifted a drum. “We will dance with words today. When you hear a word, your feet will tell the story.”

She tapped the drum softly. Boom. “Hello,” she said.

Miko stepped forward and made a small bow. “Hello,” he said back. He took two light steps, heel-to-toe. Tap, tap. The floor felt smooth under his toes.

“Please,” said Miss Lila, with a brighter drum. Boom-boom.

Miko opened his hands and showed a kind face. “Please,” he said. He spun once, slowly. His scarf whispered, “Swish.”

“Thank you,” said Miss Lila, with a soft, long drum. Boooom.

Miko placed his hand over his heart. “Thank you,” he said, bowing lower. The children clapped. Clap, clap, clap!

Lina giggled. “I want to try!”

“Me too!” said Omar.

Kiko peeked from behind a chair. She looked shy. Miko saw her small frown and felt a tiny worry in his tummy. He remembered his job. He was the helper. He had responsibility. He took initiative.

He walked over gently. “Hello, Kiko,” he said softly. “Will you dance with me? Please?”

Kiko looked at the floor. “I’m scared. I don’t know the words.”

Miko nodded. “We can learn together. Words are friends. Listen.” He pointed to the board. “Hello. Please. Thank you.” He tapped the letters with a gentle finger. Tap, tap, tap. “We can say them slow. We can sing them. We can dance them.”

He hummed, “He-llo,” like the piano. The piano answered. Ding-ding. Kiko’s eyes lifted, just a little.

Miko held out a blue scarf. “Would you like this scarf, please?”

Kiko touched it. The scarf was soft like a cloud. “Please,” she whispered.

Miko’s smile grew. “Thank you for trying,” he said.

They stood side by side. Miss Lila tapped the drum. Boom. “Hello.”

Miko took a tiny step. Kiko copied. Their feet made gentle taps together. Tap… tap.

“Please,” said Miss Lila. Boom-boom.

Miko made a small circle with his arms. Kiko made the same circle. The scarf went swish-swish.

“Thank you,” said Miss Lila. Boooom.

Miko bowed. Kiko bowed too. Her scarf fluttered like a butterfly. The children cheered. “Yay, Kiko!”

Kiko’s smile bloomed. “Hello,” she said louder. “Please. Thank you.”

Miss Lila clapped her jingling bracelets. “Wonderful! You are speaking the language of kindness. You are dancing your words.”

Miko felt warm and proud. He had helped. He had used words to make a friend feel brave. He had kept the room neat and ready. He had danced with care.

At cleanup time, he said, “Please pass me the books.” The children handed them over. “Thank you,” he said each time. The room became quiet and tidy. The scarves rested in their basket. The drum sat still, like a sleepy heart.

Miss Lila nodded. “Miko, you showed responsibility. You took initiative. And you taught us to love words. When we use kind words, our music grows.”

Miko’s tail curled with joy. “Words can dance,” he said. “Words can hug.”

“Yes,” Miss Lila said. “Words can help.”

Miko looked at Kiko. “Hello, friend,” he said.

Kiko grinned. “Thank you, Miko.”

The End.
