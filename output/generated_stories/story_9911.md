# Rocco’s Rhythm in the Closet

Rocco the raccoon loved music more than anything. He tapped with his paws. He hummed little tunes. He even played the buttons on his vest like tiny drums. Tap, tap, tippy-tap!

One rainy afternoon, Rocco wanted a quiet place to practice a new song. He opened a small door and peeked inside a cozy closet hideaway. It smelled like clean shirts and pine soap. Soft scarves hung like sleepy snakes. A fuzzy blue blanket lay in a basket. It was snug and safe.

“This is perfect,” Rocco whispered. “I will make a gentle rain song.”

He went inside and closed the door with a soft click. The light was dim and golden. Rocco sat on the blanket and set out his small wooden flute, his tiny drum, and two shiny spoons. He wiggled his striped tail and smiled.

Rocco put the flute to his lips. He blew a long, sweet note. Fweeeeee. Then he tapped the drum. Dum-dum. He clicked the spoons. Clack, clack. He tried to fit the sounds together.

But the spoon slipped. The drumstick rolled under a shoe. The flute squeaked. Squeeeeak!

Rocco’s ears drooped. He tried again. Fwee—squeak! Clack—drop!

Rocco felt tight in his tummy. “Grrr,” he grumbled. His paws felt hot. His cheeks felt prickly. “Why won’t it work?”

He grabbed the drumstick and tried harder. Tap-tap-TAP. The spoon clanged. CLANG! It sounded loud and jumble-y. Not like a gentle rain song at all.

Rocco’s eyes felt wet. “I can’t do it,” he said. He stomped his foot. His tail thumped the door. His heart beat fast like a racing drum. Thum-thum-thum.

He remembered Mama’s words: “When your feelings get big, take a slow breath. Name the feeling. Then make a tiny plan.”

Rocco put his paws on his belly. He breathed in for three: one… two… three. He breathed out for three: one… two… three.

“My feeling is frustration,” he said softly. “I feel stuck.”

He looked around the closet hideaway. The scarves shimmered. The blanket felt warm. A little pull-string light dangled above his head. Rocco gave it a gentle tug. Click. The light glowed brighter. He could see his things better.

“Okay,” Rocco said. “Tiny plan. First, find the runaway drumstick.”

He peeked under the shoe. He listened. The closet was quiet except for the soft patter of rain on the window outside. Pit-pat, pit-pat. He spotted the drumstick near a red boot. “Found you,” he said, smiling a little.

Just then, the door wiggled. “Knock, knock,” said a small voice. It was Pippa the mouse, who liked to nap on the scarf shelf. “Is that you, Rocco? The clanging woke me.”

Rocco’s ears drooped again. “Sorry, Pippa. I’m trying to make a gentle rain song, but I keep messing up. I feel so frustrated.”

Pippa climbed down a scarf like a ribbon slide. “I hear your feeling,” she said kindly. “Frustration is hard. What if we make a plan together?”

Rocco blinked. “Together?”

Pippa nodded. “We can take turns. We can make hand signals. We can listen to the rain. We can keep it soft, because I still need a nap.”

Rocco thought. He liked the idea of a plan. He also liked the idea of helping Pippa nap. “Okay. Let’s make a deal,” he said. “I will play softly in the closet for ten minutes. Then I will take a quiet break so you can nap. Is that fair?”

Pippa smiled. “Fair and kind,” she said. “Also, if you play soft, I can hum along.”

They shook on it—tiny paw to tiny paw.

Rocco sat on the blanket again. He put the spoon between two fingers and held it gently. He placed the drumstick on the drum very lightly. He raised the flute to his lips and breathed slowly.

“Let’s listen first,” Pippa whispered. The rain said pit-pat on the window. The scarves swished a little when the house sighed. Rocco closed his eyes and felt his breath go in and out like ocean waves.

“Ready,” Rocco said.

He played one soft note. Fweee. He tapped the drum like raindrops. Tip… tip… tip-tip. He clicked the spoons like tiny pebbles in a stream. Tick… tick.

Pippa hummed a cozy hum. Mmm-mmm. Her hum was warm like cocoa. Rocco matched the rhythm of the rain. Pit-pat… tip-tip… fweee…

The song grew, gentle and smooth. The closet felt like a small, safe cloud. Rocco’s body felt calm and light. He wasn’t hot or prickly anymore. He was steady.

After ten minutes, Rocco stopped. He held up his paw. Pippa gave a thumbs-up with her tiny paw. “Thank you,” she whispered. “Nap time now.”

Rocco nodded. “Break time for me, too.” He wrapped his instruments in the fuzzy blanket so they wouldn’t clatter. He breathed in for three, out for three. He smiled.

Pippa curled up and closed her eyes. “Your rain song helped,” she murmured. “It was like a lullaby.”

Rocco tiptoed to the door. Before he left, he whispered, “When I felt frustrated, I breathed, I named my feeling, and I made a tiny plan. I also made a fair deal with a friend.”

He tapped his chest, gentle-gentle. “I can handle big feelings. I can listen. I can solve problems.”

Outside the closet, the rain still sang. Rocco hummed along as he walked down the hall, light on his paws and proud of his calm, kind heart.

The End.
