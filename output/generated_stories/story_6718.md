# Joey Hops and the Tinker-Time Plan

Joey was a joyful kangaroo joey with bright eyes and bouncy feet. One sunny morning, he peeked into the garage workshop. The big door was open, and warm light spilled onto the floor. Tools hung on the wall like shiny silver fish. The air smelled like fresh wood and orange soap. A small fan hummed, “Whirr, whirr.”

Joey’s tail wiggled. “Wow,” he whispered. “So many interesting things!”

Mama Roo followed behind him. She smiled and touched his ear. “This is the workshop,” she said softly. “We can make and fix things here. It’s a busy place.”

Joey hopped in place. “I want to do everything right now! Hammer! Paint! Buttons! Zzzip!” He zoomed in a circle and almost bumped a tall stack of boxes. The boxes wobbled.

Mama held up a paw. “Pause, little hopper.”

Joey froze and took a breath. “Pause.”

Mama nodded. “Your body is excited. Let’s practice self-regulation. That means we slow down, make a plan, and keep safe.”

Joey looked at his shaking paws. “Slow down… make a plan,” he said, sounding out each word like a song.

Mama pointed to a low table with soft edges. On it were safe things: wooden blocks, chunky screws, a toy screwdriver, big colorful stickers, and a little timer shaped like a red tomato. There was also a basket of cozy ear muffs and a small apron.

“This is your Tinker Table,” Mama said. “You may be curious. That is good. You may be independent. That is good, too. And you may be calm. That is very good.”

Joey slid on the tiny apron. The ties felt smooth under his paws. He tried the ear muffs. “Mmm,” he hummed, “they are soft and snug.”

Mama set the timer down. “First, we plan. Then we do. Then we clean up. Let’s say the Tinker-Time Plan together.”

Joey liked the sound of that. “Tinker-Time Plan!” he chirped.

Mama held up three fingers. “One: Stop and look. Two: Breathe and choose. Three: Try it slow.”

Joey copied her fingers. “Stop and look. Breathe and choose. Try it slow.”

He looked around. The workshop had many sounds: tap-tap from the rain starting outside, whirr from the fan, creak from the stool. The wood smelled sweet. The stickers smelled like bubble gum. Joey took a slow breath in and a slow breath out. “I choose the toy screwdriver,” he said.

He picked up a chunky blue screw and a wooden board with holes. “Twist, twist,” he said. His paws wanted to go fast. His tail swished and bumped the table leg. The screw slipped and clinked to the floor.

Joey’s cheeks felt warm. He stamped one foot. “Grr… I want it now!”

Mama touched his shoulder lightly. “Let’s use our plan. What is step one?”

Joey closed his eyes. “Stop and look.”

He opened them and looked. The screw was on the floor, not far away. He saw his tail swishing side to side. He noticed his tummy was tight.

“What is step two?” Mama asked.

“Breathe and choose,” Joey said. He put a paw on his tummy and took a slow breath in. He let it out like a long whisper. “Hoooo.” His tummy felt softer. “I choose to tuck my tail.” He tucked his tail around his feet like a warm scarf.

“And step three?” Mama whispered.

“Try it slow.”

Joey bent down, picked up the screw, and put it in the hole. He twisted slowly. “Twist… pause… twist… pause.” The screw went in straight. “I did it!” he giggled. His eyes sparkled.

Mama clapped quietly. “You used self-regulation. You helped yourself.”

Joey felt big and proud. He wanted to do more. He picked a block and added stickers. The stickers were smooth and shiny. Peel, press. Peel, press. He imagined the block was a tiny rocket. “Five, four, three, two, one—whoosh!” He moved it through the air. The fan blew a cool breeze on his whiskers. It felt like rocket wind.

He saw a cup of safe, washable paint and a fat brush. His paws tingled. “I want to paint SO BIG!” he said.

Mama smiled. “Let’s check the plan.”

Joey nodded. “Stop and look.” He looked at the table, the brush, the paper, and his apron. “Breathe and choose.” He breathed in. “I choose a small stroke first.” He dipped the brush, tapped it gently, and made a little blue line. Then a yellow dot. Then a red zigzag. “Try it slow,” he sang. The colors made him feel happy and calm.

Soon, his rocket block had bright windows and a smiley face. Joey imagined a garage moon high up on a shelf. “Rocket to the moon!” he said. He hopped once, twice—but paused. He put a paw on his tummy and checked. “Body ready? Yes.” He walked, not hopped, and set the rocket on the low shelf. “Safe landing,” he whispered.

The timer went, “Beep-beep!” Joey blinked. “Is tinker time done?”

Mama nodded. “Yes. What is next?”

“Clean up,” Joey said. He put the brush in the water cup. Swish, swish. He stacked blocks. He peeled one sticker off the floor. He folded his apron. His tail stayed tucked and calm.

Mama hugged him. Her fur was warm. “You were curious. You were independent. And you used self-regulation.”

Joey smiled. “When I feel fast inside, I can stop, breathe, and try it slow.”

Mama winked. “That is your superpower.”

Joey looked at his rocket and the neat table. He felt proud, like sunshine in his chest. “Tomorrow,” he said, “I will use my Tinker-Time Plan again.”

The End.
