# Bravery Boots for Momo

Momo was a clever cat with bright, curious eyes and soft, striped fur. He loved to explore the garage workshop. It smelled like warm wood, orange peel soap, and a little bit like oil. Sunlight made shiny spots on the floor. Hammers hung on hooks. Nails slept in little jars. A big blue toolbox sat like a treasure chest.

One morning, Momo heard a tiny creak. Then—crash! A gust of wind blew the side door open. A wobbly shelf shook. A small wooden birdhouse tumbled down and broke into two pieces on the floor.

“Oh no,” Momo whispered, his whiskers twitching. “That little house needs help.”

Momo took a deep breath. The garage felt extra big and echo-y. Tap-tap-tap went a loose board in the ceiling. Vrrr went a distant drill. The sounds made Momo’s ears flick. He felt a little scared. He wanted to run to his cozy bed. But his heart said, Be brave, Momo. The birdhouse needs you.

Momo sat tall. “I can try,” he said out loud. “I will fix it the honest way. I will do my best.”

First, Momo looked closely. He picked up the roof piece and the box piece. He nuzzled them together. “They fit! But they wobble,” he said. He found wood glue on a shelf. It smelled sweet and sticky. He read the label slowly, sounding out the words he knew. “A bit of glue… then press… then wait,” he murmured.

He dabbed just a tiny bit. “Not too much,” he said. “I will be careful and true.” He pressed the pieces together. Squeeze. He counted, “One… two… three…” The house felt less wobbly.

“But I need clamps,” Momo said, looking around. He saw two small clamps on the bench. He clipped them on carefully. “Click. Click.”

Suddenly, the wind blew again. Whoosh! The side door banged. The noise made Momo’s tail puff up. The tall ladder near the door shook. One leg slipped. Thump. It leaned closer to the birdhouse.

Momo’s eyes grew wide. “I am scared,” he whispered. “But I can be brave.” His paws were a little shaky, but he stood strong. “Safety first,” he said. “I will fix the ladder before I go on.”

Momo padded over. The ladder felt cold and smooth under his paw. “Hello, ladder,” Momo said softly. “Let’s make you steady.” He checked the floor. He found a flat piece of wood and slid it under the short leg. He tested it with a careful paw. “Steady now,” he said. The ladder stood straight. Momo smiled. “Bravery means taking small, safe steps.”

Back at the bench, Momo peered at the birdhouse. “Glue needs time,” he told himself. “I will not rush. I will do it right.” He listened to the tick-tick of the clock and the quiet hum of the fridge in the corner. He breathed in the warm wood smell. His ears settled.

While he waited, Momo had an idea. “This house needs a strong base.” He found a smooth scrap of wood. He measured with a short ruler. He drew a line with a little pencil. Scratch, scratch. He picked up a tiny hand saw. “Slow and safe,” he said. “Paws away from the teeth.” He cut along the line. Zip-zip. Wood dust floated like soft snow. “Achoo!” Momo giggled and brushed his nose.

He sanded the edges with a square of paper. Shhh, shhh. The base felt silky. He dabbed glue and pressed the house onto the base. Clamp. “There,” Momo said. “Strong and steady.”

When the glue was set, Momo checked his work. He pressed gently. No wobble. He looked at a corner. A small crack peeked at him. Momo could cover it with paint and no one would see. He felt a little tug inside.

Momo put a paw on his chest. “I will do the honest thing,” he said. “Integrity means telling the truth.” He found a small note card and a purple crayon. He wrote slowly, “I fixed your birdhouse. There was a crack. I glued it and made a base. It is strong now. From Momo.”

He tied the note to the little porch of the birdhouse with a soft string. He dipped his brush into bright yellow paint and touched up the roof. Swish, swish. The color shone like sunshine. He added one tiny red heart on the side. “For courage,” he whispered.

Just then, the door creaked again, but this time gently. Momo took his brave breath. He stood tall and spoke out. “Hello? It’s safe in here. The ladder is steady. I fixed the birdhouse.”

Grandma Jo, the workshop owner, peeked in. She wore blue overalls and smelled like cinnamon and sawdust. “Momo!” she said with warm eyes. “You were brave to stay and help.”

Momo’s ears perked. “I was scared,” he said. “But I took small, safe steps. I told the truth about the crack.”

Grandma Jo knelt down and felt the birdhouse. “It is sturdy,” she said. “And this note shows your good heart. That is integrity. And this strong base? That is smart building.”

Momo purred, a deep, happy rumble. “Bravery is trying, even when you feel afraid,” he said.

Grandma Jo nodded. “Yes, and integrity is being honest, even when no one is watching. You did both. I am proud of you.”

They hung the birdhouse by the sunny window. Outside, a little bird chirped, peep-peep! Momo’s whiskers tingled with joy. The garage smelled like fresh paint and cinnamon, and the sunlight felt warm on his fur.

Momo curled his tail. “Today, I learned to be brave, to build carefully, and to tell the truth,” he said. “Small steps make big courage.”

Grandma Jo smiled. “That is the best lesson.”

Momo purred again. “I can be brave tomorrow, too.”

The End.
