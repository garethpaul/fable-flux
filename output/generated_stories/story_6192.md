# Brave Cub in the Balcony Garden

Benny was a friendly bear cub with soft, fuzzy fur and bright, curious eyes. He lived with Mama Bear in a cozy home on the third floor. Outside their door was a sunny balcony garden. Pots lined the railing like a rainbow: red tomatoes, green mint, purple flowers, and tiny sprigs of baby trees. Bees hummed. Leaves whispered. The soil smelled rich and warm.

“Good morning, little garden,” Benny said each day. He patted the earth with his big gentle paws. “I will take care of you.”

One breezy afternoon, Benny stepped outside with his small watering can. The can was yellow and had a smiley face. He wiggled his round nose and sniffed. Something smelled strange.

“Hmm,” Benny said. “It smells like… sour and stinky.”

He looked around. “Oh no!” A crinkly plastic bag was stuck in the mint. It was wrapped around the stems like a tight scarf. Bits of shiny wrapper and paper were trapped under the tomato plant. A cup had rolled into the corner by the baby trees.

“Ouch,” whispered the mint. Benny imagined it. “We cannot breathe.”

Benny’s heart thumped. “This is not safe for my garden friends.” He looked over the balcony. A gust of wind had blown trash from the street. Now it was crowding his plants. A tiny ladybug crawled over a wrapper and slipped.

“I will help you,” Benny said. “I am brave.”

He took a deep breath. “Mama!” he called. “Our garden needs help!”

Mama Bear came out with her big soft steps. She sniffed the air too. “You are right, Benny,” she said. “This is a problem. What can we do?”

Benny thought. He tapped his chin. “We can pick up the trash,” he said. “We can keep it out of the plants.”

“That is a smart plan,” Mama said. “Let’s be careful. Use gentle paws.”

Benny put on his little garden gloves. They were blue with tiny green paw prints. He reached for the plastic bag. “It’s okay, mint,” he whispered. The bag rustled, “crinkle crinkle.” He slowly unwrapped it from the stems. The mint sprang up with a soft sigh.

“Thank you,” Benny said. He smiled. He picked up the wrappers and the cup. He found a straw under a leaf. “Sneaky straw,” he said. He put all the trash into a big bin.

Then he saw a small bird feather trapped under a tomato vine. The vine was bent. “Oh no,” Benny said softly. “Little vine, are you hurt?”

He called, “Mama, I need help here.”

Mama Bear held the vine with two fingers. “We can make a gentle brace,” she said. “Like a tiny hug.”

Benny ran to get a stick and a bit of soft string. He tied the vine to the stick with a loose knot. The vine stood up, wobbly but proud.

“You are brave,” Benny told the vine. “You can grow.”

When the balcony was tidy, the garden looked happy again. Bees buzzed “bzz-bzz,” and sunlight warmed the leaves. But Benny still frowned a little. “The trash might come back,” he said. “The wind might blow more.”

He sat down and thought hard. His ears twitched. His nose wiggled. Then his eyes shone. “I have an idea! We can make a sign. We can use art! We can tell people to care for our world.”

Mama Bear nodded. “That is creative,” she said. “Art can teach.”

Benny got paper and paints. He dipped his brush in bright green. Swish, swish. He painted a smiling leaf. He added a happy bee. He painted a big blue earth with a little heart. He wrote, slowly and carefully, “Please keep trash out. Help plants grow!” Mama added a picture of hands putting trash in a bin. They made two more signs together.

Benny taped the signs on the balcony railing where neighbors could see. He also put a little basket on the balcony with a label: “Trash here, please.”

He stepped back. The paint smelled sweet and fresh. The paper fluttered, “flap flap,” in the breeze.

Soon, a neighbor squirrel stopped by. “Hello, Benny!” the squirrel chittered. “I saw your sign. It is very nice. I brought some old apple cores. Where should they go?”

Benny smiled. “We can make compost,” he said. “Compost helps the soil,” he explained. “It feeds the plants.”

The squirrel clapped. “That is smart!”

Later, a pigeon landed on the railing with a soft “coo.” It tilted its head. “Your sign is pretty,” it cooed. “I will tell my friends to drop seeds, not trash.”

Benny giggled. “Thank you, pigeon.”

A few days passed. The balcony stayed clean. Neighbors used the basket. Benny and Mama started a little compost jar with veggie peels and leaves. It made a warm, earthy smell. The soil became darker and soft. The plants grew strong. The tomato vine made tiny green tomatoes. The mint smelled extra fresh.

Benny felt proud. “We fixed the problem,” he said. “We were brave. We cared for our garden. We used art to teach.”

Mama Bear hugged him. “You learned something important,” she said. “Conservation means we protect and care for our home. We use less, we reuse, and we keep nature clean. Even a small cub can make a big difference.”

Benny looked at the shining leaves and the busy bees. He took a deep, happy breath. “I will keep helping,” he said. “I will be gentle with the earth every day.”

The End.
