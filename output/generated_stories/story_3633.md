# Hoot in the Attic

In a quiet attic, under soft beams and dusty sun sparkles, lived a wise old owl named Olive. The attic smelled like old wood and warm sweaters. Tiny motes floated in the light. Tap, tap went the rain on the roof. Rustle, rustle went cardboard boxes.

Olive’s feathers were soft and speckled. Her eyes were big and kind. “Hoo-hoo,” she said, very gently. “Today is a good day to learn.”

Olive looked around. She saw a small toy drum, a red ball, and a long ribbon tied in a bow. She also saw a little door high up on the wall. The little door was shut tight. Behind it, she heard a faint jingle-jangle.

“What is that sound?” Olive wondered. “Is someone there?”

“Help!” squeaked a tiny voice. “I dropped my bell!”

Olive blinked. “Hoo-hoo? Who are you?”

“I’m Pip,” said the little voice. “I am a mouse. My bell rolled into the little door. Now it is stuck.”

Olive spread her wings. “We can solve this problem,” she said. “We will think. We will try. We will not give up.”

Pip’s whiskers twitched from a crack in the floor. “I feel scared,” he said. “I feel small.”

Olive nodded. “It is okay to feel scared,” she said. “Let’s take a deep breath.” Olive breathed in slowly. Pip breathed in too. In and out. Slow and calm. The attic felt softer.

“First,” Olive said, “let’s look.” She walked to the little door. The door was high. It had a round knob. “The knob is too high for you and me to reach from here.”

Pip peeped out. “I can’t climb that high,” he said.

“We need a plan,” Olive said. “What can we use?” She looked around again. She listened to the rain. Tap, tap. She smelled the wood. She felt the ribbon in her wing. It was smooth and cool.

“I have a ribbon,” Olive said. “And here is a drumstick for the toy drum. The drumstick is long.”

Pip’s eyes shone. “We can work together!”

Olive smiled. “Yes. We can include each other. You can watch. You can tell me what you see. I will try to reach. We will use our bodies with care.”

Pip nodded. “I can help with words,” he said. “I can say, ‘left’ or ‘right.’”

Olive wrapped the ribbon around the drumstick. She made a loop. “This is our tool,” she said. “We made it ourselves.”

She climbed onto a sturdy trunk. The wood felt rough under her claws. The attic air was cool on her face. Olive stretched. The drumstick trembled a little. “I feel wobbly,” Olive said. “I will pause.”

Pip called up, “You can do it! Breathe in. Breathe out.”

Olive breathed. Her body calmed. “Thank you, Pip,” she said. “That helps.”

She reached again. “Left?” she asked.

“Left,” said Pip. “Now a tiny bit up.”

Olive moved the loop toward the knob. The ribbon brushed the knob. Swish. “Almost,” she said.

“The bell is still jingling,” Pip said. “Jingle-jangle!”

“We are close,” Olive said. “I will try one more time.” She steadied her feet. She held the drumstick firm. “Teamwork,” she whispered.

“Teamwork!” Pip squeaked.

The loop slipped over the knob. Click! Olive gave a gentle tug. The little door popped open with a soft creak. Creeeak.

A small brass bell rolled out and chimed. Ding-a-ling! Pip clapped his tiny paws. “My bell!”

But then the bell rolled toward the edge of the attic. It rolled fast. Roll, roll, roll!

“Oh no!” Pip cried. “I feel worried again!”

Olive fluttered down, her wings whooshing the dust. “We can solve this too,” she said. “We will think and act with care.” She glanced at the red ball. She glanced at the ribbon. “We can guide the bell.”

“Like a little fence,” Pip said.

“Yes,” said Olive. “You hold one end of the ribbon. I will hold the other. We will stand apart. We will move together. Slow and steady.”

Pip held the ribbon with both paws. Olive held the other end with her wing. They stretched it low like a soft wall. The bell reached the ribbon. It bumped, ting! The ribbon hummed. Olive and Pip stepped in the same rhythm. Step. Step. The bell turned and rolled toward Pip.

“You are doing great,” Olive said. “Keep your eyes on the bell.”

“I feel proud,” Pip said. “I can help!”

“You can,” Olive said. “You are brave.”

They guided the bell to a safe corner. The bell stopped. Pip hugged it. “My bell is back,” he said. His nose twitched with joy.

Olive smiled and patted Pip’s head with a gentle feather. “We solved two problems,” she said. “We looked. We planned. We breathed. We worked together. We moved with care. We did not give up.”

Pip rang the bell softly. Ding. “I learned I can feel scared and still help,” he said. “I learned to say how I feel.”

“And I learned,” said Olive, “that friends of every size can try, can think, and can do.”

The rain slowed. The attic felt warm and bright. Pip and Olive sat together, listening to the soft drip-drop outside. The ribbon lay in a neat loop. The drumstick rested by the drum. The little door stayed open, just a bit.

Olive looked at Pip. “What will we do next time there is a problem?” she asked.

Pip grinned. “We will stop, breathe, look, and make a plan. We will include each other. We will try.”

“Hoo-hoo,” said Olive. “That is the wise way.”

The End.
