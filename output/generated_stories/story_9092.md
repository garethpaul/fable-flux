# Benny Beaver’s Dinner Talk

Benny was a busy beaver. He loved to build, stack, and chew sticks. Tonight, Benny was busy in a different place—the dining room. The room smelled like warm corn bread and sweet apple slices. The wooden table felt smooth under his paws, and the chairs made tiny squeaks as everyone sat.

Mama Beaver set bowls down. “Soup is ready,” she said. Steam curled like little clouds. Papa Beaver passed a basket. “Corn bread for all!” The warm butter melted and made the bread shine.

Benny tapped his tail. He had so many words. He wanted to tell everyone about his big day. He had built a tiny bridge from blocks! He had learned a new song! He had helped a friend!

Benny opened his mouth. “I—”

“Me first!” squeaked his little sister, Bella. “I painted a sun!”

“I have something to say,” said Papa.

“I have something to say,” said Mama.

Benny felt his face get warm. His chest felt tight like a knot. He wanted to talk. He wanted to be heard. His tail thumped softly on the floor. Thump. Thump.

Benny tried again. “I—”

“Oops, sorry,” said Bella, giggling. “I forgot. I want to tell more!”

Benny took a brave breath. He remembered when he learned to dive. He had felt scared then, too, but he had used brave breaths. In and out. Slow and strong. He could do that now. In. Out.

He lifted his paw. “Mama? Papa? Bella? Can we take turns?” His voice was gentle, but it did not shake. He felt brave.

Mama looked at Benny’s raised paw. She smiled. “Thank you for using your words, Benny.”

Papa nodded. “Good idea. Let’s use a talking spoon. Whoever holds the spoon can talk. Then we pass it.”

Bella clapped. “I want to try!”

Papa placed the spoon in Benny’s paw. “Benny, you asked. You go first.”

The spoon felt cool and a little slippery. Benny held it tight. He smiled. “Today I built a bridge. It was small, but strong. I helped Max move blocks. Then we sang a new song—la la la!” Benny hummed a happy tune. The soup smelled like carrots. The corn bread felt soft and crumbly in his mouth. His tummy felt warm, like a cozy hug.

Mama said, “Thank you for sharing, Benny.” She looked proud.

Benny passed the spoon to Bella.

Bella held it up high. “I painted a big sun. It was yellow, and it smiled. I got paint on my nose!” She touched her nose and giggled.

Everyone laughed. Benny laughed, too. “Your sun sounds happy,” he said.

Bella passed the spoon to Mama.

Mama said, “I baked apple slices. I tried a new cinnamon. It smells sweet and spicy. Do you like it?”

Benny took a bite. “Mmm! Yes!” His tongue tingled, and he felt a little dance in his mouth.

The spoon went to Papa. Papa said, “I fixed the wobbly chair. It no longer squeaks.” He gently rocked. The chair moved smooth and quiet.

They kept passing the spoon. They took turns. No one had to shout. No one had to rush. Benny felt calm. His knot melted away.

Then something tricky happened. Bella reached for the spoon before Papa was done. Her paws bumped the basket. Plunk! The basket tipped. Corn bread toppled to the table.

Bella’s eyes got big. “Oh no!” She pressed her lips together. Tears made her eyes shiny.

Benny took another brave breath. He used calm words. “It’s okay, Bella. Let’s fix it together.” He picked up a piece and set it back.

Mama handed Bella a napkin. “Accidents happen. Thank you, Benny, for kind words.”

Bella sniffled and smiled. “Thank you.” She waited with her paws in her lap. When Papa finished, he passed her the spoon.

Bella said, “I will wait now.” She sounded proud, too.

Benny felt cozy and tall inside, like a strong tree. “Talking and taking turns helps us all,” he said.

Papa raised his cup. “To brave voices and good listening.”

They touched cups gently. Clink. The sound was soft and happy.

After dinner, the family played a game. They sat at the table. They used the talking spoon again. They took turns telling silly animal sounds.

“Ribbit,” said Papa.

“Meow,” said Mama.

“Quack,” said Bella.

Benny held the spoon and said, “Roar!” He giggled at his own loud roar. Then he passed the spoon.

Before bed, Mama hugged Benny. Her fur smelled like cinnamon. “I am proud of you,” she whispered. “You used your words. You were brave. You helped us take turns.”

Benny snuggled into his soft blanket. His room felt quiet and safe. He closed his eyes and smiled. He had learned something big at dinner: When he felt a knot in his chest, he could take a brave breath, raise his paw, and use kind words. He could ask for turns. He could listen, too.

And that made dinner sweet, like warm apple slices.

The End.
