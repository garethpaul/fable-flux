# Tully the Turtle Plans the Play Day

Tully was a shy turtle with a smooth green shell. He lived in a busy basement that smelled like crayons and warm laundry. The basement hummed with noises—clink, clank, whirr—and was filled with boxes, bouncy balls, soft blocks, and a bright red wagon. Sunlight peeped through a tiny window and made a warm spot on the carpet.

Today was Play Day. Tully wanted to build a tall block city and have a parade with the red wagon. His tummy fluttered. “I can do it,” he whispered. “I will get ready first.”

Tully took a deep breath. In, two, three. Out, two, three. He made a plan. He drew simple pictures on a sticky note: a broom, a basket, a clock, and a smiley face.

“Step one,” Tully said softly. “Clean.” He swept toy crumbs with a little broom. Swish, swish. He put loose cars in a blue basket. Click, clack. He rolled up a long cord and stuck it to the wall. “Safe and neat,” he said, and his heart felt steady.

“Step two,” he whispered. “Supplies.” Tully stacked soft blocks near the warm sun spot. He filled a small bin with stickers, tape, and a tiny bell. He set a water cup on a table. “For sips,” he smiled.

“Step three,” Tully told himself. “Time.” He set a timer for play and for clean-up. Beep! The little timer light blinked green.

Just then, friends came to the busy basement. Miko the mouse squeaked, “I want the red wagon!” Pippa the puppy wagged and barked, “Blocks! Blocks now!” Benny the bunny bounced so high his ears flopped. “Let’s go fast!”

Tully’s tummy squeezed. The basement felt even busier: thump, wag, squeak, hop. He pulled into his shell a little. He remembered his plan. He looked at his sticky note. Breathe, he told himself. In, two, three. Out, two, three.

“I have a plan,” Tully said in his gentle voice. “We can all play if we get ready.”

Miko tapped the wagon. “I want it first.” His whiskers twitched.

Pippa pawed the blocks. “I want to build a puppy tower!”

Benny bounced to the bell. “Ring, ring!”

Tully felt heat rise in his cheeks. He wanted to shout, “Stop!” But he remembered anger is a feeling, not a boss. He put his head and feet out of his shell and placed a hand on his heart.

“I feel mad,” Tully said slowly. “I want to yell. I will breathe.” He took two slow breaths. In, two, three. Out, two, three. The heat cooled. The basement still hummed, but softer now.

“I can use my words,” Tully told himself. He looked at his friends. “We can share. We can take turns. We can make a deal.”

Pippa tilted her head. “What is a deal?”

“It’s a fair plan,” Tully said. He pointed to the timer. “We can use this. Three minutes for the wagon. Three minutes for the blocks. Then we switch.”

Miko squeaked, “I like turns.” Benny nodded. “A fair plan feels good.”

Tully smiled. “Thank you. First, we prepare.” He handed out little jobs. “Miko, please check the floor for tiny things. Pippa, please bring the block bin to the sun spot. Benny, please place the bell on the table.”

“Team Prep!” Pippa barked. Her tail thumped like a drum.

They worked together. Swish, click, pat. The basement looked safe and ready. Tully’s sticky note had one last picture: a smiley face. He put it on the wall. “Step four,” he said. “Play and be kind.”

The timer beeped. Beep! “Wagon time for Miko,” Tully announced. Miko climbed into the red wagon and held the sides. Tully pulled slowly. Squeak, roll, squeak. “Gentle,” Tully said. “We prepared a path.”

Pippa stacked blocks. “One, two, three,” she counted. Benny stuck a star sticker on top. “Shiny!”

Tully watched the timer. He took sips of water. He checked the path. He felt proud. His plan was working.

Beep! The timer blinked. “Switch!” Tully said. Miko hopped out. “My turn to build,” Miko squeaked. Pippa put her paws on the wagon. “My turn to ride,” she said. Benny rang the bell softly. Ring-a-ling! “Parade time!”

Pippa wiggled so much the wagon wobbled. Tully felt worry tickle his shell. He wanted to say, “No!” But he remembered calm words. He tapped the wagon. “Paws down, Pippa. Safe hands.” Pippa took a breath. “Okay,” she said. She put her paws in her lap. The wagon rolled smooth again.

They played and switched and built and laughed. The block city grew tall. It had a bunny bridge, a mouse house, and a turtle tower. The red wagon parade circled the city. Ring-a-ling! Squeak, roll!

When the last beep came, Tully pointed to the clock picture. “Clean-up time,” he said. They sang a clean-up song. “Blocks in the bin, wheels by the wall, we worked together, good job all!” Their voices echoed in the busy basement. Clap, clap!

Miko squeaked, “Tully, your plan helped.”

Pippa wagged. “Your calm words helped too.”

Benny bounced gently. “The deal was fair.”

Tully’s shy smile grew warm. “Thank you,” he said. “I felt mad at first. I took breaths. I used my words. We made a deal. We prepared. That made play safe and fun.”

They sat in the warm sun spot and shared cool water. Tully touched his sticky note and added a new tiny picture: a heart. “My last step,” he said softly. “Say thank you.”

“Thank you,” they all said together.

Tully looked around the busy basement. It was still full of sounds and things, but now it felt friendly. He felt brave and ready for next time.

“Next Play Day,” Tully whispered, “we will prepare again.”

The End.
