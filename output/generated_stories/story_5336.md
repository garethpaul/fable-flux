# Tully the Turtle Tries First

Tully was a shy little turtle with a soft green shell. He liked quiet places. His favorite place was the closet hideaway. It was cozy and dark, with a soft rug, a tiny lamp that glowed warm like a firefly, and a basket of buttons that clinked like little raindrops.

One morning, Tully peeked out from his shell and whispered, “Good morning, closet.” He could smell the clean cotton of shirts and the sweet soap on the shelf. He could hear the tiny hum of the lamp. He felt safe.

Then he heard voices in the hallway. “Let’s play!” chirped Pip the sparrow. “Let’s build a castle!” squeaked Mimi the mouse.

Tully tucked his head a little. He loved his friends, but sometimes big noise made his tummy feel fluttery.

Pip fluttered to the doorway. “Hi, Tully! Can we play in your closet hideaway?”

Mimi tiptoed in and wrinkled her nose. “It smells nice in here! Like a hug!”

Tully took a small breath. He wanted to play. He also wanted quiet. He remembered something he had been practicing: taking initiative. That meant trying first, speaking up kindly, and helping everyone know what to do.

Tully lifted his head. “I have an idea,” he said, voice tiny but brave. “We can build a quiet castle. We can whisper while we build.”

Pip tilted her head. “A whisper castle?”

Mimi’s whiskers twitched. “I like quiet. But I also like squeaking when I’m excited.”

Tully listened. He nodded slowly. “We can make a plan together.”

He shuffled to the basket of buttons. They clicked and clacked. “These can be shiny treasure,” he said. He reached for a stack of soft scarves that smelled like lavender. “These can be castle walls.”

Pip hopped closer. “What can I do?”

Tully took another breath. Initiative, he told himself. Try first. “Pip, you can hang the scarves. Mimi, you can sort the buttons by color. I will make the floor soft with the rug.”

Mimi squeaked, “Okay!” Pip chirped, “Okay!”

They started to build. Pip hung blue and green scarves like waves. Mimi sorted buttons: “Red, blue, yellow,” she whispered, tapping them into neat piles. Tully tugged the rug into the middle. The lamp made the scarves glow. It looked like a sea-castle.

Soon, Pip forgot to whisper. “Look at this!” she chirped loudly, flapping her wings so the scarves fluttered. Mimi squeaked long and high. The closet felt bigger and louder.

Tully’s tummy fluttered again. He wanted to hide in his shell. But he remembered listening and compromise. He had started the plan. He could help fix it, too. He gently tapped his shell. Tap, tap. “Friends?” he said softly.

Pip paused in mid-flap. “Yes?”

Tully said, “I like our plan. But the sounds are getting big. Can we make a trade? For five counts, we whisper and move slow. Then, for five counts, we can be a little louder. We can take turns.”

Mimi’s ears perked up. “A sound turn?”

Pip smiled. “I can do that!”

Tully smiled back, a little wider. “I will count. Ready? One… two… three… four… five. Whisper time.”

They whispered. Pip placed scarves gently. Mimi placed buttons like tiny raindrops: plink, plink. Tully smoothed the rug. The lamp hummed, and the closet felt like a calm cave.

“Now louder time,” Tully said. “One… two… three… four… five!”

Pip chirped a happy chirp, not too big. Mimi did a small squeak and clapped her paws. Tully giggled, his laugh like a soft tick-tick.

They took turns with quiet and louder, quiet and louder. The castle grew tall and bright. Button treasure sparkled in a tiny bowl. A scarf doorway swished when they walked through. It smelled like clean cotton and lavender and a little like Pip’s feathers.

When they were done, they sat in the middle and looked around. “It’s beautiful,” Mimi whispered, eyes wide.

“It shines!” Pip said, flapping once softly.

Tully felt warm and proud in his shell. He had spoken up first. He had made a plan. He had listened. He had helped them take turns and make a good trade. His tummy was calm.

“Thank you for listening,” Tully said.

“Thank you for telling us your idea,” Pip chirped.

“Thank you for counting,” Mimi squeaked. “That helped me wait. Patience is hard.”

Tully nodded. “Patience is like slow steps. Slow steps still get there.”

They all smiled. They cuddled on the rug. The lamp hummed like a lullaby. The scarves swayed, whispering shh, shh. The button bowl glowed like tiny suns.

Pip said, “What should we do tomorrow?”

Tully took initiative again. “Tomorrow,” he said, “we can make a story show. We can plan with quiet turns and loud turns.”

“Deal,” said Mimi.

“Deal,” said Pip.

Tully felt brave. He was still shy. That was okay. Being shy and taking initiative could live together, like quiet and loud did in their castle. He learned that trying first, listening next, and making kind trades helped everyone feel good.

They turned off the lamp. The closet hideaway felt cozy and safe. Tully tucked into his shell with a happy sigh. “I can try first,” he whispered. “I can listen, and I can wait.”

And his friends whispered back, “We can, too.”

The End.
