# Leo Roars Softly

On a sunny morning, a brave little lion cub named Leo padded onto the balcony garden. Green mint leaves tickled his paws. Tiny tomatoes hung like red beads. Bees hummed a lazy song. The air smelled like wet soil and lemony thyme.

“Today, I will water every plant,” Leo said proudly. He puffed his chest and swished his tufted tail. “I can do it all by myself!”

He found the little blue watering can. It felt cool and smooth in his paws. “Hello, plants,” Leo said. “Get ready for a lovely drink.”

Leo tipped the can. Water sparkled in the sunlight and made a gentle shhh sound. The soil turned dark and soft. A butterfly with yellow wings fluttered over the basil. “Hi, Butterfly,” Leo whispered. “I’m working hard.”

Then a wind puffed. The watering can slipped. Splash! Water poured onto Leo’s paws and onto a tidy pile of pot labels. The labels stuck together like gum.

Leo’s ears got hot. His whiskers trembled. “No, no, no!” he growled. “My labels!” He felt a big ROAR rising inside.

His heart thumped. His face felt like a hot pepper. The roar pushed up, up, up—like a balloon.

Leo froze. He remembered Mama Lion’s calm voice. “When the roar feels too big, use your paws and your breath. Stop. Breathe. Think.”

Leo put the can down gently. He pressed his paws on his chest and belly. “Paws on heart, paws on tum,” he murmured. He breathed in like smelling a flower. He breathed out like blowing a cloud. In… out. In… out. The hot feeling melted into warm honey.

The butterfly landed on the rail. It opened and closed its wings slowly, like breathing. Leo copied. His whiskers relaxed.

“Okay,” Leo said softly. “The labels are sticky. I can fix sticky things.” He blinked and looked around. “I need a plan.”

He used his logical-thinking paws:
- Problem: Wet, sticky labels
- Tools: Sun, cloth, clips
- Steps: Dry, sort, write new ones

Leo spread the labels on a sunny tile. He found cloths in a basket and patted the water. Pat, pat, pat. He clipped the labels to a string with tiny wooden clips. They flapped like little flags.

Just then, a sparrow hopped onto the railing. “Chirp! Chirp!” It pecked at a seed tray and flipped a bit of dirt onto the floor.

“Hey!” Leo snapped. “That’s my seed tray!” His tail twitched. The hot feeling tried to come back.

Stop. Breathe. Think.

Leo looked at the sparrow’s bright eyes and tiny feet. “You are hungry,” he guessed. “You didn’t mean to make a mess.”

He took a slow breath. “I can be tolerant,” he told himself. “I can share a little.”

Leo sprinkled a few seeds in a small dish away from the tray. “Here, little friend. You can eat here.” The sparrow chirped happily and pecked the dish. No more dirt flew. The garden felt peaceful again.

“Good plan,” Leo said, smiling. He wiped the floor with the cloth. Swish, swish. The tiles shone.

Next, Leo checked the tomatoes. “One cup of water for you,” he said. “One cup for you.” Plip, plip. He gave the mint a tiny shower. He smelled fresh minty air. “Mmm! Like toothpaste!”

A ladybug crawled across his paw. Its tiny feet tickled. Leo giggled. “Hello, little helper.”

The labels were dry now. But some letters were smudged. Leo felt a pinch of worry. “How will I know which is which?”

He thought again. “I can forgive the spill. I can start fresh.” He sniffed each plant. “Mint smells minty. Basil smells peppery. Rosemary smells like pine trees.” He held up a label. “This one says M… I… N… T. Mint!” He matched each label by scent and shape. Click went the clips as he fixed them back in place.

Just then, a breeze tumbled a small pot of thyme. Soil sprinkled onto Leo’s fur. He gasped. Then he remembered. Stop. Breathe. Think.

He placed the pot upright and pressed the soil gently around the roots. “It’s okay, little thyme,” he whispered. “I forgive the wind.” He gave the plant a tiny sip. The leaves shivered, then stood tall.

At last, Leo looked around the balcony garden. The seed dish was tidy. The labels were neat. The floor was clean. The air smelled green and bright. Bees hummed a happy tune.

Mama Lion peeked through the doorway. “Leo, how’s the garden?”

Leo’s eyes shone. “I had a big roar inside, Mama. I used my paws and my breath. I stopped. I breathed. I thought. I made a plan. I finished my watering. I shared with a bird. I forgave the wind. And I fixed the labels!”

Mama Lion nuzzled him. “I’m proud of your brave, soft roar,” she said. “That’s called anger-management. You kept your body calm, your mind clear, and your heart kind.”

Leo purred. His chest felt light, like a floating leaf. He took one more flower breath in and one cloud breath out.

He looked at the butterfly, the sparrow, the ladybug, and the swaying plants. “Today, I learned that my roar can be gentle,” he said. “When I stop, breathe, and think, I can be patient, tolerant, and forgiving. Then I can finish my tasks, one by one.”

The balcony garden glowed in the warm sun. Leo curled his tail and smiled. “Tomorrow,” he said, “I will sing to the plants.”

The End.
