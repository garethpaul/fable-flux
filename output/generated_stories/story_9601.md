# The Little Thump in the Attic

Rubin was a small, determined rabbit with soft gray ears and a bright, brave heart. One sunny afternoon, Rubin hopped up creaky wooden stairs to the quiet attic. The door sighed, and the air smelled like old books and clean dust. Soft light came through a little round window, making tiny dust sparkles dance.

“I can do this,” Rubin whispered. His whiskers twitched. “I am brave. I am kind.”

Rubin was looking for a missing button. It was Mama’s favorite shiny blue button, and it had rolled up, up, up to the attic and disappeared. Rubin wanted to help.

He listened. The attic was still. Then—thump.

Rubin’s ears stood up. “Hello?” he called, voice small but steady.

Thump…thump. A box wiggled. A stack of old hats slid and made a soft flop. Rubin took a careful hop. His paws felt the cool wood. He smelled a hint of lavender from an old pillow. He could hear his own breath, slow and brave.

“I am a determined rabbit,” Rubin said. “I won’t give up.”

He peeked behind a trunk. No button. He looked under a fluffy scarf. No button. Thump.

Rubin’s paws shook just a little. He put a paw on his chest. “It’s okay to feel a little scared,” he told himself. “I can be brave and gentle at the same time.”

He thought about Mama’s smile. He thought about how happy she would be if he found the button. He also thought, Maybe something else is up here. Maybe it needs help too.

Rubin took a deep breath—sniff, sniff—and followed the sound. The thump was near a big cardboard box with a picture of a lamp on the side. The box had a small tear. A tiny nose peeked out, pink and quivery.

“Oh!” Rubin said softly. “Hello there.”

A little mouse blinked. Its eyes were shiny and wet. “Squeak,” said the mouse. “I am stuck.”

Rubin’s heart warmed. He forgot about his own shake. He crouched low so his face was friendly and not big. “I’m Rubin,” he said. “I can help. Are you hurt?”

The mouse shook its head. “No. Just scared. It is dark in here. I tried to get out. Thump, thump. But I could not.”

Rubin nodded. “It’s okay to feel scared,” he said. “I felt a little scared too when I heard the thumps. But I am here now.”

He gently lifted the box flap. It made a scritch-scritch sound. Light slipped in. The mouse sniffed the air. “Thank you,” squeaked the mouse. “My name is Miri.”

Rubin smiled. “Hi, Miri. Let’s get you free.”

He held the flap open wide. Miri wiggled out and stood on the wood floor, tiny paws tapping. “You were brave,” Rubin said.

Miri’s whiskers lifted. “You were kind,” she squeaked. “You listened to my thumps.”

Rubin’s ears wiggled with pride. It felt good to help. Then he remembered the shiny blue button. “Miri,” he asked, “did you see a blue circle? Small and shiny?”

Miri tapped her chin. “Yes! It rolled under the old blanket. It went bing and then hush.”

Rubin laughed. “Bing and hush! That’s a good clue. Let’s look together.”

They walked, paw in paw—one soft rabbit paw, one tiny mouse paw—to a pile of folded blankets. Rubin lifted the corner. He felt the fabric, warm and a little dusty. Miri pointed. “There!”

The blue button winked in the light. Rubin picked it up carefully. “I did it!” he cheered. “We did it,” Miri said, smiling.

Rubin’s chest felt big and bright. He had been brave. He had been kind. He had not given up. He had listened when someone else was scared. He had used gentle words.

“Would you like to come downstairs?” Rubin asked. “It’s cozy. There is tea. We can sit by the window.”

Miri looked back at the dark box and then at Rubin’s kind eyes. “Yes, please.”

Rubin led the way to the attic door. The stairs creaked again—creak, creak—but now it sounded friendly, like a song. They went down together. Rubin found Mama in the kitchen. The room smelled like warm cinnamon. Sunlight lay on the floor like a soft blanket.

“Mama!” Rubin said. “I found your button. And I helped a new friend. This is Miri.”

Mama’s eyes shone. “Thank you, my determined rabbit,” she said. “You were brave and caring.”

Rubin’s ears stood tall. Miri tucked close to Rubin’s side and squeaked, “Rubin listened to my feelings. He used gentle words. That helped me feel safe.”

Mama nodded. “Listening is love,” she said. “When we listen and try to understand, we show empathy.”

“Emp-a-thy,” Rubin repeated slowly. “That means I care about how you feel.”

“That’s right,” Mama said.

Rubin gave the button to Mama. She sewed it on with bright blue thread. Stitch, stitch. Miri watched, sipping a tiny thimble of warm tea. Rubin took a little carrot cookie. Crunch! It was sweet and soft.

Later, Rubin and Miri drew a picture. It showed a rabbit opening a box flap and a mouse stepping out into the light. They drew a big heart over both of them.

Rubin wrote, with careful letters, We listen. We help. We are brave and kind.

He taped the picture at the bottom of the attic stairs. When he looked at it, his heart felt calm and strong.

Rubin smiled at Miri. “If you ever feel scared again,” he said, “I will listen.”

Miri smiled back. “And I will listen to you.”

The End.
