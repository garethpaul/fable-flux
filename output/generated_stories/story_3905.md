# Tully the Shy Turtle’s Home Office Helpers

Tully was a shy turtle who loved quiet places. He felt safe in his shell and safe in his home office. His office was a small desk by a sunny window. The desk held a soft mouse pad, a round lamp, and a big, friendly computer. The keys on the keyboard made a gentle click-clack sound. The lamp hummed softly. The air smelled like paper and lemony cleaner. Outside the window, a breeze tickled the curtains.

Tully wore blue headphones to keep sounds gentle. He liked to do little jobs on his computer. He made labels, tiny schedules, and bright, sticky notes. He liked to help his neighbors by organizing their notes. He felt proud, and also a little shy.

One morning, Tully got an email. Beep! The little envelope popped up. Tully read, moving his finger under each word.

“Hello, Tully! We are new neighbors. We want to make our home safe. We want to label our plugs and cords. Can you help? From: the Nam Family.”

Tully’s eyes grew wide. New neighbors. New people. He felt his heart thump-thump. He wanted to help, but he felt shy. He tucked into his shell just a little.

He whispered, “I can help from my office. That is safe.”

Soon, there was a gentle knock at the door. Rap-rap. Tully peeked out. On the porch stood three turtles with warm smiles: Mama Nam, Papa Nam, and a little turtle named Nari. They wore bright scarves with pretty patterns. Mama Nam’s scarf had red flowers. Papa Nam’s scarf had tiny gold stars. Nari’s scarf had blue waves.

“Hello,” Tully said in a small voice. “Come in.”

“Hello!” said Mama Nam. “We are happy to meet you.”

“It smells nice in here,” said Nari, sniffing. “Like lemons!”

Tully smiled a tiny smile. “Thank you.”

They sat by the sunny window. Tully kept his headphones on, a little off one ear. He liked to hear and also feel calm.

Papa Nam said, “We lived far away before. We used different words there. Sometimes I forget the new words for things.”

“That’s okay,” Tully said. He tapped a sticky note. “We can use pictures and colors.”

Nari looked around with bright eyes. “Your mouse pad is so soft,” she said, rubbing it with a finger. “It feels like a cloud.”

“It helps my hand feel comfy,” Tully said. He felt a little less shy.

Mama Nam pulled out a little bag. Inside were small labels with many languages on them. The letters curved in different ways. Some were tall and straight. Some were round and swirly.

“These are from our old home,” Mama Nam said. “Can we use them too?”

Tully’s heart felt warm. “Yes! We can make labels with many words. That can keep everyone safe.”

They stacked cords on the desk. The cords felt smooth, like long noodles. The computer hummed. The keyboard went click-clack as Tully typed.

Tully asked, “What is this plug for?”

Papa Nam said, “For our tea kettle. We make tea with ginger and honey.”

“I like honey,” Tully said softly. He made a label with a little teacup picture, the word “Kettle,” and the Nam Family word for kettle too.

Nari clapped. “Two words and a picture! That is smart.”

Tully made more labels: “Lamp/Luz,” “Fan/Ventilador,” “Printer/Impresora,” and the Nam Family words, too. He used color dots to show what was safe for kids. Green dot: okay. Yellow dot: ask a grown-up. Red dot: do not touch.

“We use dots like a traffic light,” Tully explained. “Green means go. Red means stop. Yellow means wait and ask.”

Nari nodded. “That helps me remember.”

They talked softly while they worked. The room felt cozy and kind. Tully listened to the Nam Family’s stories. He learned that their old home had music with drums and flutes. He learned they ate dumplings on special days. Nari showed Tully a hand clap game. Clap-clap, clap. Tully tried it. He giggled when he missed a clap and his shell made a tiny bonk on the chair.

“Oops,” Tully said, smiling wider now. “I am learning.”

Mama Nam said, “We learn from you, too. Your labels help us be safe.”

When they finished, Tully showed a safety plan. He pointed to a simple map he drew in big shapes.

“This is your living room,” he said. “Here is a safe path. Here is a quiet corner. If there is a loud sound, you can go to the quiet corner and breathe like this.” He puffed in and out, slow and gentle. In… out… in… out. The lamp hummed. The curtains swished. The gentle breathing felt good.

Nari copied him. “I like the quiet corner,” she said. “It can be my calm place.”

Papa Nam smiled. “We like your careful thinking.”

Then Tully saw a problem. The kettle cord crossed a walkway. Someone could trip. Tully felt brave. “I have an idea,” he said. “We can use a cord clip.” He reached into a drawer and took out a small clip. It felt smooth and cool. He clicked it onto the desk. The cord stayed neat and safe.

“Good solution,” Mama Nam said.

Tully sat up taller. He felt proud and steady. He had helped from his safe home office. He had listened. He had learned new words. He had shared his plan. And he had made new friends.

Before they left, Nari handed Tully a small dumpling in a paper napkin. It was warm and smelled like ginger and soup.

“For you,” she said. “Thank you for helping.”

Tully took a tiny bite. The dumpling was soft and tasty. “Mmm,” he said. “I love learning new things.”

Nari waved. “We do too!”

After the door closed, Tully looked at his desk. The labels, the dots, the map, the little clip—each one helped. He felt a glow inside. He whispered, “Different words. Different foods. Different games. We can share, be safe, and be kind.”

He wrote a note to himself and stuck it by the lamp: “Be open. Be careful. Be brave.”

Tully smiled. His home office felt even warmer now, like sunshine on a pond.

The lesson was clear: When we welcome different cultures, listen, and share safe ideas, everyone can learn, feel calm, and be strong—together.

The End.
