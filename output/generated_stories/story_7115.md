# Millie Mouse and the Big Choice Day

In a comfortable nursery with a sunny window, a soft rug, and shelves of bright bins, lived a kind mouse named Millie. Millie’s nest was tucked in a tiny wooden dollhouse near the bookshelf. Her whiskers were warm from the sun, and her little heart felt brave. Today was Big Choice Day.

“Good morning, nursery,” Millie squeaked, stretching her tiny paws. She could hear the gentle hum of the air vent and smell the sweet, clean scent of soap. She wiggled her nose. “I can do things by myself today,” she whispered. “I will try. I will decide.”

Millie peeked out and saw three bins under the window: a blue bin with a sign that said “Paper,” a green bin with a sign that said “Plastic,” and a brown bin with a sign that said “Trash.” The signs had little pictures, too. Millie loved the pictures. They helped her choose.

On the rug sat a pile of things: a crinkly water bottle, a soft paper roll, a shiny snack wrapper, and an empty cardboard box that smelled like crayons. The nursery felt busy with quiet. The clock went tick-tick. The curtains whispered, swish-swish.

“I want to help,” Millie said. “I can choose where things go. I can be independent.” Her whiskers trembled with happy jitters.

She climbed down the dollhouse steps and patted the cardboard box. Tap, tap. “Hello, box,” she giggled. “You are light and a little bendy.” She put her ear to it. “You are quiet, too.”

Millie looked at the bins. “Where should you go?” she asked. “Blue for paper, green for plastic, brown for trash.” She thought. She looked again at the pictures. The blue bin had a picture of a sheet of paper. The cardboard felt like thick paper.

“I will decide,” Millie said. “I choose the blue bin.” She pushed the box with both paws. Scritch, scritch. It slid across the rug. She puffed a little. “I can do it. I am strong.”

Next was the crinkly water bottle. It made a funny sound. Crinkle-crackle! “Hello, bottle,” Millie said. “You are see-through and squishy.” She tapped it. “Plink.”

She looked at the green bin. It had a picture of a bottle. Millie nodded. “Green is for plastic. I choose green.” She held the bottle and walked, step-step-step, and dropped it in. Plunk! She smiled. “I did that all by myself.”

The shiny snack wrapper sparkled like a tiny moon. “You are pretty,” Millie told it, “but where do you go?” She looked at the pictures. No wrapper picture. Millie sat crisscross on the rug and took a big breath in. In, out. Calm. “When I am not sure, I stop and think.”

She touched the wrapper. It felt crinkly but not like the bottle. “Is it plastic?” she asked. She checked the green bin picture. It showed a bottle and a cup. Not a wrapper. She checked the blue bin picture. It showed paper. Not shiny. The brown bin had a picture of a banana peel and a little trash can.

“I will choose,” Millie said. “I will put the wrapper in the brown bin.” She walked carefully and dropped it in. Rustle. “I made a smart choice.”

The soft paper roll made a tiny whoosh sound when she picked it up. “You are paper. Blue bin for you,” Millie said proudly. She marched to the blue bin. Marchety-march. In it went. Shoof.

Just then, Millie heard a gentle voice. It came from a friendly plush turtle on the shelf. The turtle had a stitched smile and soft green flippers. “Hello,” the turtle said, in a voice like a slow lullaby. “You are busy today.”

Millie blinked with surprise. “Hello, Turtle! I am choosing where things go. I am helping all by myself.”

“That is a good feeling,” said Turtle. “How did you decide?”

“I looked at the pictures,” said Millie. “I touched and listened. I thought and then I chose.” She placed a paw on her chest. “I can do it.”

Turtle nodded slowly. “You used your senses and your mind. That is smart and kind. You are caring for the nursery and for the Earth.”

Millie’s ears perked up. “For the Earth?”

“Yes,” Turtle said softly. “When you put paper and plastic in the right bins, they can be used again. That is called recycling. It helps keep our world clean.”

Millie smiled so big her whiskers wiggled. “I like helping the Earth.” She looked around and saw a small stack of colorful bottle caps. Blue, red, yellow. “What about these?”

She felt them. Click, click. She looked at the green bin picture. It showed a cap, too. “Green is for plastic,” she decided. “In you go, little caps.” She dropped them in. Tink, tink, tink!

Now the rug was clean. The bins were tidy. The nursery smelled fresh and felt calm. Sunlight made little sparkles on the floor. Millie stood tall, as tall as a mouse can.

“I did it,” she said softly. “I made choices. I helped by myself.”

Turtle smiled. “You were careful and brave. When you are not sure, you stop, look, and think. That is good decision-making.”

Millie nodded. “Stop, look, think. Choose. I can be independent. I can recycle. I can help.”

She climbed back into her dollhouse and tucked a tiny note under her pillow. The note said, “I can do it!” Her heart felt warm, like a cozy blanket.

From the window came a tiny breeze. The curtains went swish-swish again, like quiet applause. Millie waved to the bins. “Thank you for helping me learn,” she giggled.

She curled up for a rest, proud and peaceful. “Tomorrow,” she yawned, “I will choose again.”

The End.
