# Sunny Sprout Rescue

The sun was warm and bright in the backyard. The grass felt cool and tickly. A breeze smelled like fresh leaves. Birds chirped, “Cheep-cheep!” and a tiny bug buzzed by.

In the middle of the yard, a determined rabbit named Rumi wiggled her nose. Her fur was soft and gray. Her ears stood tall. Rumi loved to hop, sniff, and explore. Today, she wanted to discover something new.

“I will find a new friend or a new surprise,” Rumi said, hopping past the flower pots.

Then Rumi stopped. She saw a little green sprout. It was very small and very droopy. One leaf curled down, and the soil around it looked dry and cracked.

“Oh no,” Rumi whispered. “Little sprout, are you thirsty?”

The sprout did not answer, but the leaf trembled in the breeze. Rumi’s tummy felt tight. She wanted to help. She was a determined rabbit, and determined rabbits try and try.

“I will find water,” Rumi said. She looked around the sunny backyard. The birdbath was empty. The hose lay in a sleepy loop. A small blue bucket sat near the fence.

Rumi hopped to the bucket. “Hello, Bucket,” she said. “Can you help my new friend, Sprout?”

Bucket was light and dusty. “Clink,” it said when she nudged it. Rumi peeked inside. Only a few drops of water hid at the bottom.

“That is not enough,” Rumi said. “I need more.”

Just then, Rumi heard tiny feet patter-patter. Her friend, Tiko the squirrel, scampered down the tree. His tail was fluffy like a duster.

“Hi, Rumi! Why do you look so serious?” Tiko asked, tilting his head.

“My new friend, Sprout, is thirsty,” Rumi said. “I want to help. But I don’t know how to get water.”

Tiko blinked. “We can think together,” he said. “Friends help friends.”

Rumi’s ears perked up. “Yes! Let’s solve the problem.”

They sat in the soft grass. Rumi tapped her paw. Tiko tapped his tiny fingers.

“Maybe we can use the hose,” Rumi said.

They pulled the hose. It felt rubbery and warm from the sun. Rumi turned the knob. The hose hissed, then puffed. No water came out.

Tiko sighed. “The hose is sleeping.”

Rumi twitched her nose. She did not give up. “What else can we try?”

Tiko looked at the sky. “The clouds give rain,” he said. “But the sky is all blue today.”

Rumi smiled a small smile. “We can make our own rain.”

“How?” asked Tiko.

Rumi sniffed the air. She heard a “plink, plink” near the shed. She hopped over and found a metal bowl under the roof edge. It had a little bit of old rainwater inside. A tall watering can stood nearby. A stack of cups lay on their side. An empty jar sat in the shade.

Rumi’s eyes shone. “We can collect water when it rains,” she said. “That is saving water. That is good for the Earth.”

Tiko nodded fast. “Sustain… sustaina…” He tried the big word.

“Sustainability,” Rumi said slowly. “It means we care for our home.”

“But Sprout needs water now,” Tiko said.

Rumi looked at the bowls and cups. “We will use what we have today,” she said. “Then we will get ready for tomorrow.”

They poured the old rainwater into the blue bucket. “Swish, swish.” It made a tiny pool.

“We need more,” Rumi said. “Let’s be careful and kind to the backyard.”

They checked the birdbath. A corner held a spoonful of water. They tipped it into the bucket. “Plop.”

They found a damp sponge near the steps. Rumi gently squeezed it over the bucket. “Drip, drip, drop.”

Tiko ran to the big leafy plant. “The leaves caught dew!” he squeaked. He shook the leaves softly. Little beads fell like tiny diamonds. “Tinkle, tinkle,” they sang into the bucket.

Rumi peered inside. “We have enough for a small drink,” she said. “We will water slowly.”

They carried the bucket together. It felt cool against Rumi’s paws and made a soft sloshy sound. They poured a little water around the sprout. The soil drank. “Gulp, gulp.” They waited, then poured a little more.

The sprout lifted its leaf. It looked greener. Rumi’s heart felt warm and proud.

“Hi, Sprout,” Tiko whispered. “We are your friends.”

Rumi patted the soil with her paw. “We will take care of you.”

Tiko pointed to the jar and cups. “Let’s make a water station,” he said.

They set the jar under the roof edge to catch rain. They put the cups upside down to stay clean. They filled the watering can with the tiny water they found, just a little, and set it by the sprout. Rumi made a small sign from a leaf and a stick. It said, “For Sprout.”

The sun slid a little lower. The breeze felt softer. The sprout stood taller now, happy and bright.

“We did it together,” Rumi said.

“Yes,” Tiko agreed. “Friends share ideas. Friends share work. Friends share care.”

Rumi smiled big. “When we are friends, we can solve problems. We discovered new ways to help. We saved water, too.”

Tiko wiggled his tail. “Tomorrow, we will check the jar. Maybe there will be more rainwater.”

Rumi nuzzled the sprout gently. “Good night, little friend. We are here for you.”

The backyard glowed golden. The birds sang good-night songs. Rumi and Tiko felt peaceful and proud. They had been determined. They had been kind. They had been good friends.

The clear lesson was bright in Rumi’s heart: Friendship means helping each other, sharing ideas, and caring for our world.

The End.
