# Jumpy Joey and the Bath-Time Story

Jumpy Joey was a joyful kangaroo joey. He loved to bounce, bounce, bounce! One sunny afternoon, it was bath time. The bathroom was warm and steamy. The mirror looked foggy like a cloud. The tub was full of bubbly suds that smelled like oranges. Pop, pop, pop went the bubbles.

“Bath time!” called Mama Roo with a smile. “Hop in, little jumper.”

Joey peeked at the tub and giggled. “Hi, bubbles! I’m Jumpy Joey, and I love stories,” he said. He wiggled his toes in the warm water. It felt cozy, like a soft blanket.

But Joey had a problem. He wanted to tell a big, long story right now. Mama Roo had a plan for bath time—wash, rinse, and then bedtime story. Joey wanted to tell his story first. He puffed his cheeks and thought, I can tell it while I splash!

“Mama,” Joey said, “I want to tell my story now! It’s about a rocket banana and a brave spoon!”

Mama Roo chuckled. “That sounds silly and fun. I can’t wait to hear it,” she said gently. “But first, let’s wash your ears and paws.”

Joey crossed his little arms and bounced in place. Splash! Water sprinkled on the mirror. “I want to tell it now, now, now!” he shouted.

Mama Roo looked surprised. A soap bubble landed on her nose. “Pop!” went the bubble. She took a deep breath. “Joey,” she said softly, “I hear you. Your story is special. But we must respect bath time rules. We wash first. Then we tell stories.”

Joey felt grumpy. His tail drooped. The bathroom felt too quiet. The bubbles didn’t pop as happy anymore. He looked at the orange soap bottle, the fluffy towel, and the rubber ducky floating by. Squeak, squeak went the duck.

He wanted to choose. He wanted to decide. But what was the kind choice?

Mama Roo sat on the bath rug and touched Joey’s paw. “We can make a good plan together,” she said. “You can help decide. Would you like to: one, wash-wash-wash and then tell your big story? Or two, tell a tiny beginning now, and save the rest for after rinsing?”

Joey blinked. A plan? He liked plans. Plans felt like little steps. Left foot, right foot. Bounce, bounce.

He whispered, “Can I tell the beginning now?”

Mama Roo nodded. “Yes. A tiny beginning. Then we wash with respect. We take turns—story turn, wash turn.”

Joey sat up tall. “Okay. Once upon a time,” he began, “there was a rocket banana who said, ‘Zoom!’ and a brave spoon who said, ‘Scoop!’ They wanted to help the moon. The end of the beginning!”

Mama Roo clapped softly. “Bravo! I can’t wait for the middle and the end. Now it’s wash turn.”

Joey smiled. He felt proud. He made a choice. A kind choice. Mama Roo dipped the soft washcloth into the warm water. Swish. She hummed a happy tune. Joey closed his eyes while she washed behind his ears. The washcloth felt tickly. “Hee-hee!” he giggled. “That tickles!”

“Arms up,” said Mama Roo.

“Arms up!” echoed Joey. They laughed together. Squeak, squeak went the duck again.

Soon, Joey was sparkly clean. “Rinse time!” said Mama Roo. She poured warm water. Whoosh. Bubbles slid down like tiny clouds.

“All done,” Mama Roo said gently. She wrapped Joey in a big, fluffy towel. It smelled like sunshine. Joey’s fur felt snuggly and dry.

“Now it is story turn,” Mama Roo said. “I’m ready to listen with my big ears.”

Joey’s eyes shone. He stood on the bath rug, warm and brave. “Once upon a time,” he said, “the rocket banana and the brave spoon looked up. The moon looked sleepy. ‘We will help,’ they said. The rocket banana zoomed past stars that went twinkle-twinkle. The brave spoon scooped silver moonlight and said, ‘Here you go, Moon!’”

Mama Roo leaned close and listened. She didn’t talk over Joey. She nodded and smiled. Joey felt respected. His voice felt strong and calm.

“And then,” Joey said, bouncing a tiny bounce, “they shared their plan. They took turns. Zoom, scoop, zoom, scoop. The moon woke up and said, ‘Thank you, friends.’ The end!”

Mama Roo hugged him. “What a wonderful story. You made kind choices. You told it so clearly. You took turns and showed respect.”

Joey beamed. He felt warm inside. “I decided to tell a tiny beginning, then wash,” he said. “Taking turns is good. It helps everyone.”

Mama Roo kissed his forehead. “Yes. Stories are powerful. When we tell stories, we share feelings. We learn. We practice good choices.”

Joey nodded. “Next time, I can help choose again. Maybe I tell the middle during tooth brushing,” he giggled.

Mama Roo laughed. “We can make a new plan together.”

Joey bounced once more. The mirror was clear now. The bathroom smelled like oranges and clean towels. Squeak went the duck, as if it was clapping too.

Joey whispered to the duck, “I respect bath time. And my story can still shine.”

Together they turned off the light. Click. Joey felt proud, clean, and ready for bed—with more stories to tell tomorrow.

The End.
