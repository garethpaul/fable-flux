# Hootie Owl and the Busy Bathroom

Hootie was a wise old owl with soft gray feathers and big, gentle eyes. One sunny morning, Hootie flew to a little house to visit his friends. He landed on the bathroom window ledge with a quiet tap-tap, and he peeked inside.

The bathroom smelled like bubble soap. The mirror was shiny, but the sink was full of tiny toothpaste dots. Towels were on the floor like sleepy clouds. The faucet dripped, drip… drip… drip. Hootie tilted his head and listened.

“Whoooo is there?” Hootie asked softly.

“It’s me,” said Mia, a small child with curly hair. She stood on a stool, holding a toothbrush. “I’m trying to clean my teeth, but it’s messy in here. My brother, Max, likes his towel on the floor. I like it on the hook. I feel mad.”

Hootie blinked kindly. “Feelings are okay,” he hooted. “We can accept feelings, and we can accept people. We can also choose what to do next.”

Max peeked in, holding a toy boat. “I like the towel on the floor so I can sit on it,” Max said. “I don’t like hooks. Hooks are pokey.”

Mia crossed her arms. “I want it neat.”

Hootie spread his soft wings. “Let’s take a deep breath together. In… and out. Listen to the water drip. Drip… drip… drip. Now, we will make a plan. We will use our kind voices. We will keep our bathroom happy.”

Mia and Max nodded. They breathed with Hootie. The bathroom felt calmer, like warm bathwater.

“First,” Hootie said, “we do our chores. Chores help our home feel safe. Chores help us care for each other. We can make chores fun!”

“How?” Max asked, rocking his boat.

“Music,” Hootie said. “And games. And choices.”

Hootie hummed a soft hoot-hoo song. Mia giggled. Max bopped his boat to the beat.

“Second,” Hootie said, “we will organize. Organization is when things have a place. A home for everything.”

Mia pointed. “Toothbrushes in the cup!”

Max held up his boat. “Boats on the shelf!”

Hootie nodded. “Yes. And towels on…?”

Max frowned. “Not the floor?”

“We can make a choice,” Hootie said. “A choice that feels good for both of you. You can accept that you like different things. You can still be a team.”

Max looked at Mia. Mia looked at Max.

“I accept that Max doesn’t like hooks,” Mia said quietly.

“I accept that Mia likes it neat,” Max said, squishing his toes on the tile.

Hootie smiled. “Wise words. What could we do?”

Mia looked around. “What if we put one towel on the hook, and one towel on a basket?”

Max’s eyes grew wide. “A basket! Soft, not pokey!”

Hootie fluffed his feathers. “A fair plan. You made a decision together.”

They found a basket under the sink. It was soft and woven. Max touched it. “It feels like a nest.”

“Now,” Hootie said, “let’s save water. That is called conservation. We turn off the tap when we brush.”

Mia looked at the dripping faucet. “Drip… drip… drip.” She turned the handle. The drip stopped. “We saved water!”

Max turned the water on to wet his brush, then turned it off. “We save water, we save the fish,” he said, thinking about his boat.

“Wise,” Hootie hooted. “Now we brush for two hoots.” He made two long hoots. “Hooooo… Hooooo…”

They brushed. The toothpaste smelled like mint. The bubbles tickled their tongues. Mia giggled. Max spit into the sink—plop! The sink was cleaner now.

“Time for chores,” Hootie said. “We wipe the sink.” He handed Mia a little cloth. She wiped the tiny toothpaste dots. Wipe, wipe, wipe. Max put toys on the shelf. Clack, clack, clack. Hootie folded a towel with his wings, slow and careful. Fold, pat, smooth.

“Look,” Mia said. “The mirror is shiny. The sink is happy.”

“My boat has a home,” Max said, placing it on the shelf.

Hootie nodded. “You organized. You did chores. You saved water. And you accepted each other.”

Max pointed to the basket. “My towel will live in the basket.”

Mia hung her towel on the hook. “Mine will live on the hook. And that is okay.”

Hootie ruffled his feathers. “That is more than okay. That is kind.”

They all stood still a moment. The bathroom felt quiet and cozy. It smelled like mint and soap. The light was bright and warm.

Mia took Max’s hand. “Thank you, Max.”

Max squeezed back. “Thank you, Mia.”

Hootie smiled. “Thank you for choosing together. When we accept each other, we find good answers. When we do our chores, our space is calm. When we organize, we can find our things. When we save water, we help the Earth.”

Mia and Max nodded. “We can do it every day,” Mia said.

“Every day,” Max echoed.

Hootie flapped his wings, ready to fly. “I will visit again. Keep making kind choices. Keep your bathroom happy.”

“Goodbye, Hootie!” they called.

Hootie tipped his head and flew out the window. The house felt peaceful. The bathroom was neat and clean, with a towel on the hook and a towel in the basket. The faucet was quiet. The shelf was tidy. Mia and Max felt proud.

They had learned a big lesson in a small room: accept each other, work together, make good choices.

The End.
