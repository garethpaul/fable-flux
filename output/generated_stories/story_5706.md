# Jump, Joey, Jump!

In a sunny backyard, a joyful kangaroo joey named Kip bounced out of his cozy shade spot. The grass felt cool and tickly under his toes. Birds chirped, “Cheep-cheep!” and a soft breeze smelled like sweet flowers and fresh dirt.

“Good morning, backyard!” Kip giggled. “I’m ready to jump!”

Kip loved to jump high, low, and sideways. He boinged past a blue watering can, a red ball, and a row of tiny carrots growing in a neat line. The sun felt warm on his fur, and his tummy gave a small rumble.

“Oh! I’m hungry,” Kip said, patting his belly. Mama Roo called from the porch, “Self-care first, Kip! Water, snack, sun hat, and sunscreen.”

Kip nodded. “Self-care helps me feel strong.” He put on his soft, floppy sun hat. It felt snug and safe. He squirted a little sunscreen on his paw. “Squish!” It smelled like coconut. He rubbed it on his nose. “Cool and creamy,” he said with a smile.

Mama Roo handed him a cup. “Sip-sip.” The water was cold and crisp. Kip munched a crunchy apple slice. “Crunch, crunch!” His tummy felt happy. “Thank you, Mama. I’m ready now.”

“Listen to your body,” Mama Roo said kindly. “If you feel hot, rest in the shade.”

Kip tapped his chest. “I can do that!”

He hopped toward the carrots. A little ladybug sat on a leaf. “Hello, tiny friend,” Kip whispered. The leaf smelled green and earthy. Kip gently waved. “I will be careful with your home.”

Kip saw a piece of paper flapping by the fence. “Oh no,” he said. “This doesn’t belong on the ground.” He picked it up and looked around. “Where should it go?” Nearby, he noticed two bins. One was blue with a sign that showed arrows in a circle. The other was gray.

Mama Roo called, “That paper can be recycled, Kip. The blue bin is for recycling. This is how we care for our home and planet.”

Kip smiled. “I can help!” He walked, not ran, to keep the paper from flying away. “Into the blue you go,” he said, dropping it in. It made a soft “swish.” Kip felt proud.

“Good civic helper,” Mama Roo said. “You are taking care of our backyard for everyone.”

Kip bounced again. He made a big jump—boing!—and his hat slipped over his eyes. “Oops!” he laughed. He pushed it back and took a deep breath. The air tasted warm and sunny. Then his cheeks felt hot and his legs felt a little wobbly.

“My body is talking,” Kip said softly. “It says, rest.”

He hopped to the shady tree and sat on the cool ground. He put his paw on his chest and felt it go thump-thump. “I can calm down,” he whispered. He took three slow breaths. In… and out. In… and out. In… and out. The leaves whispered back, “Shhhh.”

“Would you like a cool cloth?” Mama Roo asked.

“Yes, please,” Kip said. The cloth was damp and gentle on his forehead. “Ahh. That feels nice.” He sipped more water. “Sip, sip.”

Soon he felt steady and comfy again. “Self-care helps me play,” he said.

Kip stood up and looked around. A little trail of tiny pebbles was on the garden path. “These could trip someone,” he said. He picked them up one by one and put them by the flowerbed. “Safe and tidy.” He saw the red ball in the middle of the path. “This belongs by the porch.” He rolled it back. “There you go.” The ball made a happy “thump” as it stopped.

The backyard looked bright and neat. Bees hummed, “Bzzzz,” around a yellow flower. Kip waved. “I will watch, not touch.” He remembered to be gentle with nature.

Just then, a small ant line marched by a crumb near the porch step. Kip crouched low. “Hello, little ants,” he said. “I won’t step on your path.” He brushed the crumb into a tiny dish to move it off the step. “Safe for feet, safe for ants.”

“Kip,” Mama Roo said, smiling, “you listened to your body, cared for your space, and helped the Earth. That is self-care, civic care, and Earth care.”

Kip’s heart felt like a warm sunbeam. “When I care for me, I can care for others,” he said. He took another drink and checked his hat. “Hat on, sunscreen on, water in, ready to begin!”

He hopped to the carrot patch and saw a wilted leaf. “You need a little water,” he said kindly. He lifted the blue watering can. “Pour, pour,” he sang. The soil drank the water with a soft “glug.” The air smelled like rain, even in the sunshine.

Kip clapped. “The backyard is happy. I am happy!”

Mama Roo opened her arms. “Time for a stretch, then story time in the shade.”

Kip stretched his arms up, then out, then gave himself a gentle hug. “I take care of me,” he said. He picked up a tiny wrapper he found by the step and popped it into the gray bin. “Trash in the trash, recycling in the blue.”

He curled up beside Mama Roo under the leafy tree. The shade was cool, the grass was soft, and the birds sang a sleepy song.

Kip whispered, “Self-care helps me feel safe, strong, and kind.”

“Yes,” said Mama Roo. “And when we care for our backyard and our neighbors, we make the world bright.”

Kip smiled and closed his eyes for a moment, feeling peaceful and proud.

The End.
