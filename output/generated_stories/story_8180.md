# Bucky Beaver Shares the Porch

Bucky was a busy beaver with bright eyes and strong teeth. He loved to build and fix things. His favorite spot was the peaceful front porch of his little blue house. The porch smelled like warm wood and fresh leaves. The wind made the chimes sing, “ting-ting-ting,” and the porch swing creaked, “cree-eak,” in a friendly way.

One sunny morning, Bucky lined up his tools. Tap-tap went his tiny hammer. Scritch-scratch went his brush as he painted a little sign. “Welcome!” the sign said in big, happy letters. Bucky smiled. “Today I will make the best porch puppet show,” he said. “I will be ready. I will be fair. I will share.”

He practiced lifting the little curtain. Swish! He practiced the voices. “Hello!” squeaked the squirrel puppet. “Good day!” rumbled the bear puppet. He set out cushions, cups of cool water, and a bowl of crunchy apple slices. He checked his list. “Curtain? Check. Puppets? Check. Snacks? Check. Seats? Check.” Bucky felt proud. He had prepared.

Soon, footsteps pattered on the path. “Hi, Bucky!” called Sunny the rabbit, her ears bouncing. “Can I watch?”

“Of course!” said Bucky. “There’s a seat here.”

Flappy the duck waddled up. “Quack! Me too?”

“Yes,” said Bucky. “Please sit.”

Then Rolo the raccoon peeked from behind a bush. “I brought a bell,” he said, holding a tiny silver bell that jingled softly. “May I ring it at the end?”

Bucky nodded. “That sounds fair. We cheer at the end.”

Last came Tiny the turtle. Tiny moved slowly-slowly, with a gentle smile. “Am I late?” he asked. His shell had a crack, and sometimes he needed extra time.

“You are right on time,” said Bucky. “I saved you a front seat.”

But when Tiny reached the porch steps, Fizz the frog bounced in with a big leap—boing!—and landed right on the front cushion. “Front is for fast!” Fizz giggled. “I got here first.”

Tiny looked down. “Oh,” he whispered. His eyes were shiny. “I guess I will sit far back.”

Bucky’s chest felt tight. He heard the chimes go, “ting-ting,” like a tiny worry. That didn’t feel fair. Tiny had been on his way first. He had taken small steps, but he had been trying very hard. And Bucky had said he saved that seat.

Bucky took a deep breath. He smelled the sweet paint on his sign, and it helped him think. “I want everyone to have fun,” he said to himself. “Fun is better when it’s fair.”

He walked to the steps. “Fizz,” he said in a kind voice, “you are very fast. That is great. But Tiny was already moving to this seat. He needs the front to see and hear. We can make it fair.”

Fizz crossed his arms. “But I jumped first,” he said. “I want the best spot.”

“We can make more best spots,” Bucky said. He looked around the porch with his builder eyes. He saw a sturdy crate and a soft blanket. He saw a little stool and the stack of extra cushions he had prepared. “Watch this.”

Tap-tap went Bucky’s hammer. Scritch went his brush as he dusted the crate. He slid the crate next to the porch rail and set the stool on top. He placed two cushions and the soft blanket. “Now there are two front seats,” he said. “One for Tiny, and one for Fizz. Fair means everyone gets what helps them do their best.”

Tiny smiled wide and sat on the cushion by the curtain. “Thank you, Bucky,” he said. He could smell the fresh wood and see the puppets clearly.

Fizz looked at the new perch. The stool made him just the right height. He bounced once, then nodded. “Okay,” he said. “I like this seat. It is bouncy and fun.”

Sunny clapped her paws. Flappy quacked. Rolo jingled his bell softly. The porch felt peaceful again.

Bucky lifted the curtain. Swish! The squirrel puppet squeaked, “Hello, friends!” The bear puppet rumbled, “We take turns. We share.”

The show made everyone giggle. When the squirrel and bear both wanted the same acorn, they counted, “One, two,” and took turns. They said, “Fair for me, fair for you,” and split the acorn. Crunch! Everyone laughed and cheered.

At the end, Rolo rang his bell—ding-a-ling!—and Bucky bowed. “Thank you for coming,” he said. “Please have some water and apples.”

As they munched, Fizz looked at Tiny. “I am sorry I hopped in front,” he said. “I will ask next time.”

Tiny nodded. “Thank you,” he said. “I will save you a spot on my bench someday.”

Bucky’s heart felt warm, like sunshine on the porch. He set up a little box of “Helper Seats”—extra cushions, a small ramp, and a second bell. He wrote a new line on his welcome sign: “We are fair. We prepare. All friends can share.”

He showed the sign to everyone. “Fairness means we think about what each friend needs,” Bucky said. “Preparation helps us be fair. Inclusion means all friends belong.”

The chimes sang, “ting-ting-ting,” the swing creaked softly, and the friends laughed together on the peaceful front porch. Bucky smiled. He had solved the problem with kind words, good plans, and extra seats. And that felt just right.

The End.
