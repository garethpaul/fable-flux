# Ollie Owl Helps the Home Office

Ollie was a wise old owl with soft gray feathers and bright golden eyes. He lived in a cozy home office at the top of a quiet house. The room smelled like warm paper and lemon cleaner. Sunlight slid in through the window and made little dust sparkles dance in the air. The computer hummed, “Hmmmm,” and the clock said, “Tick-tock, tick-tock.”

“Good morning,” hooted Ollie softly. “Today is a good day to help.”

Ollie liked to help. He believed helping was part of being a good neighbor. That is civic responsibility. Civic means everyone. Responsibility means doing your part.

He stretched his wings and looked around the room. The recycling bin was full and wobbly. Old papers leaned like a little tower. Sticky notes were stuck to the desk, the chair, and even to a pencil cup.

Ollie blinked. “Hmm,” he said. “This office keeps the house working. I can do my part. I can keep it safe and tidy.”

Just then, Ollie heard a tiny squeak. “Eee!” came a voice from the floor.

It was Dot the dust bunny. Dot was small, soft, and shy. “Hello, Ollie,” Dot whispered. “The floor is very slippery. The paper tower might fall.”

Ollie nodded. “Thank you for telling me, Dot. Safety first,” he hooted. “We will fix it.”

Ollie took a deep breath. He felt calm and brave. “I can start. I can use my own wings,” he said. That is initiative—starting to help all by yourself.

He flapped gently down to the recycling bin. “Rustle, rustle,” went the papers as he sorted them. “Paper here. Plastic there,” he said. He read the little blue sign by the bin. “Recycling keeps our world clean. It helps everyone.”

A pencil rolled toward the edge of the desk. “Clink!” It almost fell.

“Careful,” said Ollie. “Tools belong in their place.” He placed the pencils in a cup. “Click, click,” went the tips against the rim. He unplugged a charger and wound the cord. “Cords can trip feet,” he said. “We keep them tidy for safety.”

Dot peeked out. “You are making the room feel better,” she said.

Ollie smiled. “When we help our place, we help our people,” he hooted.

He looked at the window. It was open a little. A cool breeze brushed his feathers. “Whoosh,” it sang. A paper flew up. “Flip!” It floated to the floor.

Ollie caught it with his wing. “Important papers need clips,” he said. He found a shiny clip and pressed it on. “Snap!” He set the paper in a neat tray labeled, “To Do.”

He heard tiny tapping on the door. “Tap, tap.” It was Pip the mouse from the hallway. Pip wore a bright yellow ribbon like a safety vest.

“Hi, Ollie,” Pip squeaked. “The hallway light is out. It’s dark. I brought this small flashlight.” Pip clicked it on. “Click!” A warm circle of light glowed.

“Thank you, Pip,” said Ollie. “You noticed a problem, and you told a helper. That is good civic responsibility. We will write a note for the grown-up to fix the light safely.”

Ollie used a big crayon. “Dear grown-up, the hallway light is out. Please help. Thank you.” He added a smiley face. Pip nodded. “I will take the note,” said Pip, holding it carefully.

Ollie turned back to the room. He checked the smoke alarm button. “Beep!” it chirped once. “Good,” said Ollie. “Safety helps everyone.”

He set a small basket by the door. “This is the ‘Found Things’ basket,” he said. “Lost paper clips and buttons can live here.” He dropped in a button. “Plink!”

Dot twirled like a tiny cloud. “Ollie, can I help?”

“Yes,” said Ollie gently. “You can be our ‘Floor Watcher.’ If you see a spill or a slip, tell us right away.”

Dot puffed up with pride. “I can do that!”

Ollie swept the last crumbs into a pan. “Swish, swish.” He washed his claws in warm water. “Squeak,” said the soap pump. He dried with a soft towel. The room smelled fresh, like lemons and clean paper again.

The clock smiled with its steady, “Tick-tock.” The computer hummed, “Hmmmm,” but now the desk was clear. Sunlight made a golden path across the tidy floor.

Pip came back, hopping with joy. “The grown-up got the note. The light will be fixed today,” he said.

Ollie fluffed his feathers. His heart felt light. “We did our part,” he hooted. “We noticed, we started, we stayed safe, and we asked for help when needed. That is how we care for our place and our people.”

Dot, Pip, and Ollie stood together. They listened to the gentle quiet of the clean room. It sounded like peace.

Ollie winked. “Little helpers can do big good,” he said. “When we see a need, we can take good care, tell a helper, and keep everyone safe. That is civic responsibility.”

Dot giggled. Pip squeaked. Ollie hooted a soft, happy “Hoo-hoo.”

The home office felt proud, too.

The End.
