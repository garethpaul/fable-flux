# Benny Beaver Builds a Bright Idea

Benny was a busy beaver with bright eyes and a big, flat tail. One sunny morning, he scampered into the garden shed. The shed smelled like fresh wood and warm earth. Sunbeams danced through dusty windows. Tools hung on the wall—clink, clank!—and a little radio hummed softly.

Benny wiggled his whiskers. “Today, I will make something special,” he said. “I will build a tiny stage for garden shows!”

He looked around. He saw wooden boards, a box of nails, a little hammer, a paintbrush, and a pile of colorful fabric scraps. He heard birds chirping outside, “Cheep, cheep!” He felt excited, like bubbles in a fizzy drink.

Benny set the boards on the floor. “Tap, tap, tap,” went the hammer. “I can do this,” he said, feeling proud. But when he tried to lift a long board, it wobbled and slid. “Oh no!” The board bumped the paint can. “Splat!” Yellow paint splashed on the floor and on Benny’s toes.

Benny’s ears drooped. “This is hard,” he whispered.

Just then, the shed door creaked. “Eeeek!” In came a breeze full of garden smells—minty leaves and sweet flowers. Benny took a deep breath. “I can solve this. I am a problem-solver,” he said slowly.

He looked again. He saw the paint, the boards, the nails, the fabric, and a short stool. He put his paw on his chin. “Hmm.” His tail gave a soft thump on the floor. “First, I will clean the paint.” He found a rag and wiped the yellow puddle. Swish, swish. “There! Step one.”

Benny smiled a little. “Next, I will make the board stay still.” He used the stool as a helper. He slid one end of the board onto the stool. “Now it won’t wobble,” he said. The board rested like a sleepy log.

“Tap, tap, tap,” went the hammer. “Nice and slow,” Benny said. He held a nail. He breathed in. He breathed out. “I can try again.”

Soon, two boards made a square. Then four. The little radio played a happy tune. Benny nodded to the beat. “I’m building! I’m brave!” he sang softly.

But then, “Uh-oh!” The last board was too short. There was a little gap. Benny frowned. “My stage will have a hole.”

He felt a tiny twist in his tummy. He wanted to quit. He sat. He patted his tail. Thump, thump. “I need a new idea,” he said. “I can think. I can choose.”

Benny looked at the fabric scraps. Red, blue, green, and yellow pieces smiled up at him. “A curtain!” he said. “I can make a curtain to cover the gap. And I can make it beautiful.” His eyes sparkled.

He found string and tied it to two nails. He clipped the fabric pieces to the string. Swish! The scraps made a bright, patchy curtain. Benny poked his nose through. “Peek-a-boo!” He giggled.

He still needed a strong top for the stage. Benny looked again. Behind a bucket, he found a wide, flat piece of wood. “Just right!” He dragged it over with a grunt. “I can do hard things,” he said. He slid the wood across the frame. It fit! He tapped in four nails. Tap, tap, tap. “Safe and snug.”

Benny dipped his brush into the paint can. “Swoosh!” He painted sunny swirls. He added blue dots and green leaves. Splish, splash, dab! The stage looked bright and happy. It smelled like lemony paint and fresh wood.

He stood back and clapped his paws. “I did it!” His chest felt warm. His smile felt big.

The door creaked again. A little ladybug flew in and landed on the stage. “Hello, Benny!” she seemed to say with a tiny buzz.

Benny laughed. “Welcome to the show!” He bowed. “Today’s show is called The Bright Idea.” He made a deep voice. “Once there was a brave beaver.” He made a squeaky voice. “He solved problems!” He waved the curtain. Swish! He danced his tail. Thump-thump!

Soon, a snail peeked in from the door. A bumblebee buzzed by the window. The radio hummed. The shed felt warm and full and safe.

Benny finished with a twirl. “Ta-da!” He bowed again. The ladybug flew in a little circle, as if cheering. The snail clapped his feelers. Benny giggled.

“Thank you, tools,” he said, patting the hammer. “Thank you, fabric. Thank you, stool. Thank you, brain, for good ideas.” He touched his head and smiled. “I am grateful.”

Benny looked at the shiny stage. He remembered the mess, the wobble, and the gap. He also remembered how he cleaned, how he steadied, and how he invented a curtain. His heart felt strong.

He said softly, “When something is hard, I can stop, look, and think. I can try one step at a time. I can use what I have. I can make mistakes into something new. I can be me.”

Outside, the birds sang, “Cheep-cheep-cheer!” Benny packed the tools neatly. Clink, clink. He washed his yellow toes. Splash, splash. He turned off the radio. Click.

Before he left, Benny gave the stage one gentle pat. “Good job, Benny,” he said to himself. “I’m proud of you.” He winked at the ladybug. “Come back tomorrow for another show!”

Benny stepped into the sunshine, feeling brave, creative, and kind. He knew he could solve the next problem, too—one thoughtful step at a time.

The End.
