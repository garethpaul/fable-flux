# Ollie Otter’s Balcony Garden Break

Ollie the playful otter lived with Nana in a sunny apartment. Outside their door was a small balcony garden. It was full of green leaves, tiny tomatoes, and pots that smelled like mint and lemon. Bees hummed softly. A little wind chime sang, ting-ting-ting.

“Good morning, garden!” Ollie chirped. He wiggled his whiskers and patted the smooth wooden rail. His paws felt warm in the sunshine.

Nana brought out a blue watering can. “Time to care for our plants,” she said. “And time to care for ourselves too.”

Ollie bounced. “I want to do everything! I’ll water, weed, pick, and play!” He grabbed the watering can with both paws.

“Slow down, dear,” Nana chuckled. “We take turns. You, me, and even the plants. Taking turns keeps us safe and calm.”

Ollie’s tail twitched. He didn’t like waiting. The tomatoes looked so shiny and red! He wanted to pluck them all at once. He wanted to splash the water like a mini river.

Nana knelt by a pot of mint. “Let’s make a plan. First, we drink water.” She handed Ollie a small cup. “This is self-care. Your body needs a drink before work.”

Ollie sipped. The cool water felt like tiny raindrops in his mouth. “Ahhh,” he sighed. His tummy felt happy.

“Now,” Nana said, “we take turns. You water the mint. I will water the tomatoes. Then we rest in the shade. After that, we pick just three tomatoes.”

Ollie nodded fast. “Okay, okay!”

He lifted the can and poured. Drip, drip, drip. The mint leaves sparkled. The smell floated up, fresh and tickly. “Mmm! It smells like toothpaste and snow!” Ollie giggled.

“Nice gentle pour,” Nana said. “Plants don’t like big splashes. They like steady drinks, just like us.”

Ollie leaned close. He listened. The soil made a soft, slurpy sound. “The dirt is drinking!”

Nana smiled. “You’re learning. That is knowledge. We watch, listen, and learn. Knowledge helps us care.”

Ollie watched a bee visit a yellow flower. “Hello, buzzy buddy,” he whispered. The bee buzzed like a tiny motorcycle: bzzzz!

Now it was Nana’s turn to water. Ollie danced from paw to paw. Waiting was hard. He wanted to water more. He wanted to pick and taste and splash.

Nana looked up. “What helps when waiting feels wiggly?”

Ollie thought. He pressed his paws to his chest. “Breathe?” he said.

“Yes,” Nana said softly. “Let’s try. In like smelling a flower.” They breathed in together, slow and deep. “Out like blowing a bubble.” They breathed out, slow and round.

Ollie’s shoulders lowered. His paws felt still. “I feel soft inside,” he whispered.

“Good self-care,” Nana said. “Your breath is a tool. It helps your body rest.”

When Nana finished watering, she pointed to the chair in the shade. “Shade break. We sit, sip, and stretch.”

Ollie flopped into the chair. The fabric felt cool. The wind brushed his fur. He nibbled a slice of apple from Nana’s plate. Crunch! Sweet juice dribbled down his chin.

“Taking care of me helps me take care of the garden,” he said with a proud smile.

“That’s right,” Nana said. “We are part of the garden team.”

After their rest, Ollie stood up tall. “Tomato time!” He reached for the reddest one.

“Wait,” he told himself softly. “We said three.” He wiggled his whiskers and counted out loud. “One… two… three.” He picked them gently. Pop! Pop! Pop!

He placed the tomatoes in a tiny basket. They felt smooth, like little suns. He sniffed. They smelled sweet and green.

Nana clapped. “Kind hands. Careful turns. Smart plan.”

Ollie spotted a dry pot with droopy leaves. His heart squeezed. “That plant looks thirsty!”

“What do you know about thirsty plants?” Nana asked.

Ollie thought of the slurpy soil. He touched the dirt. It was dry and dusty. “Dry dirt means water time,” he said. “But gentle, not splashing.”

“Would you like to teach me?” Nana asked.

Ollie’s eyes grew bright. “Yes! I pour slow. I watch the soil. I stop when it looks dark and wet.” He demonstrated. Drip, drip. Dark, dark. Stop. “All done!”

They listened together. The wind chime sang again. A cloud sailed by like a white boat.

Nana stretched. “We did our work with care. Now we wash our paws.”

Ollie followed inside and rubbed his paws with warm, soapy water. The bubbles smelled like oranges. “This is self-care too,” he said. “Clean paws keep me healthy.”

Back on the balcony, Nana sliced the tomatoes. They tasted sunny and bright. “Thank you, garden,” Ollie said between bites. Juice tickled his whiskers.

Nana tapped her nose. “What did you learn today?”

Ollie counted on his paws:
- “I breathe when I feel too wiggly.”
- “I drink water and rest in the shade.”
- “I take turns so everyone has a job.”
- “I use my eyes, ears, and paws to learn what plants need.”

Nana hugged him. “That is wise, Ollie.”

Ollie hugged back. He looked over the green leaves and the tiny world of the balcony garden. “When I care for me,” he said softly, “I can care for others too.”

The End.
