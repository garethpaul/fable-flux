# Buttons the Bear Cub and the Bathroom Bubble

Buttons was a friendly bear cub with soft, fuzzy ears and a nose that twitched when he was curious. One sunny morning, Buttons padded into the bathroom. The tiles were cool under his paws. The room smelled like minty soap, and the mirror was shiny like a lake. Drip, drip, drip went the faucet. Plink, plink, plink went the drops.

Buttons loved the bath. He loved the warm water and the fluffy towel. But today, something new tickled his tummy—he wanted privacy.

Mama Bear peeked in. “Good morning, Buttons,” she said gently. “Bath time.”

Buttons wiggled his shoulders. “Mama,” he said, “I want a quiet bath, please. I want my own space.”

Mama Bear smiled. “You are growing, little cub. It’s okay to want space. What do you need?”

Buttons took a brave breath. He sniffed the soap. It smelled like sweet pine. “I need the door half-closed,” he said. “And I want to say yes or no about help.”

Mama nodded. “Those are your body boundaries,” she said. “You can say yes. You can say no. You can say, ‘Not right now.’ I will listen.”

Buttons felt warm and safe. “Thank you, Mama.”

He hopped into the tub. Splash! The water was cozy on his fur. Swoosh went his paws. He made a tiny wave. He giggled. The mirror fogged a little. He drew a smiley face in the steam with one paw.

Then, Buttons’ big sister, Bree, skittered into the bathroom with a bounce. “I want to play bubbles!” Bree chirped. She reached for Buttons’ rubber duck.

Buttons’ belly got tight. His cheeks got hot. He did not like that Bree grabbed the duck. He wanted a calm bath. His thoughts felt like a rumbling train.

Buttons squeezed his paws. He remembered Mama’s words: You can say yes. You can say no. You can say, “Not right now.”

Buttons took a slow breath in. One… two… three. He let it out like a soft bear huff. “Bree,” he said, “I need space. This is my bath. Please stop and give the duck back.”

Bree blinked. She didn’t mean to bother him. “Oh,” she said. “But I wanted to play.”

Buttons felt the rumble again. He wanted to shout. He wanted to splash big, fast splashes. But he noticed his feelings. “My tummy feels tight,” he said out loud. “That means I feel mad.” He put his paws on his chest and counted again. One… two… three.

Mama Bear stepped closer but kept her paws to herself. “Buttons,” she asked softly, “do you want help with words?”

Buttons nodded. “Yes, Mama. Help with words.”

Mama crouched. “Try this: ‘Bree, I like you. I want to play later. Right now, I need privacy. Please leave the bathroom and close the door.’”

Buttons looked at Bree. His voice was firm and kind. “Bree, I like you. I want to play later. Right now, I need privacy. Please leave the bathroom and close the door.”

Bree’s ears drooped a little. “Okay,” she said, “I can do that.” She put the rubber duck back into Buttons’ paws. “I will wait in the hall. When you are done, we can do five jumping jacks together.” She smiled a small smile.

Buttons’ belly untwisted. He smiled back. “Yes,” he said. “Five jumping jacks later.” Exercise felt fun to think about.

Bree tiptoed out. Click went the door as it closed halfway, just like Buttons asked.

Buttons relaxed in the warm water. He felt proud. He had used his words. He had used his breath. He had set a boundary. He hummed a little bath song. “Hum, hum, hum,” he sang. The duck bobbed. The bubbles popped, pop, pop, pop, like tiny stars.

After his bath, Mama handed him a fluffy towel. “Would you like help drying your ears?” she asked.

Buttons thought. He liked doing things by himself. But sometimes help felt nice. He made a decision. “Yes,” he said, “help with ears, please. I can dry my paws.”

“Good choice,” Mama said. She patted his ears soft and slow. “I am proud you told us your needs.”

Buttons stood tall and wiggled into his clean, soft pajamas. He opened the door. Bree was in the hall, tapping her toes like a drum. Tap-tap-tap.

“Ready?” Bree asked.

“Ready,” Buttons said. They counted together. “One, two, three, four, five!” They did five gentle jumping jacks. Bounce, bounce, bounce. Their bodies felt wiggly and calm at the same time.

Bree grinned. “Thank you for telling me your boundary,” she said.

Buttons nodded. “Thank you for listening.”

Mama Bear hugged them both. “In our den,” she said, “we use kind words. We make choices. We listen to ‘yes,’ ‘no,’ and ‘not right now.’”

Buttons felt brave and peaceful. His tummy was soft now. He patted the bathroom door. “This is a place for bubbles,” he said, “and also for privacy.” He tapped his chest. “And this is my body. I can say what I need.”

Bree clapped. “And I can respect what you need.”

Buttons took one more slow breath. In. Out. He smiled his warm, fuzzy smile. “I did it,” he whispered. “I set my boundary, I used my breath, and I made a good decision.”

Mama winked. “That is big bear cub growth.”

Buttons and Bree paw-bumped. Then they skipped off to play a game they both chose together.

The lesson was clear in Buttons’ heart: My body is mine. My voice matters. I can notice my feelings, use calm breaths, and say what I need. And the ones who love me will listen.

The End.
