# Daisy's Big Dream

Daisy the deer fawn sat on the soft, fluffy rug in her cozy bedroom. Her spots were like little brown polka dots on her golden fur. The room smelled like warm cookies from downstairs and felt safe and snug.

On her small wooden desk sat a big box of colorful crayons and blank white paper. Daisy wanted to draw a beautiful picture for the forest art show tomorrow.

"I want to make something amazing," Daisy said to herself. She picked up a red crayon and started to draw.

But the tree she drew looked wobbly. The sun looked like a lumpy circle. Daisy frowned at her paper.

"This is terrible!" she said, crumpling up the paper. "I can't draw anything good!"

She threw the paper in the trash and crossed her arms. Her ears drooped down sadly.

Mama Deer peeked into the room. "What's wrong, little one?" she asked gently.

"I'm not good at drawing," Daisy sniffled. "My pictures look silly. I give up!"

Mama Deer sat on the bed and patted the soft quilt. "Come here, sweet Daisy."

Daisy climbed up and snuggled close. Mama smelled like pine trees and sunshine.

"You know," Mama said, "I wasn't good at drawing when I first tried either. But I kept practicing. Every day, I got a little bit better."

"Really?" Daisy asked, her big brown eyes wide.

"Really! And do you know what the magic word is?"

Daisy shook her head.

"YET!" Mama said with a smile. "Instead of saying 'I can't draw,' try saying 'I can't draw well YET.'"

Daisy thought about this. "I can't draw well... yet?"

"That's right! Your brain grows stronger every time you try something new. Like a muscle!"

Daisy felt a warm, tingly feeling in her chest. She hopped down and got a fresh piece of paper.

"I can't draw a perfect tree yet," she said, picking up a green crayon. "But I can try!"

This time, Daisy drew slowly. When the tree looked wobbly, she didn't give up. She added more lines to make it stronger.

"Good job!" Mama cheered. "Your brain just grew a little!"

Daisy giggled. She drew flowers next to the tree. They weren't perfect, but they were colorful and happy.

"I can't draw perfect flowers yet," she said, "but these ones make me smile!"

She drew a bright yellow sun with wavy rays. Then she added a family of deer playing under the tree.

"Mama, look!" Daisy called excitedly. "I drew our family!"

Mama looked at the picture. It had wiggly lines and crooked shapes, but it was full of love and joy.

"It's beautiful, Daisy! I can see how hard you worked. You didn't give up!"

Daisy beamed with pride. "I want to practice drawing every day so I can get better and better!"

"That's the spirit!" Mama said, giving her a warm hug.

The next day at the forest art show, Daisy hung her picture proudly. A little rabbit came over to look.

"I wish I could draw like that," the rabbit said sadly.

Daisy smiled kindly. "You can't draw like this yet," she said. "But if you practice, your brain will grow stronger! Want to draw together?"

The rabbit's eyes lit up. "Yes, please!"

As Daisy shared her crayons and showed the rabbit how to hold them, she felt proud and happy. She had learned that with practice and the word "yet," she could do amazing things.

The End.
