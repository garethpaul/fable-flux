# Nutty Words in the Pantry

Suri the squirrel was small, quick, and very clever. She lived near a big, cozy house. One sunny morning, Suri slipped through a tiny crack and scampered into the pantry. The pantry smelled like warm bread, sweet honey, and crunchy crackers. It was quiet, except for the soft hum of a little fridge.

“Hello, pantry,” Suri whispered. “I am here to learn and to snack!”

On the lowest shelf, Suri found a basket full of jars and boxes with bright, colorful labels. Each label had words. Suri loved words. They felt like little treasures.

She touched a jar and read slowly, “Honey.” The golden jar seemed to glow. “Honey,” she said again, tasting the sound like a treat. “Ho-ney.” Her tail swished with joy.

A tiny voice answered from a nearby box. “In my home, we say miel.” It was a friendly tea tin with a green leaf on it.

“Miel?” Suri repeated, her eyes wide. She smiled. “Miel! That is a lovely word.”

Another bright bag rustled. “In my kitchen, we say honig,” said the bag, its paper crinkling like soft music.

Suri clapped her little paws. “Honey, miel, honig! So many ways to say something sweet.”

She jumped to a shelf with soft flour that puffed up like white clouds when she landed. “Achoo!” she sneezed, and then giggled. A round jar shimmered. “Jam,” Suri read. She could almost smell strawberries.

“Confiture,” whispered a purple label with a tiny crown. “We say confiture.”

“Džem,” hummed a red jar with a polka-dot top.

“Jam, confiture, džem,” Suri sang. “Words are like little birds. They fly in, and they make me smile.”

A gentle, warm loaf of bread on the counter seemed to listen. The bread’s crust was golden, and it smelled cozy, like a hug. “Bread,” Suri said softly. She touched it with one paw.

“Pan,” said a soft voice from a flour sack.

“Pain,” replied a paper bag with a striped ribbon.

“Roti,” added a small tin with a flower painted on it.

Suri nodded. “Bread, pan, pain, roti.” She felt the words on her tongue—smooth, round, and friendly. “So many words, but they all share the same warm, yummy feeling.”

She peered up at a shiny metal can. It had a picture of beans. “Beans,” Suri read. Her belly made a tiny rumble. “Be-ans.”

“Frijoles,” said the can, proud and tall.

“Haricots,” whispered a quiet jar nearby.

“Fasulye,” chimed a bag with a blue stripe.

Suri tilted her head. “Beans, frijoles, haricots, fasulye.” She practiced each one, slowly, carefully. “I can learn. I can improve.”

But then, she heard a faint drip-drop from the sink. “Oh no,” Suri said. “A leaky leak!”

She looked around. The floor had a tiny puddle. The bread was safe, the jars were safe, but the paper bags could get soggy. Suri wanted to help. She was resourceful. She thought hard.

“I will fix it,” she said. “I will try, and I will use my words.”

She found a soft towel and tugged, tugged, tugged. The towel slid down with a cozy whoosh. “I need help,” Suri said to the pantry friends. “Can you cheer me on in your words?”

“Yes!” said the tea tin. “Ánimo!”

“Courage!” called the striped bag.

“Shabash!” sang the flowered tin.

“Bravo!” chimed the purple label.

Suri smiled. “Thank you! I can do it.”

She pushed the towel under the drip. Plip. Plop. The drops landed on the towel. The puddle stopped growing. Suri looked up at the shiny pipe and saw a loose little twisty part. She found a rubber band in a drawer and wrapped it around the twisty part to slow the drip. She turned the knob, just a tiny bit. The drip slowed to a gentle stop. Silence. The pantry hummed again, soft and happy.

“You did it!” said the honey jar. “Bravo!”

“Muy bien!” cheered the bean can.

“Très bien!” whispered the jam jar.

“Bahut acchha!” said the flour sack.

Suri’s ears perked up. Her heart felt warm, like toast. “Thank you,” she said, placing the towel neatly to dry. “Today I learned many words. They sound different, but they all mean care, warmth, and yum.”

She sat by the bread, and the pantry felt like a friendly classroom. “When we share our words,” Suri told her new friends, “we share our hearts. I can learn your words, and you can learn mine. That is how we grow.”

The bread smelled like sunshine. The honey gleamed like morning. The jam looked like a rosy sunset. Suri whispered, “Honey, miel, honig. Jam, confiture, džem. Bread, pan, pain, roti. Beans, frijoles, haricots, fasulye.” Each word was a little step, and each step made her braver.

She tucked a tiny list in her pouch with all the new words, so she could practice. “I will keep learning,” Suri said. “Little by little, I improve.”

The pantry sighed, happy and dry. Suri waved her tail. “Good-bye, friends. Thank you for your words.”

“Come back soon,” the labels sang. “We have more to share!”

Suri scampered back outside, feeling proud, kind, and curious. She had fixed a small problem and found big new words. She knew that many languages could live together, like jars on a shelf, side by side.

And that day, Suri learned a simple truth: different words can say the same love.

The End.
