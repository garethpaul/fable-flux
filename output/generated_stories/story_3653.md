# The Fair Share in the Attic

Tiptoe Tap was a gentle elephant calf. He had soft grey ears, a tiny tail that flicked, and feet that went tip-toe tap on wooden floors. One sunny afternoon, Tiptoe Tap climbed the stairs, step by careful step, to the quiet attic.

The attic smelled like old paper and pine. Dust sparkles danced in the sunbeams. A little window made a warm square of light on the floor. “Ooo,” Tiptoe Tap whispered. “It is calm and cozy up here.”

He heard a whisk-whisk sound and a soft chirp. “Hello?” Tiptoe Tap said.

Out from behind a box peeked Little Mouse, with bright black eyes. On a beam above, Birdie, a tiny brown bird, chirped again. In the corner, Squeak the Squirrel held a dry leaf and swished his fluffy tail. The attic was their quiet place too.

Tiptoe Tap saw a treasure: a box of colorful buttons, shiny marbles, and old ribbons. “Look!” he squeaked. “Sparkly things!” His trunk wiggled with happy wiggles.

Birdie fluttered down. “Tweet! I need a ribbon for my nest. I want the long blue one.”

Little Mouse bounced. “I want the blue ribbon too! It is smooth and nice.”

Squeak held the ribbon in his paws already. “I found it first,” he said. “I keep it.”

Tiptoe Tap watched them. His big heart felt tight. “This doesn’t feel fair,” he thought. “We all want a turn. We all want to be happy.”

“Let’s be fair,” Tiptoe Tap said in his gentle voice. “Fair means everyone gets a turn or a share. No one takes everything. We can be kind.”

Squeak hugged the ribbon close. “But I like it so much,” he whispered. His tail trembled.

Tiptoe Tap nodded. “I hear you, Squeak. You really like it. It is okay to say what you want. Your feelings are important. But Birdie and Little Mouse have feelings too.”

Birdie hopped. “Tweet, tweet. I need soft things for my nest.”

Little Mouse’s whiskers twitched. “I would like to make a tiny blanket.”

Tiptoe Tap had an idea. He felt a brave bubble in his tummy. “We can make a plan,” he said. “That is called using initiative. It means I try to help fix the problem.”

He looked in the box. There was a green ribbon, a red ribbon, and a short piece of blue ribbon too. He peered around. He saw a roll of soft fabric, some cotton fluff, and small sticks. He could hear the attic quiet again, except for the soft hum of the house.

Tiptoe Tap spoke kindly. “Squeak, will you please share the long blue ribbon for a short time? You can keep it first. Then we take turns. We will also use other things so everyone has enough.”

Squeak blinked. “If it is my turn first, I can try.” He smiled a tiny smile.

“Thank you,” said Tiptoe Tap. “That is fair.”

He turned to Birdie. “Birdie, would a short blue piece and soft fluff help your nest?” Birdie listened. Tiptoe Tap waited. He did not touch Birdie. He did not grab. He remembered personal boundaries. He asked first. “May I give you these?”

Birdie chirped. “Yes, please! That is kind.”

He turned to Little Mouse. “Little Mouse, would a green ribbon and fabric make a cozy blanket? May I pass them to you?”

Little Mouse nodded. “Yes! Thank you for asking.”

Tiptoe Tap lifted the short blue ribbon, green ribbon, and fluff with his trunk. He moved slow and careful. The attic floor creaked. The air smelled like cotton. The ribbons felt smooth and cool. When he gave the things, he said, “Here you go,” and kept a gentle space so everyone felt safe.

Squeak held the long blue ribbon for his turn. He wrapped it around his leaf to make a pretty bundle. “I like it,” he said. “When my turn is done, Birdie can have a turn, then Little Mouse, then back to me.”

“That’s fair,” Tiptoe Tap said. He drew a tiny chart on a dusty box with his trunk tip: Squeak, Birdie, Mouse, Squeak. “This is our turn line.”

They all agreed. Birdie chirped a happy tune. Little Mouse hummed along. Tiptoe Tap’s ears flapped softly.

Then Tiptoe Tap saw something else. Sunbeams shone on a cracked jar filled with old buttons. A few had rolled onto the floor. “We should not waste,” he said. “Let’s take care of the things up here. That is called conservation. We use only what we need, and we put things away.”

He picked up the rolling buttons and put them back in the jar. Squeak found a lid. “I can push it on,” Squeak said. Tiptoe Tap asked, “May I help you?” Squeak grinned. “Yes, please.” Together, push, click! The lid was on. Birdie brought a small twig to keep the jar from tipping. Little Mouse swept crumbs into a tiny pile. The attic felt tidy and safe.

Birdie fluttered to Tiptoe Tap’s shoulder. “Tweet! You were fair. You asked before you gave. You helped us share.”

Tiptoe Tap’s heart felt light. “We listened to each other. We took turns. We kept our space kind. We used only what we needed. That is fair.”

Squeak finished his turn and passed the long blue ribbon to Birdie. “My turn is done,” Squeak said. Birdie bowed her head and took it gently. Little Mouse clapped his tiny paws. Everyone smiled.

Tiptoe Tap tiptoed in a happy circle. The quiet attic was still quiet, but now it felt full of warm, good feelings.

He said, “Today we learned:
- Fair means everyone gets a turn or share.
- Initiative means we make a kind plan to fix a problem.
- Personal boundaries mean we ask first and keep gentle space.
- Conservation means we take care of things and do not waste.”

They all said together, “That is fair!”

Tiptoe Tap looked out the little window. The sun was soft and golden. He felt proud, calm, and kind.

The End.
