# Milo Mouse and the Art Room Helpers

Milo was a kind mouse with soft gray fur and tiny pink paws. He loved the creative art room at the community center. The room smelled like orange glue sticks and fresh paper. Sunlight made warm squares on the floor, and colorful paints lined the shelves like a rainbow parade.

“Today I will help,” Milo said, puffing up with pride. “I will volunteer and make the art room tidy and happy.”

Milo put on a little apron that said HELPER in bright letters. He swept up paper scraps with a whiskery smile. He lined up paint brushes so their bristles looked like neat little fans. He placed crayons in a row, tick-tick-tick, by color—red, orange, yellow, green, blue, purple.

Children came in with big smiles. “Hi, Milo!” they said.

“Hello!” Milo squeaked. “Let’s make art!”

Milo helped a little rabbit named Rosie squeeze out yellow paint. He helped a puppy named Pip find a round sponge. He helped a turtle named Tully open a sticky bottle of glitter glue. The art room buzzed with happy sounds—tap of scissors, swish of brushes, and soft hums as everyone worked.

Milo also had his own art to make. He wanted to paint a picture of a sunny cheese slice with little holes. He chose a small spot at the end of a low table. He taped down his paper, smooth-smooth-smooth. He picked a tiny brush that fit just right in his paw.

“Plop!” A bear cub named Benny sat next to Milo. Benny was big and bouncy. He liked to move fast and talk loud. “Hi, Milo! I like your spot,” Benny boomed. “I’ll paint here too!”

Milo smiled. “Okay,” he said. “We can share.”

Benny’s brush went swoosh-swoosh, splashing blue paint. Some drops landed on Milo’s paper. Splish! A dot of blue fell on the sunny cheese.

Milo blinked. His chest felt tight-tiny. He took a breath. In… and out. He remembered a little rhyme he learned from Miss Dot, the art teacher: My body is mine, my space is mine. Use kind words. Draw a line.

“Benny,” Milo said in a soft, brave voice, “I like to keep my paper clean. Please keep your brush over your own paper.”

Benny froze. His eyes went round. “Oh,” he said. “Oops. I didn’t see. I was too bouncy.”

“It’s okay,” Milo said. “We can still share. Let’s make a line between our papers.” He placed a long ruler in the middle. “This is my space. That is your space.”

Benny nodded. “Thanks, Milo.” He scooted his cup of paint to his side. “I will try again.”

They painted. Swish. Tap. Drip. This time, the drops stayed on Benny’s paper. Milo’s chest felt soft and calm again.

Soon, a crowd gathered. “Help! We need more paper!” Pip barked, wagging his tail. “The paper stack is gone.”

“I can help,” Milo said. He put down his brush and went to the supply shelf. The big paper stack was high, higher than his head. Milo reached up on tiptoe. He couldn’t get it.

He tried again. Tiptoe… reach… almost! The pile wobbled. A few sheets slid down like slow snow. “Oops,” Milo whispered. He felt a tiny twist of worry in his tummy. But he remembered resilience means keep trying, safely and calmly.

Milo looked around. “I can ask for help,” he told himself. “Asking is strong.”

“Miss Dot,” Milo called. “May I have help with the paper?”

Miss Dot smiled. “Yes, Milo. Great asking.” She slid the stack down to a lower shelf. “Thank you for volunteering,” she said. “You are helping everyone.”

Milo carried the paper to the table. “Here you go!” he sang. The kids cheered.

Back at his spot, Milo found Benny’s big fuzzy arm leaning across the ruler again. It was right over Milo’s little paint cup. The cup wiggled. “Wobble… wobble…”

“Benny,” Milo said, with a gentle hand on his own chest, “I need space for my arm. Please move your elbow to your side.”

Benny looked down. “Oh no! Sorry, Milo.” He pulled his elbow back. “Thank you for telling me. I forget sometimes.”

Milo smiled. “Thank you for listening.”

They painted and painted. Milo added tiny white dots to the cheese. Dip, dot, dip, dot. The cheese looked friendly and bright, even with one little blue splish. He decided the blue spot was a funny blueberry hole. He giggled.

When the art time ended, Miss Dot rang a soft bell. “Ting-a-ling! Helpers, please help clean up.”

“I will!” Milo said. He and Benny worked side by side. Milo wiped the table with a warm, soapy cloth. It smelled like lemons. Benny carried the water buckets carefully. “I can be careful,” Benny said. “I can keep to my space.”

“You’re doing great,” Milo said.

They put brushes bristles-up, stacked paper, and closed the glue with a twist-twist-twist. Pip and Rosie came to help too. “We like volunteering,” Rosie said. “It feels good!”

“It does,” Milo agreed. “We take care of our room and each other.”

Miss Dot clapped softly. “Friends, today we learned something special. Milo showed us how to use kind words to make a boundary. He told Benny what he needed. Benny listened and tried again. That is how friends grow.”

Milo felt warm in his heart. “I learned I can say, ‘Please stop,’ or ‘Please give me space,’” he said. “I can help others, and I can ask for help too.”

Benny smiled. “I learned I can listen and move my body back,” he said. “I can fix mistakes.”

Everyone held up their art. The room glowed with color and pride. Milo’s cheese with a blue dot made everyone laugh.

Miss Dot said, “When we speak up kindly, listen carefully, and keep trying, we make our art room safe and happy.”

Milo nodded. “My body is mine, my space is mine. Use kind words. Draw a line,” he whispered.

They all took a deep, happy breath. The art room smelled like lemons and crayons and smiles.

The End.
