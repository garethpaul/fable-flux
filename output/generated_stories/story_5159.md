# Ellie’s Attic Parade

Ellie was a gentle elephant calf with soft gray ears and bright, curious eyes. One sunny morning, Ellie climbed the creaky wooden steps to the quiet attic. The attic smelled like old books and warm dust. Sunbeams slid through a tiny window and made golden stripes on the floor.

“Hello, attic,” Ellie whispered. She liked to use a calm voice in small spaces. Her feet made soft thump-thump sounds on the floorboards.

Ellie opened a big trunk. Inside were colorful scarves, tiny bells, and a globe that could spin. The globe felt cool and smooth under her trunk. She tapped it gently and watched the blue oceans swirl. “So many places,” Ellie said. “So many friends I have not met yet.”

She wrapped a red scarf around her neck and put a jingly bell on her tail. “I will make a parade of the world,” Ellie giggled. “A quiet parade. I can practice being gentle and kind.”

Ellie set the globe on a box and spun it slowly. “Where are you, friends?” she asked. The globe stopped with her trunk on a green spot. “This is a faraway place,” Ellie thought. “What do friends there like to eat? What songs do they sing?”

Ellie decided to listen first. She sat down, took a deep breath in, and let it out slowly. “I can use my calm,” she said. The attic was quiet. She heard the soft tick-tick of a clock and the hush of wind at the window.

“Listening helps me learn,” Ellie said. “Listening is respect.”

She opened another box and found a small drum, a tiny wooden flute, and a round hat with bright patterns. “These things come from different places,” she whispered. “I will learn about them with care.”

Ellie touched the drum with one light tap. Tap. The sound was warm and gentle. She smiled. Her heart felt steady. “I will tap softly,” she promised. “I do not want to scare anyone.”

She made a little path with scarves and hats. “Welcome, world friends,” she said to the quiet room. “We can march together.”

Just then, Ellie heard a little squeak. A mouse peeked out from behind a book. “Hello,” Ellie said softly. “I am Ellie. I am making a world parade.”

The mouse twitched his whiskers. “I am Pip,” he squeaked. “I like parades. But loud sounds make me nervous.”

Ellie nodded. “I understand. We can be quiet and kind.” She showed Pip the tiny flute. “May I try a gentle note?”

Pip took a tiny breath. “Yes, please.”

Ellie pressed the flute to her trunk and blew the softest puff. The sound was a little whisper, like a happy bird. Pip’s eyes sparkled. “That feels nice,” he said.

Next, a moth fluttered near the window. Its wings made a pap-pap sound. “Hello, Moth,” Ellie said. “Would you like to join our parade?”

The moth rested on Ellie’s scarf. “I like light and soft sounds,” it seemed to hum.

Ellie smiled. “We will keep it soft.” She looked at the globe again. “Friends live all over the world,” she said. “They eat different foods. They wear different clothes. They speak different words. But we can all be kind. We can all listen.”

Pip nodded. “We can learn from each other.”

Ellie felt brave. “I can lead the parade,” she said. “I can try new things.” She took a deep breath to steady her big, excited body. “I use my calm. I use my kind.”

They started to march around the attic. The bell on Ellie’s tail made a tiny ching-ching. Pip held a small flag made from a blue scarf. The moth fluttered like a soft drumbeat.

Ellie sang a simple song: “Hello, friend, near and far. You are you, just as you are.”

She tapped the drum in time with her steps. Tap… tap… tap. She watched Pip’s ears and the moth’s wings. If they twitched too fast, she slowed down. “I can change my pace,” Ellie said. “That is self-regulation.”

They paused by the window. The sun felt warm on Ellie’s back. She saw the garden below, with green grass and tiny flowers. “After our attic parade,” Ellie said, “we can go outside and wave to the world.” She imagined children in other places waving too. She imagined sharing snacks, stories, and songs.

Pip smiled. “We can say, ‘Welcome.’”

Ellie touched the globe. “Welcome,” she echoed. “Welcome to everyone.”

She found a small book with many pictures. There were children wearing bright clothes, eating new foods, and playing games. Ellie pointed with her trunk. “Look, Pip,” she said. “They are different, and they are the same. They laugh. They play. They share.”

Pip sighed happily. “I like learning about friends far away.”

Ellie felt tall and proud inside. “I can be a good neighbor to the whole world,” she said. “I can be gentle. I can be brave. I can listen.”

They made one last loop around the attic. Ellie kept her steps steady and slow. She was careful not to bump boxes or books. The bell made a soft goodbye ching.

Then Ellie opened the attic window just a tiny bit. Fresh air slipped in, cool and sweet. She waved her trunk toward the sky. “Hello, world,” she said. “We respect you. We care about you.”

Pip waved his little scarf. The moth fluttered in a happy circle.

Ellie closed the window and put the drum, flute, and hats back in their box. She wrapped the scarves neatly. “We took turns,” she said. “We were careful. We learned.” She felt calm and confident.

Pip squeaked, “What did we learn today?”

Ellie smiled. “We learned that the world is full of friends. We show respect by listening and being gentle. We use our calm to help others feel safe. We believe in ourselves when we try new things. That is how we are good global neighbors.”

Pip clapped his tiny paws. “I feel brave too.”

Ellie nodded. “Tomorrow, we can learn a new hello.” She tried a soft, silly hello and giggled. Pip giggled too. The moth fluttered once, like a clap.

Ellie took one more deep breath. “I am Ellie. I am gentle. I am kind. I am part of the big, big world.”

And the attic felt warm and friendly, like a hug.

The End.
