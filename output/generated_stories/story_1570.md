# Milo’s Morning Helpers

Milo was a kind mouse who lived behind a warm kitchen wall. Every morning, the kitchen smelled like toast, cinnamon, and sunny oranges. The floor felt smooth and a little cool. Pots hummed softly. The window let in golden light.

Milo had a routine. He whispered, “First I wash. Then I sweep. Then I check for safe.” He smiled. “A good routine helps everyone.”

He washed his little paws in a tiny cup. Swish, swish! The water felt fresh. He dried them on a soft leaf. “Clean paws help keep food safe,” he said.

Next, he pulled out his straw broom. “Time to sweep,” Milo chirped. Sweep, sweep, sweep. Crumbs slid into a neat pile. “No slipping on crumbs today!” He pushed the pile into a small dustpan made from a walnut shell.

Milo peeked under the table. “Any sharp or sticky?” he asked. He found a bent paper clip. “This is not safe on the floor,” he said, and he dropped it into a little tin. He found a sticky strawberry dot. “Sticky can bring ants,” he said, wiping it up with care.

Just then, a voice called, “Good morning, Milo!” It was Penny the spoon, shining on the counter.

“Good morning, Penny,” said Milo. “I am doing my routine.”

“That sounds helpful,” Penny chimed. “The kitchen feels happy when it is safe and clean.”

Milo’s whiskers twitched. He heard a rattle. He turned and saw the recycling bin wobbling near the door. “Oh dear,” Milo said. “That bin is too full. It might tip.”

He walked to the bin and patted it. “We must sort and stack. That is civic responsibility,” he told himself. “We take care of our shared place.”

He pulled out a box and folded it flat. “Fold, fold, fold,” he sang. He stacked bottles by color—green, clear, and brown. Clink, clink. The sounds were bright, like tiny bells.

A potato sack on the shelf gave a rustle. “Hello, Milo,” said Spud the potato. “Building a neat stack?”

“Yes,” said Milo. “I am building a safe stack. Then the bin will not tip.”

But the bottles slid and rolled. Clink, clatter! One bottle tapped Milo’s tail. “Eek!” he squeaked, and then he took a breath. “It’s okay. I will try again. I can be careful.”

He looked around and found a rubber band. “This will help,” he said. He looped it around the bottles gently. “Secure and safe.” He smiled. “When we keep our area tidy, everyone is safer.”

Milo went back to his routine. “Check the stove,” he said. The stove was off. “Good. Off is safe when no one cooks.” He checked the floor for wet spots. “Dry is safe,” he whispered.

He heard a drip. Drip… drip… drip. “A leak?” Milo scampered over. A small pipe under the sink was dripping into a puddle.

“This needs attention,” he said. “I will build a little catcher and tell the big people.” He gathered a jar lid and a cloth. He slid the lid under the drip and set the cloth to soak it up. “This is a good temporary fix.”

Penny clinked. “You are very responsible, Milo.”

“Thank you,” said Milo. “But I must tell the cook. That is part of safety.” He rang a tiny bell on the counter—ding, ding! The cook came in, smiling.

“What’s that sound?” the cook asked gently.

Milo pointed with his paw. “Drip under the sink,” he said in his small mouse way. He stepped back to stay safe.

“Oh! Thank you,” said the cook. She knelt and turned the little valve. The drip stopped. “Good eyes, little helper.” She set a small sign that read, “Wet Floor—Walk Slow.”

Milo nodded. “Signs help everyone.”

He finished sweeping, humming his routine song. “Wash, sweep, check for safe. Sort, stack, care for our place.”

Then he noticed a tiny crack in the mouse-sized bridge he used to reach the counter—a bridge made of craft sticks and string. “This needs fixing,” he said.

He gathered safe tools: a dab of glue, two small sticks, and a soft cloth to wipe extra glue. He spoke to himself gently. “Slow and steady. Building takes patience.”

He placed one stick. Press. He counted softly, “One, two, three, four, five.” He placed the second stick. Press. “One, two, three, four, five.” He tied the string snug. Tug-tug. The bridge felt strong.

He tried to cross. Creak… pause… creak. “Almost,” he said. The string slipped a little. Milo did not give up. “I will keep trying. I can ask for help.”

“Penny, may I lean on you?” he asked.

“Of course,” Penny chimed.

With Penny steadying the bridge, Milo tied a double knot. He tested it again. No creak! He beamed. “Safe and strong.”

At last, the kitchen was neat and calm. The air smelled like fresh bread. Sunlight warmed Milo’s ears. The cook waved. “Thank you, little helper. Your routine keeps us safe.”

Milo’s heart felt big and bright. He stretched and yawned. “Routine helps me remember important jobs,” he said. “I can care for my home. I can care for others. I can stay safe.”

He tucked his broom away, washed his paws one more time, and whispered, “See you tomorrow, routine.”

The End.
