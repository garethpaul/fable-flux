# Sunny Squirrel’s Thank-You Mittens

In a warm kitchen, a small squirrel named Sunny stood on a stool. Sunny’s tail was fluffy like a duster, and her eyes were bright and curious. The kitchen smelled like toast and cinnamon. The kettle sang a soft, happy hiss.

“Good morning, warm kitchen!” Sunny chirped. “Thank you for keeping me cozy.”

Sunny was a resourceful squirrel. She liked to make things. She liked to fix things. Today, Sunny had a plan. She wanted to make thank-you mittens for her friends. They had helped her all week, and her heart felt full.

Sunny set out soft yarn, safe blunt needles, wooden buttons, and felt scraps. She patted the yarn. It was squishy and warm. She sniffed the cinnamon again and smiled.

“I will make something with care,” she said. “I will show my thanks.”

Knock, knock. The door opened. Bree the bluebird peeped in. “Cheep! I found your lost acorn yesterday,” Bree said. “Do you need anything today?”

“Thank you, Bree,” said Sunny. “Yes, I do need space to work. I will ask for quiet so I can pay attention.”

Sunny stood tall on her stool. “Friends,” she said kindly, “I am crafting. Please whisper. When I count stitches, I need quiet.”

Bree nodded. “Cheep-quiet,” she whispered.

Sunny began to knit: in, around, through, off. She hummed as the needles clicked, click, click. The yarn slid smooth across her paws. She counted softly. “One, two, three.” The mittens grew, one stitch at a time.

The kitchen was warm. The sun made golden squares on the floor. The toast popped! Sunny giggled. “Thank you, toaster,” she said. “You help me make snacks.”

Tap-tap. Mousy the mouse peeked from behind the flour jar. “Squeak! I brought a thimble for you,” Mousy said. “It kept my paw safe. Maybe it can help you, too.”

Sunny felt shy and glad. “Thank you, Mousy. That is very kind.” She slipped the thimble on and tried a stitch. “Oh! It does help. I learn a new skill today—how to use a thimble.”

Bree watched with bright eyes. “You are very good at making things,” Bree said.

Sunny smiled. “I am learning. I practice. I make, I fix, I try again.”

Sunny tied a neat knot and held up one mitten. It was small and soft and blue like Bree. She stitched a tiny felt feather on the cuff. “This is for you, Bree,” Sunny said. “Thank you for finding my acorn.”

Bree’s eyes sparkled. “Cheep! I feel warm in my heart and my wings,” she said.

The kettle began to bubble-bloop. Steam curled like little clouds. Sunny poured warm water into a mug and dipped a tea bag. Sweet spice filled the air. She took a careful sip. “Mmm. Thank you, tea. You help me rest,” she said.

Clatter! The wooden spoon toppled and bumped the yarn. The ball rolled across the counter and dropped to the floor. It bounced and bounced—boing, boing—toward a sticky spot of jam.

Sunny’s whiskers twitched. She took a deep breath. “It is okay,” she told herself. “I can fix this.”

She hopped down and caught the yarn just in time. She wiped the sticky spot with a warm, soapy cloth. “Thank you, cloth. You help me clean,” she said. She wound the yarn back into a neat ball and smiled. “I used my fixing skills.”

The door creaked again. Old Mr. Badger poked in his nose. “Oh! Hello, Sunny,” he rumbled. “I thought I smelled cinnamon.”

“Hello, Mr. Badger,” Sunny said. “Would you like a seat? The kettle is hot.”

“That is kind,” said Mr. Badger. He sat on a sturdy chair. “You are busy.”

“I am,” Sunny said. “I am making thank-you mittens. I am also learning to speak up. I need my space to work.”

Mr. Badger nodded. “A wise worker asks for what they need.”

Sunny started the second mitten, red like a tiny apple. She remembered how Mousy shared the thimble. She smiled and stitched a little felt cheese on the cuff. “This one is for Mousy. Thank you for keeping me safe,” she said.

She held the mittens up to the light. They glowed a little in the sunny kitchen. Her heart felt warm and light.

Bree fluffed her feathers. “I brought a small ribbon,” she whispered. “Would you like it?”

“Yes, please,” Sunny said. “Thank you for sharing.” She tied the ribbon into bows on each mitten. Then she pressed the buttons on, one-two, with gentle, careful paws. Click, click.

At last, Sunny put the mittens in tiny paper bags she had folded herself. She drew a heart and wrote: Thank you. She breathed in the cinnamon air. “I am thankful for my friends,” she said softly. “I am thankful for this warm kitchen. I am thankful for my own two paws that learn and make.”

Sunny handed Bree the blue mittens. “These are for you.”

“Cheep-cheep! Thank you, Sunny,” Bree said, slipping them over her wings.

Then Sunny found Mousy by the flour jar. “These are for you,” she said.

Mousy squeaked with joy. “I feel seen,” Mousy said. “Thank you, Sunny.”

Sunny looked at Mr. Badger. “And thank you for listening,” she said, pouring him tea. “Sometimes I need to say, ‘Please wait,’ and sometimes I need help. Asking is okay.”

Mr. Badger smiled. “I am grateful for your brave voice.”

Sunny wiggled her toes on the warm floor. She felt proud. She had made gifts. She had used her skills. She had asked for quiet. She had fixed a small mess. She had thanked her friends, the kitchen tools, and even the tea.

Sunny tapped her chest. “When I say thank you, my heart feels bigger,” she said.

And in the warm kitchen, the friends sipped, and stitched, and smiled together.

The lesson: Gratitude makes work brighter, voices kinder, and hands more skillful. Saying thank you, asking for what you need, and helping others make every day feel warm.

The End.
