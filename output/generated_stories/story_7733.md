# Poppy Panda Fixes the Wobble

Poppy Panda loved visiting Grandpa Chen’s garage workshop. It smelled like warm wood, lemon oil, and fresh paint. Sunlight made bright rectangles on the floor. Little tools hung on hooks, shiny and neat. Hammers slept in a row. Brushes wore colorful hats.

“Today,” said Grandpa Chen, smiling, “we will fix your wobbly wagon.”

Poppy’s red wagon wobbled and squeaked. “Squeeeak, squeeeak,” it sang.

Poppy felt a tickle of worry in her tummy. “What if I can’t do it?” she whispered.

Grandpa Chen patted her paw. “You are patient. You can learn. We will take it step by step.”

Poppy took a deep breath. “Okay. I can try.”

They rolled the wagon onto a soft mat. Poppy touched the cool metal handle. She felt the rough wooden sides. She listened to the squeak and the tiny click of a loose wheel.

“First, we look,” said Grandpa Chen. “We use our eyes and our ears.”

Poppy knelt close. “I see a loose bolt,” she said. “And I hear a squeak.”

“Good noticing,” said Grandpa. “That is leadership—looking carefully and helping us choose what to do.”

Poppy’s cheeks felt warm with pride. “I can lead,” she said softly.

Grandpa Chen handed her a small wrench. It felt heavy and smooth in her paw. “Lefty loosey, righty tighty,” he said.

Poppy turned the wrench. “Lefty… loosey.” The bolt wiggled. The wheel wobbled more. “Oh no!”

“It’s okay,” Grandpa said gently. “We are learning.”

Poppy took another breath. “I can be patient.” She tried again, turning the other way. “Righty… tighty.” The bolt clicked snug. Poppy smiled.

They tested the wheel. “Roll, roll,” went the wagon. No squeak!

But then, Poppy bumped a paint can with her fluffy tail. “Clink… splat!” Blue paint splashed onto the floor. A tiny drop landed on Grandpa’s shoe.

Poppy’s ears drooped. Her tummy felt wiggly. “I’m sorry,” she said, voice small. “I did not watch my tail.”

Grandpa looked at his blue shoe, then at Poppy. He smiled kindly. “Thank you for the honest apology. That is responsibility.”

Poppy nodded. “I will clean it.” She found a cloth. It felt soft and damp. She wiped the floor, slow and careful—wipe, wipe, wipe. She dabbed Grandpa’s shoe. “All clean,” she said.

“You led the fix. You made it right,” Grandpa said. “That shows strong self-confidence.”

Poppy blinked. “Self-confidence means I believe I can learn and help,” she said.

“Yes,” said Grandpa. “Even when you make a mistake, you try again.”

Poppy stood taller. “I can do the next step.”

They checked the other wheels. One was sticky with old mud. Poppy smelled the earthy dirt. “It smells like rain,” she giggled.

“Brush time,” said Grandpa. He gave her a little brush. Swish, swish, swish went the bristles. The wheel spun free.

“Now the handle,” Poppy said. She tapped it. “Clack.” It felt a little loose.

“Leader Poppy, what do we need?” asked Grandpa.

Poppy thought. She touched the bolts. She listened. She looked. “We need the small wrench and a tiny twist,” she decided.

Grandpa handed her the small wrench. “Show me.”

Poppy tightened the bolt. She kept her paws steady. She kept her breath slow. Tight. Snug. Still gentle.

The handle felt firm. “We did it!” Poppy cheered.

“You led the whole project,” Grandpa said. “You were patient and brave.”

Poppy felt warm and bright inside, like a little sun. “I believed in me,” she said.

Just then, a friend peeked in. It was tiny Tiko Turtle with a toy car in his hands. “Hi,” he said shyly. “My car wheel fell off.”

Poppy looked at Grandpa. Grandpa nodded. “Leader Poppy?”

Poppy smiled at Tiko. “We can help. First, we look and listen.”

Tiko held the car close. Poppy listened. No squeak, just a quiet rattle. She looked. A tiny peg was out.

“We need a gentle push,” Poppy said. She showed Tiko the peg. “Want to try?”

Tiko’s hands trembled. “I’m scared.”

Poppy remembered her own worry. She said, “It’s okay. We can be patient. I will help you. You can believe in you too.”

Together, they pressed. Click! The wheel popped on.

Tiko beamed. “I did it!”

Poppy clapped. “You did it! You led your fix.”

Tiko giggled. “Thank you.”

Poppy said, “You’re welcome.” She looked at Grandpa. “Self-confidence grows when we try.”

Grandpa nodded. “And when we say sorry and make it right, we show responsibility. When we guide others kindly, we show leadership.”

They rolled Poppy’s wagon outside. The sun felt warm on their faces. The wagon wheels hummed, smooth and happy.

Poppy pulled the wagon. “No wobble!” she laughed. The air smelled like cut grass. Birds sang, “Cheep, cheep.”

Grandpa waved. “I am proud of you, Leader Poppy.”

Poppy waved back. “I am proud of me too.”

She whispered to her heart, “I can learn. I can help. I can lead.”

The End.
