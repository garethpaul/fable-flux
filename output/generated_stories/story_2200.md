# Birdie Sings in the Attic

In a quiet attic, dust danced in the sunbeams. Soft boxes sat in neat rows. A little singing bird named Birdie lived by the small round window. Birdie had bright blue feathers and a warm, clear voice. When Birdie sang, the attic hummed like a happy hive.

“Tweet, tweet! Good morning!” Birdie chirped each day, stretching tiny wings. The air smelled like old books and pine wood. The floor felt smooth and cool under Birdie’s feet. Birdie loved the attic. It was calm. It was safe. It was home.

One day, Birdie wanted to do something big. “I want to make a singing place,” Birdie said. “A tidy, cozy stage for songs.” Birdie looked around. There were ribbons, buttons, pencils, and hats. There were baskets, blankets, and a small toy drum.

Birdie hopped to a soft wool blanket. “This can be my stage,” Birdie said. Birdie fluffed the edges. Birdie set a ribbon as a border. Birdie placed the toy drum to the side. The drum went “pat, pat” when Birdie tapped it.

But there was a problem. “My things get mixed up,” Birdie sighed. Buttons rolled under boxes. Ribbons tangled. Pencils tipped. Birdie felt a squiggle in the tummy. “I want to do this myself. I can try,” Birdie said. Birdie took a deep breath. “Tweet. I can make a plan.”

Birdie remembered a word: plan means a smart way to do things. That is a strategy. Birdie said, “Step by step.”

Step one: sort. Birdie picked up buttons. Click, click. Into a small tin they went. Birdie picked up ribbons. Swish, swish. Into a basket they went. Birdie lined up pencils. Tap, tap. Into a cup they went. Birdie was careful and kind to the things.

Step two: label. Birdie found sticky notes. Birdie drew a button picture for the tin. Birdie drew a ribbon bow for the basket. Birdie drew a pencil for the cup. “Now I know where things go,” Birdie said. Birdie felt proud and steady. Independence felt like sunshine in Birdie’s chest.

Just then, the attic door creaked. Friends peeked in. There was Minka the mouse, with soft whiskers. There was Pip the puppy, with waggy paws. There was Lolo the ladybug, tiny and bright.

“Can we listen?” asked Minka.

“Can I drum?” asked Pip.

“Can I hum?” asked Lolo.

Birdie smiled. “Yes. Everyone can be part of the song.” Inclusion means we welcome friends. Birdie also remembered fairness. “But we must take turns,” Birdie said. “And we must keep things tidy so we can all play.”

Birdie showed the labels. “Buttons here. Ribbons here. Pencils here. Drum sits here. We put things back when we are done.”

Minka nodded. “That is fair.”

Pip wagged. “I can wait my turn.”

Lolo buzzed. “I can help clean up.”

They began. Birdie stood on the soft blanket stage. Sun warmed Birdie’s feathers. Birdie sang, “La, la, la!” The sound was clear and sweet. Minka shook a tiny bell, ting ting. Lolo hummed, “Mmm-mmm.” Pip tapped the drum, “Pat, pat, pat,” very softly.

Then Pip’s paw bumped the pencils. Clatter! The pencils rolled. Minka’s bell fell over. Lolo’s wings fluttered fast. The quiet attic felt busy and messy. Birdie’s heart thumped. Birdie wanted to call for help. But Birdie paused. “I can try first,” Birdie whispered. “I can use my plan.”

Birdie took a slow breath. In. Out. “Friends,” Birdie said in a calm, warm voice, “let’s use our strategy. We can fix this together.”

Step one: stop and look. Birdie looked at the pencils. “They go in the cup.”

Step two: take turns. “Pip, you hold the cup. Minka, you roll the pencils. Lolo, you cheer us on.”

“Go, team!” hummed Lolo.

The pencils made little tap sounds as they went into the cup. Pip held steady. Minka rolled carefully. Birdie guided with a wing. Together, they were organized. The attic grew calm again.

“Thank you,” Birdie said. “We did it.” Birdie felt that sunshine feeling again. Birdie had stayed calm. Birdie had used a strategy. Birdie had been independent and also asked friends to help. Independence means trying your best and making smart choices. It can also mean knowing when to include others.

They started the music once more. Birdie sang about colors and clouds. Minka played the bell in time. Lolo hummed sweet and small. Pip tapped the drum gently, pat-pat-pat. The sounds blended like a warm blanket of music. The attic smelled like cookies now, because Minka had brought a tiny crumb to share later.

When the song ended, Birdie said, “Now we clean.” They used the labels. They put each item back. It was fair and easy. Birdie felt tall on the inside.

Minka squeaked, “Your plan is smart.”

Pip wagged, “You led us kindly.”

Lolo glowed, “You included everyone.”

Birdie fluttered. “I learned that I can do things by myself, step by step. I can also welcome friends, keep things fair, and stay organized. That makes the music strong and the attic happy.”

The sun slipped, and the attic grew cozy. Birdie curled by the round window. The day had been busy and bright. Birdie hummed a soft good-night tune, proud and peaceful in a tidy, friendly place.

The End.
