# Ollie Otter and the Attic Helpers

Ollie was a playful otter with soft whiskers and bright, curious eyes. One rainy afternoon, Ollie padded up the creaky steps to the quiet attic. The air felt cool, and it smelled like old wood and lavender. Dust sparkled in the soft light from a tiny window. “What a cozy place,” Ollie whispered, swishing his tail.

Grandma lived downstairs, humming a gentle tune. Ollie loved Grandma. She told stories and made warm oat cookies. Today, Grandma asked, “Ollie, dear, could you tidy the attic? Just the toys and books. Please and thank you.”

Ollie puffed his chest. “I can help! I am a Super Helper!” He picked up a small broom. It felt smooth in his paws. Swish, swish. The broom made a friendly sound on the floor.

As Ollie worked, he found a box of shiny buttons, a red scarf that smelled like cinnamon, and a little bell that chimed, ting-ting. “Ooh!” Ollie giggled. “Treasure!” He wanted to play right away.

Just then, his friends Pop the pigeon and Tilly the tiny turtle peeked through the attic door. “Hi, Ollie!” cooed Pop. “Can we play in here?”

Ollie blinked. He liked sharing, but the attic was quiet and small. The boxes were stacked high. Some things were special to Grandma. Ollie remembered Grandma’s kind voice: “Please and thank you.”

Ollie took a deep breath. The air tasted dusty but sweet. “We can play after I finish my chore,” he said. “And we can only touch the toy box and the books. The other boxes are private.”

Pop tilted his head. “Private?”

Tilly nodded slowly. “Private means we don’t touch unless we are told it’s okay.”

Ollie smiled. “Yes. Thank you for listening.”

Pop flapped his wings happily. “We can help! I will put books on the shelf.” He picked up a picture book with a yellow sun on the cover.

Tilly tugged a little basket of blocks. “I will stack these blocks. Click, clack,” she said, giggling.

Ollie tied back the red scarf and swept again. Swish, swish. The bell chimed, ting-ting, when he moved the box. Together they worked. They hummed a little song: “Help and care, here and there. Please and thanks, everywhere.”

Soon, Pop saw a pretty music box on a tall shelf. It had tiny flowers painted on it. “Ooo,” Pop cooed. “Can I open it?”

Ollie looked up. His whiskers twitched. He wanted to see it too. But he remembered his words. “That is Grandma’s special box,” he said. “It is private. Let’s not touch it without Grandma here.”

Pop drooped for a moment. “But I am curious.”

Tilly patted Pop’s wing. “Curious and careful,” she said. “We can ask.”

Ollie nodded. “We can ask Grandma. If she says no, we say, ‘Thank you for telling us.’”

They kept tidying. Ollie folded the red scarf neatly. Pop dusted the window with a soft cloth. Tilly arranged the buttons by color—blue, green, gold. The attic felt calm and warm now. The light looked brighter. The floor smelled like clean wood.

When they were done, Ollie called, “Grandma, we finished!”

Grandma climbed the stairs slowly, her slippers whispering on each step. She peeked into the attic and smiled. Her eyes crinkled like happy stars. “Oh my,” she said softly. “It looks lovely. Thank you, helpers.”

Ollie’s chest felt fizzy and light. “You are welcome,” he said. “We only touched the toys and books. And we saved questions for you.”

Pop pointed to the music box. “May we open it with you?”

Grandma smiled and placed a gentle hand on the box. “Thank you for asking. This is special to me. Today, I would like to keep it closed.” She looked at their faces. “Is that okay?”

Pop took a breath and nodded. “Okay. Thank you for telling us.”

Tilly smiled. “We can listen another day if you choose.”

Grandma hugged them all, soft and warm. “I am grateful for your helping hands and your careful hearts.”

Ollie’s whiskers wiggled. “We are grateful for you, Grandma,” he said. “Thank you for trusting us.”

Grandma handed them each a small oatmeal cookie. It was chewy and sweet, with a hint of cinnamon. “A little thank-you treat,” she said. The bell chimed when she moved the tray, ting-ting, like happy music.

They sat on a soft old blanket. The attic felt peaceful. Ollie took a bite and licked a crumb from his paw. “Helping feels good,” he said.

Tilly nodded. “Chores make our space neat. Neat feels nice.”

Pop fluffed his feathers. “Asking before touching keeps things safe.”

Ollie looked around the tidy attic. “And saying please and thank you shows gratitude,” he said. “Gratitude makes hearts warm.”

Grandma patted his head. “That is a wonderful lesson, Ollie.”

Ollie beamed. “Today I learned: do my chores with care, ask before I touch, and say thank you for help and kind answers.”

Pop and Tilly chimed together, “Please and thank you, every day!”

They all laughed, and the attic hummed with happy quiet. Outside, the rain slowed to a soft patter. Inside, hearts felt full and thankful.

The End.
