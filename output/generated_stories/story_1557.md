# The Little Builder in the Treehouse

Sunlight peeked through the leaves. Birds chirped softly. In a cozy treehouse, a determined rabbit named Remy twitched his nose and smiled. The wood smelled warm and clean. The floor felt smooth under his paws. Remy loved his treehouse. He loved to make things, fix things, and dream up new ideas.

“Today,” Remy said, “I will make a leaf lantern. It will shine like a soft moon.” His whiskers wiggled with excitement.

Remy gathered fallen leaves that were bright and crisp. He picked red leaves, yellow leaves, and one big orange leaf that smelled sweet, like pumpkins. He hummed a happy tune. Tap, tap, tap went his little paw as he thought and planned.

Remy set his leaves on a flat board. He found a clean jar from the shelf. It clinked gently. He found a brush and a small pot of clear glue that smelled like apples. “I can do this,” he said. “I am brave. I am honest. I am kind to trees.”

Just then, Remy’s friend, Tessa the turtle, peeked up the ladder. “Hello, Remy,” she said in a soft, slow voice. “What are you making?”

“A leaf lantern!” Remy said. “It will glow in the dark. But I will use only leaves from the ground, not from the tree. That keeps the tree strong.” He patted the treehouse wall. It felt rough and friendly.

“That is smart,” said Tessa. “You are caring for nature. That is called conservation.”

Remy nodded. “Yes! I will make art and care for the tree.”

He brushed a thin layer of glue on the jar. It was sticky and cool. He pressed the red leaf on the jar. It crinkled softly. “Oh!” Remy giggled. “It looks like a little sunset.”

A breeze whooshed through the open window. The orange leaf fluttered. It slipped off the board and sailed out the window.

“My special leaf!” cried Remy. His heart thumped. He looked out. The leaf had landed on a high branch far away.

Tessa looked too. “That is a big jump,” she said.

Remy took a deep breath. “I can be brave,” he whispered. His paws tingled. The branch swayed with a soft shhh sound. Remy climbed out and reached. The sun felt warm on his ears. The bark was rough and crumbly under his tiny paws.

But the leaf was still far. Remy felt wobbly. He climbed back in. “I do not like that,” he said quietly. “I feel scared.”

Tessa nodded. “That is honest,” she said. “Telling the truth helps.”

Remy thought and thought. He tapped his paw again. Tap… tap… tap. Then his eyes lit up. “I have an idea!” he said. “I can make a leaf catcher. I can use a string, a stick, and a tiny basket.”

He looked around the cozy treehouse. There was a little basket from berries, a long string, and a smooth stick. He tied the basket to the stick with the string. He tugged the knot. It held tight.

Remy stood at the window. “Leaf catcher, leaf catcher,” he sang in a small, brave voice, “reach and scoop and bring it back.” He stretched the stick out, slow and steady. The basket swayed. The wind whispered. The leaves made a gentle rustle, like paper kisses.

Scoop! The basket slid under the orange leaf. The leaf slid in with a soft shhh. Remy grinned so big his whiskers bounced. “I did it!” he cheered.

“You were brave,” said Tessa. “And you used your creativity.”

Remy pulled the basket inside and hugged the leaf to his chest. It felt thin and soft. “Thank you, tree, for this gift,” he said. He put the orange leaf on the jar with a careful touch. He added the yellow leaf. He added a tiny green leaf shaped like a heart.

When the jar was covered, Remy placed a little candle light inside. He clicked it on. The lantern glowed like warm honey. The colors shined on the walls: red, orange, yellow, and green. The treehouse looked cozy and safe. It smelled like apples and leaves.

Tessa sighed happily. “It is beautiful.”

Remy’s ears drooped for a moment. “Tessa,” he said, “I must tell you something. I broke a little twig on the branch when I tried to reach. I did not mean to. I am sorry, tree.”

He patted the bark again. “I will be gentle. I will help you.” Remy took a tiny jar with water and a handful of seeds he had saved. “Tomorrow I will plant seeds and bring water to the soil. I will clean up the leaves on the floor of the treehouse, too. I will not waste glue. That is conservation.”

Tessa smiled. “That is honest and kind.”

Remy smiled back. His chest felt warm and proud. “Today I learned three things,” he said. “Be creative. Tell the truth. Be brave and care for nature.”

They sat together and watched the lantern glow. Outside, the wind hummed a lullaby. The sky turned soft purple. Remy wiggled his toes and felt calm. He whispered, “I can solve problems. I can try again. I can help the earth.”

The lantern shimmered. The tree felt strong and happy around them. Remy’s determined heart beat steady and light.

“Good night, leaf lantern,” said Remy. “Thank you, tree. Thank you, ideas.”

Tessa yawned. “Good night, brave builder.”

The End.
