# Joey Roo Helps the Backyard

The sun was warm and golden in the sunny backyard. Flowers smelled sweet like honey. Bees buzzed, “Bzz, bzz,” and a soft breeze tickled the leaves. A joyful kangaroo joey named Joey Roo bounced out the door with a happy hop, hop, hop.

“Good morning, backyard!” Joey Roo sang. His tail swished, and his ears perked up. He loved mornings. They felt fresh and sparkly.

Joey Roo looked around. He saw colorful leaves scattered on the grass. He heard the gate creak, “Creeeak.” He saw a little paper cup rolling by the garden bed. The cup made a whispery sound, skritch, skritch.

Joey Roo paused. His smile wiggled. “Hmm,” he said. “This looks messy.” His chest felt tight and squiggly. Was it worry? He took a deep breath. In. Out. The air smelled like mint from Mama’s herb pot. The tight feeling softened.

Mama Roo came outside with a watering can. “Morning, Joey,” she said, splish-splashing water on the tomato plants.

“Mama,” said Joey Roo, “the backyard looks tired. The leaves are everywhere. The gate is squeaky. A cup is rolling and rolling.”

Mama Roo nodded. “You noticed a problem. That is caring. Caring is a big part of helping our home. Helping our home is civic responsibility.”

“Civic… re-spon-sa-bi-li-tee,” Joey Roo said slowly. The big word felt bouncy in his mouth.

“It means we take care of our shared places,” said Mama. “We can do small jobs. Small jobs make a big difference.”

Joey Roo’s eyes shone. “I want to help! I can try by myself.”

“That’s independence,” said Mama with a smile. “I’ll be nearby if you need me.”

Joey Roo put on his little sun hat. He felt proud. He hopped to the rolling cup. “Hello, cup,” he said kindly. “You belong in the bin.” He picked it up. Crinkle, crinkle. He carried it to the recycling bin and dropped it in. Clonk!

“That felt good,” Joey Roo giggled. His heart felt warm, like sunshine inside.

Next, Joey Roo looked at the leaves. They were red, yellow, and brown. They smelled earthy, like rainy dirt. A rake leaned by the fence. Joey Roo took a deep breath. “I can do this,” he whispered. He gripped the rake. His paws felt the wood, smooth and warm.

Scritch, scritch, scritch went the rake across the grass. Leaves swished together. “You can be a neat pile,” Joey Roo told them. He made one pile, then two, then three small piles. Sweat tickled his forehead. He felt a little tired.

Joey Roo paused. His tummy rumbled. A tiny worry popped up. “What if I can’t finish?” he thought. He put his hand on his chest and said, “It’s okay to feel tired. I can take a little rest.” He sat in the shade for a moment. The breeze cooled his cheeks. He drank a sip of water from his cup. Ahhh.

“I’m ready again,” he said softly. He hopped back up and raked the last leaves. Scritch! The piles looked like soft, crunchy pancakes. He carried the leaves to the compost bin. Rustle, rustle. “Good job, leaves,” he said. “You will help new plants grow.”

Then Joey Roo went to the gate. “Creeeak,” it complained. He touched the hinge. It felt dry and dusty. Mama Roo came over with a small bottle of oil.

“You may use a tiny drop,” she said. “This is a careful job.”

Joey Roo watched closely. Drip. “Now you try moving the gate,” Mama said.

Joey Roo pushed gently. The gate whispered, “Squeak… squeak…” and then, “Shh.” It swung smoothly. Joey Roo’s eyes grew wide. “I fixed it! I helped our backyard!”

Mama Roo clapped softly. “You noticed, you planned, you tried, and you cared. That is civic responsibility. You also listened to your feelings and took a rest. That is emotional awareness. And you did many steps by yourself. That is independence.”

Joey Roo’s tail thumped happily. He looked around. The grass was neat and green. The air smelled clean and bright. The bees buzzed, “Bzz,” and the gate was quiet. The backyard felt calm.

Neighbor Bird landed on the fence. “Cheep!” he chirped. “Nice yard!”

“Thank you, Bird,” said Joey Roo. “We all share this place. When we help, everyone feels good.”

Mama Roo handed Joey Roo a small sticker shaped like a star. It was silver and shiny. “A thank-you for helping the home we share,” she said.

Joey Roo pressed the star on his hat. It felt smooth and cool. “I am a helper,” he said proudly. “Tomorrow I can check the sidewalk for chalk drawings and wipe the table after snack. Small jobs, big difference!”

Mama Roo winked. “Yes. Helping our home is something we do every day.”

Joey Roo took one more deep breath. The sun warmed his fur. He felt brave, kind, and strong.

“What did I learn today?” he asked out loud.

He smiled and said, “I learned that caring for our shared places is important. I can notice a problem, use my feelings to guide me, and try my best. When I help, the world feels happy.”

The End.
