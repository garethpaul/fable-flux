# Rooftop Hops and Helping Words

Sunny was a joyful kangaroo joey with soft brown fur and big bouncy feet. One bright morning, Sunny hopped up the stairs to the rooftop garden of the tall building where she lived. The roof felt warm under her toes. A gentle breeze brushed her ears. She sniffed the air—mmm, the tomatoes smelled sweet and the mint smelled cool.

“Hello, sky!” Sunny laughed. “I love this rooftop.”

Up on the roof stood Mr. Finch, the friendly building caretaker. He had a blue hat and a watering can. “Good morning, Sunny,” he said. “The garden needs a little help today.”

Sunny’s tail wiggled. “I can help! I like to help.”

Mr. Finch smiled. “That’s kind of you. We have a small problem. The garden club is coming after lunch to plant seeds, but the signs blew away in the wind. We need new signs so everyone knows where to plant. And the watering can is stuck. It’s very heavy.”

Sunny’s eyes grew round. She wanted to help so much. She felt a little flutter in her tummy. She wanted to fix everything right away. “I will do it!” she said.

Sunny hopped to the shelves. She saw a big stack of sticks and a pile of paper. She grabbed a crayon with her little paws. She drew a quick carrot on one paper and taped it to a stick. “This must be the carrot bed,” she said, setting the sign by the soft soil.

She drew a tomato, too, a bright red circle. She put that sign by a row of tall green plants. “Done!” Sunny chirped.

Then she tugged the watering can. “Unngh!” It did not move. She pulled harder. “Oof!” The can scraped, and a tiny splash sprinkled her toes. The water felt cool, and Sunny giggled, but the can still sat heavy.

Mr. Finch watched kindly. “Sunny, you are trying so hard. Thank you. But we should make sure the signs match the beds. The garden club has a map.”

“A map?” Sunny blinked. She had not thought about a map. Her ears twitched. She wanted to show she could help. She wanted to show she knew things. But she remembered: it is okay to ask. Asking is smart.

Sunny took a deep breath. “Mr. Finch,” she said, “I need to know more. Can you please show me the map? I want to do it right.”

Mr. Finch nodded and opened a small book. Inside was a picture with shapes and words. “Here it is. Carrots go in Bed A. Tomatoes are over here. Mint is in the blue pots.”

Sunny looked and listened carefully. She traced the beds with her paw. “Oh! I put the tomato sign in the carrot bed. I made a mix-up.” She felt her cheeks grow warm, but Mr. Finch chuckled softly.

“It’s okay,” he said. “You asked, and now you know. That’s good communication, Sunny.”

Sunny smiled. “Let’s fix it!” She hopped to each bed and moved the signs to match the map. She talked as she worked, saying the names out loud. “Carrots here. Tomatoes there. Mint in the blue pots.” Her words helped her remember. Her words helped Mr. Finch know what she was doing, too.

Next, Sunny looked at the big watering can. It still seemed too heavy. She pressed her paw to the cool metal. “Mr. Finch,” she said, “can we do this together? Maybe we can pour it into smaller jugs.”

“Great idea,” said Mr. Finch. He brought two little pitchers. “You use one, and I’ll use one.”

They tilted the big can slowly. Water splished and splashed, shining in the sun like tiny stars. Sunny’s pitcher filled up. It felt just right in her paws. She hummed a happy tune and watered the mint. The mint leaves shivered and smelled extra fresh. “Ahh,” Sunny breathed. “Minty!”

“Lovely,” said Mr. Finch. “You took initiative and you asked for help. That’s smart.”

Just then, a door creaked open. The garden club children came up to the roof with hats and trowels. “Hi!” they called. “We’re ready to plant!”

Sunny bounced over. “Welcome to the rooftop! The signs are ready,” she said. “We used the map to put them in the right places. If you need something, please tell me. I’m happy to help.”

A little boy pointed to a packet. “Where do we plant the beans?”

Sunny checked the map. “Beans go next to the carrots,” she said. “Let’s read the packet together.” She sounded out the simple words with the boy. “Plant two seeds. Cover. Water.” They giggled when a puff of soil tickled their noses.

“Thank you, Sunny,” said a girl in a sun hat. “You explain things so clearly.”

Sunny felt proud and warm inside. “When we talk and listen, we understand,” she said. “Talking helps us help each other.”

By afternoon, the rooftop was full of soft sounds: pat-pat of soil, sip-sip of water, buzz-buzz of a bee, and happy chatter. The signs stood straight. The seeds were tucked in. Mr. Finch clapped. “Wonderful work, everyone.”

Sunny looked at the sky, now golden and gentle. She breathed the green smell of plants and the sweet smell of tomatoes. She thought about her morning. She had rushed. She had made a mix-up. But then she had asked, listened, and shared her words. Together, they solved the problem.

She bounced once, lightly. “I learned something,” she said. “When I’m not sure, I can ask. When I know a little, I can listen for more. When I want to help, I can use my words. Communication helps us help.”

Mr. Finch tipped his blue hat. “And that is real knowledge,” he said. “Thank you for volunteering, Sunny.”

Sunny grinned. “Anytime! Rooftop helpers, hip-hip-hooray!”

The End.
