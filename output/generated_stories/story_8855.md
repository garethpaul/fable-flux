# Poppy Penguin and the Cozy Guest Room

Poppy was a cheerful penguin with bright eyes and a waddly walk. One sunny morning, Poppy slid down the hallway on her smooth belly—shoop!—and stopped at a door she didn’t know very well. It was the guest room. The door was open just a little, and a soft, warm light glowed inside.

“Hmm,” Poppy chirped. “What is in there? I am curious!”

She peeked in. The guest room smelled like clean sheets and lemon soap. The carpet felt soft like moss under her little penguin feet. There was a big, puffy bed with a blue blanket that looked like a calm ocean. A small lamp made a cozy, golden puddle of light. On the table, a tiny fan hummed, whirr-whirr, like a friendly bee. In the corner, a suitcase waited, quiet and closed.

Poppy waddled in. “Hello, room,” she said. “I want to learn about you.”

Mama Penguin called from down the hall, “Poppy, remember to listen and be careful. The guest room is for visitors.”

“I will listen, Mama,” Poppy called back. “I will be careful and kind.”

Poppy stood very still. She looked with big, curious eyes. She listened. She sniffed. She touched the blanket with one flipper. It felt cool and smooth. “Ooh,” she giggled. “Like an ice slide!”

On the bedside table, Poppy saw a little notepad and a pencil. There were shapes drawn on the top page—circles and squares. “I wonder what this is for,” she said. “Maybe our guest likes to make lists.”

Poppy smiled. “I can be helpful. I can get the room ready!”

She remembered Mama’s words about listening. Poppy took a breath. “First, I will ask.” She waddled to the door. “Mama, may I help in the guest room? I want to be careful.”

Mama peeked in and smiled. “Yes, Poppy. You may help. Use your gentle flippers. If you are not sure, ask me.”

“Okay!” said Poppy. Her heart felt warm and proud.

Poppy began with the pillows. She patted them, pat-pat, to make them fluffy. The feathers made a soft whoosh, and a tiny puff of scent like fresh clouds floated up. She smoothed the blanket—swish, swish—until it was flat and neat.

Next, she looked at the suitcase in the corner. “I am curious,” she whispered. “What is inside?” She remembered Mama’s words again: If you are not sure, ask me.

“Mama,” Poppy called, “may I open the suitcase?”

Mama shook her head kindly. “Not that, dear. The suitcase is private. Good listening.”

Poppy nodded. “Okay. I will not open it.” She felt proud that she listened, even when she really, really wanted to peek.

On the windowsill, Poppy saw a little plant with tiny leaves like green hearts. The leaves drooped a little. “Maybe the plant is thirsty,” she said.

She waddled to the kitchen and got a small cup of water. It was cool and splashy. She came back slowly, step by careful step. “I will pour a little,” she told herself. “Just a little.” Drip, drip. The soil drank it up. The leaves looked happier already.

Poppy looked around. “What else can I do?” She saw some books on a shelf. One book was sideways. She straightened it, tap-tap, so the covers lined up like friends in a row.

She turned and almost bumped the little fan. It wobbled. “Oh!” Poppy steadied it quickly. The fan hummed again, whirr-whirr. “Sorry, fan,” she said. “I will be more careful.”

Soon, the house door opened. Auntie Wren, their guest, stepped inside. She wore a bright scarf that smelled like oranges and sunshine. “Hello, Poppy!” she sang. “What a cozy home.”

Poppy waddled forward. “Hello, Auntie Wren! I helped in your room. I fluffed the pillows and cared for the plant. I did not open your suitcase, because that is private.”

Auntie Wren’s eyes twinkled. “Thank you, Poppy. You are curious and kind. You listened, and you helped. May I see the room?”

They went together. Auntie Wren touched the smooth blanket. “So neat,” she said. She sniffed the air. “So fresh.” She looked at the plant. “So happy!”

Poppy’s cheeks felt warm with joy. “I like to learn,” she said. “When I am curious, I look, and I listen, and I ask.”

Mama smiled. “That is independence, Poppy. You tried to help all by yourself, and you knew when to ask. That is smart.”

Auntie Wren opened her notepad and drew a little star. “This star is for Poppy,” she said. “One star for curiosity. One star for listening. One star for helpfulness.”

Poppy clapped her flippers. Clap, clap! “Thank you!”

They all sat on the bed. The blanket felt cool. The lamp hummed softly, and the fan whispered in the corner. Auntie Wren told a story about a penguin who found a glowing shell by the sea and asked good questions to learn what it was. Poppy listened, eyes wide, mind buzzing with gentle wonder.

That night, as Poppy tucked herself in, she whispered, “Curiosity helps me learn. Listening keeps me safe. Helping makes hearts happy.”

Mama kissed her forehead. “Yes, sweet Poppy. Keep asking, keep listening, keep helping.”

Poppy smiled and closed her eyes. Her dreams were full of soft blankets, little plants, and shiny stars for being curious and kind.

The End.
