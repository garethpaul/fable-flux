# Leo Roars for Help

In a cozy study corner, sunlight made warm squares on the floor. Soft crayons waited in a tin. A little desk held big paper and a book with bright letters. A brave little lion cub named Leo sat on a cushion. His paws were small, his mane was fuzzy, and his eyes were bright and curious.

Today, Leo wanted to learn his letters. He sniffed the paper. It smelled like trees and a little like glue. He touched the crayons. They felt smooth and cool.

“I can do it,” Leo said. His voice was small but strong. “I am brave.”

Leo opened the book. The first letter was A. “A is for apple,” he read. He drew a red apple with a green leaf. “Crunch,” he giggled, pretending to take a bite. He could almost taste sweet juice.

The next letter was B. “B is for ball,” he said. He drew a round blue ball and bounced it with his paw. “Boing, boing!” The rubbery sound in his mind made him smile.

Then came C. “C is for cake,” he whispered. He drew a cake with rainbow sprinkles. He imagined warm vanilla and sugary frosting. His tummy rumbled. “Yum!”

Leo felt proud. His tail swished. But when he turned the page, the letters looked twisty and tight. D, E, F sat close together like a knotted string.

Leo tried to sound them out. “D…E…F…” The sounds tangled. His paws felt big and clumsy. His whiskers drooped. The room felt too quiet. The ticking clock sounded loud and sharp.

“I don’t get it,” he said softly. His chest felt heavy, like a stone. He wanted to hide under the cushion. He wanted to roar and make the letters go away.

Just then, Mama Lion stepped into the study corner. She smelled like sunshine and clean grass. “Hello, brave cub,” she said in a gentle voice. “How is learning today?”

Leo blinked. His eyes felt watery. “The letters are hard,” he said. “I feel stuck. I want to learn. But I need help.”

Mama’s eyes were warm. “Thank you for telling me,” she said. “That is brave. Asking for help is a strong roar.”

Leo looked up. “It is?” He sniffled and sat taller. His ears perked.

“It is,” Mama said. She sat beside him and wrapped her tail around them both. “We can try a new way. We can go slow. We can make it fun. You can tell me what you need.”

Leo thought for a moment. He took a deep breath. He felt the cool air go in and the warm air go out. “I need a break,” he said. “And a sip of water. And a song.”

Mama smiled. “Good self-advocacy,” she said. She poured a tiny cup of water. Leo sipped. The water felt fresh and kind in his mouth.

They took a short wiggle break. Leo shook his paws. He wiggled his tail. He stretched like a cat in sunshine. He felt lighter.

“Ready?” Mama asked.

“Ready,” Leo said. “But can we use colors?”

“Yes,” said Mama. “Let’s give each letter a color.”

She pointed to D. “Let’s make D a drum. What color is your drum?”

“Red!” Leo said. He colored D red and tapped the table gently. “D-d-drum,” he chanted, tapping a steady beat. The soft thump-thump made him giggle.

They moved to E. “E can be an elephant,” Mama said. “What does an elephant say?”

“Eeeeee!” Leo trumpeted in a squeaky voice and colored E gray. He drew big ears and felt the crayon scratch the paper.

Then F. “F is a feather,” Leo whispered. He colored F yellow and tickled his nose with a real soft feather from the craft box. “F-f-feather,” he said, sneezing a tiny sneeze. “Achoo!” He laughed, and Mama laughed too.

The letters did not look so tight now. They looked friendly. Leo’s heart felt light. He could hear birds chirping outside, like tiny bells.

“Leo,” Mama said, “how do you feel?”

Leo put a paw on his chest. “I feel proud. I asked for help. I said what I needed. You listened. We found a way.”

Mama nodded. “We accept all the feelings that come. It is okay to feel stuck. It is okay to take breaks. It is okay to learn in your way.”

Leo thought about his friends. “Some friends like quiet. Some like songs. Some like to move,” he said.

“That’s right,” Mama said. “We accept differences. We learn together. We cheer for each other.”

Leo smiled. He looked at his colorful letters. “I like my way,” he said. “And I like their way, too.”

He closed his book and hugged Mama. Her fur was warm and soft. The study corner felt safe and bright again.

“I am brave,” Leo said. “I can ask. I can learn. I can accept me.”

“And I accept you,” Mama said, kissing his fuzzy head. “Just as you are.”

Leo took one more look at D, E, and F. He traced them with his paw. “See you tomorrow,” he told the letters. “We will play again.”

The clock ticked gently now, like a calm heartbeat. The crayons clicked softly as he put them back in the tin. The sunlight made the paper glow.

Leo padded out of the study corner, tail swishing. He felt strong, kind, and ready for more tomorrow.

The lesson was clear in his brave little heart: Accept yourself. Ask for help. Learn in your way. Accept others, too.

The End.
