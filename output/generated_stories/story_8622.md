# Finley's Song of Finishing

In a cozy, comfortable nursery, soft lamps glowed like warm stars. A gentle hum came from a sleepy fish tank nightlight. On the rug lay building blocks, picture books, and tiny drums. The air smelled like clean cotton and a hint of lemon polish.

Finley was a playful dolphin puppet who lived on the nursery shelf. He had shiny blue felt, a stitched smile, and a flippy, floppy tail. Every morning, when the clock chimed ding, ding, ding, Finley wriggled his fins and whispered, “Good morning, nursery!”

On this busy day, the caregiver, Nana Jo, clapped her hands and smiled. “Friends, we have three things to do before song time,” she said. “First, tidy the blocks. Second, feed the fish. Third, practice our music.”

Finley splashed his fin in the air. “Ooo! Let’s sing now!” he squeaked.

Nana Jo shook her head softly. “Songs are sweeter when the jobs are done. We will sing better if we finish our tasks.”

Finley bounced on a pillow. The pillow felt squishy and cool. “But singing feels fun right away,” he said.

A tiny toddler named Bea patted Finley’s head. “We can do it,” she said. “We can finish.”

Finley puffed out a pretend bubble. “Hmm. Finley can do it! But where do we start?”

Nana Jo pointed to the blocks scattered like bright candy on the rug. “Step one,” she said, holding up one finger. “Blocks in the blue bin.”

Finley peered at the floor. “Blue bin?” he murmured. He looked around. There was a red drum, a yellow box, and—oh!—a blue bin hiding by the bookshelf.

“I found it!” Finley called. “Friends, blue bin is over here!”

Bea and the other children toddled over. Finley used his flippers to nudge a block. Clack. Another block went click. The sounds made a little beat.

“Clack-click, clack-click,” Finley hummed. “Can our hands make the same beat?”

Bea laughed. “Clack-click!” She plopped two blocks into the bin. Finn, another child, copied: “Clack-click!”

Soon, the beat turned into a tidy-up song. Finley sang, “Pick up, plop in, pick up, plop in,” and everyone joined. The blocks slid into the blue bin like little boats into a harbor. The rug looked smooth again.

Nana Jo nodded. “Task one, done,” she said. “Great listening. Great teamwork.”

Finley felt warm pride, like sunshine. “What’s next?” he asked.

“Step two,” said Nana Jo, holding up two fingers. “Feed the fish.”

They went to the fish tank nightlight. Bubbles went glub, glub. Two goldfish swished their tails. The water looked bright and clear.

Bea pointed to the shelf. “Food?” she asked.

Finley squinted. There were two jars. One had big brown kibble. One had tiny red flakes. Finley thought hard. “Which one is for fish?” he wondered. “Let’s use our eyes. Let’s use our words.”

Nana Jo smiled. “That is smart thinking, Finley. Use clues. Use communication.”

Finley read the simple labels. “This says ‘Dog.’ This says ‘Fish.’ Aha!” He tapped the jar of red flakes. “Fish food!”

“Only a little,” Nana Jo said. “Too much makes the tank messy.”

Finley took a deep breath. He wanted to shake a lot. But he remembered the plan. Finish carefully, finish well. He sprinkled tiny flakes, shhh, like soft snow. The fish flicked their fins and zipped to the top. Swish, swish. “Thank you,” their tiny mouths seemed to say.

“Task two, done,” Nana Jo said. “You used your brain and your words.”

Finley’s tail felt tingly with happy energy. “Now music?” he asked, eyes bright.

“Step three,” Nana Jo said, holding up three fingers. “Practice our music.”

Finley wiggled to the music corner. There were drums, shakers, a small piano with rainbow keys, and a little bell. He grabbed a shaker. “Chicka-chick!”

Bea picked the drum. “Boom, boom.” Finn tapped the rainbow keys: plink, plonk, plink.

Finley started to shake fast, but it sounded messy. Chicka-chick-chick—boom—plink—clang! He paused. His smile drooped. “It sounds like spilled soup,” he said.

Nana Jo knelt beside them. “Music sounds best when we take turns and keep a steady beat,” she said. “Let’s make a plan. First, we count to four. Then, we play together.”

Finley nodded. “A plan helps us finish,” he said. He looked at his friends. “Ready? We count. One… two… three… four.”

Chicka-chick on the beat. Boom, boom on the beat. Plink, plonk on the beat. The sounds lined up like ducks in a row. It felt smooth and calm. Finley’s heart thumped happily. He added soft words:

“We tidy up, we feed the fish,
We keep a beat, we make a wish.
We finish jobs, then sing hooray,
We practice, learn, and love our day!”

Bea giggled and clapped. Finn rang the little bell with a bright ding! The nursery glowed even warmer. Finley felt proud and peaceful, like floating on gentle waves.

Nana Jo gave a thumbs-up. “All tasks finished. Listen how sweet your music is now.”

Finley swished his felt tail. “I wanted to skip the jobs,” he said. “But finishing first made the song better.”

Bea hugged the dolphin puppet. “We did it together,” she whispered.

Finley smiled wide. “Yes. We made a plan, we used our words, we thought carefully, and we practiced. Finishing tasks can be fun!”

They sang their tidy-up song one more time, soft and steady, while the goldfish swished and the lamp hummed. The nursery felt calm, safe, and happy.

The End.
