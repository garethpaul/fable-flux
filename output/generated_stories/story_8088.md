# Ellie’s Colorful Snack Plan

Ellie was a gentle elephant calf with soft gray ears and bright, curious eyes. Today was art day. She stepped into the creative art room, where the walls were sunny yellow, and the tables were covered with paper, brushes, and cups of sparkling water. Paints smelled a little sweet, like berries. The room hummed with friendly chatter.

“Welcome, artists!” said Ms. Rosa, the teacher, with a warm smile. “Today we will paint our favorite snacks.”

Ellie’s heart did a small jump. Snack time was hard for her. Sometimes the crunchy crackers hurt her tummy. Sometimes sugary treats made her feel tired. She didn’t want to be different. She wanted to paint like everyone else and feel good too.

Her friend Pip, a tiny parrot with green feathers, fluttered down beside her. “What will you paint, Ellie?” he chirped.

Ellie looked at the paints. Red, orange, yellow, green, blue, purple—like a rainbow. She loved colors. She loved to learn. She wanted a snack that helped her feel strong and bright, just like a rainbow.

“I don’t know yet,” Ellie said softly. “My tummy gets upset sometimes.”

Pip tilted his head. “Maybe we can learn together!”

Ms. Rosa clapped her hands. “Artists, before we paint, let’s talk about food that helps our bodies. Food is fuel. It helps us move, think, and play. Colors in food give us different kinds of help.”

Ellie’s ears perked up. She stepped closer to listen.

Ms. Rosa showed a big picture of a colorful plate. “Red foods like strawberries help your heart. Orange foods like carrots help your eyes. Green foods like spinach help you grow strong. Brown foods like whole-grain bread give you steady energy. White foods like yogurt help your tummy. Water helps every part of you.”

Ellie felt a warm glow in her belly. Knowledge was like a tiny light turning on. “So colors are clues,” she whispered.

“We are all different,” Ms. Rosa added. “Some bodies like certain foods. Some bodies need extra gentle foods. That’s okay. We can make snack plans that fit us.”

Ellie took a deep breath. She wanted to be brave. “Ms. Rosa,” she said, “sometimes crunchy crackers hurt my tummy. What can I choose instead?”

Ms. Rosa nodded kindly. “Thank you for telling me. That is smart and brave. Let’s think. What about soft foods? Soft bananas, smooth yogurt, and warm oatmeal can be gentle. And we can add color with blueberries or soft peaches.”

Pip flapped happily. “Banana-blueberry rainbow!”

Ellie smiled. “That sounds nice.”

“Let’s paint our snack plans,” Ms. Rosa said. “Use colors to show what helps you.”

Ellie dipped her brush in yellow paint. She swished a bright banana shape. It looked sunny and soft. Then she used blue for blueberries—plop, plop, little dots like stars. Finally, she added a small bowl in white for yogurt and a glass of clear water with a shiny highlight.

Pip painted a crunchy carrot with orange zigzags and a green leafy hat. “My beak likes crunch,” he chirped. “It gives me pep!”

Ellie giggled. “Your snack gives you pep. Mine gives me calm.”

They looked around. Tasha the turtle painted a leafy green salad. Milo the mouse painted a tiny brown slice of whole-grain toast with a thin spread of peanut butter. Sunita the squirrel painted a rainbow fruit salad, swirling red strawberries, purple plums, and green kiwi.

Ms. Rosa walked by, her bracelets softly chiming. “Wonderful!” she said. “We are all choosing foods that help our own bodies. That is knowledge. That is care.”

Ellie felt proud. But when it was snack time, a worry bubbled up. The class helper set out a big plate of crackers beside the fruit. “Oh no,” Ellie thought. “What if there aren’t soft snacks left for me?”

She felt her tummy get fluttery. She wanted to hide under the table. Her ears drooped.

Pip leaned close. “Remember your plan. You can ask for help.”

Ellie closed her eyes and took a slow breath. In, out. She remembered the picture she painted. She remembered Ms. Rosa’s words. She lifted her trunk.

“Ms. Rosa,” Ellie said in a small voice that grew a little bigger, “may I have banana and yogurt today? Crackers hurt my tummy.”

Ms. Rosa smiled and nodded. “Yes, Ellie. Thank you for using your voice.” She brought a bowl, scooped smooth, cool yogurt that smelled like vanilla, and sliced a banana into soft moons. Blueberries tumbled in like tiny night skies. “Here you go. A gentle, colorful snack.”

Ellie tasted a banana moon. It was creamy and sweet. The yogurt was cool and soothing. The blueberries popped softly and made her tongue tingle. Her tummy felt calm. Her heart felt brave.

Pip pecked at a carrot, crunch-crunch. “We did it,” he chirped. “We learned and we asked.”

Ms. Rosa gathered everyone. “Artists, what did we learn?”

Tasha raised a flipper. “Food has colors that help us in different ways!”

Milo squeaked, “We can pick snacks that give us steady energy!”

Sunita swished her tail. “We can celebrate our different bodies and needs!”

Ellie raised her trunk high. “Knowing what my body needs helps me feel good. Asking for help is brave. My snack can be a rainbow that is right for me.”

Everyone clapped. The room sounded like gentle rain, and it made Ellie’s skin feel sparkly with joy.

Ellie looked at her painting. It was bright and true. She added one more detail: a little golden heart above the bowl.

“This,” she said, “is my Colorful Snack Plan.”

Ms. Rosa nodded. “And that is resilience—trying, learning, and choosing again and again.”

Ellie finished her snack, sipped cool water, and felt strong. She and Pip cleaned their brushes and hummed a happy tune. The creative art room smelled like paint and fruit and hope.

Ellie’s tummy was calm, her mind was clear, and her heart was full. She knew something important now: with knowledge, kindness, and brave words, she could take care of her body, even if her choices were different.

The End.
