# Milo Monkey and the Wobbly Wheel

Milo was a courageous monkey with bright eyes and quick hands. One sunny morning, he clapped his little hands and said, “Today, I will ride my red scooter to the park!” The scooter was shiny and fast. The park had a tall slide and soft grass. Milo could almost feel the wind whooshing by his ears.

Milo rolled his scooter out to the garage workshop. The workshop smelled like pine wood and orange oil. Sunlight made dusty sparkles in the air. Tools hung on the wall: a small hammer, tiny wrenches, soft cloths, and a green toolbox that went click when it closed.

Milo put on his little helmet and gave the scooter a push. Bump-bump. Uh-oh. The front wheel wobbled. The scooter shook and squeaked, “Squee-eeeak!”

Milo frowned. “Oh no. I want to ride to the park,” he said. “But the wheel is wobbly.”

He took a deep breath. “I can try. I can be independent. I can fix small things on my own.” His tail curled with courage.

Milo pulled the scooter into the garage workshop. He touched the rubber tire. It felt squishy. He wiggled the wheel. It went wiggle-wobble. He listened close. Squee-eeeak.

“I will find the problem,” said Milo. “Step by step.”

First, he looked with his eyes. He knelt down and peeked. The nut on the wheel was loose. It was a shiny, round nut, not tight at all.

Second, he felt with his hands. He gave the nut a tiny turn. It moved too easily.

Third, he found what he needed. He opened the green toolbox. Click! Inside were wrenches small enough for monkey paws. Milo picked a little silver wrench. It felt cool and smooth.

“Hello, wrench,” he said with a smile. “Let’s work.”

Milo held the wheel steady. He turned the wrench slowly. Righty-tighty, lefty-loosey. He sang it softly so he would remember. “Righty-tighty, lefty-loosey.”

Clink. Clink. The nut got snug. He tested the wheel. Spin. Spin. It spun, but still squeaked.

“Hm,” said Milo. “The wheel is tight, but it is dry. It needs a tiny bit of oil.”

Milo looked around. He saw a small bottle of bike oil on a high shelf. It was too high for him. He did not climb the shelf. He did not pull or jump. Milo knew about integrity. He said, “I will do the right thing in a safe way.”

He found a short step stool. He checked its legs. Steady. He moved it under the shelf. He climbed one step. Two steps. He reached up carefully and took the small oil bottle. He climbed down slowly. “Safe and honest,” he said. His heart felt warm and proud.

Milo put one tiny drop of oil on the wheel. Drip. He wiped the extra with a soft cloth. The oil smelled a little like lemons. He spun the wheel again. Swish. No squeak!

“Better!” Milo cheered. He took a breath. “But I will make sure all is safe.”

He checked the other wheel. He checked the handlebar. He used the wrench. Clink. Snug and safe.

Milo pushed the scooter to the driveway. He tested it slowly. Roll, roll. Smooth and quiet. The sun felt warm on his fur. A breeze brushed his cheeks. He smiled so big his cheeks felt round.

Just then, his neighbor, Pip the puppy, wagged his tail and said, “Milo, can you play at the park?”

“I fixed my scooter all by myself,” said Milo. “I used my eyes, my hands, and my mind. I asked tools to help. I worked safely and told the truth to myself.”

Pip sniffed the air. “It smells like lemon. It smells like new!”

Milo laughed. “That is the oil. One tiny drop.”

They scooted down the sidewalk. Swish, swish. The trees whispered. The birds chirped. At the park, Milo showed Pip the slide. They took turns. They waited in line. They said, “Your turn,” and “Thank you.” The grass felt cool on their toes when they sat to rest.

After a while, a little squirrel named Suri came by with a sad face. She held a small wagon. The wheel on the wagon wobbled. Suri said, “My wagon is wobbly. I want to carry leaves, but it goes clunk.”

Milo’s eyes lit up. “I can show you what I learned,” he said. “You can be independent too.”

Back at the garage workshop, Milo and Suri looked, felt, and listened. They found a loose nut. They said, “Righty-tighty, lefty-loosey.” Milo held the wagon. Suri turned the wrench. Clink. Clink. Milo gave one tiny drop of oil. Drip. They tested the wheel. Spin. Swish. No squeak!

Suri smiled wide. “I did it!”

“You did it,” said Milo. “You were brave and careful. That is independence. You tried first. You used your tools. You were honest and safe.”

They high-fived with soft little pats. Pip barked happily. The garage smelled like pine and orange oil again. Sunlight danced in the dusty air.

Milo put the wrench back in the toolbox. Click. He put the stool away. He said, “We fix, we clean, we care.”

On the way back to the park, Milo felt the wind on his ears and the sun on his back. His scooter rolled smooth. His heart felt strong.

He said, “When we try our best, step by step, we can solve problems. We can be independent, resourceful, and kind.”

The End.
