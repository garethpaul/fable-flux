# Tito Tortoise Teaches Bathroom Respect

Tito was a wise tortoise with a smooth, shiny shell. He lived with a kind family in a cozy house. Every morning, he took a slow stroll to the bathroom. The bathroom was bright and warm. The mirror sparkled. The soap smelled like sweet oranges. The floor felt cool on Tito’s little feet.

One sunny morning, Tito heard giggles and splashes. “Splish! Splosh!” Water hit the sides of the bathtub. The door was open a crack. Inside, two little siblings, Lina and Max, were brushing their teeth at the sink. Toothpaste dots were on the mirror like tiny stars. A towel lay crumpled on the floor like a sleepy cloud. The bath mat was crooked, and a comb was near the drain.

Tito blinked his gentle eyes. “Good morning,” he said in his soft, slow voice. “May I come in?”

Lina looked up. “Hi, Tito!” she chirped.

Max waved his toothbrush. “We’re brushing fast! Zoom zoom!”

Tito smiled. “Thank you for saying hello. May I use the bathroom after you? I will wait my turn.” He settled by the door, patient and calm.

Lina nodded. “We can share,” she said. She scooted a little to the side.

Tito shook his head kindly. “We can share space, but we must also share time. Respect means we think about what others need. I can wait, and you can finish.”

Max hummed and brushed. “Mmm mmm,” he said with toothpaste foam cheeks. He looked like a little squirrel. Lina giggled.

Tito sniffed the air. “I smell orange soap and minty toothpaste,” he said. “It’s a good morning for clean hands and safe steps.”

Max finished brushing and spat carefully. “Ptoo!” Some water splashed onto the floor.

“Oops,” said Max. “Just a little splash.”

Tito nodded. “Water can make the floor slippery. Respect for others means we keep the floor dry so no one slips. Slipping is not safe.”

Max grabbed a towel. He dabbed the drops. “Dab, dab, dab.” The towel made a soft shuff sound. “All dry!” he said proudly.

Lina hopped down from the stool. “I want to wash my hands!” She turned the faucet on very fast. Water rushed like a tiny river. “Whoosh!”

“Let’s slow it down,” Tito said gently. “We save water for everyone. Turn the handle halfway. Listen.” The stream softened to a friendly trickle. “Shhh.” It sounded like a whispering breeze.

Lina rubbed the orange soap. Bubbles popped on her fingers. Pop! Pop! They smelled sweet and bright. “I love bubbles!” she giggled.

“Bubbles are fun,” said Tito. “And clean hands show respect for our bodies and for others. Clean hands help keep friends healthy.”

Lina rinsed and shook her hands—drip, drip, drip—and then reached for the towel. She wiped carefully, then hung the towel straight. “All neat,” she said.

Max reached for the comb near the drain. “Look, Tito. The comb is lost!”

“Good eyes,” said Tito. “If we leave things on the floor, someone could step on them. Respect means we put things back where they belong.” Max placed the comb in a little cup. It clicked softly. “Thank you,” Tito said. “That was thoughtful.”

Lina looked at the mirror with toothpaste dots. “It’s messy,” she said.

Tito nodded. “Would you like to make it shiny again?”

Lina sprayed a little water on a cloth and wiped in small circles. Swish, swish. The dots disappeared. The mirror gleamed. Lina smiled at her reflection. “Hello, shiny Lina.”

Max lined up the toothbrushes, bristles up. “Standing tall!” he said.

Tito peeked into the bathtub. The bath toys were scattered—ducks and boats and a purple cup. “Quack,” said Max, making the duck wobble.

“Play is wonderful,” Tito said. “When play is done, we put toys away. That shows respect for the next person who comes in. They will find a calm space.”

Together, the children scooped the toys into a basket. Plop, plop, plop. The basket made a soft rustle as it filled.

“Now,” Tito said, “look around. How does the bathroom feel?”

Lina took a breath. “It smells nice,” she said. “Like oranges.”

Max tapped the straight bath mat with his toe. “It looks neat,” he said. “And not slippery.”

Tito nodded. “It feels safe and kind. You took care of the space. That is respect.”

Lina tilted her head. “Respect is being kind to people,” she said, thinking.

“Yes,” said Tito, “and respect is also being kind to places. We care for our home and for shared things. We wait our turn. We listen. We clean up. We keep each other safe. When we practice, we improve every day.”

Max puffed out his chest. “We improved!” he said with a grin.

“You did,” Tito said warmly. “I am proud of you both.”

Lina opened the door wide and bowed playfully. “Your turn, Tito.”

Tito smiled and moved slowly inside. “Thank you for waiting with me,” he said. He washed his tiny tortoise hands. Splash, splash. He dried them and hung the towel straight. Then he turned to the children. “Respect makes the bathroom a happy place,” he said.

Lina and Max nodded together. “We will remember,” they promised.

Tito’s shell gleamed in the soft light as he headed back to the hall. His heart felt calm and glad.

The End.
