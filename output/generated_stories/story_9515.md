# Buttons, Brooms, and Bear Hugs

Bumble the bear cub loved the little garden shed behind his home. It smelled like pine wood and sweet soil. Sunlight made tiny dust sparkles dance in the air. In the corner, there were colorful buttons in jars, soft paintbrushes, and a big, bristly broom that went swish-swish on the floor.

“Today,” Bumble said, his round ears wiggling, “I will make a pretend rocket ship!” He patted the wooden boxes with his fuzzy paws. Thump-thump. “Zoom!”

His friend, Pip the mouse, peeked in. “Squeak! Can I play?” Pip’s whiskers trembled with excitement.

“Come in!” Bumble said. “Let’s build!”

Bumble stacked boxes. Pip lined up buttons like star lights. The shed hummed with busy sounds: clink-clink, tap-tap, swish. Bumble’s tummy felt happy-warm. He clicked a jar lid. “We can use this for a rocket cap!”

Just then, Daisy the duck waddled in. “Quack! I brought shiny ribbons,” she said, dropping a bundle that smelled like soap and sunshine.

“Perfect!” Bumble cheered. “Ribbons for rocket tails!”

They worked and giggled. Bumble’s paws got sticky with glue. Pip sneezed at the dust. “Ah-choo!” Daisy flapped her wings to wave the dust away. “Whoosh-whoosh!”

When the rocket was ready, Bumble climbed into the biggest box. “Five, four, three—” he began.

But Pip jumped up too. “Me too!” Pip squeaked, hopping onto Bumble’s lap.

Daisy wiggled in next, soft feathers tickling Bumble’s nose. “Quack! I want to sit!”

Bumble’s eyes went wide. The box felt tight. His knees bumped. His fur got hot. He loved his friends. He loved rocket time. But his tummy squeezed. He needed a little space.

“Um,” he said softly, “I feel squished.”

Pip didn’t hear. “Blast off!” Pip shouted.

Daisy flapped again. “Zoom, zoom!”

Bumble’s paws shook. He took a slow breath in. He remembered Mama Bear’s words: “Your body is your own. You can say what you need with kind, clear words.”

Bumble put his paw on his chest. “Friends,” he said in a gentle voice, “I need more space. Please don’t sit on me.”

Pip blinked. “Oh! Did I make you squish-sad?”

“A little,” Bumble said. “I like playing with you. I like hugs too. But not right now in the rocket. My body needs room.”

Daisy tucked her wings. “Quack, I didn’t know. Thank you for telling us.”

Pip climbed down at once. “I can be the button pilot outside the box!” Pip squeaked, pointing to the button stars. “Beep-beep!”

Daisy waddled back and made a cozy seat with ribbons. “I will be the wind,” she said. “Whoosh-whoosh!” She flapped gently this time, making a soft breeze that felt cool on Bumble’s face.

Bumble’s shoulders relaxed. He smiled. “Thank you for listening,” he said. “That was brave of me to say. And kind of you to stop.”

“Friends care,” Pip said. “We listen to words and bodies.”

Bumble nodded. “Yes. If I say stop, please stop. If you say stop, I will stop too.”

Pip’s whiskers wiggled. “Deal!”

Daisy quacked happily. “Deal!”

“Ready now?” Bumble asked.

“Ready!” said Pip.

“Ready!” said Daisy.

Bumble held a wooden spoon like a steering stick. Pip made button beeps. Daisy made wind whooshes. The rocket box creaked a little. The ribbon tails fluttered. Bumble said, “Five, four, three, two, one—BLAST OFF!”

They pretended the shed roof opened to the sky. In their imaginations, the garden became a galaxy. The jars were moons, round and shiny. The broom was a tall planet tree swaying in slow space wind. The paintbrushes were fuzzy comets, swish-swish, swish-swish.

“Look!” Bumble pointed at a jar lid. “It’s a silver saucer!”

“Beep!” Pip pressed a blue button. “Landing lights!”

“Quack-quack!” Daisy sang. “Soft landing!”

Bumble lowered the spoon. The rocket box touched down. Thump, but gentle. “We did it,” he said. “We are space friends!”

They cheered. Then Bumble reached for the glue, but Pip held up a tiny paw. “Bumble, can I have a tiny paw hug?” Pip asked. “If you want. If not, that’s okay.”

Bumble felt his heart smile. “Yes, a tiny paw hug is okay.” They tapped paws. Daisy asked, “Feather high-five?” Bumble laughed. “Yes, a feather high-five is okay too!”

They each took turns asking and listening:
“Can I sit close?”
“Not now.”
“Okay!”
“Can I hold the spoon?”
“Yes, your turn!”

The shed felt safe and bright. It smelled like happy wood and ribbon soap. Bumble felt calm and proud. He had used clear words. His friends had listened. They were all different—big, small, furry, feathery—but they worked together with kindness.

Before they left, Bumble shared the jar of buttons. “Take some for your own projects,” he said.

Pip took tiny blue ones. “I will make star roads!”

Daisy chose red and gold. “I will make a shiny nest!”

Bumble kept green ones for leaf lights. “I will make a gentle door sign,” he said, gluing buttons on a small board.

When it dried, he held it up. It read: “Please Ask First.”

They all smiled.

Bumble said, “We can play in many ways. We can be close or not close. We can say yes or no. We can ask and we can listen.”

Pip squeaked, “That keeps bodies safe.”

Daisy added, “That keeps hearts happy.”

Bumble hugged his own shoulders, a cozy self-hug. “I feel safe,” he said. “I feel loved.”

They stacked the boxes, put away the glue, and swept the floor. Swish-swish. The garden air drifted in, smelling like mint and sunshine.

“Tomorrow,” Bumble said, “we can build a ribbon rainbow slide!”

“Beep-beep yes!” Pip giggled.

“Quack-quack hooray!” Daisy cheered.

And they all agreed to ask first, listen well, and celebrate the special way each friend likes to play.

The End.
