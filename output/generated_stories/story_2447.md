# Rocco Raccoon Tells the Truth

Rocco was a musical raccoon with soft gray fur and bright eyes. He loved to make music with anything he found. Tap-tap on a teacup! Ding-ding on a spoon! In the quiet guest room, he wiggled his striped tail and hummed, “La-la-la!”

The guest room was cozy. The curtains were silky and blue. The bed was fluffy and smelled like fresh soap. Sunlight made little dust sparkles dance in the air. A shiny glass snow globe sat on the bedside table. Inside, tiny silver flakes looked like snow.

Rocco whispered, “That snow globe would make a pretty sound!”

He tiptoed closer. The rug felt soft under his paws. He wiggled his fingers and tapped the table. Tap-tap. The glass made a gentle clink. Rocco smiled. “I can make a song,” he said.

He picked up two wooden spoons from a basket. “Ready, set, music!” he cheered. He made soft beats: tap, tap, tap. He swayed his body and stomped, stompy-stomp, to the rhythm. His tail swished side to side. “Stomp and sway, stomp and sway,” he sang.

Rocco looked at the snow globe and wondered, “What sound do you make?” He reached out and gave it a tiny shake. The glitter fell like snow. “Oooh,” he said. The glass felt cool and smooth.

Then—oh no! His spoon slipped. Clink! The spoon touched the snow globe. The snow globe wobbled. Wobble, wobble… CRACK! A tiny line appeared on the glass.

Rocco froze. His ears drooped. “Oh no,” he whispered. His tummy felt twisty. He didn’t mean to make a crack. He didn’t want anyone to be upset.

Rocco looked around the room. He thought, Think, think, think. What should I do? He could hide. He could run. He could pretend it didn’t happen. He put his paws on his cheeks and took a deep breath. He remembered something his grandma said: “Honesty keeps your heart light.”

Rocco put the spoons down. He tried a smart plan. First, he checked the globe. He gently touched the crack. Was it safe? The crack was small, and the globe did not leak. “Okay,” he said softly. “Now I need to tell the truth.”

Just then, Aunt Bea walked into the guest room with a warm smile. She carried a basket that smelled like cookies. “Hello, Rocco,” she said. “I heard music. Tap-tap, stomp-stomp!”

Rocco’s paws felt heavy. His heart thumped. Thump, thump. He took another deep breath and stood tall. “Aunt Bea,” he said, “I made a mistake.” His voice shook a little. “I was making a song. My spoon slipped. I tapped the snow globe. Now it has a little crack. I am sorry.”

He waited. He looked at his toes. He felt a little scared. Then Aunt Bea put a gentle hand on his shoulder. “Thank you for telling me the truth, Rocco,” she said. “That was very honest. Accidents happen.”

Rocco blinked. “You’re… not mad?”

“I’m glad you were brave and honest,” Aunt Bea said. “Now we can fix it together.”

Rocco’s ears perked up. “Together?”

Aunt Bea nodded. “Yes. Let’s think.” She tapped her chin. “First, we make the globe safe. Second, we move it to a safe place. Third, we choose better instruments.”

Rocco smiled a little. “That’s a good plan.”

They worked step by step. Step one: safety. Aunt Bea wrapped the snow globe with a soft cloth. Rocco held it carefully with both paws. Step two: safe place. They put it on a high shelf, away from bumping and tapping. Step three: better instruments. Aunt Bea opened her basket. Inside were two little shakers, a tiny drum, and a ribbon stick.

Rocco’s eyes lit up. “Wow!”

Aunt Bea handed him the shakers. “These are good for music in the guest room,” she said.

Rocco shook them gently. Chika-chika! He tapped the tiny drum with soft fingers. Pum-pum. He twirled the ribbon stick and moved his body. He marched in place—march, march, march. He swayed slow—sway, sway. He hopped—hop, hop, hop. “Music with moving!” he giggled.

Aunt Bea clapped the beat. “Tap-tap, clap-clap!” she sang. They made a new song together. It was happy and bouncy. Rocco’s heart felt light, just like Grandma said.

When the song ended, Rocco said, “I’m still sorry about the crack.”

Aunt Bea smiled. “You said sorry. You told the truth. You helped fix the problem. That is honest and smart.”

Rocco stood a little taller. “Next time, I will think first,” he said. “I will ask, ‘Is this safe? Is this okay?’ And I will choose good instruments.”

Aunt Bea nodded. “That’s using your brain and your heart.”

Rocco wagged his tail. “My brain to think. My heart to be honest. My body to move!”

They laughed together. The guest room felt warm and calm again. The silky curtains swished. The tiny drum sat ready for new songs.

Rocco hummed. “La-la-la,” he sang, shaking his shakers softly. He felt proud. He had made a mistake. He told the truth. He helped make it right.

And that day, Rocco learned a clear lesson: Honesty makes problems smaller, and honest hearts make the best music.

The End.
