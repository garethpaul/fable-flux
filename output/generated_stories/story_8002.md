# Polly Paints the Plates

Polly was a bright, creative parrot with green wings, sunny-yellow cheeks, and a speckled red tail. She loved to make things look special. One rainy afternoon, Polly flapped into the dining room. The room smelled like warm bread and sweet jam. The big wooden table felt smooth under her feet, and the plates shone white like little moons.

“Squawk! I want to help,” Polly chirped. “I can make dinner fancy!”

Mama set a basket of bread on the table. “That sounds kind,” Mama said. “But we need the table ready soon.”

Papa brought in a pot of soup that smelled like carrots and gentle pepper. “We need the napkins folded and the plates set,” Papa said. “Can you do it?”

Polly puffed her feathers. “I can! I can add colors too,” she said, tapping her beak with a happy click. She grabbed a stack of blue napkins and began to fold. Her wings swished and her claws clicked softly on the chair.

Polly folded one napkin into a triangle. “Hmm,” she said. “What if it looked like a boat?” She folded and folded until it looked like a tiny blue boat. It made her giggle. “Sail, sail, napkin boat!”

She placed the boat on a plate. It wobbled. Plop! It fell over.

Polly’s cheeks warmed. “Oh,” she whispered, feeling a little twitch in her tummy. “Try again.”

She tried a flower shape. Twist, tuck, poke. The napkin popped open like a jack-in-the-box. “Gah!” Polly squawked. Her tail fluffed with frustration.

Mama looked over kindly. “It’s okay to feel upset,” Mama said. “Take a slow breath.”

Polly breathed in slow. She smelled soup and bread and a hint of lemon from the clean plates. She breathed out slow. “I can try again,” she said softly.

Polly found a simple fold. Fold, pat, smooth. This time the napkin stayed. “Yes!” she chirped. She folded more, pat-pat, smooth-smooth. Soon, a neat row of folded napkins sat like little blue mountains.

“I did it!” Polly said. Her eyes shined.

Papa smiled. “Great work, Polly.”

Then Polly had a new idea. “I want to paint the plates with food colors,” she said. “Just tiny dots. Pretty polka dots!”

Papa paused, holding a spoon. “Hmm. We don’t want the plates too messy.”

Polly fluffed again. “But I am creative,” she said, her voice small. “I want to help in my way.”

Her tummy felt twisty again. She wanted to shout. She wanted to give up. She looked at the neat napkins. She took another slow breath.

“I can be brave,” she told herself. “I can use my ideas and listen too.”

Polly tapped her beak. “How about a little compromise?” she asked. “No big paint. Just a tiny dot on each napkin. A color corner! It will look fun and still be clean.”

Mama tilted her head. “That’s a thoughtful idea,” Mama said. “What do you think, Papa?”

Papa nodded. “A tiny color corner sounds nice.”

Polly’s feathers relaxed. “Thank you!” she chirped.

She dipped a clean cotton swab in food color. Dot. A small red dot in the corner of one napkin. Dot. A tiny blue dot on the next. Dot. A cheerful yellow dot. The dots looked like little stars.

The dining room glowed with warm light. The table smelled of fresh wood and soup steam. Polly hummed a soft tune while she worked. “Dot, dot, don’t stop,” she sang.

Soon the table was ready. Bowls of soup, basket of bread, cups of water that clinked with ice. The napkins were neat, each with a friendly color dot.

Baby Mia toddled in, clapping. “Pretty!” she said, pointing at the dots.

“Polly made those,” Mama said.

Polly lifted her chin. “Yes, I did,” she said, proud and calm. “I had a big idea. It felt hard. I got frustrated. I took a breath. I asked for help. I used my own way and we made a plan together.”

They all sat. Slurp, slurp went the soup. Crunch, crunch went the bread. The tiny color dots made the table smile.

Papa raised his spoon. “To Polly,” he said. “For being creative and brave.”

Polly’s heart felt warm like sunshine. “To us,” she chirped. “For listening and making a good plan.”

After dinner, Polly flapped to the window. The rain had stopped. A soft rainbow stretched across the sky.

She whispered, “I can be me, and I can work with others too. I can do hard things.”

Mama hugged her. “Yes, you can,” Mama said. “You believed in yourself, and you listened. That’s real strength.”

Polly smiled and touched one tiny dot. “Small dots, big confidence,” she said.

The lesson was clear in her bright, happy heart: believe in yourself, take a breath when you feel upset, and find a way to work together. Being different can make things more beautiful.

The End.
