# Roary’s Quiet Roar

Roary was a brave little lion cub with soft golden fur and bright, curious eyes. Today, Roary padded into the study corner. The study corner was cozy and warm. It had a small desk, a tiny chair, a soft rug, and a basket of crayons that smelled like sweet wax. Sunlight made little squares on the floor. The room felt quiet, like a whisper.

Roary loved to learn new things. “Today, I will draw and practice letters,” he said, swishing his tail. He took a deep breath. The air smelled like paper and pine shelves. Roary felt brave and ready.

Just then, his friend Tilly the turtle peeked in. Tilly wore a bright green bow. Her shell went tap-tap on the floor. “Hi, Roary!” Tilly said. “Can I join you?”

Roary smiled. “Yes, Tilly! Let’s share.”

They sat together. Crayons clicked and rolled. Roary picked a blue crayon. Tilly picked a red one. They both reached for the same notebook at the same time.

“My turn?” Roary asked.

“My turn?” Tilly asked.

They stopped and looked at each other. Roary felt a rumble in his chest. “I want to try first,” he said.

Tilly pulled back a little. “I want to try first too,” she said softly.

Roary’s brave heart beat fast. He remembered his big lion roar, but this was the study corner. It was a quiet place. He could roar quietly. He could use his words. He took another deep breath. It smelled like crayons and calm.

“Let’s talk,” Roary said. “We can share. We can take turns.” He placed the notebook gently between them. “What do you think?”

Tilly blinked. “Okay,” she said. “But how?”

Roary thought. He tapped the desk with one paw. Tap, tap. “We can count. I draw for five counts. Then you draw for five counts. We can say, ‘My turn, your turn.’”

Tilly’s eyes sparkled. “That sounds fair,” she said. “Thank you, Roary.”

They smiled. Roary counted out loud in a soft roar. “One… two… three… four… five!” He drew a blue circle that looked like a round, shiny moon. Then he slid the notebook to Tilly. “Your turn!”

Tilly counted, “One… two… three… four… five!” She drew a red triangle that looked like a bright, silly hat. Then she slid the notebook back. “Your turn!”

They giggled. The crayons felt smooth and warm in their paws. The desk felt cool. The pages sounded like shhh as they turned. They took turns and talked. It felt good to share.

Soon, their friend Pip the parrot fluttered in. His feathers were green and gold. He smelled like fresh air. “Squawk! What are you doing?” Pip asked, head tilted.

“We are drawing and taking turns,” Roary said.

“We are counting and sharing,” Tilly added.

Pip hopped closer. “Can I draw too?” he asked.

Roary thought about the small desk and the small notebook. Three friends. One notebook. Roary’s tummy fluttered. He wanted to be brave and kind. He remembered a big word he loved: acceptance. Acceptance means we make space. Acceptance means we say, “You can be you here.”

“Yes, Pip,” Roary said. “You can join us. We will make room.” He scooted his chair. Tilly moved the crayons so everyone had some.

“How will we do turns now?” Pip asked.

Roary smiled. “We can make a plan together. Let’s talk.”

They all leaned in. Roary’s whiskers tickled his cheeks. He felt calm and strong.

Roary said, “We can do circle turns. First me, then Tilly, then Pip. We count to three this time. Short and sweet.”

Tilly nodded. “I like that.”

Pip fluffed his feathers. “Me too! Squawk!”

They tried the plan. “One… two… three!” Roary drew a blue letter R. “R for Roary,” he said.

“One… two… three!” Tilly drew a red letter T. “T for Tilly,” she said.

“One… two… three!” Pip drew a green letter P. “P for Pip!” he squawked.

They clapped softly. The notebook now had shapes and letters and colors. It looked like a bright, happy garden.

Then Pip wanted to draw a big swoosh across the page. Roary wanted to draw tiny dots. Tilly wanted to draw neat lines. They all stopped again.

Roary felt that rumble in his chest, but he remembered the quiet roar. He used his words. “We like different things,” he said. “That is okay. We can accept that. We can make space for all our ideas.”

“How?” asked Tilly.

“We can make three parts,” Roary said. He drew two light lines to make three sections. “This part is for swooshes. This part is for tiny dots. This part is for neat lines. We all belong.”

Pip chirped, “I like that plan!”

Tilly smiled wide. “Me too!”

They drew and drew. Swoosh. Dot-dot-dot. Line-line-line. The page looked busy and beautiful. It sounded like gentle scratch-scratch. It smelled like crayons and happy hearts.

When they were done, Roary looked at his friends. “We did it,” he said. “We talked. We listened. We took turns. We made a plan. We accepted each other’s ideas.”

Tilly nodded. “I feel calm. I feel heard.”

Pip flapped softly. “I feel proud!”

Roary felt warm and brave inside. “When we accept each other and use our words, we can solve problems. We can make space for everyone.”

They hugged. The study corner felt even cozier now. The sunlight looked brighter. The little squares on the floor looked like tiny gold pillows.

Roary placed the notebook on the shelf. “This is our together picture,” he said. “It shows our turns. It shows our talk. It shows our big, kind hearts.”

They all smiled.

The End.
