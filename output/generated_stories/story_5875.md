# Mango on the Rooftop

On a sunny morning, a courageous monkey named Miko climbed to the flat rooftop of his home. The roof felt warm under his little feet. The air smelled like sweet mango and fresh wind. Miko’s tail swished with excitement.

“Today is a building day!” Miko said. “I will make something new.”

Miko loved to try new ideas. He loved to fix little problems. He loved to think, “What if?” and “How else?” That is called innovation. Miko liked that big word. He said it slow: “In-no-va-tion.”

Miko looked around. On one side, clothes danced on a line. On another side, a big mango tree leaned over the wall. Yellow mangoes hung high, like tiny suns.

Miko’s tummy rumbled. “I want a mango,” he said. He reached up, but the mangoes were too far. He could not jump safely. The wall was high. The ground looked very far down.

Miko took a deep breath. He felt brave, but he also felt careful. “I need a smart way,” he said. “I can make a tool!”

He found things on the rooftop: a long broom, a basket, some soft rope, and a cloth. He touched each thing. The broom felt rough and dry. The basket smelled like straw. The rope was a little scratchy. The cloth was smooth and blue.

Miko rubbed his chin. “What if I tie the basket to the broom?” he asked himself. “What if the basket catches the mango?”

He tied the basket to the broom handle with the rope. He tugged the knots. “Tight and right,” he said. He added the cloth to make a gentle nest so the mangoes would not bruise. He smiled. “This is my Mango Catcher 1!”

Miko stood by the wall, reached out the broom, and tried to scoop a mango. Swoosh! The basket swung. The mango slipped. Thump! The basket bumped the wall.

Miko frowned a little. He was not angry. He was thinking. “Hmm. It did not work yet,” he said. “I will try again. I will change it.”

He moved the cloth to the side and made the basket lower on the broom. He added a second small rope to keep it steady. He wiggled it. It felt firm. “Mango Catcher 2,” he said.

He tried again. Swoosh. Tap. The basket touched a mango. The mango wobbled. Plop! It fell—but not into the basket. Miko watched it land safely on a soft leaf on the tree. He sighed.

A little bird landed on the line and chirped, “Tweet! Need help?”

Miko smiled. “Thanks, Bird. I am okay. I want to try my own idea first.” He put his hand on his heart. “I can do it. I can think. I can change. I can learn.”

The bird chirped, “You are brave,” and waited.

Miko looked at the mango stems. They were thick. “What if I add a gentle hook?” he asked. He looked around and found a bent wire near a plant pot. He wrapped the wire to make a soft hook and covered it with cloth so it would not hurt the fruit. He tied the hook under the basket, so the hook could touch the stem and the basket could catch the mango.

“Now I have Mango Catcher 3,” he said. “Hook, twist, and catch!”

Miko stood tall. The sun warmed his fur. A breeze kissed his cheeks. He reached out, steady and slow. He whispered, “Easy… easy…” The hook touched a stem. Twist… twist… Snip! The mango let go. Plop! The mango fell into the soft cloth in the basket.

“I did it!” Miko cheered. He hugged the broom. The bird flapped and sang, “Tweet-tweet! Clever monkey!”

Miko pulled in the basket and held the warm mango. It smelled sweet and sunny. He giggled. “Innovation helped me! I tried, I changed, and I tried again.”

He picked two more mangoes, one for now and one for later. Then Miko looked at the rooftops around him. He saw two neighbor kids, Tia and Ravi, peeking over their wall.

Tia waved. “Hi, Miko! What is that cool thing?”

Miko beamed. “This is my Mango Catcher. I made it to reach safely. Do you want to try?”

Ravi nodded. “Yes, please!”

Miko showed them each step. “First, think of the problem. Then, look at what you have. Try one idea. If it does not work, do not stop. Change it. Try again. That is innovation!”

Tia tried the Mango Catcher. Twist… plop! She caught a mango. “I did it!” she laughed.

Ravi tried next. The mango missed the basket. He looked a little sad. Miko smiled and said, “It is okay. We can change it.” He moved the second rope a little. Ravi tried again. Plop! Success!

They all sat on the warm rooftop and shared slices of mango. The juice was sticky and sweet. They licked their fingers and smiled.

Miko said, “Today I learned to be brave in my mind. I learned to use what I have. I learned to share my idea.”

Tia said, “Innovation is trying new ways!”

Ravi added, “And trying again!”

Miko nodded. “Yes. We can be self-reliant and also share. We can help ourselves and help our friends.”

The sun began to set, painting the clouds pink and gold. The rooftop felt cozy. Miko tied his Mango Catcher neatly and put it away.

He waved to his friends. “Good night! Try new ideas tomorrow!”

They waved back. “Good night, Miko!”

Miko looked at the mango tree and smiled. His heart felt warm and proud. He whispered, “I can think, I can try, and I can share.”

The End.
