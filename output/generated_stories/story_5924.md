# Tilly Takes Turns in the Laundry Room

The washing machine hummed like a sleepy bee. The dryer made a soft whoosh, whoosh, like wind in a cozy cave. Warm soap smell floated in the air. Tilly, a gentle elephant calf with big, floppy ears, padded into the laundry room on quiet feet. Her trunk swayed like a little swing.

Mama Elephant stacked a basket full of colorful clothes. There were tiny socks, big towels, and shirts of every shade—red, blue, yellow, purple, and green. “Today, we help with laundry,” Mama said with a smile. “We take turns.”

Tilly’s eyes sparkled. “I want to push the buttons!” she trumpeted softly. She loved the shiny buttons that blinked like stars.

“Hold on, Tilly,” said Auntie Hana, a zebra with soft stripes. She was visiting to help. “I will sort the colors first.”

Tilly’s cousin, Pip the panda, clapped. “I can fold small things!” he said. Pip’s paws were small but quick.

Tilly felt her feet shuffle. “But I want to go now,” she whispered. Her trunk wiggled toward the button.

Mama Elephant touched Tilly’s shoulder. “We will take turns,” she said calmly. “First sort, then load, then soap, then buttons, then fold. You will have a turn, too.”

Tilly took a slow breath. In, out. She listened to the hum and whoosh. “Okay,” she said. “I can wait.”

Auntie Hana sorted the clothes into piles like little rainbows. “Dark colors here, light colors there,” she said. “This helps keep the colors bright.” Tilly watched. Waiting felt big, like a heavy towel. She squeezed her trunk and tried to count the dots on Auntie’s stripes. One, two, three…

“Now loading!” Pip giggled, reaching for the small shirts.

Tilly felt her toes tingle. “Pip, can I help?” she asked.

Pip nodded. “Yes! You take the big towels. I’ll take the little socks.”

They worked side by side. The towels were warm and soft like sunshine. The socks were tiny clouds. Tilly moved carefully. She tried not to squish, even though she was strong. “I am gentle,” she told herself. “Gentle and patient.”

Grandpa Otter waddled in with a basket of fresh lavender. “Hello, helpers,” he said kindly. “We all do our part. That’s integrity. We do what we say.”

Tilly blinked. “Inte… gri… tee?”

“It means we keep our promises,” Grandpa Otter explained. “You promised to take turns.”

Tilly nodded. “I will.”

When the washer was full, Mama held the cup of soap. “Who will pour?” she asked.

Tilly’s breath caught. “May I?” she said.

Mama smiled. “Yes, Tilly. Your turn.”

Tilly lifted the cup with her trunk. The soap smelled lemony and clean. It felt slippery and cool. She poured slowly. A tiny drop splashed onto her foot. “Ooh!” she giggled. “Tickly!”

“Good job,” Auntie Hana said. “You are improving your careful pour.”

It was button time now. The buttons glowed—click, clack, beep. Tilly looked at Pip. Pip looked at Tilly.

“I know you love buttons,” Pip said.

Tilly remembered waiting. She remembered how Pip had shared the loading. She took a breath. “Let’s take turns,” she offered. “I press start. You press extra-rinse.”

Pip’s face lit up. “Yes, please!”

Tilly pressed the start button. Beep! The washer swirled. Pip pressed extra-rinse. Beep! The water whooshed louder. They grinned. It felt good to share.

While they waited, they played a laundry game. Auntie Hana held up a tiny sock with stars, a long rainbow scarf, and a hat with a shiny button. “Who wears this?” she asked.

“The stars sock is for Baby Bunny,” Pip guessed.

“The rainbow scarf is for you, Auntie Hana!” Tilly said. “It matches your smile.”

Grandpa Otter tried on the hat and made silly faces. Everyone laughed. Tilly’s heart felt warm, like the dryer.

When the dryer dinged, the room smelled like sunshine and lavender. It was folding time. Mama folded big towels. Auntie folded shirts. Pip folded socks like little tacos. Tilly tried to fold a sheet, but it flopped over her head. “Oh!” She peeked out and giggled. “I need help.”

“Let’s improve together,” Mama said. “Hold one corner, Tilly. I’ll hold the other. We move step by step.”

They folded slowly—corner to corner, edge to edge. Tilly watched, then tried again. This time, the sheet stayed neat. “I did it!” she cheered.

Pip held up two socks that didn’t match—one blue, one yellow. “Different,” he said.

Auntie Hana smiled. “Different is good. Our clothes are all colors. We are all different, too. That’s diversity. We still work together.”

Tilly nodded. “Different and together.”

They finished the last basket. Tilly placed the folded towels in a stack as tall as a small mountain. She patted the top gently. “Soft mountain,” she whispered.

Mama gathered everyone close. “You all did so well,” she said. “We sorted, loaded, poured, pressed, and folded. We took turns. We kept our promises. We learned and got better.”

Tilly’s ears wiggled with pride. “Waiting was hard,” she said. “But sharing felt good. Taking turns helped everyone.”

Pip bumped her trunk with his paw. “Next time, you can press extra-rinse first,” he said.

Tilly smiled. “And you can press start. We’ll keep taking turns.”

The washing machine hummed quietly, like it was happy too. The laundry room felt warm and bright. Tilly’s heart felt calm and big.

“Gentle and patient,” Tilly whispered. “Different and together.” She took one more deep breath. “We did it—with turns!”

The End.
