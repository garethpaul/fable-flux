# Tilly Turtle Finds Her Song

Tilly was a shy turtle with a smooth green shell. She lived with her family. One rainy day, they stayed in the warm, cozy house. The guest room smelled like clean sheets and lemon soap. Soft light came through the curtains and made the room glow.

Mama said, “We can have a little music time while we wait for the rain to stop.”

Tilly’s heart thumped. Music time? In the guest room? She liked music, but she was shy. She worried her legs would feel wobbly. She worried her voice would come out too small.

Mama set a basket on the bed. The basket was full of simple instruments. There was a tiny drum, a pair of egg shakers, a wooden block, and a comb with a paper strip for humming. The drum felt smooth. The shakers felt bumpy. The comb was funny and tickly.

“Would you like to try?” asked Mama.

Tilly tucked her head halfway into her shell. “I’m scared,” she whispered. “What if I make a wrong sound?”

Mama knelt down. “All sounds can belong in music,” she said gently. “Music can help our feelings. When we feel shy, music can be a friend.”

Tilly peeked out. The guest bed had a soft blue blanket. The floor was a warm rug. The room was quiet except for the little patter of rain. Tippy-tap. Tippy-tap.

Tilly listened. “The rain is making a beat,” she said softly.

“That’s right,” Mama smiled. “You found a beat.”

Tilly touched the tiny drum. Tap. Tap. Tap. Her taps matched the rain. Tap. Tap. Tippy-tap. The sound was soft like raindrops on a leaf. Tilly took a small breath. Her tummy felt warm and calm.

“I can make rain music,” she said.

Papa poked his head in. “I hear a lovely rain song!” he said. He picked up the egg shakers and shook them gently. Shaka-shaka. Shaka-shaka. The sound was like tiny pebbles rolling.

Tilly watched. She wanted to try the shakers. She took one shaker. It felt safe in her hand. She shook it once. Shh. Then twice. Shaka-shaka.

“This is fun,” Tilly said, smiling a little.

Then she looked at the mirror on the closet door. In the mirror, she saw a shy turtle with bright eyes. She saw her shell. She saw her small smile. She felt a little brave.

“I want to dance,” Tilly said, surprised at her own words.

Mama moved a chair to make space. “We have a dance floor,” Mama said, pointing to the rug.

Papa hummed a gentle tune. “La-la, la-la, la-la,” he sang softly. His voice was low and warm, like hot cocoa.

Tilly stood tall on the rug. She took a slow step. Her shell swayed. Step. Sway. Step. Sway. The rug brushed her feet. It felt fuzzy and safe. Tilly lifted her arms and wiggled her fingers. She twirled very slowly. Her shell made a quiet swish against her shirt.

“I am a rain turtle,” Tilly giggled. “Swish-swish, shaka-shaka, tap-tap!”

But suddenly, the rain got louder. Patter turned to plop. The sound boomed on the window. Tilly jumped. Her body felt tight. Her smile faded.

“I feel scared again,” she said. “It’s too loud.”

Mama nodded. “It’s okay to feel scared. What can help?”

Tilly looked around the guest room. She saw the soft blue blanket on the bed. She saw the gentle light. She saw the comb with paper. “Maybe I can make a quiet sound,” she said. “A calm sound for my body.”

She picked up the comb. She put the paper on it. She hummed slowly, “Mmm-mmm,” and the comb buzzed, “Bzzzz.” It sounded like a sleepy bee. Buzz-buzz. Slow and steady. Tilly’s shoulders lowered. She took a deep breath.

“I like the sleepy bee,” Tilly said. “It helps my belly feel soft.”

Papa shook the shakers very quietly. Sh… sh… sh… Mama tapped the drum with one finger. Tip… tip… tip…

Tilly smiled again. “I can make my own calm,” she said. “Music can be big or small.”

She began to dance again, tiny steps, soft sways. Her shell moved like a boat on a gentle pond. She looked in the mirror and waved to herself. “Hello, brave turtle,” she whispered.

The rain slowed down. The room felt cozy again. Tilly held the drum and the shaker at the same time. She made a pattern: tap, shake, tap, shake. Tap, shake, tap, shake. She counted, “One, two, one, two.”

Papa grinned. “You found a rhythm!”

Mama clapped quietly. “I see you listening to your feelings. You used music to help. That is very resourceful.”

Tilly stood tall. “When I feel shy or scared,” she said, “I can listen. I can make a sound that matches my feeling. I can make a calm sound. I can dance slow or fast. Music can help my heart.”

She took a little bow. Her shell clicked softly. Everyone laughed kindly. Tilly laughed, too.

“Can we have music time again tomorrow?” she asked.

“Yes,” said Mama and Papa together. “Any time you need it.”

Tilly hugged the tiny drum. The guest room still smelled like lemon and clean sheets. The window made a soft drip-drip sound. Tilly felt brave and peaceful.

She whispered, “Thank you, music. Thank you, feelings. Thank you, dance.”

And the shy turtle was shy no more—just gentle, brave, and full of song.

The End.
