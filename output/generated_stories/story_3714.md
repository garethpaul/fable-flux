# Ollie Otter and the Practice Room Beat

Ollie the playful otter slid down the shiny hallway on his smooth belly—whoosh! His whiskers tickled the air. He pushed open the door to the musical practice room. It smelled like fresh wood and lemon polish. Soft lights glowed. Drums, a piano, tiny bells, and a bright red xylophone waited inside.

“Hello, music!” Ollie giggled. He wiggled his paws. “I’m ready to play!”

A big poster on the wall said: “First, warm up. Then, follow the steps.” Next to it sat a little timer and a note from Teacher Tessa: “Ollie, please start with claps. Count to four. Then play slowly.”

Ollie’s tail swished. “I want to play fast! Fast is fun!” He picked up two wooden mallets and tapped the xylophone. Ting-ting, TANG! He tapped faster. Ting-a-ting-a-ting!

But the sounds got messy. The notes bumped into each other, like marbles crashing. Ollie’s nose scrunched. His paws felt tight. “Why won’t it sound nice?” he grumbled.

He tried again—faster this time. Ting TANG TUNG—clunk! A mallet slipped and bonked the floor. The loud clack made Ollie jump. His eyes felt hot and watery. His shoulders rose up, up, up.

“I’m mad,” Ollie said. “I can’t do it!”

He stomped one paw. Stomp. He wanted to throw the mallets. He wanted to quit. His tummy felt twisty.

Just then, a soft voice floated from the doorway. “Hi, Ollie,” said Penny, a tiny mouse with big round ears. She wore little music-note socks. “I heard taps and clacks. Are you okay?”

Ollie sniffed. “No. I tried to play fast, and it sounded bad. I feel so mad.”

Penny nodded. “It’s okay to feel mad. Let’s help your feelings. Want a calm breath?”

Ollie blinked. “A calm breath?”

Penny placed a paw on her chest. “Smell the flower.” She sniffed in. “Blow the candle.” She puffed out, slow and soft.

Ollie copied. In… out. Again. In… out. His shoulders dropped. His paws loosened. The room felt quieter. He could hear the hum of the lights and the soft tick-tick of the little timer.

“Thank you,” Ollie whispered.

Penny pointed to the poster. “Teacher Tessa left steps. We can follow directions. That helps the music grow.”

Ollie looked up. “First, warm up. Count to four. Then play slowly.”

He picked up the mallets again, gently this time. “Smell the flower… blow the candle,” he said to himself. He clapped softly. “One, two, three, four.” The claps made friendly pats in the air. Pat, pat, pat, pat.

“Good beat!” Penny cheered.

Ollie tapped the xylophone slowly. Ting… ting… ting… ting. The notes shimmered like little stars. “Oh,” Ollie smiled, “that sounds nice.”

He remembered the timer. “Teacher said start slow.” He turned the timer to one minute. Tick… tick… tick… Ollie played the same four notes slowly. Ting… ting… ting… ting. He wanted to go faster, but he waited. He followed the steps. He breathed when his paws felt wiggly.

When the timer dinged, Ollie grinned. “I did it!”

Penny clapped. “You did it the honest way, with the directions.”

Ollie’s ears twitched. He looked at a drum in the corner. On top sat a shiny triangle with a tag: “Please wait for Teacher Tessa.” Ollie really wanted to try it. The triangle glowed like moonlight. His paws tingled.

He took one step. Then he stopped. “The tag says wait,” he said. “I will tell the truth. I want to play it, but I will not touch it until Teacher says yes.”

Penny smiled. “That’s integrity. You listen, even when no one is watching.”

Ollie stood tall. “I can do hard things. I can wait.”

Just then, a small sigh floated from under the piano. “Oh no,” squeaked Benny Bunny. He crawled out, holding a wobbly stack of sheet music. Pages were slipping everywhere. Swish, slide, flutter!

“Help!” Benny yelped. “My music is a mess!”

Ollie blinked. He looked at his mallets, then at Benny’s shaky paws. “I can help,” he said. He set the mallets down carefully. “I will be gentle.”

Together, Ollie and Penny knelt. The paper felt smooth and cool. They listened to the rustle-rustle of pages and the soft hum of the room. Ollie breathed in and out when a page bent the wrong way. “It’s okay,” he told himself. “Slow and steady.”

They stacked the pages. One, two, three, four. Ollie counted each pile like a song. Benny’s ears wiggled with relief. “Thank you,” Benny said. “You followed directions even with papers!”

Ollie giggled. “Counting helps me stay calm.”

The door opened, and Teacher Tessa peeked in. “Hello, musicians! I see tidy pages, happy faces, and careful paws.”

Ollie stood up. “Teacher, I felt mad. I wanted to play fast. But I took calm breaths, followed your steps, and told the truth about the triangle.”

Teacher Tessa’s eyes sparkled. “That is wonderful integrity, Ollie. And wonderful frustration-coping. When we feel big feelings, we can breathe, go slow, and ask for help.”

“Can I try the triangle now?” Ollie asked.

“Yes,” Teacher said. “Thank you for waiting.”

Ollie lifted the little beater. Ting! The triangle rang like a tiny bell in a snowy night. The sound felt cool and bright in his ears.

He smiled at Penny and Benny. “Today, I learned to calm down, follow directions, tell the truth, and help friends.”

Teacher Tessa nodded. “That is music for the heart.”

Ollie took one more calm breath. In… out. Then he tapped the xylophone slowly. Ting… ting… ting… ting. The notes lined up like ducks in a row. His tummy felt soft and warm.

“I can do it,” Ollie said.

“Yes,” said Penny and Benny together. “You can.”

The End.
