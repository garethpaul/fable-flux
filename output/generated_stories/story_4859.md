# Roo’s Rhythm in the Garage

Roo was a joyful kangaroo joey with big soft ears and a bouncy tail. One sunny morning, Roo hopped into the garage workshop with a happy boing-boing. The garage smelled like fresh wood and warm rubber. Sunlight made little dust sparkles dance in the air. On the workbench were shiny tools, colorful buckets, and old pots that went clink and clank when tapped.

“Hello, tools!” Roo giggled. “Let’s make music.”

Roo loved music. Roo loved friends, too. Friends from all around the world were coming to visit. There was Mei from China, Samir from Egypt, Ana from Brazil, Yara from Kenya, and Tom from Australia. Roo wanted to make a welcome song in the garage workshop.

Roo picked up a wooden spoon and tapped a metal pan. Tink, tink! “Listen!” Roo said. “This can be our drum.”

Mei clapped gently. Clap, clap. “I can keep a slow beat,” she said.

Samir shook a jar filled with beans. Sh-sh-sh! “I can make a soft rain sound,” he said.

Ana plucked rubber bands stretched over a box. Twang, twang! “I can make a guitar sound,” she said, smiling.

Yara slid a paintbrush across a washboard. Zzzzzip-zzzip. “I can make it buzz,” she said.

Tom blew across a glass bottle. Hooo. “I can make wind,” he said.

Roo bounced with joy. “We have a band!” said Roo. “Let’s play!”

Everyone started at once. Tink, clap, sh-sh, twang, zzzzip, hooo! The garage filled with sounds. It was loud and messy. Tools rattled. Buckets wobbled. Roo tried to bounce in time but kept bumping a box. Thunk! The sounds crashed together and made Roo’s ears droop.

“This is hard,” Roo said softly. “I want it to sound nice.”

Mei nodded. “We are different,” she said kindly. “We can listen first.”

Roo took a deep breath. The garage smelled like oranges from a sticker on a toolbox. Roo smiled a little. “Yes. Let’s listen.” Roo’s tail made a slow swish on the floor.

They tried again. Mei started with a slow clap. Clap… clap… clap…

Samir listened and shook his beans between the claps. Sh-sh… sh-sh…

Ana plucked one twang, then another, like little steps. Twang… twang…

Yara made a soft zzzzip after Ana’s twang. Zzzzip…

Tom breathed out a long hooo at the end of the line, like a hug for the sound.

Roo bounced to the beat, not too fast, not too slow. Boing… boing… boing…

It felt better. Roo smiled wide. “I can feel the beat in my feet,” Roo said. “Boing on one, boing on two!”

But then Roo tried a big jump. Boing-boing-boing! Roo knocked the pan. Clang! Everyone stopped. Roo’s cheeks felt warm. “Oh no,” Roo said. “I made a mistake.”

Tom shook his head gently. “It’s okay,” he said. “My breath was wobbly too.”

Yara smiled. “We all do things in our own way.”

Ana said, “Different is not wrong. Different is special.”

Mei clapped softly. “We can use our differences,” she said. “We can take turns.”

Samir added, “We can make a pattern.”

Roo’s ears perked up. “A pattern?” Roo asked.

“Yes,” Samir said. “Like clap, clap, sh-sh, twang, zzzzip, hooo, boing!”

Roo’s eyes shone. “Let’s try.”

They made a plan. Mei would clap two times. Clap, clap. Samir would shake two times. Sh-sh. Ana would twang once. Twang. Yara would zzzzip once. Zzzzip. Tom would hooo once. Hooo. And Roo would boing two times. Boing, boing.

They counted together, “One, two, ready, go!”

Clap, clap. Sh-sh. Twang. Zzzzip. Hooo. Boing, boing.

They did it again. Clap, clap. Sh-sh. Twang. Zzzzip. Hooo. Boing, boing.

The sounds lined up like friends holding hands. Roo felt the beat in paws, tail, and tummy. The garage workshop felt warm and happy. Tools hummed in the stillness. The floor vibrated gently under Roo’s feet. The music sounded like kindness.

Roo bounced carefully, matching the claps. Boing on the beat. “We are doing it!” Roo laughed.

They tried adding little surprises. Mei tried a soft clap. Samir shook his jar like a tiny rain. Ana plucked a high twang. Yara brushed a long zzzzip. Tom breathed a big hooo. Roo made a small boing, then a big boing.

They looked at each other and grinned. Their eyes were bright. Their bodies moved together. They were like one big heart with many beats.

When they finished, the last hooo drifted up to the rafters. The sound faded like a whisper. The friends were quiet for a moment. Then everyone cheered.

Roo said, “Our song is special because we are different. We listened. We took turns. We worked together.”

Mei nodded. “We came from many places,” she said.

Samir smiled. “We shared one rhythm.”

Ana said, “We learned coordination. We moved together.”

Yara added, “We made room for everyone.”

Tom said, “That is global friendship.”

Roo’s tail swished happily. “Yes,” said Roo. “Around the world, we can be friends. We can celebrate our differences. We can make music that feels good and kind.”

They played the song again, and again, and again. The garage workshop felt like a big hug. Each sound had a place. Each friend had a smile.

Roo looked around at the shiny tools and the bright faces. “When we listen and share,” Roo said, “our differences make us strong.”

Everyone nodded. Together, they bowed to the garage. The dust sparkles danced. The welcome song was ready for any friend, from any place, any day.

The End.
