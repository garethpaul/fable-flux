# Polly's Voice

In a busy basement workshop, there lived a bright green parrot named Polly. She had beautiful rainbow feathers and a cheerful orange beak. The basement smelled like wood shavings and paint. Hammers went "tap-tap-tap" and saws made "buzz-buzz" sounds.

Polly loved watching the workshop friends work together. There was Carlos, who carved wooden toys. Maria painted pretty pictures on pottery. And Jin made colorful kites that danced in the wind.

Every morning, the friends would gather around a big wooden table. They would share ideas for new projects. Carlos would speak in his deep, gentle voice. Maria would show her sketches with excitement. Jin would demonstrate how his kites could fly.

But when it was Polly's turn to share, something happened. She would open her beak, then close it again. Her bright feathers would ruffle with worry.

"I have ideas too," Polly whispered to herself. "But what if they don't like them?"

One sunny Tuesday, the friends were planning a special project. They wanted to make gifts for the children at the nearby school.

"We could make wooden trucks," said Carlos, his hands moving as he spoke.

"Or painted flower pots," suggested Maria, her eyes sparkling.

"Maybe paper airplanes," added Jin, folding a piece of paper.

Polly's heart fluttered. She had a wonderful idea! She could see it clearly in her mind - beautiful dream catchers with feathers and beads from different countries. Each one would be special and unique.

But again, Polly stayed quiet. She tucked her head under her wing.

The friends worked all day. But something wasn't quite right. The wooden trucks were nice, but plain. The flower pots were pretty, but simple. The paper airplanes flew well, but looked ordinary.

"Something's missing," sighed Maria. "These gifts need more... magic."

Polly's feathers tingled. She knew exactly what was missing! Her dream catcher idea would add the perfect touch. But her voice felt stuck in her throat.

Then Polly remembered what her grandmother had taught her: "Your voice matters, little one. Your ideas are gifts too."

Polly took a deep breath. She fluffed her feathers and stood tall on her perch.

"Excuse me," Polly said clearly. Her voice was small but brave. "I have an idea."

The three friends turned to look at her with surprise and smiles.

"I was thinking," Polly continued, her voice growing stronger, "what if we made dream catchers? My grandmother from Mexico taught me about them. We could use Carlos's wooden hoops, Maria's colorful paints, and Jin's knowledge of strings and knots."

Carlos clapped his hands. "¡Qué buena idea!" he said happily.

"That's brilliant!" exclaimed Maria. "We could paint them with symbols from different cultures."

"And I know how to weave the most beautiful patterns," added Jin with excitement.

Polly felt warm and proud. "I should have spoken up sooner," she said. "I'm sorry I waited so long to share."

"Thank you for being brave and telling us your idea," said Maria gently.

Together, the four friends made the most beautiful dream catchers anyone had ever seen. Each one told a story of friendship and creativity from around the world.

When they gave the gifts to the children, the kids gasped with wonder. "These are magical!" they said.

That evening, Polly preened her feathers happily. She had learned that her voice was important. Her ideas mattered. And when friends work together, sharing their different talents and cultures, they create something truly special.

From that day on, Polly always spoke up with confidence, knowing that her creative voice made the world a more colorful place.

The End.
