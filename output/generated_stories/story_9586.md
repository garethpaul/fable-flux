# Fawn's Brave Guest Room Helper

A gentle deer fawn stepped into the guest room. The door made a tiny creak. The room smelled like clean sheets and lemon soap. Sunlight made little squares on the floor. The fawn’s hooves were soft and quiet.

“I can do it,” the fawn whispered. “I can be brave.”

The fawn’s big eyes watched the fluttering curtains. The wind whooshed and made the curtain look like a big, waving ghost. The fawn’s heart went thump-thump. The guest room was new to the fawn. New things can feel big.

Mama Deer had asked, “Fawn, can you help tidy the guest room? We have a friend coming to stay. Volunteering to help is kind and brave.”

The fawn had nodded. “I will try.”

Now, inside the guest room, the fawn looked around. A soft bed with a blue quilt waited. A chair sat by the window. A little basket of towels was on the floor. A tiny clock ticked. Tick. Tock. Tick. Tock. The sound felt slow and steady, like a calm breath.

But then—rustle, rustle—from under the bed.

The fawn froze. “Oh!” The ears pricked up. “Who’s there?”

A tiny voice squeaked, “I’m sorry! I’m stuck!”

The fawn bent down, nose close to the floor. Dust smelled like old books. A shiny button rolled out. Then a tiny mouse peeped out, whiskers twitching.

“I’m Pip,” said the mouse. “I came to help, but my tail got caught on this button. I am scared.”

The fawn felt the thump-thump again. But another feeling grew too—warm and soft, like a hug. Empathy. The fawn knew what scared felt like.

“It’s okay, Pip,” the fawn said gently. “I will help you. We can be brave together.”

Pip blinked. “Together?”

“Yes,” said the fawn. “I will count to three, and we will breathe. One… two… three.” They took a deep breath. In through the nose. Out through the mouth. Whoooo.

The fawn used a careful hoof to push the button away. Then the fawn nuzzled Pip’s tail free.

“You did it!” Pip squeaked. “Thank you!”

“We did it,” the fawn said. “Now, let’s tidy this room for our guest. I volunteer to fold the towels.”

“I will pick up the buttons,” Pip said with a tiny smile. “I volunteer too!”

They started to work. The towels felt fluffy and soft. “Fold, fold, fold,” the fawn sang in a small voice. Pip made the buttons clink in a neat little cup. Clink, clink, clink.

They found a lost sock under the chair. “Discovery!” Pip cheered. “A sock!”

They found a book on the floor. “Discovery!” the fawn laughed. “A bedtime story!”

They found a teacup by the window with a ring of sweet tea at the bottom. It smelled like honey. “We will wash this later,” said the fawn.

Then the wind whooshed again. The curtain waved like a white flag. The fawn felt the thump-thump in the chest again.

Pip looked at the fawn. “Are you okay?”

The fawn took one brave step closer to the window. “I am practicing courage,” the fawn said softly. “Courage is when you feel a little scared, but you try anyway.”

Pip nodded. “I will stand with you.”

The fawn reached out and touched the curtain with a gentle hoof. It was cool and smooth. “You are just cloth,” the fawn said kindly to the curtain. “You are not a ghost.”

Together they pulled the curtain back. Sunlight poured in, warm and yellow. Dust sparkles danced in the air like tiny stars.

“Oh,” the fawn sighed happily. “It is bright and nice. I did it.”

“You were brave,” Pip said.

The fawn smiled. “You were brave too.”

They made the bed nice and neat. They set the book on the pillow. They put the teacup by the door to take to the kitchen. The little clock kept ticking. Tick. Tock. The room felt calm, ready, and kind.

Just then, a soft voice came from the hallway. “Hello?”

A hedgehog with a small suitcase peeked in. She wore a pink scarf. “I’m Hazel,” she said. “I am the guest. Is this the room?”

“Yes!” said the fawn. “Welcome, Hazel. We prepared it for you.”

Pip waved. “We volunteered to help!”

Hazel stepped in and breathed in. “It smells fresh and sweet. The bed looks cozy. Thank you, helpers.” She patted her scarf. “I was a little worried to stay in a new place. But now I feel safe.”

The fawn nodded. “I was a little worried too. The curtain looked scary. But I found courage. I took a breath. I tried. I learned the curtain is just cloth.”

Hazel smiled. “That is brave and wise. When we help others, we also help our own hearts.”

The fawn felt warm and proud. The guest room was tidy. Pip was free and happy. Hazel felt welcome. The fawn had practiced courage, empathy, and volunteering. The fawn had made a new friend and a new discovery: the guest room was not scary at all.

The fawn looked at Pip and Hazel. “When we are scared, we can breathe. We can ask for help. We can help others too. That is courage.”

“Yes,” said Hazel. “Brave hearts make kind rooms.”

Everyone laughed gently. The sunlight glowed like honey. The clock said, Tick. Tock. All was well.

The End.
