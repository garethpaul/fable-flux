# Rooftop Rhythm: Rocky Practices

Rocky was a small, musical raccoon with bright eyes and nimble paws. He lived near a tall building with a sunny rooftop. On the rooftop, the wind hummed, the city buzzed, and birds sang. Rocky loved to make art and music there. He wore a tiny scarf to keep dust off his whiskers and kept a little water bottle nearby to stay clean and fresh.

One warm morning, Rocky carried his little drum and a paint set to the rooftop. The sun made the roof feel toasty. The breeze smelled like fresh leaves. Pigeons cooed, “Coo, coo.” Rocky smiled. “Today, I will play a song and paint the sky!” he said.

Rocky tapped his drum. Tap-tap… thump. Oh no. The sound was clunky. His paws slipped, and the beat felt wobbly. He dipped his brush in blue paint to paint a round, happy cloud, but the paint dripped in a messy line. Uh-oh. Rocky’s ears drooped.

“I want my drum to sound smooth,” he whispered. “I want my painting to look bright.” He felt a little grumpy and a little sad. The breeze tickled his fur. A pigeon hopped closer.

“Coo?” asked the pigeon.

“I’m trying,” said Rocky. “But my drum is thumpy, and my cloud is drippy. I think I can’t do it.”

The pigeon tilted its head. “Coo-coo,” it cooed softly, as if to say, “Keep trying.”

Rocky sat down. He took a sip of water. He wiped his paws on a clean cloth. “Okay,” he said, taking a deep breath. “I will practice.”

He started with slow taps. Tap… tap… tap. He listened to the wind. Whoosh. He matched the beat to the breeze. Tap… tap… whoosh… tap. His paws felt steadier. He counted softly, “One, two, tap-tap.” He smiled a tiny smile.

“Good job, paws,” Rocky said. “We can learn.”

Then he looked at his paint. The brush was very wet. “Maybe less paint,” he said. He dabbed the brush, drip-drip, on a small cloth. He added a little water from his bottle to a tiny cup, just a little, not a lot. “We must not waste water,” Rocky reminded himself. “Water is precious.” He listened to a distant sprinkler hiss, then turned it off after rinsing, saving the extra for later.

With a gentle stroke, he tried again. Swish. The cloud was softer now. “Oh!” Rocky’s eyes brightened. He practiced making circles. Round and round. Swish, swish. He made a cloud, a moon, and a smiling raccoon face. He giggled.

A curious child from the next roof waved. “Hi, Rocky!”

“Hi!” Rocky waved back. “I’m practicing my beats and my brush.”

The child clapped. “Can I listen?”

“Yes,” said Rocky. “But I need a tiny break to wash my paws.” He used a small, damp cloth to wipe paint from his paws and whiskers. “Clean paws help my drum,” he explained. “Hygiene helps me feel good.”

Rocky set a neat spot for trash and another for things to use again. He put lids on the paints so they wouldn’t dry out. He saved paper scraps for sketching. “I take care of my tools and the rooftop,” he said. “We share this place with birds and the wind.”

“Ready?” asked the child.

“Ready,” said Rocky.

He practiced the beat again. Tap-tap-tap, tap-tap. He added a soft rub on the drum’s side. Thrummm. He listened. He tried again. Tap-tap-tap, thrummm, tap. The rhythm started to dance. The pigeons bobbed their heads. The wind whispered, “Whooo.”

Rocky grinned. “Practice makes it better!”

He painted to the rhythm. Swish-swish, tap-tap. He made a bright blue sky and a little green tree. He used the leftover green to make tiny leaves. “We don’t waste,” he said. “We use what we have.” He rinsed his brush in a small jar, not pouring it down the roof drain, and later carried it downstairs to dispose properly. “We keep our water clean,” he told the child.

Rocky tried a trickier beat. Tap tap, rest. Tap tap, rest. He counted. “One-two, pause. One-two, pause.” He missed the pause. He frowned, then took a breath. “It’s okay to try again.” He tapped slowly until his paws remembered. Then he played the full pattern. Tap tap, rest. Tap tap, rest. “Yes!” Rocky cheered.

The child clapped louder. “You did it!”

“I practiced,” said Rocky. “My paws learned. My ears helped. My heart stayed calm.”

Rocky finished his painting with a tiny golden sun. He admired his work. The rooftop felt happy. It smelled like warm stone and wet paint. The drum felt smooth under his paws.

“Thank you for listening,” Rocky told the child and the pigeons. “Thank you, wind.”

“What did you learn today?” asked the child.

“I learned that practice helps me grow,” said Rocky. “I learned to keep clean so I can make good art. I learned to use water and paint carefully to care for our world. I learned to listen and understand—my drum, my brush, and my friends.”

The sun dipped low. Rocky put his lids on tight, wiped the roof clean, and packed his things. He waved good-bye to the birds and to the child.

“See you next time,” the child called.

“Next time, I will practice again!” Rocky said, smiling.

And on the peaceful rooftop, Rocky felt proud, patient, and ready to learn more, one tap and one swish at a time.

The End.
