# Rocco’s Calm Counting Song

Rocco was a musical raccoon with soft gray fur and bright, curious eyes. He loved playing little tunes on his tin-can drum and his stringy broom-guitar. His home was a cozy basement lab under Granny Dot’s house. The lab smelled like lemon soap and warm wood. Lights blinked softly. Beakers clinked. A tiny fan hummed.

Rocco used the lab to explore and learn. He called it his “Think-and-Try Room.”

“Good morning, Think-and-Try Room!” Rocco sang, patting his drum. “I want to make a Rainbow Bubble Machine.”

He pulled on his blue apron and his big orange goggles. He checked his notebook. On the cover, he had drawn a bright sun with a smile. Inside, he kept facts, pictures, and steps. Knowledge lived in that notebook.

“Step one,” Rocco read slowly. “Mix safe bubble juice.” He sniffed a bottle. It smelled like sweet soap. “Step two. Turn the cranks. Step three. Play a happy song while it works.”

Rocco poured the bubble juice into a bowl. Plip, plop. He turned one crank. Then another. The machine rattled and hummed. Rocco strummed his broom-guitar and sang, “Bubbles up, colors pop, rainbow fun that will not stop!”

But the machine sputtered. It burped. It coughed. It shot out one tiny gray bubble. Pop.

Rocco’s whiskers drooped. “Oh.”

He tried again. This time, the machine spit a big, sticky blob. Sploosh! It hit Rocco’s apron. Cold and icky!

Rocco felt his face get hot. His tail puffed. “Grrr,” he grumbled. “I worked so hard!”

He stomped his paw. His drum rattled. He wanted to yell. He wanted to kick the crank. His tummy felt tight, like a knotted string.

Just then, a gentle voice drifted from the stairs. It was Granny Dot. “I hear a grr,” she said softly. “Are you okay, Rocco?”

Rocco stood still. He took a breath in. He let it out. “I feel mad,” he said. “My machine won’t work.”

Granny Dot nodded. “Thanks for telling me. Mad feelings are okay. What can help your body right now?”

Rocco looked at his drum. He tapped it lightly. Tippy-tap. Tap. “Maybe a calm song?”

Granny Dot smiled. “Good idea. You know your body best.”

Rocco closed his eyes. He counted on his fingers as he sang, “One… two… three… breathe in with me. Four… five… six… blow out, slow and quick.” His voice was soft, like a warm blanket. He felt his tummy loosen. His tail un-puffed.

“Better,” he said. “Thank you, Granny.”

“Proud of you,” Granny Dot said. “Do you want help, or do you want to try by yourself first?”

Rocco thought. He liked being independent. He also liked learning with others. “I will try by myself, and if I get stuck, I will ask,” he said.

“Sounds wise,” said Granny. She went back upstairs, humming.

Rocco opened his notebook. Knowledge could help. He turned the page. He had written, “If bubbles turn gray, add light.” He looked around. The lab lights were dim. He flipped a switch. Click! The room glowed brighter.

He tried again. The machine hummed nicely, but only a few bubbles came out. Hmm.

Rocco read another note. “If bubbles are sticky, stir, not shake.” He had been shaking! He picked up a wooden spoon. Stir, stir, stir. Slow circles. The soap smelled like fresh rain.

He turned the cranks. Whirr, whirr. He played a gentle tune on his drum to keep a steady beat. “Tap-tap, stir-stir, we can learn and not be hurt.”

Two bubbles floated up—blue and green! Rocco clapped. “It’s working!”

Just then, a tiny mouse in a yellow vest peeped under the door. “Squeak! Is it safe? It smelled like soap in here.”

Rocco smiled. “Hi, Pip! I’m making a Rainbow Bubble Machine. I felt mad, but I used my calm counting song. Do you want to watch?”

Pip nodded, whiskers twitching. “I get mad too,” Pip said. “My tower fell today. I squeaked loud.”

Rocco said, “That is hard. I understand.” He put his paw on his heart. “We can learn together.”

Pip pointed with a tiny paw. “The crank looks stuck.”

Rocco peered. A little sticker was bent and rubbing the gear. “Good eye, Pip!” Rocco gently peeled the sticker off. He stuck it in his notebook to fix later.

“Now,” Rocco said, “we test again.”

He took three slow breaths. He counted softly. “One… two… three.” He stirred smooth circles. He turned the cranks. He played a calm, steady beat. Pip tapped a teaspoon along, ting-ting-ting. The machine purred.

Bloop… bloop… bloop…

Out floated bubbles—red, blue, yellow, orange, purple! They shimmered like tiny moons. They smelled faintly of lemon and sugar. They tickled Rocco’s nose. “Achoo!” he giggled.

Pip danced under the bubbles. “It worked! It worked!”

Rocco bounced and sang, “Think and try, don’t give up. Learn and breathe when it gets tough!”

Granny Dot peeked in again. “Oh, my stars,” she said. “A rainbow!”

Rocco turned to Pip. “Thank you for helping. Your eyes found the sticker. That was cooperation.”

Pip smiled. “You shared your song. That helped me. I feel calm now.”

Rocco wrote in his notebook: “Calm song helps my body. Light helps colors. Stir, not shake. Check for sticky stickers. Ask for help when needed. Share what you know.”

He closed the book and patted it. “Knowledge is like a lamp,” he said. “It shows the way when we feel lost.”

Granny Dot hugged them gently. “You used knowledge, you managed your mad, and you cared for each other. That is smart and kind.”

Rocco nodded. “When I feel mad again, I will stop, breathe, and look in my notebook. I will listen to my body. I will use what I know. I will ask a friend.”

They watched the bubbles float up, soft and bright. The lab hummed like a happy song. Rocco strummed his broom-guitar and whispered, “We can learn, we can care, we can try.”

The End.
