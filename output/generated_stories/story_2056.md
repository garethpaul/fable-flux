# Fawn Finds Her Feelings

In a warm kitchen, sunlight made golden squares on the floor. A gentle deer fawn named Dottie stood by a wooden chair. Her soft ears twitched, and her tiny hooves clicked on the tiles. The air smelled like cinnamon toast and sweet strawberries.

Dottie’s friend, a small robin named Pip, hopped on the windowsill. “Good morning, Dottie!” chirped Pip. “Let’s make a snack!”

Dottie smiled. “Yes, please,” she said softly. She liked the cozy kitchen. The kettle hummed. The fridge made a little buzz. A pot of oatmeal bubbled, blip-blip, on the stove. The sounds felt like friendly whispers.

Pip flew to the counter. “Let’s stir fast!” he sang. He dipped a spoon into the oatmeal and swirled in circles. The oatmeal went swish-swish.

Dottie’s tummy felt wiggly. She watched the spoon splash. Some oatmeal splattered on the counter. Plop! A bit landed on her nose. It felt warm and sticky. Dottie blinked.

“Oops,” said Pip, laughing. He flapped his wings, and a gust of air made the napkins flutter. “Let’s add lots of cinnamon. A big shake!” He tipped the shaker. A puff of cinnamon dust rose up like brown snow. Dottie smelled it. It tickled her nose.

Dottie’s chest felt tight. Her heart thump-thumped fast. Her ears drooped. “I feel… funny,” she whispered, touching her nose. Her voice was small like a tiny bell.

Pip didn’t hear. “And strawberries! Let’s toss them in!” Plop-plop-plop went the berries. The kitchen was busy and loud.

Dottie took a small step back. She didn’t like the mess. She didn’t like the rushing. She liked slow. She liked neat. She liked gentle. A warm tear slid down her cheek. She felt hot and then chilly. Her hooves pressed into the cool tile.

“I don’t feel good,” Dottie thought. “What is this feeling?” Her tummy made a soft twist. She took a deep breath. In. Out. She remembered something her Mama Deer said: “Feelings are like weather in your heart. Say their names, and the weather starts to change.”

Dottie tried. “My body feels tight,” she whispered to herself. “My chest is squishy. My head is loud.” She touched her chest. “I think I feel… overwhelmed.” The word was big, but it fit.

Pip looked up and saw Dottie’s wet eyes. He stopped flapping. “Oh,” he said, tilting his head. “Dottie, are you okay?”

Dottie took another breath. She felt the warm cinnamon air. She heard the soft kettle hum. She saw the sun squares on the floor. “I need to use my voice,” she told herself. “I can say what I need.”

In a slow, clear voice, Dottie said, “Pip, I feel overwhelmed. It’s too fast and too messy for me. Can we slow down, please?”

Pip blinked. “Thank you for telling me,” he chirped gently. “I didn’t know. I can help.” He fluttered softly to the counter and put the shaker down. The kitchen grew quieter. The napkins stopped dancing.

“What would help you feel better?” Pip asked.

Dottie’s ears lifted a little. She took initiative and looked around. She saw a small broom and a cloth by the sink. “I can wipe the counter,” she said. “And we can stir slowly. One shake of cinnamon. We can count together.”

Pip nodded. “That’s a good plan.”

Dottie ran the cloth under warm water. It felt cozy on her hoof. She wiped the counter in little circles. The sticky spots went away. Swish, swish. She smiled a tiny smile. Pip held the oatmeal pot steady.

“Ready?” asked Pip.

“Ready,” said Dottie.

They counted, “One… two… three,” in soft voices. Dottie stirred slowly. The oatmeal made a happy ploop-plop. Pip sprinkled a gentle dust of cinnamon. The air smelled just right—sweet and calm.

Dottie’s body felt different now. Her chest loosened. Her heart slowed down. She noticed the golden sun squares again. They looked like warm blankets on the floor.

“Thank you, Pip,” Dottie said. “When it was too fast, I felt overwhelmed. Saying it out loud helped. Slowing down helped too.”

Pip smiled. “I learned something,” he said. “Feelings can hide, and friends can listen. Next time, I will ask, ‘How do you feel?’ before I zoom.”

Dottie giggled. “And I will use my words when my tummy twists.”

They made two bowls of oatmeal. They added three strawberries to each—counting together, “One, two, three.” They took little bites. The oatmeal was warm and creamy. The strawberries were juicy and bright. Dottie licked a drop of pink juice from her nose. It tasted like sunshine.

After the snack, they made a “calm plan” chart with crayons. Dottie drew a tiny heart and a tiny cloud. “When feelings are stormy,” she said, “we breathe, we name the feeling, we ask for help, and we make a plan.”

Pip drew a small ear to show listening. “I can listen,” he chirped. “I can slow down.”

Dottie felt proud. She had noticed her feeling. She had spoken up. She had taken steps to help herself. And she had taught her friend.

The kitchen felt warm and safe again. The kettle hummed. The sun squares stretched like sleepy cats. Dottie wiggled her ears and smiled.

“Today,” she said, “we practiced feelings.”

“And friendship,” added Pip.

They bumped noses and beaks—boop!—and laughed together.

The End.
