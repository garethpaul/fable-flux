# Fawn’s Gentle Sorry at Supper Time

In a cozy house with big windows, a gentle deer fawn named Fawn tip-tapped into the dining room. The sun made golden stripes on the wooden floor. The air smelled like warm carrot soup and sweet apples. Fawn’s soft ears twitched, and his little hooves made quiet clacks on the floor.

“Dinner time!” called Mama Deer, placing bowls on the table. “We will eat together.”

Papa Deer set a plate of green pea pancakes in the middle. “Teamwork made this meal,” he said with a smile. “I cooked. Mama stirred. Fawn can help serve.”

Fawn’s eyes shone. “I can help!” he chirped. His tail flicked with joy. Helping felt big and brave.

He stood on a sturdy stool beside the table. He could see the shiny spoons, the warm soup that went swish in the bowl, and the apples shining like red moons. Fawn lifted the juice pitcher. It was cool and smooth. Inside, the juice was orange and sweet-smelling.

“I will pour,” said Fawn.

“Slow and steady,” said Mama Deer, her voice gentle. “Two hooves on the handle. Eyes watching the cup.”

Fawn nodded. He wanted to do it just right. He held the pitcher and looked at the cup. He could hear the soup bubbling softly and the clock going tick-tock, tick-tock. He felt proud.

But then—oops! A tiny hoof slipped. The pitcher tipped too far. Splash!

Juice splashed over the table. It ran like a river. It touched the pea pancakes. It dripped on the floor with a sticky plop-plop.

Fawn’s eyes grew big. His chest felt tight. “Oh no,” he whispered. “I ruined dinner.”

Papa Deer blinked in surprise. Mama Deer took a step back. The room was quiet except for the clock. Fawn’s eyes filled with tears. He felt hot and sad all over.

“I’m sorry,” Fawn said in a small voice. He looked at Mama and Papa. “I made a mess. I didn’t pour slow. I feel upset.”

Mama Deer knelt so her eyes were kind and close. “Thank you for your honest words,” she said softly. “Saying ‘I’m sorry’ is brave.”

Papa Deer nodded. “Everyone makes mistakes,” he said. “We can fix this together. That is teamwork.”

Fawn sniffled. “Can I help fix it?” he asked.

“Yes,” said Mama Deer. “First, take a deep breath.”

Fawn breathed in slow. He smelled oranges and warm soup. He breathed out. He felt his shoulders soften.

“Now,” said Mama, “we clean.” She handed Fawn a small cloth. It felt warm and fuzzy. Fawn wiped the table. Swish, swish. Papa Deer placed a towel on the floor. Pat, pat. Mama Deer moved the pancakes to a dry plate. “They’re still good,” she said with a wink. “A little juice won’t stop dinner.”

Fawn peeked up. “You’re not mad?” he asked.

“I felt surprised,” Papa said, “but I also feel proud. You said a clear sorry. You are learning.”

Fawn’s ears perked. “I want to do better,” he said. “Can we try again?”

Mama smiled. “Of course. We improve by practicing.”

Papa placed an empty cup on the table. “Here is a helper cup,” he said. “It has a little line. Pour to the line.”

Fawn climbed back on the stool. He put both hooves on the small pitcher now. His eyes watched the cup. The juice made a tiny glug-glug sound as it poured. Fawn stopped at the line. Not a drop spilled.

“I did it!” Fawn said, his tail swishing.

“We did it,” Mama said. “You, me, and Papa. Teamwork.”

They sat at the table. The soup was warm and sweet. The pea pancakes were soft and pea-green. Fawn took a bite. “Mmm,” he said. “These give me energy.”

“Good foods help our bodies,” Mama said. “Carrots for eyes, peas for strong tummies, apples for crunch and smiles.”

Fawn took a crunchy bite of apple. “Crunch!” The juice felt cool on his tongue. He giggled. “This tastes like sunshine.”

When dinner was done, Fawn picked up the napkins. “I can help bring these,” he said. He walked slow and careful. No spills. No slips.

At the sink, Fawn looked up at Mama and Papa. “I’m glad I said sorry,” he said. “It helped my heart feel better.”

Mama hugged him. “A real sorry uses kind words and kind actions,” she said. “You said it, and you helped clean, and you tried again.”

Papa patted Fawn’s head. “Forgiveness is like a soft blanket,” he said. “We put it around each other when we make mistakes.”

Fawn smiled. His chest felt light and warm. “Next time, I will pour slow,” he said. “I will watch the cup. I will ask for help.”

“That is improvement,” Mama said. “That is learning.”

As the clock went tick-tock and the dishes clinked, the dining room felt cozy again. The table was dry. The plates were clean. The family worked side by side.

Fawn whispered, “Sorry, forgive, improve,” like a tiny song. He swished his tail and helped Papa push in the chairs. The air smelled like soap and sweet apples.

Fawn felt brave, kind, and ready for the next dinner.

The End.
