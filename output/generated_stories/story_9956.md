# Feather's Song of Saving

Feather was a small singing bird with a bright yellow chest and tiny blue feet. Feather loved to sing. Every morning, Feather flew to the musical practice room. The room smelled like lemon polish and warm wood. Sunlight made soft squares on the floor. A piano sat in the corner, and music stands stood tall like thin trees.

“Good morning, room!” chirped Feather. “Tweet-tweet! Time to sing and stretch!”

Feather warmed up. “La-la-la!” Feather flapped wings slowly, up and down. The feathers rustled like soft paper. “One, two, stretch and spin!” Feather hopped from a drum to a chair, then balanced on the piano keys. Plink, plonk! The keys made little raindrop sounds.

This was Feather’s exercise time. Wing circles! Tail wiggles! “My body works better when I move,” Feather sang. “Move, move, then sing smooth.”

On a music stand, Feather found an old song page from Grandma Bird. The paper was light and smelled like old books. On top, in curly letters, it said: “Family Song for Sharing.” Feather’s heart felt warm.

“I will learn this song,” said Feather. “I can do it by myself.” Feather stood tall. Independence felt exciting and a little wobbly, like standing on one leg. Feather took a deep breath. “La-la—”

But suddenly, something went “crinkle-crinkle” under Feather’s feet. “Oh no!” On the floor was a crumpled plastic wrapper. Next to it was a bottle with a lid. The bottle was empty. A rolling cap spun away, making a tiny whir sound before it stopped.

Feather hopped closer. “Who left this? The room looks sad.” Feather imagined the bottle rolling out the door and into the park, then into a little stream. The water would carry it away. Fish might see it. Birds might peck it. Feather felt a twist in the tummy. “This is not safe,” Feather whispered. “The earth is our home. We must take care.”

Feather looked around. A small broom leaned by the door. A blue bin had a label with three arrows in a circle. Feather chirped, “Recycle!” Feather picked up the wrapper with the tip of a wing. “Light as a leaf,” Feather said. The bottle made a hollow sound—toc-toc—when Feather tapped it. Feather put the bottle in the recycle bin. The wrapper went in the trash, because it was crinkly and not for the blue bin.

“Clean room, happy song,” Feather sang. “Clean room, safe earth.” The room looked brighter already.

Just then, the piano gave a soft sigh when the sun warmed its wood. Feather laughed. “Thank you, piano. Let’s keep you clean too.” Feather gently dusted the keys with a cloth. Swish, swish. The lemon smell grew sweeter.

Feather went back to the old song page. The notes looked like little birds on a wire. Feather sang the first line. “La-la-la-la, share what you know.” Feather paused. “Share what you know,” Feather repeated, nodding. “I can share how to care for our home.”

There was a tiny tap at the window. Tap-tap! A friend was there. It was Pip, a curious mouse with big ears and quick feet.

“Hi, Feather!” Pip squeaked. “I heard singing. Can I listen?”

“Yes, Pip! Come in,” said Feather. Pip scurried inside. His whiskers twitched at the lemon smell. “It smells clean,” Pip said.

Feather smiled. “We cleaned up. We recycle to help the earth. Watch!” Feather pointed a wing to the bins. “Blue bin for bottles and paper. Trash bin for things that cannot be used again.”

Pip nodded. “I can do that too! Can I help?”

“You can,” said Feather. “Let’s do our exercise, then sing and share.” Feather and Pip stretched—reach up high, touch your toes, wiggle your nose! They giggled. Their feet made soft pat-pat sounds on the wood floor.

After moving, Feather sang Grandma Bird’s song. The notes were bright and happy. “Share what you know, care where you live. Use less, pick up, and give, give, give.” Pip clapped tiny paws. Clap-clap!

Feather showed Pip how to use a little water to wipe, not a lot. “We save water,” said Feather. “Turn off the tap. Drip-drop, stop!” Pip practiced. Twist, twist. The tap went quiet.

Feather and Pip found a box of old ribbons. They tied a green ribbon on the recycle bin. It looked friendly. Feather said, “Green means grow.” Pip added a leaf sticker on the window. The sun shone through it, making a soft green glow on the floor.

“Feather,” asked Pip, “why do you care so much?”

Feather thought of the stream and the fish and the birds. Feather thought of Grandma Bird’s song and the warm music room. “Because this is our home,” said Feather. “When we care for our home, our songs sound sweeter, our feet feel lighter, and our friends are safe.”

Pip smiled. “I can share this with my family. I can clean my nest. I can recycle too.”

Feather puffed up with pride. Independence felt strong now, like a steady drumbeat—dum, dum, dum. “You can do it,” Feather said. “You can be a helper.”

They sang together one more time. Their voices blended like honey and tea. The piano hummed along, plink-plonk, as if it agreed.

At the end, the room was tidy, the bins were marked, and the air felt fresh. Feather rolled up the old song page carefully. “Traditions are for sharing,” Feather said. “Now the song goes to you, Pip.”

Pip held the rolled page like a treasure. “I will share it.”

Feather chirped, “Today we learned: Move your body, use your voice, and care for your world. Little actions help a lot.”

Pip nodded. “Pick up, recycle, save water, share the way!”

They waved goodbye at the door. The sun made a warm hug around them. Feather took a deep breath and sang a soft, happy note.

The End.
