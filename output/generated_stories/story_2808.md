# Kiki the Calm Koala and the Pantry Plan

Every morning, Kiki the calm koala woke up with a soft stretch and a tiny yawn. She liked her routine. She brushed her fuzzy cheeks, patted her round tummy, and whispered, “I can do this myself.” Her leaves were in the pantry, and today she wanted to make her own breakfast.

Kiki tiptoed to the pantry. The wooden door smelled like warm toast. When she opened it, cool air tickled her nose, and the light clicked on with a blink. Inside were jars, baskets, and boxes. There were crunchy oat crackers, tiny jars of honey, and a big basket of eucalyptus leaves just for Kiki.

“Good morning,” Kiki said to the pantry. She liked to talk to things. Talking helped her think. “Today I will make a calm koala breakfast. I will do it by myself.”

Kiki took a deep breath. In and out. She remembered her routine: wash, gather, make, clean.

First, wash. She slipped on her little apron with green leaves on it. Swish, swish. She washed her paws at the sink until they were squeaky and cool. “Clean paws are happy paws,” she said.

Next, gather. Kiki looked around with curious eyes. “I need leaves, a plate, and a tiny drizzle of honey,” she whispered. She could smell the soft minty scent of eucalyptus leaves. She could hear the gentle rattle of jars when she moved them.

The plate was on a high shelf. Kiki blinked. The shelf looked tall. “Hmm,” she hummed. “What can I try?”

Kiki remembered her calm plan. “I can be resourceful. I can try one safe step at a time.”

She spotted a sturdy step stool in the corner. It was small, with rubber feet. Kiki checked it with her paw. “Steady?” she asked the stool.

“I am steady,” she answered in a tiny pretend stool voice. That made her smile.

She placed the stool by the shelf and climbed one step. Tap. She climbed the second step. Tap. “Slow and safe,” Kiki said. She reached, reached—and touched the plate. “Got it!” she giggled. The plate was smooth and cool.

Now the honey. The jar was not heavy, but the lid was tight. Kiki tried to twist. Twist, twist. It would not open. She felt a little frown in her forehead.

“It’s okay to feel stuck,” Kiki told herself. “I can try another way.”

She wrapped a small cloth around the lid to help her grip. Twist. Pop! The lid opened with a happy little sound. “Thank you, cloth,” she said.

Finally, the leaves. She picked soft, fresh leaves and laid them on the plate in a circle. She drizzled a tiny ribbon of honey. Drip, drip. The honey smelled like flowers in the sun.

Kiki sat at the little pantry table. She took a bite. Crunch. Sweet. Cool. “Mmm,” she sighed. “I made this myself.”

Just then, a little ant peeked out from behind a jar. “Hello,” said the ant in a squeaky voice. “That smells nice.”

“Hello, Ant,” Kiki said gently. “I made a calm breakfast. It is part of my routine.”

The ant wiggled its feelers. “I am small, and I can do small things by myself,” the ant said proudly. “I carry crumbs.”

Kiki smiled. “We are different, and that is okay. I am big and calm. You are small and quick. We can both be ourselves.”

Kiki ate another leaf. She watched the ant carry a tiny breadcrumb. Step by tiny step, the ant moved along the edge of the plate.

“You are very strong,” Kiki said.

“I have a plan,” said the ant. “One crumb, then another. That is my routine.”

Kiki nodded. “I have a plan too. Wash, gather, make, clean.” She felt warm and happy inside. Doing things herself felt good and steady.

After breakfast, it was time to clean. Kiki wiped the table with a soft cloth. Swish, swish. She rinsed the plate. Splash, splash. She put the honey back on the shelf. Tap. She folded her apron and hung it on a little hook shaped like a leaf.

“Pantry, thank you,” Kiki said. The pantry smelled like honey and leaves and a tiny bit like lemon from the clean cloth.

The ant waved a feeler. “Bye, Kiki!”

“Bye, Ant! Have a good crumb day,” Kiki said.

Kiki took one more deep breath. In and out. She felt calm and proud. “I did it myself,” she whispered. “I tried new ways. I was safe and careful. I followed my routine.”

She patted her round tummy. “When I need help, I can ask,” she added, “and when I can, I can do things by myself.”

Kiki looked around the tidy pantry. Everything had a place, and everything was in its place. She accepted that some things were high, some things were small, and some lids were tight. With patience and curious trying, she found her own way.

Kiki gave a tiny koala clap. “Today was a good pantry plan,” she said. “Tomorrow, I will do it again.”

The End.
