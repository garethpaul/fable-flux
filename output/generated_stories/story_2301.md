# Ping-Pong Panda’s Pantry Parade

Ping-Pong was a small, soft panda with round ears and bright eyes. He lived with his family in a cozy house. One rainy morning, Ping-Pong padded into the pantry. The pantry smelled like warm bread and sweet cinnamon. Shelves stood tall like gentle giants, holding jars and boxes. It was quiet except for the pitter-patter of rain and the soft hum of the fridge.

“Good morning, pantry,” Ping-Pong whispered.

His friend, Tiko the tiny tiger, peeked around a basket of apples. “Hi, Ping-Pong! I want to build a snack parade!” Tiko’s stripes wiggled with excitement.

Ping-Pong clapped his fuzzy paws. “A snack parade sounds fun. We can make safe snack floats! We can make a crunchy carrot car and a banana boat.”

From the top shelf, Nana Bear called, “Only use safe things on the low shelves, please. No climbing. Ask for help if you need it.”

“Yes, Nana,” Ping-Pong answered. He looked up at the tall shelves. They were too high. He felt a little wiggly inside. He wanted the shiny jar with rainbow sprinkles, but it was far, far up. “I will be patient,” he said to himself. “I can take turns, and I can ask for help.”

Tiko pointed to the bottom shelf. “Let’s start with crackers and fruit. I saw some peanut butter too!”

Ping-Pong took a breath. “We can take turns choosing,” he said kindly. “You choose first, Tiko.”

Tiko’s eyes sparkled. “I choose square crackers for wheels!” He opened a small box on the low shelf and smiled at the crunchy smell.

“Good choice,” Ping-Pong said. “My turn. I choose the banana for the boat.” The banana was bright yellow and curved like a smile. It felt smooth and cool in his paws.

They set the snacks on a clean tray on the floor. Ping-Pong carefully spread peanut butter with a short, safe butter knife. It was brown and sticky and smelled nutty. “Slow and steady,” he said.

“Zoom!” Tiko made vroom-vroom noises with the cracker wheels.

Ping-Pong giggled. “Now we glue the wheels on with peanut butter. Your turn to spread.”

Tiko took the butter knife. He wiggled with excitement and almost splashed the peanut butter over the edge. Ping-Pong placed a gentle paw on Tiko’s shoulder. “Let’s be safe. Slow hands, please.”

Tiko nodded. “Slow hands,” he repeated. He spread the peanut butter carefully. The crackers stuck to a carrot like tiny wheels. “We did it!”

Ping-Pong and Tiko cheered softly. “Hooray!”

Nana Bear peeked in. “What a creative idea! I see safe choices and careful hands.”

“Thank you, Nana,” Ping-Pong said. He looked up again. The rainbow sprinkles still sparkled on the tall shelf. His heart went thump-thump. “I want sprinkles,” he whispered. But then he remembered: no climbing.

Ping-Pong took a deep breath and counted to three. “One… two… three.” He waited. He waited his turn to talk. “Nana,” he said, “may we please have the sprinkles? We didn’t climb. We are taking turns and being safe.”

Nana smiled. “Yes. Thank you for asking and for waiting. That was very patient.” She reached up and handed them the sprinkle jar. It rattled softly like tiny bells.

“Your turn to sprinkle first,” Ping-Pong told Tiko.

Tiko shook his head. “You asked. You can go first.”

Ping-Pong felt warm and happy. “Thank you.” He sprinkled a little rainbow rain on the banana boat. The colors looked like confetti at a party. He passed the jar back. “Your turn.”

Tiko sprinkled gently. “It’s so pretty!”

Next, they made a berry balloon float with grapes on toothpick sticks, but Nana handed them safer, round-tipped sticks instead. “Let’s use these,” she said. “Safe hands, safe tools.”

They pushed the grapes on slowly. Pop, pop, pop. The grapes were cool and juicy, and a tiny sweet smell filled the pantry.

Soon, their snack parade was ready: the carrot car with cracker wheels, the banana boat with rainbow sprinkles, and the berry balloon float with safe sticks. Ping-Pong and Tiko lined them up on the tray.

“Before we eat,” Ping-Pong said, “let’s take turns marching.” He tapped the tray softly like a drum. “Boom-boom, boom-boom.”

Tiko marched first, shoulders back, tail swishing. “My turn!” he sang.

Ping-Pong waited, paws still, eyes bright. When Tiko finished, Ping-Pong took his turn, marching slowly, smiling big. “Now we both had a turn,” he said.

They sat on a small mat. “We can take turns eating too,” Ping-Pong suggested. “One bite for you, one bite for me.”

“Okay!” Tiko took a tiny bite of cracker wheel. Crunch. Ping-Pong took a tiny bite of banana boat. Squish-sweet. They giggled, chewing carefully.

Nana Bear clapped softly. “You were patient. You took turns. You were safe. And you were so creative!”

Ping-Pong’s heart felt full. “Taking turns makes the fun last longer,” he said.

Tiko nodded. “And waiting keeps us safe.”

Ping-Pong licked a sprinkle from his paw and smiled. “We can make another parade tomorrow,” he said. “We can think of new snack floats and new turns to take.”

“Tomorrow,” Tiko agreed, “I’ll wait while you choose first.”

Ping-Pong hugged his friend. The pantry smelled sweet and warm. The rain slowed to a soft tap-tap. Their tray looked like a tiny festival.

Ping-Pong whispered, “Patience, safety, and taking turns make everything better.”

The End.
