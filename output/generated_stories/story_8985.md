# Berry Fair in the Treehouse

Sunlight sprinkled through the leaves and danced on the wooden floor of the treehouse. A friendly bear cub named Beni rubbed his eyes and sniffed the morning air. It smelled like pine, sweet sap, and a little bit like blueberries from yesterday’s snack.

“Today is Treehouse Game Day!” Beni cheered. His small paws pattered across the floor. He loved Game Day. He loved the ladder, the rope swing, and the funny blue button that made the bell go “ding!”

Beni peeked out the window. “Hello, little birds! Hello, wiggly squirrels!” he called. His voice was warm and kind, like a cozy blanket. He opened a basket. Inside were bright red caps, yellow beanbags, and a shiny medal that said, “Great Helper.”

Beni had a goal. “I will run the best game ever,” he said. He made a tiny plan on a leaf with a charcoal stick. “Step one: set up. Step two: share. Step three: cheer.”

Soon his friends climbed up. Pip the chipmunk brought a tiny drum. Lila the rabbit carried carrot sticks. Miri the mouse held a little kite.

“Welcome to the Treehouse Fair!” Beni announced. “We have a beanbag toss, a rope swing turn, and the bell ring.”

“Oooo!” said Lila. “Can I go first?”

“I want first!” Pip squeaked, bouncing. Miri’s whiskers twitched. “I can wait,” she said softly.

Beni looked at the beanbags. His paws felt tingly with excitement. “I set it up,” he thought. “Maybe I should go first.” He grabbed a beanbag and tossed it. Plop! Right in the bucket.

“Me next!” Pip reached out.

Beni scooped up another beanbag. “I will try again,” he said quickly, tossing. Plop!

Lila’s ears drooped. Miri wrung her tail. Pip’s cheeks puffed.

“Beni,” Lila said in a small voice, “it doesn’t feel fair.”

The treehouse went quiet. Beni’s heart thumped. He looked at his friends. Pip’s tiny drum sat still. Miri’s kite strings dangled. He could hear the wind whispering through the leaves: whooo, share, whooo.

Beni’s tummy felt heavy. He remembered his plan. Step two: share. He had skipped it.

He took a deep breath. “You are right,” he said softly. “I took too many turns. That was not fair.” He placed the beanbags down. “I’m sorry.”

Pip’s cheeks softened. Lila’s ears perked up. Miri gave a tiny smile. Beni felt warm inside, like the sun found his heart again.

“I have an idea,” Beni said, taking initiative. He found a smooth stick and drew a circle on the floor. “Let’s make a Turn Circle! We stand in the circle and move one step after each turn. That way, everyone knows when it is their turn.”

“Yes!” Lila clapped her paws.

Beni picked up the shiny medal and hung it on a hook. “The medal is for the ‘Great Helper’ today,” he said. “Whoever helps others gets it for a while.”

They tried the new plan. Lila stepped into the Turn Circle. She tossed a beanbag. Plop! “Good toss!” Beni cheered.

Pip stepped next. He missed. Thunk. His whiskers trembled.

“It’s okay,” Miri said kindly. “Try again later.” She showed him how to aim. Pip tried again—plop!

“Great helping, Miri,” Beni said. He placed the medal around her tiny neck. Miri’s eyes sparkled.

Next was the rope swing. The rope felt rough and friendly in Beni’s paws. “Remember,” he said, “one swing each, then we step in the circle.” The breeze smelled like green leaves as they swung, one by one, tails wiggling, ears flapping, fur ruffling. Everybody laughed.

Then the bell. Ding! Ding! Ding! The sound was bright and happy. Each friend rang it once. Beni waited his turn, paws quiet, eyes smiling.

At snack time, they shared carrot sticks and blueberries. Sweet juice popped on their tongues. “Mmm,” said Lila. “Fair tastes yummy.”

Beni nodded. “I have a new goal,” he said. He drew a tiny star on his leaf plan. “My goal is to be fair every day.”

“How will you do it?” Miri asked.

Beni counted on his claws. “One: make a plan. Two: share turns. Three: use kind words. Four: say sorry if I forget, and fix it.”

Pip tapped his drum softly. “Boom-boom. That sounds good.”

The sun slid across the floor, making gold stripes. They played one last game. Beni watched carefully. He kept the Turn Circle moving. He handed beanbags to friends before himself. When he took a turn, he smiled and stepped back.

At the end, Beni rang the bell once—ding. “Thank you for playing fair,” he said. “Today was fun because we all got a turn. That is fair.”

Miri lifted the medal. “You helped too, Beni,” she said. “You saw a problem, you said sorry, and you made a plan.” She passed him the medal. It felt cool and smooth against his fur.

Beni’s eyes shone. “We can share the medal,” he said. “Fair for all.”

They watched the sky turn peach and pink. Leaves whispered. The treehouse felt safe and happy. Beni hugged his friends. His hug was big and gentle, like honey on warm toast.

“What did we learn today?” Beni asked.

Pip drummed. “Share turns!”

Lila giggled. “Use kind words.”

Miri nodded. “Say sorry and fix it.”

Beni smiled. “Fairness makes fun for everyone.”

The End.
