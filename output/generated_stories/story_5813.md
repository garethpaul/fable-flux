# Roo and the Garden Shed Clock

Roo was a small, determined rabbit with soft gray ears and bright, curious eyes. He liked to hop fast, think fast, and finish things fast. One sunny morning, Roo stood outside the garden shed. The wooden door smelled like pine. The old hinges squeaked, “Eee-eee,” every time it opened.

Grandpa Hare handed Roo a little list. “Roo,” he said kindly, “today we will fix the watering can and tidy the shed. The seedlings need water by noon. Here are the directions. Follow them step by step.”

Roo puffed out his chest. “I can do it! I’m brave and quick.”

Grandpa Hare smiled and pointed to a round clock on the shed wall. “We have two hours. The big hand and little hand will help you with time. If we follow directions, we will be done on time.”

Roo nodded, but his whiskers wiggled with impatience. “I can do it faster if I do it my way!”

They stepped inside. The shed was cool and smelled like soil and lemon soap. Sunlight slipped through a tiny window and made dust sparkle in the air. Tools hung on hooks: a small hammer, a green tape measure, a box of screws. The watering can sat on the table with a tiny leak.

Grandpa Hare tapped the list. “Step one: Put on safety goggles and gloves.”

Roo grabbed the hammer. “I don’t need those. I’ll just fix it quick!”

Clink! Clank! Roo banged the side of the watering can. The hammer slipped. “Oh!” he squeaked, jumping back. A screw rolled off the table and dropped to the floor—plink!

Grandpa Hare’s ears lifted. “Roo,” he said gently, “brave is not the same as rushing. Courage can be slow and careful. Let’s read the directions together.”

Roo’s cheeks felt warm. He looked at the clock. The big hand was moving. He took a breath. “Okay. I will try again.”

He slid on the goggles. The strap felt snug. He pulled on gloves. They were soft and smelled like clean cotton. Grandpa Hare’s eyes crinkled. “Good. Step two: Find the right patch and seal. They are in the blue box on the second shelf.”

Roo hopped to the second shelf. There was a green box, a red box, and—ah!—a blue box. He opened it. Soft patches and a tiny tube of sealant sat inside. “Found them!” Roo said proudly.

“Step three: Wipe the can dry and clean the hole,” Grandpa Hare read.

Roo dabbed the spot with a cloth. The cloth felt rough. The metal felt cool. He could see the little hole like a tiny mouth. “All clean.”

“Step four: Put sealant around the hole. Then place the patch on top. Press and hold for ten slow counts.”

Roo held the tube. His paws shook a little. “I’m a little scared I’ll do it wrong,” he whispered.

Grandpa Hare put a gentle paw on Roo’s shoulder. “Courage means doing the right thing even when you feel unsure. Directions are here to help you.”

Roo squeezed a thin circle of sealant. He placed the patch carefully. “One… two… three…” he counted slowly. The shed was quiet, except for the ticking clock and his soft voice. “Ten.”

He let go. The patch stayed. Roo’s eyes shone. “I did it!”

Grandpa Hare smiled. “Step five: Wait five minutes for it to set. While we wait, we can tidy up. The list says: ‘Put tools back on hooks. Sweep the floor. Stack pots by size.’”

Roo looked at the clock again. The big hand moved a little. He nodded. “We have time.” He followed the list. Hook by hook, tool by tool. The hammer went on the hammer hook. The tape measure snapped shut and hung neatly. Roo swept. Swish, swish. Dust made a little cloud, then settled. He stacked small pots inside big pots—clink, clink.

“Time,” said Grandpa Hare. “Step six: Test the can.”

Roo carried the can outside. The handle felt smooth in his glove. He filled it at the spout with cool water. The water made a friendly glug-glug sound. He held his breath. Not a drop fell out. “It works!” he cheered.

Grandpa Hare checked the clock. “Perfect. It’s eleven fifty-five. Five minutes before noon. You managed your time well.”

Roo hopped high. “I followed the directions, and we finished on time!”

They walked to the garden beds. Tiny green seedlings reached up like little hands. Roo poured water gently. The air smelled like damp earth and fresh leaves. A butterfly floated by, yellow and light. Grandpa Hare watched with warm eyes.

Roo looked back at the shed. The tools were tidy. The floor was clean. The watering can was fixed. His heart felt big and calm. “Grandpa,” he said, “going slow and following the steps helped me be brave.”

Grandpa Hare nodded. “Yes, Roo. Directions keep us safe. Good timing helps us finish our work. And courage helps us try, even when we feel unsure.”

Roo’s whiskers twitched with pride. “Next time, I will read the list first.”

Grandpa Hare laughed softly. “That is a wise plan, Roo.”

They finished watering right as the clock chimed noon—ding, ding. The seedlings looked happy, and Roo did too.

The End.
