# Benny Beaver’s Pantry Parade

Benny was a busy beaver with bright, shiny teeth and a tail that went thump-thump when he was excited. He lived with Mama Beaver and Baby Bea in a cozy home with a small, cool pantry. The pantry smelled like sweet apples, warm oats, and a little bit of cinnamon. Jars clinked softly, and paper bags rustled like leaves.

Benny loved to help, but sometimes he rushed. One sunny morning, Mama Beaver said, “Benny, today you are in charge of the pantry. That is a big job. We must be responsible. Responsible means we do what we say we will do.”

Benny puffed up with pride. “I can do it! I will sort the snacks,” he said. His tail thumped the floor. Thump-thump!

Mama gave him a warm smile. “Thank you, Benny. I’m grateful for your help.” She put a little sticky note on the door. It said, “Thank you” in three languages: “Thank you. Gracias. Merci.”

Benny traced the words with a paw. “Thank you. Gracias. Merci,” he repeated, feeling the smooth paper. Baby Bea clapped. “Gra-ci-as!” she giggled.

Benny opened the pantry door. Cool air brushed his whiskers. Crinkle! went the chip bag. Tap-tap went the glass jars of beans. “I will make neat rows,” Benny said. “Crunchy snacks here. Fruity snacks there. Spices on the top.”

He started working, but a happy song bubbled up inside him. “Thump-thump, swish-swish,” he sang, tapping his tail and swishing it on the floor. He took a tiny dance step. Then another. The bag of rice wiggled. The flour puffed. Poof! A white cloud dusted his nose.

“Achoo!” Benny sneezed. The cans rolled. Clink-clonk, clink-clonk! A box of pasta slid to the edge. “Oh no!” Benny gasped. He caught the box just in time, but the pantry was now a big, messy puzzle.

His ears drooped. “I was supposed to be responsible,” he whispered. He felt a wobbly feeling in his tummy.

Mama peeked in. She sniffed the soft flour in the air and saw the little mess. “Benny, what happened?” she asked gently.

Benny looked at his paws. “I danced and made a mess. I did not do my job. I am sorry,” he said, his voice small. “I want to fix it.”

Mama nodded. “Thank you for telling the truth. That is responsible. What is your plan?”

Benny took a deep breath. He listened to the tiny sounds: drip from the sink, rustle of a bag, hum of the fridge. He felt calmer. “First, I will sweep up the flour. Then I will sort the food by kind and by date. Old in front, new in back. I will use labels.” He picked up the sticky note and grinned. “I will write ‘Thank you’ on each shelf, so I remember to be grateful.”

Mama smiled. “That is a smart plan. I am grateful for you.”

Benny found the small broom. Swish-swish went the bristles. He wiped the jars with a soft cloth. Wipe, shine, wipe, shine. He lined up cans: beans, corn, tomatoes. Clink. Clink. Clink. He smelled the sweet apples and put them in a basket, stems up like tiny hats.

Baby Bea waddled in with a crayon. “Hep?” she asked.

“Yes,” Benny said. “You can help.” He drew little labels and said the words slowly so Bea could learn. “Beans. Frijoles. Haricots.” He pointed to the “Thank you” note. “Thank you. Gracias. Merci.”

Baby Bea repeated, “Tank you. Gwa-see-as. Meh-see.” She giggled and clapped.

Benny smiled. “Good words help our hearts,” he said. “They remind us to be kind.”

He stuck the labels on the shelves. He put crunchy snacks together. He put fruity snacks together. He put spices up top and closed the lids tight. Click! Click! He checked the dates, moving older items to the front. “Old in front, new in back,” he sang softly.

He wanted to dance again, but he remembered his job. “Dance later,” he told himself. “Work first.” He thumped his tail once, gently. Thump.

When the pantry was neat, Benny stood back. The rows were straight like little soldiers. The air smelled clean, and the floor felt smooth under his paws. The words on the shelves made a happy rainbow of labels.

Mama came back. Her eyes grew warm. “Benny, this is wonderful,” she said. “You were responsible. You made a plan. You fixed the problem. Thank you.”

Benny’s chest felt light and bright. “Thank you. Gracias. Merci,” he said. He turned to Baby Bea. “Would you like a little dance now?”

“Yes!” Baby Bea squealed.

Benny looked at the pantry. “We can dance with care,” he said. He made a soft, tiny dance: tip-toe, tail-tap, side-step. Tip-tap, step-step. Baby Bea copied him, her feet pat-pat on the floor. Mama clapped a gentle rhythm. Clap, clap, clap.

They danced in the hallway, not in the pantry. They listened to the happy sounds and kept the shelves safe. Benny felt proud. He had fun and he did his job.

That night, they had apples and warm oats with cinnamon. Mama said, “I am grateful for our food. I am grateful for you.” They all said together, “Thank you. Gracias. Merci.”

Benny smiled and rubbed his paws. “Today I learned,” he said, “that being responsible means doing my job, telling the truth, and fixing mistakes. I can have fun and still take care.”

Mama nodded. “That is a good lesson.”

Benny’s tail thumped one last time. Thump. He looked at the neat pantry and whispered, “Thank you,” to the shelves, the food, and his family.

The End.
