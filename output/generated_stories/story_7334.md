# Rooftop Hops and Friendly Stops

Sunny Roo, a joyful kangaroo joey, loved the rooftop garden. The tiles felt warm under his big feet. The air smelled like mint leaves and rain. Tiny bells on the railing went ting-ting in the breeze. Every morning, Sunny followed his routine: stretch, breathe, hop, and wave.

“Good morning, sky!” Sunny said, lifting his arms. He took a deep breath. In, and out. “Ahhh.”

Nana Koko, the kind koala who tended the rooftop garden, smiled from her stool. “Morning, Sunny. Ready for your hop-exercise?”

Sunny nodded. “Stretch, breathe, hop, and wave!” he sang.

He stretched his arms like a star. He breathed in the smell of tomatoes and soil. He hopped—boing! boing!—soft and steady, counting to five.

Then came Zara Zebra and Pip Pup, Sunny’s friends. Their hooves and paws clicked and pattered on the tiles.

“Play tag!” Pip barked. “Now! Now!”

Zara swished her tail. “And let’s race! Fast, fast, fast!”

Sunny smiled. He liked to play, but he also liked his routine. “First I finish my three sets,” he said gently. “Then we play.”

Pip bounced closer. “Tag!” He tapped Sunny’s arm with his paw. “You’re it!”

Sunny’s ears twitched. Pip had touched him without asking. He felt a hot bubble in his tummy. His tail swished. He wanted to hop away fast. But he remembered Nana Koko’s calm voice: “Use your words. Set your space. Breathe.”

Sunny put his paws on his chest. “Pause,” he whispered to himself. He took one deep breath. In, and out.

Then he looked at Pip and Zara. “Please ask before you touch me,” Sunny said. “I’m still doing my hops.”

Pip blinked. “Oh.” Zara’s ears drooped. “We forgot,” she said softly.

Nana Koko nodded. “Good words, Sunny. Clear and kind.”

Sunny smiled a little. “You can watch me finish. Then we plan a game.”

Pip and Zara stood by the mint plants. They watched Sunny hop—boing, boing, boing—slow and steady. The sun made the tomatoes glow red. A pigeon cooed from a chimney. When Sunny finished, he took another breath. “All done!”

“Race?” Zara asked.

Sunny rubbed his arm where Pip had tapped. The hot bubble had cooled, but a little heat stayed. He didn’t want to feel mad. He wanted to feel safe and happy.

“I want to play,” he said, “and I want my space.” He pointed to the chalk lines Nana Koko had drawn. “Let’s make hop lanes. We can race in lanes. No bumping.”

Pip wagged his tail. “Yes! Lanes!”

Zara smiled. “And we can say, ‘Ready?’ before we start.”

They took chalk and drew three long lines on the tiles. They made a start star and a finish flower. Sunny hopped in the middle lane. Zara took the left. Pip took the right.

“Check-in?” Sunny asked.

“What’s that?” Pip tilted his head.

“It means we ask, ‘Ready for touch?’ before tagging or high-fiving,” Sunny said. “We listen to yes or no.”

“Oh!” Pip’s eyes shone. “Ready for a high-five after the race?” he asked.

Sunny nodded. “Yes.”

Zara asked, “Ready to stand close at the start?”

Sunny looked at his belly. It felt calm now. “Yes, with space for tails.”

They lined up. Nana Koko lifted a little bell. “On your marks. Get set. Ting!”

They hopped and galloped down the lanes. Sunny felt the wind lick his ears. He stayed in his lane. He pumped his little arms. Boing, boing, boing! Zara’s hooves clacked like tiny drums. Pip’s paws pat-pat-patted.

They reached the finish flower and stopped. Pip wagged. “High-five?” he asked again.

Sunny smiled. “Yes.” Their paws met with a soft smack. Zara clapped her hooves. “Yay!”

“Again?” Pip asked.

Sunny checked in with his body. “I need a breath and a sip,” he said.

“Break time,” Nana Koko agreed, handing out cool water. The cups felt smooth and wet. The water tasted like fresh rain.

As they sipped, a big gull swooped low. Its wings whooshed. Its shadow slid over the tiles. Pip jumped and bumped Sunny’s shoulder. “Oops!” Pip gasped. “I didn’t ask.”

Sunny felt a flicker of heat, then remembered his steps. “Pause,” he whispered. He took a breath. “Please say ‘Ready?’ before touching me,” he said, calm and clear.

“I’m sorry,” Pip said. “Ready for a shoulder tap?”

Sunny checked inside. His belly felt fine now. “Yes, gentle.”

Tap. “Thank you,” Sunny said. The heat fizzled out like a tiny pop.

Zara looked thoughtful. “What if someone says no?” she asked.

“Then we listen,” Sunny said. “No is a full sentence. We find another way.”

Nana Koko nodded. “That is how we keep each other safe.”

They tried a new game: Mirror Hops. No touching, just copying. Sunny hopped high; Zara hopped low. Pip spun; Sunny wiggled. They laughed and laughed. The bells went ting-ting again, happy and light.

When the sun slid a little lower, Nana Koko clapped softly. “Clean-up time.”

Sunny loved to help. He rolled the hose, patted soil around the tomato stems, and stacked the cups. Pip and Zara gathered chalk and leaves. “Teamwork!” they sang.

Before they left, Sunny shared his routine with his friends. “Stretch, breathe, hop, and wave,” he said. “And we ask, we listen, and we respect no.”

Pip wagged. “Ask first.”

Zara nodded. “Listen to yes or no.”

Sunny waved at the sky. The rooftop smelled like mint and sunshine. His heart felt light.

“Today I learned I can say what I need,” Sunny said. “And I can be kind.”

Nana Koko hugged herself, not touching anyone. “Air hugs,” she said.

“Air hugs!” they all cheered, arms wide in the golden light.

The End.
