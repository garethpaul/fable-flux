# Pippa Parrot and the Whispering Attic

Pippa was a small, creative parrot with bright green feathers. She loved to make art with anything she found. One rainy afternoon, she flapped up a narrow stairway and peeked into a quiet attic.

The attic smelled like old books and warm dust. Sunlight slid through a tiny window, making soft squares on the wooden floor. Boxes rested like sleepy bears. A tiny fan hummed. Pippa’s feathers tickled in the still air.

“Oh! So many things to make!” Pippa chirped. “I can craft a picture!”

She waddled to a box and lifted the lid with her beak. Inside were buttons, ribbons, and paper. She smiled. “Pretty buttons! Shiny ribbons!”

She picked up a blue button. It felt cool and smooth. She rubbed it on her cheek. “I will make a rainbow bird,” she said. “Blue, red, yellow!”

From behind a trunk came a faint little voice. “Please be careful.”

Pippa froze. “Who said that?”

A soft rustle answered. “Down here.” A small cloth mouse peeked out. It had tiny stitched eyes and a pink thread nose. “I’m Mallow,” the mouse said. “I live in the attic.”

“Hello, Mallow!” Pippa chirped. “I am Pippa. I like to create.”

Mallow stepped closer, paws shy and small. “That blue button belongs to Nana’s coat,” he said. “She keeps special things in that box. They help her remember.”

Pippa blinked. “Oh. I didn’t know.” She looked at the blue button in her claw. She imagined a warm coat, soft and safe. She imagined Nana’s smile. Her chest felt heavy.

“I wanted to make art,” Pippa whispered. “But I did not ask. I’m sorry.”

Mallow nodded slowly. “Thank you for saying sorry,” he said. “It matters.” His voice was gentle, like a blanket. “Maybe we can find things that are for crafting.”

Pippa’s feathers fluffed. “Yes! Let’s look together.”

They listened. The attic was quiet, like a secret library. Pippa heard the light tap of rain on the roof. She smelled a sweet hint of old flowers from a hat box. She set the blue button back in its place. Tap, it made a tiny sound.

“Thank you,” said Mallow. “That was kind.”

Pippa’s eyes shone. “I want to help,” she said. “I will make amends.” She tried the words. “Make amends means fix what I did, right?”

Mallow smiled. “Yes. We can tidy the box. We can also make a sign that says ‘Special Memory Box—Please Ask.’”

Pippa bobbed her head. “Good idea! That is helpful.”

They worked side by side. Pippa used her beak to sort buttons by color—blue, red, yellow, green. Mallow folded the ribbons so they looked like tiny rainbows. They found a small piece of paper with paint smudges and drew a heart. Pippa carefully wrote the words, letter by letter. “Spe-cial… Mem-ory… Box—Please Ask.”

“Lovely,” said Mallow. He touched the heart with one gentle paw. “You care.”

“I do,” said Pippa. “I want to be fair. Taking without asking can make hearts feel sad.” She looked around. “What can we use for art that is okay?”

Mallow’s ears twitched. “There is a bin marked ‘Crafts.’ It is for play.”

They found the bin under a blanket. It held paper scraps, glue, and bottle caps. Pippa’s tail feathers shivered with joy. “Perfect!”

Together, they created a new bird picture. Pippa glued caps for eyes and orange paper for a beak. Mallow cut a tiny crown from a gold candy wrapper. Pippa added feather shapes with soft crayons she found in the craft bin. She named the bird Sunny. “Sunny smiles,” she said, “like a hug.”

Just then, the attic door opened. Nana stepped in, with warm eyes and silver hair. “Hello, little friends,” she said softly. “I heard the fan and wanted to check.”

Pippa stood tall. “Nana,” she said, “I took a blue button. I didn’t ask first. Mallow told me it was special. I felt sorry in my heart. We put it back. We made a sign. We cleaned your box. Is that okay?”

Nana’s face grew kind and bright. “You showed honesty,” she said. “You listened. You helped. Thank you.”

Pippa breathed out slowly. Her wings relaxed. “I want to be a caring parrot.”

Nana looked at the craft bin and Sunny the bird. “This is wonderful art,” she said. “You made a good choice to use the play bin. Would you like to hang Sunny in the window?”

“Yes, please!” Pippa chirped.

They taped Sunny in the sunlit square. Light shone through the paper. The colors glowed like a soft rainbow. The attic felt warm and happy.

Mallow leaned on Pippa’s wing. “You heard my small voice,” he said. “That is empathy. You felt what I felt.”

Pippa nodded. “Empathy helps me be gentle,” she said. “Integrity means I tell the truth. Helpfulness means I fix and care.”

Nana smiled. “You have learned big things in our quiet attic.”

Pippa flapped once, just a little, so as not to stir the dust. “Next time,” she promised, “I will ask before I take. I will listen. I will help.”

The rain slowed to tiny pats. The fan hummed a sleepy song. Sunny smiled in the window.

Pippa whispered, “My heart feels light.” She nuzzled Mallow’s ear. “Thank you for telling me.”

“And thank you for hearing me,” Mallow said.

The End.
