# Tiko the Tortoise Builds a Rooftop Garden

Tiko was a wise tortoise with a smooth, shiny shell and gentle eyes. He liked to take his time and think. One sunny morning, Tiko climbed up, step by careful step, to the rooftop of his building. The rooftop was warm. The breeze felt soft on his cheeks. He could hear birds chirp, “Chee-chee-chee!” and feel the sun glow like a hug.

“Today,” Tiko said, smiling, “I will build a rooftop garden.” He loved to build slowly and well. He loved to make things that helped others grow.

Tiko set down his cloth bag and opened it. He had prepared the day before. Inside the bag were simple tools: a small wooden spoon, a tiny rake, a roll of string, a little hammer, and a list he had drawn with pictures. There were also seeds, little pots, and a big blue watering can.

“First, we plan,” Tiko said. He unrolled a soft map he had sketched. It showed where the pots would sit, where the water would drain, and where the sun would shine most. He tapped his shell, thinking. “Plan, then build. That is wise.”

A curious pigeon flapped down, cooing, “Coo? Coo?” The pigeon’s feet made soft pat-pat sounds on the roof.

“Hello, friend,” said Tiko. “I’m building a garden. Would you like to watch?”

“Coo!” said the pigeon, tilting its head.

Tiko began. “Step one,” he said, pointing to his picture list, “we clean.” He picked up tiny twigs and bits of paper. Scritch, scritch went his little rake on the rooftop. He swept neatly into a pile. The breeze smelled fresh, like soap and sunshine.

Tiko paused. “We prepare our space so we can build safely,” he told the pigeon. “Preparation is like saying please and thank you to the place we use.”

“Coo,” the pigeon agreed.

“Step two,” Tiko said, “we set the pots.” He placed each pot on the spots he had drawn on the map. Tap. Tap. Tap. He checked the lines with his string so the pots sat in a happy row. He pushed gently with his strong legs to make each pot feel steady. Tiko moved slowly, and he did not rush. “Slow and steady helps us build strong.”

He opened a bag of soil. The soil smelled rich and earthy, like a forest after rain. It felt soft and cool in his hands. “Step three,” said Tiko, “we fill.” Scoop, plop. Scoop, plop. He used the wooden spoon to scoop soil into the pots. He hummed a little tune. The pigeon bobbed its head, cooing along.

A gust of wind swirled. Whoosh! A seed packet fluttered up and skittered across the roof. Tiko watched with calm eyes. He walked over slowly and pressed the packet under his foot. “That’s why we prepare with clips,” he said with a chuckle. He took two clips from his bag and pinned the seed packets to the map. “Preparation keeps little things from flying away.”

“Coo!” said the pigeon, impressed.

“Step four,” Tiko said, “we plant.” He poked tiny holes with one careful claw. Plip, plip, went the seeds as they dropped in. He covered them with a soft pat. “Good night, little seeds,” he whispered. “Sleep and grow.”

He made little labels with pictures: a carrot, a flower, a leafy green. He tied each with string. The tags fluttered and made a soft flit-flit sound in the breeze.

A small child named Mina peeked through the roof door. “Hi, Tiko,” she said, eyes wide. “What are you building?”

“A garden,” Tiko said. “Would you like to help water?”

“Yes!” Mina said. She lifted the blue watering can. It felt heavy, and the water sloshed. Tiko steadied it with his wise, gentle hands.

“Step five,” he said, “we water a little. Not too much.” The water poured out in a silver arc, making soft pitter-pat sounds on the soil. The smell of wet earth rose up like warm tea.

Mina giggled. “It smells nice!”

“Building can smell nice,” Tiko said. “It can sound nice. It can feel nice. We use our senses to guide us.”

They stood back and looked. The pots were in neat rows. The strings were straight. The labels were clear. The seeds were tucked in. Tiko nodded. “We build with care. We build with love. We build with a plan.”

Mina asked, “Will it grow today?”

Tiko smiled. “Growing takes time. Preparing, building, and waiting are all parts of making something good. We come back each day. We watch. We water. We share.”

The pigeon cooed, and a tiny butterfly flitted by, yellow as a lemon.

To celebrate, Tiko took out colored chalk. He drew a path on the rooftop, a winding, happy line from the door to the pots. Mina drew bright suns and smiling flowers. The chalk felt dusty on their fingers, and the colors glowed on the warm gray roof.

“This is creative expression,” Tiko said. “We can build with our hands and our hearts. We make useful things, and we make beautiful things too.”

They finished their drawings and sat quietly. The breeze tickled their faces. The rooftop garden looked ready and hopeful.

Mina said softly, “I learned that we plan first, we build slow, and we take care.”

Tiko nodded. “Yes. Preparation helps us. Building step by step makes strong things. And when we add our own ideas, our work feels joyful.”

They put away the tools. Click went the clips. Tap went the hammer. The watering can sat beside the pots like a blue moon.

“Thank you, Tiko,” Mina said.

“Thank you for helping,” said Tiko. He patted the soil one more time. “See you tomorrow, little garden.”

The sun slipped lower and turned the sky peach and pink. The rooftop felt calm. Tiko’s shell warmed in the last light. He felt proud and peaceful.

The seeds were tucked in, the plan was set, and the builders were ready for tomorrow.

The lesson was clear: prepare with care, build step by step, and express yourself with love.

The End.
