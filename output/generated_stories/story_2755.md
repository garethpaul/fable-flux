# The Paints of Peace

In a cozy basement lab, lights hummed softly and jars lined the shelves like tiny rainbows. Brushes, sponges, and little bottles made friendly clinks. It smelled like fresh paper and clean soap. In the middle sat Tiko, a wise tortoise with bright, patient eyes. He wore a tiny apron that said, “We Learn Together.”

“Good morning,” Tiko said in a calm, warm voice. “Today, we paint our big world map.”

Two friends came down the steps. Mia, who loved blue, hopped down with a bounce. Rafi, who loved red, slid down the railing with a giggle.

Tiko smiled. “Welcome to the basement lab. This is our art day. We will paint lands and seas. We will follow directions. We will include every friend.”

Mia pointed to the giant paper on the table. It showed shapes like puzzle pieces. “I want to paint the big ocean blue!”

Rafi waved a red brush. “I want to paint the big land red!”

Tiko nodded. “We will take turns. We will listen. We will share. First, we plan.”

Tiko tapped the map gently. “This is our world. Many people live here. Many languages. Many foods. Many songs. All together.”

Mia sniffed the paint. “It smells like sweet soap and lemons,” she said with a smile.

Rafi swished his brush. “It sounds like a soft swish-swish.”

Tiko laid out cards with pictures. One showed a blue wave. Another showed a green leaf. Another showed a yellow sun. “We will follow directions on these cards,” he said. “Blue is for water. Green is for forests. Yellow is for sunny deserts. Red is for warm cities and busy roads. Many colors can live together.”

Mia looked at the wave card. “So I paint the ocean blue?”

“Yes,” Tiko said. “Please start at the big ocean. Rafi, please start with red on the city dots. Small dots. Gentle dots.”

Mia dipped carefully. Swish-swish. The ocean grew blue and shiny.

Rafi dotted happily. Dot-dot. Red lights popped up like little cherries.

Then Rafi saw a big land shape. “I want to paint this all red!” he said, and he swooped his brush across the land.

Mia gasped. “No! I was going to paint the grass green!”

Rafi frowned. “But I love red.”

Mia’s voice got tight. “You are not following directions.”

Rafi’s cheeks went hot. “You are bossing me!”

Their voices bounced around the lab. The brushes shook. A red streak crossed the green card on the table.

Tiko took a slow breath. He tucked his head in a little, then peeked out with kind eyes. “Pause,” he said softly. “Let us stop and listen.”

Mia crossed her arms. “He painted over my green.”

Rafi looked down. “I wanted big red.”

Tiko nodded. “I hear you both. Mia wants green grass. Rafi loves red. Our world has room for both.”

He slid a soft sponge between them. “Let us solve this together. First, we calm our bodies. Breathe with me. In, out. In, out.”

They breathed. The room felt softer.

“Now,” said Tiko, “we use our words. One at a time.”

Mia said, “I feel sad when my plan is not followed. I want to paint the grass green.”

Rafi said, “I feel left out when I cannot use red. I want to make something big.”

Tiko smiled. “Thank you. Clear words help. Now we can make a plan.”

He pointed to the map. “Grass can be green. Cities can be red dots. But look here—hills and mountains. They can be a mix. We can paint hills with red flowers on green slopes. Red and green, together. Many places have red poppies on green fields. That shows our big world.”

Mia’s face softened. “Flowers on grass?”

Rafi’s eyes lit up. “Big, bright flowers!”

Tiko nodded. “Yes. And we follow directions. Green first for the land. Then red dots for flowers and cities. We take turns. We share space. We include each idea.”

They smiled at each other.

“I’m sorry,” Rafi said quietly. “I painted too fast.”

“I’m sorry, too,” Mia said. “I used a sharp voice.”

Tiko patted their hands with his gentle shell. “Thank you for saying sorry. Now, let us fix the red streak. We can dab with a little water.” He showed them how to dab-dab with the sponge. The streak faded.

Mia painted smooth green hills. Swish-swish. Rafi added red flowers. Dot-dot. They hummed a little song together.

Tiko mixed other colors. “The forest can be deep green. The desert can be sunny yellow. The ocean is many blues. Our world has many shades.”

They added tiny houses with brown doors, a yellow sun, white clouds, and a purple mountain at the edge. They painted little people holding hands around the map—tall, small, dark, light, all smiling. The lab filled with the soft sound of brushes, the fresh smell of paint, and the warm feeling of working together.

When they finished, Tiko held up the map. It shone with color. Blue seas hugged green lands. Red flowers danced on hills. Cities twinkled like stars.

Mia said, “We listened. We shared.”

Rafi said, “We followed directions. We used our words.”

Tiko’s eyes sparkled. “Yes. Today we learned a peaceful way. When there is a problem, we pause, we breathe, we listen, we speak kindly, and we make a plan. Our world is big, and there is room for every color, every voice.”

Mia and Rafi high-fived. “We did it together!”

Tiko placed the map on the wall. “This is our art. This is our world.”

They all stepped back. The basement lab felt bright and calm. The map smiled down at them.

“Tomorrow,” Tiko said, “we will add blue rivers and orange fish.”

Mia giggled. Rafi grinned. “Together,” they said.

The End.
