# Jumpy Joey’s Porch Parade

Jumpy Joey was a joyful kangaroo joey who loved to bounce. This morning, he bounced onto his peaceful front porch. The porch smelled like sweet eucalyptus tea. The wooden boards felt warm under his soft feet. Birds chirped a happy song, and a gentle breeze tickled his whiskers.

“Good morning, world!” Joey giggled, wiggling his ears.

On the porch stood a little table with a big bowl. The bowl held bright postcards from friends far away. Some postcards had sparkling stickers. Some had pictures of mountains, oceans, and colorful city lights.

Joey’s mama brought two cups of warm tea. “Today, we read postcards and help a friend,” she said kindly. “Helping is what good neighbors do.”

Joey clapped. “Neighbors live next door?”

Mama smiled. “Yes, and neighbors also live all around the world. We are part of one big, shared home called Earth.”

Just then, a tiny ant marched across the porch rail. It carried a crumb bigger than its head. Joey leaned close. He heard soft footsteps, tap-tap-tap. He smelled the crumb—sweet like honey bread.

“Hello, little worker,” Joey whispered.

Next came Mrs. Wombat from down the lane. She waved. “Knick-knock!” she called. “My nephew is visiting from a far country. He speaks a different language. We want to make him feel welcome. Can you help?”

Joey’s eyes sparkled. “Yes! We can help!”

Mama nodded. “We can show care with friendly faces, gentle hands, and kind words.”

Joey grabbed a postcard from a friend in a snowy place. It showed children building a round, smiling snow friend. “Maybe we can share stories,” he said. “Stories are like bridges.”

They set out a small basket of fruits on the porch table: red apples, golden bananas, and juicy green grapes. Joey felt the smooth apple skin. He listened to the grapes pop, pop when he washed them in a tin bowl. The water splashed cool on his fingers.

Soon, Mrs. Wombat’s nephew arrived. He was a shy little wallaby named Wali. He held a tiny flag with bright colors. He peeked from behind Mrs. Wombat.

“Hello, Wali,” Joey said softly. Joey put his paw on his chest and smiled. “I’m Joey.”

Wali looked at Joey’s paws, then touched his own chest and smiled back. “Wali,” he said. His voice was small, like a whispering breeze.

Mama poured warm tea into small cups. She pointed to the apples and said, “Apple.” Then she drew a quick apple shape in the air.

Wali watched and nodded. He said a word from his own language. It sounded round and musical. Joey repeated it slowly, “Ah-boo-lah?” He tried his best.

Wali laughed a tiny laugh, happy and surprised. He nodded. Joey laughed too. His belly felt warm, like sunshine.

Joey picked up another postcard. It showed a busy street with glowing lanterns. He pointed to the lanterns and said, “Light.” Wali said a word from his home. Joey listened and tried again, “Leet-tee.” Wali clapped!

They played a little game. Joey said a word. Wali shared his word. They took turns, like passing a ball. They learned “friend,” “thank you,” and “share.” Joey liked the way Wali said “thank you.” It sounded like wind ringing tiny bells.

Then, Joey got excited. He bounced high. Boing! Boing! Boing! His tail bumped the little table. Uh-oh! The postcard bowl wobbled. The fruit basket slid. Plop! A banana fell to the porch floor and rolled toward Wali’s toes. The cups rattled. A grape popped and splashed tea onto Wali’s flag.

Everything went very still.

Joey’s heart thumped. He felt hot in his cheeks. “Oh no,” he whispered. He looked at Wali’s flag with a tea spot. “I’m sorry,” Joey said. He put his paw on his chest again and bowed his head. “I made a mess.”

Wali looked at the spot and then at Joey. Joey’s eyes were shiny. He felt a lump in his throat.

Mama gave Joey a clean cloth. “Let’s fix it together,” she said.

Joey took a deep breath. He gently dabbed the tea spot. Dab, dab. Wali held the flag steady. Together, they washed the grape pop from the table. Joey picked up the banana and put it in a bowl for cooking later. He wiped the porch until it was clean and dry.

When they finished, Wali smiled big. He patted Joey’s paw. He said the word for “friend” from his home again, warm and bright. Then Wali put his own paw on his chest and said, “It’s okay.” He used Joey’s language this time.

Joey’s belly felt light, like a kite. “Thank you,” he said. He looked at Wali’s flag—it was clean and bright again. Mrs. Wombat smiled. Mama winked.

They sat back down. Joey brought out colored chalk. He drew a big circle on the porch floor. Inside the circle, he drew little houses, a river, a tree, and two smiling joeys holding hands. Wali added lanterns and mountains. Together, they drew the tiny ant carrying its crumb. They drew stars above for every friend in every place.

Mama pointed to the circle. “This is our shared world,” she said. “We are neighbors, near and far. We listen, we learn, we help, and we say sorry when we make mistakes.”

Joey nodded. He felt calm. He heard birds singing, smelled sweet tea, and felt the smooth chalk dust on his fingertips. He and Wali touched paws gently.

“Friends,” Joey said.

“Friends,” Wali echoed.

The porch felt extra peaceful now, like a soft hug around them all. Joey knew he could be a good neighbor to people close by and far away—by listening, learning new words, helping with kind hands, and saying sorry when he needed to.

The End.
