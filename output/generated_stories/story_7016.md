# Penny Penguin on the Rooftop

Penny was a cheerful penguin with bright eyes and a red scarf. One sunny day, she waddled up the stairs, step, step, step, to the very top of a tall building. The rooftop felt warm under her little penguin feet. A gentle breeze ruffled her scarf. She could hear city sounds below—beep-beep cars, whoosh-whoosh buses, and a faraway puppy bark.

“Oh! Look at the sky!” Penny chirped. The sky was big and blue, with fluffy white clouds that looked like pillows. “I want to fly a kite!”

Penny had a small kite in her flipper bag. It was yellow with a blue tail. She smiled and said, “I can do this. I can try.”

She held the kite string and ran, waddle-run, waddle-run, across the flat rooftop. The wind tickled her cheeks. The kite jumped up, then dipped down, bonk! right into a potted plant.

Penny frowned for a tiny moment. “Hmm,” she said softly. “That didn’t work.” She took a slow breath in—sniff—and a slow breath out—whooo. “I can try again.”

A little sparrow landed on the railing. “Tweet! Need help?” the bird chirped.

“Thank you,” said Penny kindly, “but first I will try by myself. I want to learn how.” She smiled at the sparrow. “You can cheer for me?”

“Tweet-tweet! I will cheer!” the sparrow sang.

Penny looked around. She felt the wind on her face. It blew from the left, cool and steady. She listened. The wind made a soft whoosh in her ears. She noticed the kite tail was tangled. “Oh! I see the problem,” Penny said. “The tail needs to be smooth.”

She sat down on the warm rooftop and gently untangled the tail. She patted the fabric, smooth-smooth. She checked the string: not too tight, not too loose. She stood up. Her scarf fluttered behind her like a little red flag.

“Ready,” she told herself. “I can do it.”

Penny held the kite high and walked slowly into the wind. The wind tugged at the kite. She let out a little string, just a little. The kite wiggled and rose a tiny bit. Penny felt excited. Her heart did a happy thump-thump.

Then the wind changed. The kite wobbled. Penny almost let go, but she didn’t. “It’s okay,” she whispered. “I can keep trying.”

She remembered a tip her grandpenguin once said: “Face the wind and take small steps.” Penny turned her body and took small penguin steps. She let out a little more string. The kite lifted higher. Up, up—oh no! A gust pushed it sideways, and it bonked a rooftop vent. Clunk!

Penny sighed. She hugged the string to her chest. “I feel a little sad,” she said. “But I am brave. I can try again.”

A friendly cat peeked from a door. “Meow! Do you want me to hold the kite?” the cat asked.

Penny smiled. “Thank you. That is so kind. I want to learn to start it by myself. Can you watch and tell me if I’m doing okay?”

“Meow, yes,” said the cat. “I will be your cheer-cat.”

Penny felt warm inside. She liked that her friends were kind. She also liked that she was learning to do this on her own. She touched the kite. She felt its smooth paper. She felt the cool breeze on her beak. She listened to her own calm voice. “I can do hard things. I can learn.”

Penny tried again. This time, she counted. “One… two… three!” On three, she lifted the kite and walked into the wind. She let the string slip through her flippers slowly, shhh, shhh, like a soft ribbon. The kite rose higher. It fluttered, then soared. Up, up, up!

“Tweet-tweet! You did it!” sang the sparrow.

“Me-ow-wow!” cheered the cat.

Penny laughed. The kite danced in the sky. It made swoops and loops. The blue tail flicked like a happy fish. The sun felt warm on Penny’s back. The breeze smelled like fresh rain, even though the day was bright.

Penny held the string steady. She felt strong and proud. “I did it,” she said. “I kept trying. I listened. I learned.”

Just then, a small gust tugged hard. The string slipped, zip! Penny held on tight. “I can handle this,” she said. She bent her knees a little, balanced her feet, and guided the string. The kite settled and flew smooth again.

A tiny chick from the next building waved. “How did you make it fly?” the chick called.

Penny smiled and called back, “I watched the wind, I fixed the tail, I took small steps, and I kept trying. You can learn, too!”

The chick clapped its little wings. “Thank you!”

Penny felt happy. She had been kind to herself by being patient. She had been resilient by trying again and again. And she had been kind to others by sharing what she learned.

When the sun began to dip and the sky turned peach and pink, Penny slowly brought the kite down, easy and gentle. She wrapped the string neat and tidy. She patted the kite. “Thank you, kite,” she whispered. “We did a good job.”

The sparrow fluffed its feathers. “Tweet, you were brave and smart!”

The cat purred. “You trusted yourself.”

Penny nodded. “Yes. I can learn new things. I can try. I can ask for cheering. I can be kind.” She felt calm and proud as the breeze tickled her scarf one last time.

And on that rooftop, with friends nearby and the sky glowing soft, Penny learned this: When I believe in myself and keep trying, I can do hard things.

The End.
