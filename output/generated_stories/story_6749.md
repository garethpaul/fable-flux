# Leo Waits His Turn

In a sunny dining room with a round table and a red-checkered cloth, a brave little lion cub named Leo sat on a cushion. The room smelled like warm honey bread and sweet mango. A ceiling fan hummed softly. Sunlight painted golden squares on the floor.

Leo’s whiskers twitched. His tummy rumbled. “I am so hungry,” Leo said, patting his belly. “Roar… I want a big piece now!”

Mama Lion placed a basket of honey bread in the middle of the table. Papa Lion set down a bowl of mango slices. Grandma Lion came with a pitcher of cool water that clinked with ice. “We share,” Mama said, smiling. “We wait. We use fairness.”

Leo scooted his cushion closer. The honey bread smelled so good—warm and soft, like a hug. He reached a paw.

“Hold on, little roar,” said Papa. “We have a plan. We pass the basket so everyone gets a turn.”

Leo’s paw hovered. “But I am hungry now,” he whispered. His tail swished. Waiting felt like a prickly blanket. His tummy grumbled again, louder this time.

Grandma poured water into little cups. “Tummies talk,” she said kindly. “But we listen to hearts too. Patience helps us all.”

Mama placed tiny plates in front of everyone: Leo, Mama, Papa, Grandma, and Leo’s friend, Tia Turtle, who had just come in with her slow, steady steps. Tia’s shell had little yellow dots like stars.

“Hi, Leo,” Tia said. “I am slow, but I am here.”

Leo smiled. He liked Tia. “Hi, Tia! Want to sit by me?”

Tia nodded and sat. The chair made a soft creak. “Thank you for waiting,” she said. “I try my best.”

Mama lifted the basket. “We will pass left,” she said. “Fair and square. Everyone will get some.”

Leo watched as the basket went to Papa first. Papa took one piece. Then the basket went to Grandma. Grandma took one. Then it went to Tia. Tia took a tiny piece with her gentle flippers. She smiled at Leo. “Almost your turn!”

Leo’s paws felt buzzy. He could smell the honey. He licked his lips. “I can’t wait,” he whispered. He reached again.

Mama touched his paw softly. “Brave cubs can wait. Take a deep breath.”

Leo breathed in. He smelled honey, mango, and a little lemon soap from clean plates. He breathed out. The fan tickled his ears. He felt the soft cushion under his paws.

The basket finally came to Leo. “My turn!” he said. He took a piece and then stopped. He saw one small piece left in the basket. He looked at the plates. Everyone had one. But the small piece was for the center, to share for extra bites after a check-in.

Papa nodded to Leo. “You remembered fairness.”

Leo put the basket back in the middle. “We share,” he said, sitting tall.

They all took small bites together. The honey bread was warm and sticky. It melted sweet on Leo’s tongue. “Mmm,” he hummed. The mango was juicy. It dripped down his chin. He giggled when Grandma handed him a napkin. “Sticky whiskers,” she teased.

After a few bites, Mama said, “Civic responsibility means we think of everyone at the table. We help the group. We wait our turns. We speak up kindly. We take only what we need.”

Leo nodded. “Like the basket going left. Like waiting for Tia.”

Tia smiled. “And like helping me reach the water.” She looked at the pitcher. It was far. Leo stood up and gently slid the pitcher closer to Tia. The ice clinked like tiny bells.

“Thank you,” Tia said. “That was kind.”

Leo felt warm inside, not just from the bread. He felt proud. He thought, My waiting helped my friends.

Then the door rattled. A breeze whooshed in. Cousin Zuzu, a fast little zebra, trotted in. “Am I late?” Zuzu asked, panting. “I ran fast, but the line outside was long.”

Mama smiled. “You are right on time for our check-in.” She looked around the table. “Is everyone ready for more? Is anyone still hungry?”

Leo looked at the small piece in the basket. He looked at Tia, at Zuzu, at Grandma and Papa. He felt his tummy. It was still a little rumbly, but not empty.

“I am a little hungry,” Tia said softly.

“I am too,” Zuzu said, eyes wide.

Papa smiled. “We can split the last piece fairly.”

Grandma took a small knife and cut the bread into four tiny squares. “Fairness means equal when we can, and kind when we can’t.” She passed a square to each of the hungry ones: Leo, Tia, Zuzu, and Papa, who had the smallest lunch earlier.

Leo took his tiny square and waited. “Let’s eat together,” he said. “Ready? Three, two, one… munch!”

They all munched. Crumbs fell like little stars on the cloth. Laughter bubbled up.

After they finished, Leo helped carry plates to the sink. The water splashed. The soap smelled like lemons again. Tia dried with a soft towel. Zuzu folded napkins into triangles. The fan kept humming. The sun squares moved across the floor.

Mama hugged Leo. “You were brave to wait.”

Papa patted Leo’s shoulder. “You were fair and kind.”

Grandma smiled. “You showed patience, acceptance, and care for the table.”

Leo’s tail swished slowly. “Patience felt hard,” he said, “but it made the meal happy.”

Tia nodded. “Waiting made room for me.”

Zuzu added, “And for me too!”

Leo took a deep breath and smiled. “Next time I feel rushed, I will breathe, look around, and remember the basket going left.”

Mama winked. “That’s a beautiful plan.”

The dining room grew quiet and cozy. The last mango scent floated in the air. Leo felt full in his tummy and in his heart.

The lesson was clear to Leo: Patience helps everyone, acceptance makes friends feel welcome, and fairness keeps things peaceful. When we wait our turn and think of others, we make the table—and the world—a kinder place.

The End.
