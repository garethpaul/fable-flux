# Poppy Panda and the Story Tool

Poppy was a patient panda with soft black-and-white fur and calm, kind eyes. Poppy loved stories. Every day after snack time, Poppy visited Grandpa Gao’s garage workshop to help and to listen.

The garage smelled like fresh wood and warm oil. Sunlight made tiny dust sparkles dance in the air. Tools hung on the wall like shiny toys—hammers, screwdrivers, paintbrushes—and there was a big wooden workbench as smooth as toast. Grandpa Gao wore a blue apron and a gentle smile.

“Good morning, Poppy,” Grandpa said. “Today we fix the squeaky wagon.”

Squeeeaaak, the red wagon cried when Poppy pulled it. Poppy giggled. “It sounds like a little mouse!”

Poppy put on small safety goggles. “I’m ready to help,” Poppy said.

Just then, a duck named Dara waddled in, flapping softly. “Hello! My wheel is wobbly,” she quacked, holding up a tiny scooter. The scooter rattled like marbles in a tin.

Poppy bowed a little. “We can help,” Poppy said. “We always help.”

Grandpa Gao nodded. “Helping is good. First, we listen and look.”

Poppy crouched and listened. The wagon squeaked. The scooter rattled. Poppy took a deep breath and felt calm, like a quiet pond. Poppy liked to go slow and be patient.

“Let’s tell the story of the sounds,” Poppy said. “Stories help us see.”

Grandpa’s eyes twinkled. “Go on, little storyteller.”

Poppy spoke in a gentle voice. “Once upon a time, a red wagon wanted to sing. It sang ‘squeeeaaak’ because it was thirsty for a tiny drop of oil. And a brave scooter went clackety-clack because its wheel wanted a hug from a new screw.”

Dara clapped her wings. “That’s a good story!”

Poppy smiled. “Stories help us understand.”

Grandpa Gao handed Poppy a small oil can that smelled like nuts. “A little drop,” he said.

Poppy dabbed one bright silver wheel. The wagon whispered instead of squeaked. “Shhhh,” it almost said. Poppy dabbed the other wheels. Soon the wagon rolled smooth, like butter on warm bread.

“Thank you,” Poppy told the wagon. “You can sing softly now.”

Next, Poppy looked at the scooter. One tiny screw was loose. Poppy found a screwdriver with a yellow handle, warm from the sun. “Twist, twist,” Poppy said. “Just right, not too tight.”

Dara stood very still. “I like your story way,” she said. “It makes me brave.”

Poppy nodded. “Stories can help us be brave.”

Then a little bunny named Benny hopped in, holding a broken toy car with a cracked blue roof. His ears drooped. “I found it like this,” he said, eyes wide.

Poppy glanced at Grandpa. Grandpa raised an eyebrow, waiting.

Poppy patted the workbench. “Tell me the story of your car,” Poppy said softly.

Benny looked at his paws. His whiskers trembled. “I was racing,” he whispered. “I went too fast. It crashed. I… I didn’t tell my dad yet.”

Poppy’s heart felt warm and careful. “Thank you for telling the truth,” Poppy said. “Honesty is brave.”

Grandpa Gao came closer. “Telling the true story helps us fix it,” he said kindly. “We can help.”

Benny took a deep breath. “Okay.”

Poppy listened again. The cracked roof made a tiny crunchy sound when she touched it. Poppy thought of another story. “Once there was a blue car who ran too fast,” she said, wiggling her fingers like running legs. “It bumped and said, ‘Ouch!’ The car needed gentle hands, a dab of glue, and time to rest.”

Benny’s ears lifted a little. “Time to rest?”

Poppy nodded. “We will be patient.”

Grandpa Gao gave Poppy sandpaper like soft sugar. “First, smooth the edges,” he said.

Shhh, shhh, went the sandpaper. Poppy smelled the tiny dust, like dry leaves. Next came the glue, sticky and sweet. Poppy pressed the roof together. “Hug, hug,” she whispered. She wrapped a small rubber band around the car like a blanket.

“Now we wait,” Poppy said. “Stories take time, and so do fixes.”

While they waited, Poppy told a circle story. “In our workshop,” she began, “we listen, we help, and we tell the truth. The wagon learned to sing softly. The scooter learned to stand strong. The car learned to rest. And the friends learned to be honest and patient.”

Dara smiled. “I like this story.”

Benny nodded. “Me too. I feel better.”

Poppy looked at Grandpa. “May I tell one more?”

Grandpa laughed softly. “Please do.”

Poppy whispered, “Once there was a panda who helped with gentle paws. The panda liked stories because stories are tools.”

Benny tilted his head. “Tools?”

“Yes,” Poppy said. “Stories help our hearts and our hands. They show us what to do.”

The glue dried. Poppy slid off the rubber band. The roof held. The blue car looked brave again.

Benny bounced. “It’s fixed!”

Poppy shook her head kindly. “We fixed it together. And you were honest. That helped most.”

Benny’s cheeks pinked. “I will tell my dad I broke it. I will say, ‘I am sorry,’ and I will go slower.”

Grandpa Gao patted Benny’s shoulder. “That is a good ending.”

Dara rolled her scooter. “No rattle!” she quacked.

Poppy pulled the wagon. It whispered, smooth and quiet. The garage felt bright and happy. The tools looked like smiling friends.

Poppy took a deep breath and spoke one last time. “Today’s story says: Be honest, help others, and be patient. Use your words like tools, and your hands with care.”

Grandpa Gao nodded. “And remember,” he said, “a true story makes strong hearts.”

Poppy grinned. “The end of today’s story,” she said, “and the start of tomorrow’s.”

They all waved and stepped into the warm afternoon, hearts as light as the soft dust sparkles in the sun.

The End.
