# Sunny Hugs and Sorry Words

The sun was warm and bright in the backyard. Bees hummed over yellow flowers. The grass felt soft and tickly under little paws. A friendly bear cub named Benny wiggled his nose and smiled. He loved mornings in the sunny backyard.

“Today I will build the tallest pinecone tower!” Benny said. His fur glowed like honey in the sunshine. He gathered pinecones that smelled like trees and earth. He lined them up near the sandbox, near the purple petunias.

Benny’s best friend, Mia the mouse, peeked through the fence. “Hi, Benny!” she squeaked. She wore a tiny blue ribbon and carried a red toy car. “Can we play?”

“Yes!” Benny grinned. “Let’s make a car garage under my pinecone tower!”

They worked together. Benny stacked pinecones. Mia zoomed her toy car softly over the smooth patio stones. The air was full of laughter, chirping birds, and the sweet smell of strawberries growing in a pot. A breeze brushed Benny’s ears like a gentle hand.

Benny’s tower grew taller. He stood on his tiptoes. His paws felt big and clumsy. “Almost there,” he said. His heart thumped with excitement. He wanted it perfect. He wanted Mia to say, “Wow!”

Mia clapped. “You can do it!”

Just then, the hose by the porch wiggled. A sleepy sprinkler head rocked and sprinkled tiny dots of water onto the grass. A surprise rainbow shimmered in the mist.

“Oh!” Benny turned his head to look. His paw bumped the very top pinecone. The tower wobbled. Pinecones tumbled and bumped, plop-plop-plop, right into Mia’s toy car. The little red car tipped over and a wheel popped off.

Mia’s eyes opened wide. Her whiskers drooped. “My car,” she whispered, hugging it to her chest.

Benny’s tummy felt heavy like a rock. Warm tears prickled his eyes. He hadn’t meant to. He wanted to run and hide behind the flower pots. His paws shook. What if Mia didn’t want to play anymore?

“I didn’t mean to!” Benny said quickly. His voice was too loud. He looked at the grass. He didn’t know what to do with his big paws.

Mia looked down at her car. A tiny sniffle came out. “It was my favorite,” she said in a small voice.

The bees kept humming. The sprinkler made soft pat-pat sounds on the leaves. Benny took a slow breath. He remembered Mama Bear’s words from last night. “When we make a mistake,” Mama had said, “we can say a real sorry. A real sorry uses gentle words, a calm voice, and helpful hands.”

Benny pressed his paws together. He looked at Mia. “Mia,” he said softly, “I’m sorry I broke your car. I bumped my tower, and your car got hurt. I see you feel sad. I feel sad, too.”

Mia blinked. She listened. Benny waited. The waiting felt long, but the breeze cooled his fur. He set his voice like a soft blanket. “Can I help fix it?” he asked.

Mia rubbed her eyes. She held out the car. “Maybe,” she said. “We can try.”

Benny’s heart lifted like a balloon. He took the car gently. The wheel smelled like warm plastic. He found the little metal peg where the wheel belonged. “Click?” he whispered, trying the wheel. It didn’t click. It wobbled.

Benny felt that heavy rock again. But he took another slow breath. “We can ask for help,” he said. “We can be brave together.”

They padded to the porch, where Mama Bear sat with a book and a cup of mint tea. The mint smell was fresh and crispy. “Mama,” Benny said, “I made a mistake. I knocked the tower. Mia’s car broke. We tried to fix it. Can you help us, please?”

Mama Bear looked at both of them with warm eyes. “Thank you for telling me the truth,” she said. “And thank you for saying a real sorry, Benny. That is brave.”

She brought a small toolbox with tiny screws. Click. Twist. Tighten. The little wheel slid back on. “There,” Mama said. “All fixed. How do you feel?”

Mia’s whiskers perked up. She smiled. “I feel better. Thank you, Mama. Thank you, Benny.”

Benny’s chest felt light and strong. “I’m glad,” he said. “I learned something. Sorry words can help hearts feel better.”

Mia nodded. “And helpful hands!”

They went back to the sunny backyard. The grass tickled their toes. Benny picked up the pinecones again. “This time,” he said, “let’s build together. We can make the tower shorter and stronger. And the garage can be over here, away from the tower.”

“Good plan,” Mia said. “Team plan!”

They stacked and giggled. They tested the tower with gentle taps. It stood steady and proud. Mia drove her car into the little garage. Vroom! The bees hummed a happy song. The sprinkler rainbow sparkled, just a little.

Benny puffed with quiet pride. He felt brave in his heart. He had made a mistake. He had said a real sorry. He had helped fix the problem. And he was still a good friend.

Mia held up her tiny paw. “Hug?”

Benny wrapped her in a warm, fuzzy bear hug. “Hug,” he said. The hug felt like sunshine.

That afternoon, they shared sweet strawberries from the pot. Juice dripped down their chins. They laughed and licked their paws.

Benny looked at the bright sky. “Today was a good day,” he said.

“It was,” Mia agreed. “Sorry words, kind hands, and friends.”

The End.
