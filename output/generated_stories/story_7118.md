# Roo’s Big Little Morning

Sunshine peeked through the curtains of a cozy bedroom. The room smelled like clean sheets and a tiny hint of lavender. Soft blankets made a little nest on the bed. A joyful kangaroo joey named Roo blinked awake and wiggled his small ears. His cozy bedroom had a bookshelf, a toy basket, and a bright blue bin with a sign that said, “Recycling.”

Roo stretched his arms and his tail. “Good morning, world!” he sang, his voice bouncy like his hops. He listened. The birds outside chirped, tap-tap-tap went the rain on the window from last night, and the clock tick-tocked very softly. Roo smiled. “Today, I try by myself,” he whispered. “I can do it!”

Mama Kangaroo called from the kitchen, “Breakfast in ten minutes, little jumper!”

Roo’s heart felt warm and brave. “Okay, Mama! I’m getting ready on my own,” he called back.

First, Roo picked out clothes. He opened his drawer and felt the smooth cotton of his favorite green shirt. “This one,” he said. He pulled one sleeve on. Then he pulled the other sleeve on. Oops! His ear got stuck in the collar. Roo giggled. “Ear, not a sleeve!” He tried again, slower. Slip, slide, smooth—his head popped through. “I did it!”

He tugged on his shorts. “One leg, two legs,” he said softly. His tail swished. The shorts got stuck on his tail. Roo took a deep breath. “Try again.” He wiggled. He turned. He held the waistband wide. Wiggle-wiggle—pop! His tail was free. Roo clapped for himself. “I’m getting independent,” he said. The word felt big and shiny in his mouth.

Next, Roo made his bed. He pulled the blankets up. Swish. He patted the pillows. Puff. He smoothed the quilt with both paws. “Nice and cozy,” he said. He sniffed. It smelled fresh and warm.

On the floor, Roo saw a cardboard tube, a crinkly paper, and a plastic bottle from yesterday’s juice. “These don’t belong here,” he said. He remembered the blue bin. “Recycling time!” Roo picked up the items, one by one. “Cardboard. Paper. Plastic bottle.” He dropped them into the bin. Plunk, crinkle, clink. His chest felt proud and bright.

He thought about his world. “Many friends around the big Earth,” Roo said. He had a picture book about children from different places. On the shelf, the book smiled at him with bright colors. Roo opened it for a moment. He saw a girl in a red hat planting a tree, a boy carrying a reusable bag, and a family riding a bus. “We all share one home,” Roo said. He patted the book gently. “I can help, too.”

Roo brushed his teeth. The mint tasted cool, like tiny snowflakes. He watched the water. “Off, please,” he told the faucet when he wasn’t using it. The drip-drip stopped. “Saving water helps everyone,” he said.

“Two minutes,” called Mama Kangaroo.

Roo packed his small day bag. He put in a clean handkerchief, a little wooden car, and his reusable water cup with the green lid. He zipped the bag. Zip! He stood tall. “I’m ready!”

Mama Kangaroo peeked in the door. Her eyes were soft and bright. “Well, look at you,” she said. “You got dressed on your own, made your bed, and sorted the recycling!”

Roo bounced once, then twice. “I did it, Mama! I was careful, and I tried again when it was hard.”

Mama nodded. “That’s independence. You used your own ideas and your own paws.”

Roo pointed to the blue bin. “I’m helping our big Earth home, too.”

Mama smiled. “Yes. When we recycle and save water, we care for our neighbors all around the world. That’s being a good global citizen.”

Roo tilted his head. “A global citizen is someone who helps our planet and our friends, even if they are far away?”

“That’s right,” Mama said. “We share the same sky!”

Roo looked at his window. The sky was bright and friendly. “I like sharing the sky,” he said. “It makes me feel close to everyone.”

They headed to the kitchen. The floor felt cool under Roo’s feet. Breakfast smelled like warm oats and sweet banana. Roo carried his spoon to the table. He placed it next to his bowl. Clink. He climbed into his chair by himself. He sat up tall.

Mama poured the oats and added banana slices. Roo took a bite. “Mmm,” he hummed. “Soft and warm.”

After breakfast, Roo wiped his face. He put his bowl in the sink with a gentle clink. He looked at the trash can and the blue bin. He held the banana peel. “Trash or recycling?” he asked.

Mama said, “The banana peel goes in the compost, the special bin for food scraps.” She pointed to a small green bucket. Roo lifted the lid and dropped the peel in. Plop.

“Now the green bin is happy, too,” Roo said.

Mama laughed softly. “Yes, it is.”

Roo bounced to the door, then stopped. He turned back, looked at his cozy bedroom, and smiled. It looked neat and calm. The air smelled nice. His heart felt brave and gentle at the same time.

“I can do many things by myself,” Roo said. “And I can help our big Earth.”

Mama gave him a small hug. “You can. When you try, when you care, and when you keep going, you learn and grow.”

Roo held his reusable cup. “I’m ready for my big little day,” he said.

Outside, the birds chirped again. Inside, the blue bin waited for more helpful sounds—plunk, crinkle, clink. Roo wiggled his ears and grinned.

“Today, I am independent,” he whispered. “And I am a good neighbor to the world.”

The End.
