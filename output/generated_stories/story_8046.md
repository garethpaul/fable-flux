# Rocco’s Sorry Song

Rocco was a musical raccoon with bright eyes and stripey paws. He loved sounds. He loved soft sounds, like shhhh, and big sounds, like BOOM! One sunny morning, Rocco padded into the musical practice room. The room smelled like clean wood and lemon polish. The lights were warm. Instruments waited all around: a tiny triangle, a shiny drum, a bouncy piano, and a little row of bells that went ting-ting-ting.

Rocco wiggled his whiskers. “Hello, friends!” he said to the instruments. He liked to talk to them. He believed they could hear him. Rocco’s tail swished with joy.

He wanted to make a new song. He wanted it to be happy and kind. He wanted it to say a new word in many ways. He wanted it to say “sorry.”

Rocco climbed onto the piano bench. Plink. Plonk. Plink. The keys felt cool under his paws. He sang softly, “Sor-ry, sor-ry,” like a lullaby. It sounded sweet and light.

Just then, his best friend, a small blue bird named Pip, hopped in through the open window. Pip chirped, “Hello!”

“Hi, Pip!” said Rocco. “Listen to my song!”

Pip hopped closer to the bells. “Ting-ting-ting!” she pecked a bell gently. The room rang with tiny silver sparkles of sound.

Rocco’s eyes grew big. He wanted to try the bells too. He jumped down quickly. But his tail bumped the music stand. Clatter! The paper sheets fell like snowy leaves. One paper slid under the drum.

“Oh!” Pip fluttered up. “My song notes! I wrote them for my show.”

Rocco felt a twist in his tummy. He had not looked. He had moved too fast. He saw Pip’s worried eyes. He wanted to say something. He wanted to fix it. He opened his mouth, but no words came out.

He bent to pick up the papers. His paws shook. The papers crinkled. He found one with little hearts drawn on top. It had Pip’s name. “Pip’s Song,” it said. Pip’s feathers drooped.

Rocco took a big breath. He put his paw on his chest. He looked at Pip’s face. “Pip,” he said slowly and gently, “I am sorry. I bumped your notes. I did not look. I made a mess.”

Pip blinked. She listened. Rocco kept going. “I am sorry,” he said again. “Lo siento.” He had learned those words in a book. “That means ‘I am sorry’ in Spanish,” he explained. He spoke softly, like a warm hug. “Je suis désolé,” he added. “That is ‘I am sorry’ in French.” He smiled a tiny smile. “Saying sorry in many ways helps my heart remember to be kind.”

Pip’s feathers lifted a little. “You said sorry with care,” she chirped. “Thank you, Rocco.”

Rocco nodded. “I want to make it right,” he said. “May I help pick up? May I help fix the pages?”

“Yes,” Pip said. “Let’s do it together.”

They crouched on the smooth floor. Rocco lifted the drum gently. Boom… soft like a heartbeat. He reached underneath and pulled out the last paper. It smelled like paper and a hint of lemon polish. He straightened each sheet. He followed the numbers in the corner. One, two, three, four. He smoothed the crinkles with his warm paw.

Pip hummed her tune. “La-la-la,” she sang. Rocco listened. He did not rush. He watched her face. He followed her hums. He tapped the triangle—ting!—when she paused. He waited when she needed quiet.

“Your turn,” Pip chirped. “Can I hear your sorry song again?”

Rocco climbed back on the bench. He touched the keys softly. Plink, plonk, plink. He sang, “Sorry means I care. Sorry means I see. Sorry means I make it right, as best as I can be.”

Pip added a harmony, a soft tweet-tweet-tweet. It sounded like sunbeams and blue sky. Rocco smiled. “Would you like to add your bells?” he asked.

“Yes!” Pip pecked the bells. Ting-ting-ting. The sorry song grew bright and kind.

Rocco stopped to speak to the room, like a teacher, like a friend. “Sometimes we make mistakes,” he said. “We bump, we drop, we forget. When that happens, we can do three things. One: say sorry with our hearts and our words. Two: ask how to help. Three: try to fix it.”

Pip nodded. “That is a good plan,” she chirped.

Rocco said, “We can also learn words that help us be kind.” He showed his paw, counting on his fingers. “Sorry. Lo siento. Je suis désolé.” He tried one more, slowly, “Gomen nasai.” He had learned it from a song. “They all mean, ‘I am sorry.’”

Pip smiled. “Your words sound like music,” she said.

Rocco’s cheeks felt warm and happy. “Words are music,” he said. “They can make hearts feel better.”

They played the song one more time. The drum went boom like a gentle hug. The piano went plink like shiny rain. The bells went ting like tiny stars. Pip sang. Rocco sang. The practice room glowed with sound.

When the song ended, Pip fluttered close and nuzzled Rocco’s cheek. “Thank you for saying sorry and helping,” she whispered.

Rocco nuzzled back. “Thank you for listening,” he said. “I will look first next time. I will move slowly in the music room. I will keep my tail out of the stands.”

They both giggled. Then they put the notes neatly on the stand. They closed the window. They turned off the lights. The room felt calm and proud, like it was smiling.

As they left, Rocco hummed their sorry song. He felt light and brave. He had learned something big: Apology is caring, and caring is kind. With kind words and helpful paws, mistakes can lead to music.

The End.
