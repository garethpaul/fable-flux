# Rosie Rabbit’s Nursery Day

Rosie Rabbit had soft gray fur and bright, brave eyes. She lived in a comfortable nursery full of cozy pillows, wooden blocks, and sunny windows. The room smelled like warm crayons and fresh storybooks. A gentle clock went tick-tock on the wall. Outside, birds sang a friendly “cheep-cheep.”

Rosie wiggled her nose. “Today, I will do my very best,” she said. “I can think. I can try. I can finish.”

Teacher Mia smiled. “Good morning, Rosie. We have three jobs today: tidy the blocks, match the colors, and water the plant. Small steps, big heart.”

Rosie’s ears stood tall. “I am ready.”

First, Rosie looked at the blocks. Red, blue, yellow, and green blocks were scattered across a soft rug. They felt smooth and warm in her paws. The pile looked big, like a colorful mountain.

Rosie took a deep breath. “This looks hard,” she whispered. Her tummy fluttered like a little butterfly.

Teacher Mia knelt beside her. “What can you do first?”

Rosie thought. “I can start with one block,” she said. “One block, then another.” She picked up a red block and put it in the red bin with a gentle clack. Next, she picked up a blue block and put it in the blue bin. Clack.

Soon she made a rhythm: pick, clack, smile; pick, clack, smile.

Her friend Leo the lion cub padded over. “Need help?”

Rosie smiled. “Yes, please. You can do green, and I will do yellow.”

Together they sorted. “Clack-clack!” went the bins. The rug looked clear and neat. Rosie’s tummy felt warm now, like cocoa.

Teacher Mia clapped softly. “How did you do it, Rosie?”

Rosie stood a little taller. “I started small. I kept going.”

“Smart thinking,” said Teacher Mia. “That’s critical thinking. You looked, you planned, and you did it.”

Next, it was time to match colors. On the table were cards with colors and cards with pictures: a red apple, a blue bird, a yellow sun, a green leaf. The cards smelled like clean paper. Rosie’s whiskers twitched.

She spread the cards out. “Hmm,” she hummed. “Which picture matches red?”

She looked closely. The apple was shiny and red. “Apple goes with red,” she said, sliding the apple card to the red color card. “Cheep!” sang a blue bird outside. Rosie smiled. “Bird goes with blue.” She paired them gently.

She looked at the sun and the leaf. “Sun is yellow,” she said, matching the sun to yellow. “Leaf is green,” she said, matching the leaf to green. She wiggled with pride.

Teacher Mia asked, “How did you figure it out?”

Rosie tapped her chin. “I looked at clues. I used my eyes. I used my brain.”

“That is thoughtful,” Teacher Mia said. “You are solving problems.”

Rosie’s ears felt warm and happy. She whispered to herself, “I am good at trying. I am good at thinking.”

Last, it was time to water the plant. The plant sat by the sunny window, with wide green leaves. The soil looked a little dry. Rosie held a small watering can. Water sloshed inside with a soft swish.

Rosie paused. “How much water does the plant need?” she asked.

Teacher Mia smiled. “Let’s think. If we give too much, the plant will be soggy. If we give too little, the plant will be thirsty. What can we do?”

Rosie put a paw on the soil. It felt dry and crumbly. “It feels thirsty,” she said. “I will pour slowly and stop when the soil looks dark and damp.”

She tipped the can. Pour, pour, pause. The soil turned dark and smelled fresh, like rain. Rosie touched it again. “It feels just right,” she said. A tiny drop sparkled on a leaf like a star.

Leo sniffed. “It smells nice!”

Rosie wiped the can and set it down. “Plant, you can drink now,” she whispered.

Teacher Mia nodded. “You used your senses. You thought before you acted. You finished your job with care.”

Rosie’s heart felt like a balloon, full and light. She looked around the nursery. The blocks were tidy. The cards were matched. The plant was watered. The room seemed to hum with happy quiet.

Leo grinned. “You did it, Rosie!”

Rosie grinned back. “We did it. I started small, I used clues, and I finished each task.”

Teacher Mia placed a soft star sticker on Rosie’s paw. It was gold and a little sparkly. “Here is a star for your brave thinking and doing,” she said.

Rosie looked at the star and then at her friends. “I can do hard things,” she said. “I can think. I can try. I can finish.”

The clock went tick-tock, and the sun made warm squares on the floor. The nursery smelled like crayons and wet soil. Rosie hopped to the window and waved at the birds. “I feel proud,” she said softly.

Teacher Mia gathered everyone. “What did we learn today?”

Rosie raised her paw. “When a job looks big, start small. Use your eyes, ears, and hands to find clues. Keep trying. You can do it.”

Teacher Mia smiled. “Yes. That is self-esteem—believing in yourself. When you believe you can, you try. When you try, you learn. When you learn, you grow.”

Rosie hugged herself. “I am growing,” she said.

And the nursery felt even cozier, full of calm, kind, can-do feelings.

The End.
