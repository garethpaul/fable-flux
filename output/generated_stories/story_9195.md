# Leo’s Thank-You Garden

Leo was a brave little lion cub with soft paws and a sunny smile. He lived in an apartment with a small balcony garden. The balcony smelled like mint and wet soil. The leaves tickled his whiskers when the breeze blew. Birds sang, “Tweet-tweet!” and the city hummed below like a quiet drum.

One bright morning, Leo stepped onto the balcony. He wore his tiny watering can like a backpack. “Good morning, garden!” he roared softly, because neighbors were nearby. Leo loved his plants. He had shiny red tomatoes, curly lettuce, and a tall sunflower that watched the sky.

Mama Lion came outside with a cup of warm tea. “Brave gardener,” she said, “do you remember our thank-you words?”

Leo nodded. “Yes, Mama. We say thank you to the things that help us grow.”

He pat-patted the soil. It felt cool and crumbly. “Thank you, soil,” Leo whispered. “You hold the roots like a hug.”

He sprinkled water. Plip-plop, plip-plop. The water glittered in the sun. “Thank you, water,” he said. “You help my plants drink.”

A bee buzzed by the sunflower. “Bzz-bzz!” Leo giggled. “Thank you, bee. You share the pollen.” The bee buzzed again, as if to say, “You’re welcome.”

Mama sipped her tea. “Leo, our garden is small, but it is part of a big world. People far away help us too.”

Leo’s ears perked up. “People far away?”

Mama pointed to a bag of soil. “This soil came from a farm outside our city. And look at these seeds.” She held a little packet. “These tomato seeds came from a sunny place across the ocean.”

Leo opened the packet. The seeds were tiny and tan. He held one on his paw. It tickled. “Across the ocean?” he asked. He could almost hear waves in his mind. Whoosh-whoosh.

“Yes,” said Mama. “People there grow plants in warm fields. They share seeds with us. We can be thankful to them too.”

Leo’s eyes grew wide. He looked at the pots. “Garden, we are not alone,” he whispered. “We have helpers everywhere!”

He wanted to learn more. Leo found his globe sticker book. He stuck a little tomato sticker on a blue spot for the ocean and a green spot for land. “This is where the seeds traveled,” he said. “Thank you, helpers. Thank you, ship. Thank you, sun.”

Leo put the seeds into the soil, one by one. He said, “Plop. Plop. Plop.” He covered them gently. He watered them just a little. “I can do this,” he said. “I am brave and careful.”

Just then, a gray cloud floated over the balcony. The wind whooshed. The sunflower wobbled. Leo felt a worry wiggle in his tummy.

“Mama, what if the wind is too strong?” he asked.

Mama knelt beside him. “You are brave, Leo. You can help your plants.”

Leo took a soft string and tied the sunflower to the railing. “There,” he said. “Safe and snug.” He moved the tiny pots closer to the wall. The wind softened, like a whisper. The sunflower stood tall again.

“Good job,” said Mama. “You are caring for your garden. That is self-reliance. You can try first, and ask for help when you need it.”

Leo smiled. He liked doing helpful things. He took a deep breath. The air smelled like wet mint and tomatoes.

A pigeon landed on the railing. “Coo-coo,” it said, tilting its head.

“Hello, Pigeon,” said Leo. “Please don’t peck the lettuce.”

The pigeon cooed again and hopped away. Leo giggled.

He sat on a cushion with his notebook. He drew his balcony garden: the sunflower with a happy face, the tomatoes like tiny suns, and the watering can with wiggly raindrops. Underneath he wrote, in big careful letters, “THANK YOU.”

Mama showed him a postcard from a friend in a far-away place. It had a picture of a big market with colorful fruits. “Our friend buys seeds there,” Mama said. “We can send a thank-you picture.”

Leo clapped his paws. “Yes! I will draw one.” He drew a boat bringing seeds, and he drew himself waving. He added hearts and a smiling sun.

When the sun began to set, the balcony turned gold. The city lights blinked like little stars. Leo gave the garden one last gentle water. Plip-plop.

“Garden,” he said, “thank you for growing with me. Thank you, bees and breezes. Thank you, farmers far away. Thank you, Mama. And thank you, Leo,” he added softly, “for trying your best.”

Mama hugged him. “Gratitude makes our hearts big,” she said. “When we say thank you, we remember our helpers near and far.”

Leo yawned. His whiskers trembled with sleep. He looked at the tall sunflower, the tiny seeds, and the quiet pots. He felt proud and calm.

“I learned today,” Leo said, “that I can help my garden. I can learn new things. And I can be thankful for everyone, everywhere.”

The balcony breeze was warm and kind. The bee buzzed one last “bzz.” The city hummed like a lullaby.

Leo curled up on the cushion. “Good night, garden,” he whispered. “Thank you.”

The End.
