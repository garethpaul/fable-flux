# Tilly Rabbit Solves the Quiet Attic Problem

Tilly was a determined rabbit with soft gray fur and big, careful ears. She loved to hop, think, and try again. One sunny afternoon, Tilly climbed the wooden stairs to the quiet attic. The attic smelled like dust and old pine. Sunlight made tiny sparkles in the air. It was very still.

“I will make a cozy reading corner,” Tilly said. “A calm place to think.”

She found a small quilt folded on a trunk. She patted it flat with her gentle paws. “Pat, pat,” went the fabric. She set a basket of books nearby. She felt proud.

Then Tilly heard a soft sound. Tap… tap… tap.

Her ears twitched. “Who is that?” she whispered.

A small mouse peeked out from behind a hat box. The mouse had bright eyes and a tiny blue scarf. “Hello,” squeaked the mouse. “I am Milo. I come here to drum. Tap tap tap!”

Tilly smiled. “Hi, Milo. I am Tilly. I like to read here. Reading is quiet.”

Milo lifted two little sticks. “Drumming is fun! It makes my heart beat fast.” He tapped again on a tin lid. Tap-tap-tap!

Tilly’s whiskers drooped. The sound bounced around the attic and made her book wobble. She felt a tight pinch in her tummy. “I can’t read,” she said softly. “It is too loud.”

Milo stopped. “I don’t want to bother you,” he said. “But I am excited to drum.”

Tilly took a deep breath. She remembered what Mama Bunny always said: “Use your words. Be kind. Think of a safe, smart plan.”

“I feel upset when it is loud,” Tilly said. “I want to read. Can we fix this?”

Milo nodded. “I want to drum. How can we both be happy?”

Tilly tapped her chin. “Let’s use our thinking ears,” she said. “We can try a plan. First, we talk. Then, we choose a safe, fair way. Are you ready?”

“I’m ready,” said Milo.

Tilly looked around. The attic had boxes, trunks, and a big window with soft light. She spotted a thick rug under a chair. “That rug is puffy,” she said. “Maybe the rug can make the sound softer.”

Milo dragged the tin lid onto the rug. He tapped. Tup… tup… tup. The sound was softer, like a heartbeat under a blanket.

“It is better,” Tilly said, “but still a little loud for reading.”

Milo thought. “We can make a rule,” he said. “We can take turns.”

Tilly smiled. “Yes! We can make a plan with steps. Step one: You drum for a little while. Step two: I read for a little while. Step three: We check if we both feel good.”

Milo squeaked, “And step four: We stop if it feels unsafe.”

They both nodded. Safety first.

Tilly set her timer. “We will try five minutes,” she said. “When the bell rings, we switch.”

Milo drummed on the rug: tup-tup-tup. Tilly covered her ears with soft cotton from a craft box. The cotton felt fluffy and warm. She could still hear a little, but it was gentle. She smiled and read her book about stars.

Ding! The timer rang.

“My turn to read,” Milo said. He tucked the drumsticks away and picked a picture book from the basket. He curled up on the quilt. Tilly did quiet stretches, feeling her muscles wake up like little smiles. The attic felt peaceful.

Ding! The timer rang again.

“How do you feel?” Tilly asked.

Milo wiggled his whiskers. “I feel happy. I got to drum. I also liked the story.”

“I feel calm,” Tilly said. “The cotton helped. The rug helped. Our plan worked.”

Just then, a shadow moved by the open attic window. The curtain fluttered. A cool breeze slid in and made the tin lid wobble toward the stairs.

“Careful!” Tilly said. “The lid might roll down.”

Milo’s eyes went wide. “That is not safe.”

Tilly hopped quickly and gently pressed the lid flat with her paw. “Let’s make a safety rule too,” she said. “We keep the walkway clear. We close the window a little so things don’t blow around.”

Milo helped slide a box to make a safe wall far from the stairs. Tilly lowered the window until the breeze felt soft and kind.

They stood back and looked at their cozy corner. The quilt was smooth. The books were tidy. The rug kept the drum quiet. The path to the stairs was open and safe.

“We solved our problem,” Tilly said.

“We did it together,” Milo said. “We talked, we listened, we tried a plan, and we checked safety.”

Tilly’s heart felt warm. “That is good conflict-solving,” she said. “When we have different needs, we can use words, make rules, and share time.”

Milo nodded. “And we can be kind.”

They set the timer once more. Tilly read about bright moonlight. Milo tapped a soft beat, like raindrops on leaves. The attic smelled like clean dust and old books. It felt friendly now.

When the last ding sounded, Tilly and Milo smiled at each other.

“Thank you for listening,” Tilly said.

“Thank you for sharing,” Milo said.

They bumped paws and tiny hands in a gentle high-five.

The End.
