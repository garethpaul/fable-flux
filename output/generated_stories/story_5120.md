# Pippa Parrot’s Rooftop Sparkle

Pippa was a creative parrot who loved to draw with her tail feathers. She lived on a sunny rooftop garden where the wind sang and the flowers nodded. Every morning, Pippa squawked, “Good day!” and scribbled swirly lines on big sheets of paper with bright, safe paints. She made rainbow waves, zigzag suns, and tiny hearts.

One warm morning, Pippa flapped to her favorite corner. “Time to write my big story,” she chirped. She dipped a feather tip into blue paint and wrote, “Pippa’s Book.” The wind smelled like fresh water and leaves. The rooftop tiles were warm under her feet.

But today, something felt sticky. “Hmm?” Pippa touched her beak. Sticky. She looked at her wings. Sticky. She looked at her paints. Drips and smears were everywhere. “Oh dear,” she said softly.

“Good morning, Pippa!” called Mia, a friendly squirrel. “What are you writing?”

“My book,” Pippa said, “but everything is messy. My feathers feel yucky.”

Mia sniffed the air. “I smell sour paint and old crumbs.”

“Old crumbs?” Pippa looked around. She had left seed crumbs on the table. A cup of water was cloudy. Brushes were hard and crusty. “Uh-oh.”

Pippa tried to write another word, but the paint made clumps. “Clump, clump.” Her wing stuck to the paper. “Squawk! I can’t write like this!”

Just then, Mr. Cloud drifted by and sprinkled a tiny mist. “Pitter, patter,” went the drops on the rooftop. The cool mist felt nice, but it made Pippa’s mess turn into slimy puddles. “Ew!”

Pippa’s heart felt heavy. She wanted her book to be clean and beautiful. She ruffled her feathers and sighed. “What is the problem?” she asked out loud.

Mia sat up, her tail twitching. “The problem is germs and grime.” She pointed to the sticky brushes and the crumbly table. “When we don’t clean, little germs have a party.”

Pippa blinked. “A germ party?” She imagined tiny creatures dancing on her paints. “No thank you!”

Mia nodded. “We need a plan.”

Pippa loved plans. “Yes! A plan to make sparkle.”

They listened to the wind. They looked at the mess. Pippa tapped her beak. “Step one,” she said, “wash my beak and claws and feathers.”

“Step two,” Mia squeaked, “clean the tools.”

“Step three,” Pippa chirped, “keep a clean space.”

“Step four,” they said together, “write and draw with happy, healthy hands!”

Pippa waddled to the rooftop sink. It had a gentle faucet and soap that smelled like lemons. “Turn, turn,” went the handle. Warm water rushed. Pippa sang, “Wash, wash, wash,” as she rubbed her claws with soap. Bubbles tickled. She rubbed the tops, the bottoms, and between each toe. She washed her beak with a soft cloth, “wipe, wipe.” She fluffed her feathers and dipped her tips in clean water, “swish, swish,” then patted them dry with a small towel. “Ahh,” she sighed. “I feel fresh!”

Mia helped clean the tools. “Brushes need a bath,” she said. They swished the brushes in a jar of clean water. “Swish, swoosh.” The water turned purple. They changed it. “Swish, swoosh.” The brushes became soft and new. Pippa wiped the table with soapy bubbles. “Wipe, wipe, wipe,” sang Pippa. The rooftop began to smell like lemons and leaves, not sour paint.

They put crumbs in the bin. They poured out the old water and filled a new cup. “Fresh!” Pippa chirped. They set out a clean cloth to catch drips. A little sunbeam landed on the table and made it glow.

Mia smiled. “Now try writing.”

Pippa dipped a clean feather into bright red paint. It felt smooth, not sticky. She wrote, “Pippa’s Book,” again, big and neat. “Look!” she said. The letters were clear. The paint shone. Her feathers did not clump. Her feet did not slip. Pippa felt proud.

She kept writing her story: “I wash. I clean. I write and dream.” She drew a shiny sink, happy bubbles, a tidy table, and a smiling brush. She added a tiny sign that said, “Clean tools, bright ideas!”

Mr. Cloud drifted by again and clapped with soft thunder. “Bravo! So bright and tidy.”

Pippa laughed. “Thank you, Mr. Cloud.”

Mia read the new page. “Your story shines, Pippa.”

Pippa puffed up. “Cleaning helped my writing. Clean feels good. Clean makes it easy to make art.”

A little pigeon landed nearby. “Coo? What is that smell?” he asked.

“Lemons and learning,” Pippa said. “Do you want to see?”

The pigeon nodded. Pippa showed the steps. “Wash hands, claws, or paws before we write. Clean our tools. Keep crumbs away. Use fresh water. Then we can make our best work.”

The pigeon cooed, “I will try!” He flapped to the sink and made bubbles. “Wash, wash!”

Pippa added one more line to her book: “Clean helps me be kind to me.” She drew a heart.

The sun slid low, and the rooftop felt cozy. The wind hummed a soft song. Pippa closed her book and smiled. “Today I had a problem,” she said. “I found a solution.” She tapped the table. “Hygiene helps me write and discover new ideas.”

Mia clapped her tiny paws. “And your rooftop sparkles!”

Pippa stretched her wings. “I feel fresh, I feel bright, I feel ready to write.” She winked at the pigeon. “Tomorrow we will make new pages.”

“Clean pages!” the pigeon cooed.

“Clean pages,” Pippa agreed.

The End.
