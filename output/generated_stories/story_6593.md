# Rilla Rabbit’s Porch Practice

Rilla was a small, determined rabbit with soft gray fur and bright, curious eyes. She loved her peaceful front porch. The porch smelled like fresh wood and mint leaves. The morning breeze felt cool on her whiskers. Birds sang sweet songs from the trees, and a bumblebee hummed near the flower box.

Rilla put a green watering can beside her. “Today I will learn to water the plants just right,” she said. “I will practice and practice.” She wanted to help the flowers, the bees, and the butterflies. She wanted to care for the Earth, starting right here.

Mama Rabbit set a bowl of crunchy carrot sticks on the porch table. “Practice makes strong skills,” Mama said with a smile. “Start gentle. Watch and listen. The plants will tell you.”

Rilla nodded. “I can do it.”

She lifted the watering can. It felt heavy and cool in her paws. She tilted it over the first pot. Splash! Too much water poured out. Mud bubbled. The dirt smelled wet and thick. A tiny green sprout leaned over, tired and droopy.

“Oh no,” Rilla whispered. Her ears drooped. “I gave the plant a rainy storm.”

A ladybug crawled onto her paw. It tickled. “It’s okay,” Mama said softly. “Problem first, then solution. What can you try?”

Rilla took a breath. She touched the soil with one paw. It felt soggy. “Next time, I will pour slow.” She picked up the watering can again and looked at the second pot. She tried to pour just a little. Drip… drip… drip… Nothing came out. The spout only squeaked.

Rilla frowned. “Now it’s too little. The plant is thirsty.”

A golden butterfly landed on the porch rail. Its wings fluttered like tiny fans. Rilla watched the wings move gentle-gentle. “I will pour like that,” she said. “Not a storm. Not a desert. Gentle-gentle.”

She practiced with a third pot. Tip… tip… tip… Little drops fell. The soil turned dark and soft, but not muddy. It smelled like the forest after a small rain. The leaves sat up tall and shiny.

“Yes!” Rilla cheered. Her tail did a happy wiggle. “I did it!”

But then, as she moved to the next pot, her paw bumped the can. The can clanked on the porch. Water splashed everywhere—on Rilla’s toes, on the wooden step, and onto the ant path. The ants rushed around in a busy, wiggly line.

“Oops!” Rilla said. She put her paw on her chest and took a slow breath. “I will keep trying. I will be careful.”

She set the can upright. “I can fix this.” She took a soft cloth and wiped the wet step. She watched the ant path and used a little stick to guide them to dry ground. “Excuse me, ants,” she said kindly. “This way, please.”

Mama smiled. “That’s caring for nature, Rilla.”

Rilla tried again with the fourth pot. Tip… tip… tip… The drops made tiny circles on the soil. She listened. The sound was quiet and happy, like rain on a leaf hat. She touched the soil. It was cool and just a little damp.

“Perfect,” she whispered.

She kept going, one pot at a time. For each plant, she stopped. She looked at the leaves. She felt the soil. If it was dry, she gave more. If it was damp, she gave less. She was patient. She practiced and listened.

A breeze swished the wind chimes. Ting-ting-ting. Rilla felt calm. She talked to the plants as she worked. “Hello, tiny tomato. Here is a sip.” “Hello, bright marigold. Just a drink.” “Hello, mint leaves. You smell so good!”

Sometimes she still spilled a few drops. Sometimes the spout squeaked, and nothing came out. Each time, she tried again. Each time, she learned a little more.

Soon the porch looked fresh and happy. The plants stood tall. The air smelled like mint and sunshine. The butterfly danced. The bumblebee buzzed a warm, low song. Even the ants made a neat, dry line along the edge.

Rilla set the empty watering can down. She wiggled her nose and smiled. “I did it! I learned the gentle pour.”

Mama Rabbit hugged her. “You practiced. You cared for the plants and the tiny friends who live here. You solved each little problem with calm.”

Rilla crunched a carrot stick. “Practice helps me grow,” she said. “When I practice, I can take care of our porch and the Earth.”

Mama nodded. “Yes. Practice, care, and persistence.”

Rilla watched the sun blink through the leaves. She felt proud. “Tomorrow,” she said, “I will practice again. I will pick up bits of litter from the path. I will keep the porch clean for the bees, the ants, and the birds.”

The wind chimes sang. The plants rustled. The front porch felt peaceful and bright.

Rilla lifted the watering can and gave one last gentle tip to a tiny sprout. “Hello, little friend,” she said. “I will keep trying. I will keep caring.”

And the sprout seemed to stand just a little taller.

The End.
