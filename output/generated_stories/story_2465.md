# Rocky’s Pantry Song

Rocky was a musical raccoon with soft gray fur and bright curious eyes. He loved to sing little tunes with his gentle paws tapping like drums. One rainy morning, Rocky slid into a quiet pantry through a tiny door. The pantry smelled like cinnamon and apples. It was cozy and still. Shelves stood tall with jars, boxes, and baskets.

“Ooo,” hummed Rocky. “What a place for a song!”

He tapped a spoon on a tin. Ting-ting! He brushed a whisk. Shh-shh! He plucked a rubber band. Boing! The pantry made music, and Rocky’s heart danced.

Rocky saw a big jar of nuts. He loved nuts. He also saw a basket of bright red apples and a bag of flour as white as snow. He reached for the jar and said, “Just one nut for a snack.”

But the lid was tight. Rocky tugged. The jar slid. It bumped a box. Thump! The box wobbled. A small pile of rice spilled like tiny rain. Tap-tap-tap on the floor.

“Oh no,” whispered Rocky. His ears drooped. “I made a mess.”

He looked around. The pantry was neat before. Now there was rice on the floor and the jar leaned on its side. Rocky’s tummy felt twisty. “I did not ask. I did not know the rules,” he said softly. “I need to learn.”

He took a deep breath. Then he sang a gentle tune, “When I don’t know what to do, I can learn, I can ask, that’s true.” The song helped him feel brave.

A soft voice came from the doorway. It was Nia, the kind child who lived in the house. She wore blue socks with stars. “Hello, little raccoon,” Nia said. “I heard music. Are you okay?”

Rocky’s whiskers shook. “I made a mess,” he said. “I tried to open a jar. I did not ask. I did not know how to put it back. I am sorry.”

Nia smiled and knelt down. “Thank you for telling me the truth,” she said. “We can fix it together. Next time, you can ask. We share, but we also try not to waste.”

“What is waste?” Rocky asked. His eyes were big.

“Waste is when we use more than we need,” Nia said. She picked up a small broom. “We save food. We keep things tidy. That is called conservation. It helps people, animals, and our world.”

Rocky nodded. “Con-ser-va-tion,” he sang softly. “Save food. Keep tidy.”

Nia handed him a little dustpan. “Let’s scoop the rice,” she said. Swish, sweep, scoop. The rice made a soft hush sound as it slid back into a bowl. “We can still use clean rice for art,” Nia said. “We can color it and make pictures. That way, we don’t throw it away.”

Rocky’s eyes sparkled. “That is smart,” he said. “You know many things.”

Nia laughed. “I learned from my mom and my teachers. When we don’t know, we can ask, read, or watch and learn. Knowledge helps us make good choices.”

Rocky touched the jar lid with one paw. “May I have one nut, please?” he asked.

“Yes,” Nia said. “And I will show you how to open and close the jar.” She turned the lid slowly. Twist, twist, pop! Then she showed Rocky how to hold the jar steady and twist the lid back on. “See? Open just enough. Take just what you need. Then close it tight.”

Rocky took one nut. “Crunch,” he said when he bit it. It tasted warm and earthy. “Thank you for sharing.”

“You’re welcome,” Nia said. “Thank you for asking. That shows respect. This pantry is for everyone in my family. When we respect others, we take care of their things.”

Rocky’s tail swished. “I want to help more,” he said. “Can we make a song for the pantry?”

“Yes!” Nia clapped. Together they made a gentle beat with spoons and bowls. Ting, ting, tap-tap. Rocky sang:

“Ask before you take a bite,
Take a little, make it right.
Use just what you truly need,
Close and tidy, kind indeed.
Learn and share what you now know,
Help the pantry ready go!”

Nia smiled. “That is our knowledge song,” she said. “It helps us remember.”

Rocky looked at the shelves. “May we put the apples in a cool basket?” he asked.

“Great idea,” Nia said. They moved the apples gently. Plop, plop, plop. The apples shone like little suns. “Cool air keeps them fresh longer,” Nia explained. “More days to enjoy. That is conservation too.”

Rocky felt happy and calm. The pantry looked neat again. It smelled sweet and clean. “I am grateful for your help,” he said. “Thank you for teaching me.”

“I’m grateful you wanted to learn,” Nia said. “When we learn, we can help. When we help, we show love.”

Rocky stood tall and sang one last time, soft and clear, “When I don’t know, I ask and learn. I take care and wait my turn. I’m respectful, grateful too. With knowledge, I know what to do.”

Nia waved. “Come back for music time,” she said. “We can make art with the rice later.”

Rocky picked up his little dustpan like a tiny drum and made a happy beat as he left the pantry. He felt light and proud. He had a plan for next time: ask, learn, use a little, and say thank you.

The pantry was quiet again, cozy and bright, ready for songs and careful hands.

The End.
