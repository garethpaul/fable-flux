# Rooftop Hops and Helpful Stops

Pip was a joyful kangaroo joey with soft, warm fur and bright, curious eyes. He lived with Mama Roo in a cozy home under a big, flat rooftop. Every morning, Pip bounced out of bed and wiggled his toes. The sun glowed golden on the roof, and the wind made a gentle whoosh that felt cool on his cheeks.

“Good morning, Roo!” Mama said, smiling. “It’s Roo-time routine.”

Pip clapped his little paws. “Roo-time routine! Hop, stop, help!” he chirped.

They climbed the little ladder to the rooftop. The roof felt warm from the sun. Pip could smell fresh toast from a nearby kitchen and hear city birds singing, “Cheep, cheep!” The rooftop garden swayed—a tiny lemon tree, a patch of mint, and a row of twinkly wind chimes.

Mama handed Pip a small basket. “Today our tasks are simple,” she said kindly. “One by one, we will: water the mint, sweep the leaves, and hang the chimes back up. Step by step. We start, we stick, we finish.”

Pip nodded. “Start, stick, finish!” He liked how the words sounded. They felt bouncy and strong.

He hopped to the mint. The mint smelled cool, like tiny green candy. Pip lifted the watering can. “Glug, glug,” the water sang as the soil turned dark and happy. Pip giggled. “Mint is having a drink!”

“Task one, done,” Mama said softly, giving him a gentle ear pat. “What comes next?”

Pip looked at the leaves scattered on the rooftop. A small broom leaned against the railing. “Sweep!” he said. He picked up the broom. It felt a little heavy. He started to push the leaves. “Swish, swish,” went the broom. The wind made a little “whoooo,” and some leaves rolled back.

Pip’s ears drooped. “Mama, the leaves are playing chase. They won’t stay in the pile.”

Mama knelt beside him. “Leaves like to dance,” she said. “Let’s make sweeping a game. We can sweep to the beat: swish, swish, stop. Swish, swish, stop. We can work together.”

Pip took a breath. “Swish, swish, stop,” he said. Mama swept too. Pip held the basket open while Mama scooped. The leaves made a papery “crunch, crunch” sound. Pip smiled. “We’re a sweeping team!”

They finished with a neat, tidy rooftop. Pip puffed out his chest. “Task two, done!”

Mama pointed to the wind chimes on the floor. They were silver and shiny, and they tinkled when the breeze kissed them. “Last task,” Mama said. “We’ll hang the chimes back up so they can sing.”

Pip reached up. He was small, and the hook was high. He stretched and stood on tiptoes. “I can’t reach,” he said. His voice wobbled a little. “Maybe I can’t do it.”

Mama gave him a warm hug. “Sometimes a task is tall,” she said. “But we can use tools, try again, or ask for help. That is resilience. We don’t give up. We find a way.”

Pip looked around. He saw a little stepping stool by the planter. “A tool!” he cheered. He placed the stool near the hook. He climbed up slowly. “Step, step,” his feet went. The stool wiggled a tiny bit, and Pip felt a flutter in his tummy.

“I feel wobbly,” he whispered.

“I’m right here,” Mama said. She held the stool steady. “Slow and safe. Start, stick, finish.”

Pip took a deep breath that smelled like mint. He stretched again and slipped the loop over the hook. “Tink!” The chimes kissed the metal and began to sing, “Ting-a-ling, ting-a-ling!” The sound was bright and happy.

“I did it!” Pip laughed. His tail thumped the roof with a soft “thup, thup.”

“Task three, done,” Mama said. “How do you feel?”

“Big and brave,” Pip said. “And helpful!”

Just then, a tiny voice squeaked near the lemon tree. A ladybug had flipped onto its back. Its little legs wiggled. “Help, please!” it seemed to say.

Pip blinked. “Oh! A little friend needs help.” He knelt down, very gentle, and used a leaf to flip the ladybug. “There you go.” The ladybug crawled onto his paw and tickled him. Pip giggled. “That tickles! You’re okay now.”

The city breeze puffed and carried the chimes’ song across the rooftop garden. Pip and Mama sat together on a small bench. Mama handed him a cup of cool water. “Sip, sip,” went the cup. Pip’s cheeks were pink, and his eyes were bright.

“Mama,” he said, “we started, we stuck, we finished. We helped the mint, helped the roof, and helped the ladybug.”

Mama smiled. “Yes, Pip. When we follow our routine and finish our tasks, we make our home safe and happy. And when something is hard, we slow down, ask for help, and try again.”

Pip nodded. “Completing tasks feels good. My heart feels like a drum—boom-boom, proud-boom.”

The sun dipped a little lower, and the sky turned peach and soft. The chimes sang a bedtime song. Pip yawned a tiny joey yawn. “Tomorrow,” he said, “I will do the steps again. Start, stick, finish.”

Mama tucked him in with a cozy rooftop blanket. “Yes,” she whispered. “Routine helps us grow.”

Pip closed his eyes, listening to the “ting-a-ling” and the “whoosh” of the wind. He felt safe, helpful, and strong.

And on that rooftop, a joyful kangaroo joey learned that finishing each task, one by one, makes big jobs small, homes happy, and hearts proud.

The End.
