# The Little Builder in the Dining Room

In a sunny dining room, a kind mouse named Miri lived behind the big blue curtain. The dining room smelled like warm toast and oranges. The table was tall like a mountain, and the chairs made little shadows on the floor. Every morning, the sun made golden squares on the rug. Miri loved those squares. They felt warm on her whiskers.

“I want to build something,” Miri said, patting her tiny toolbox. Her toolbox was a matchbox with a pencil nub, a paper-clip ruler, sticky tape, and a small notebook. “I will build a cozy bridge for crumbs, so no crumb falls and gets lost.”

Miri’s friend, Pip the sparrow, fluttered to the window sill. “Cheep! Cheep! What are you building today?” Pip asked.

“A crumb bridge,” Miri said. “We will carry crumbs safely from the plate to the basket. It is kind and fair. No crumb left behind!”

“That sounds just,” said Pip. He had heard the word “just” from the big people in the house. “Just means fair for everyone.”

Miri nodded. “Yes. That is justice. We will be fair to big crumbs and little crumbs.”

On the table, a napkin fluttered like a soft white flag. The air smelled like sweet jam. Miri climbed the chair leg with her tiny paws. Tap, tap, tap. Her paws felt the wood. She reached the top and peeped over. There were toast crumbs, tiny sugar crystals, and a strawberry seed.

“Hello, crumbs,” Miri whispered. “We will build a bridge to help you.”

Miri pulled out her notebook. She wrote in big, careful letters: PLAN. She wrote, stick straws here. Tape here. Test.

Pip tilted his head. “You can write?”

“Yes,” Miri said. “Writing helps me think. Writing helps me share my plan. When we write, we remember. When we write, we are fair. Everyone can see the plan.”

Miri found two clean straws near the napkin holder. She laid them side by side. “These are the rails,” she said. She used a little tape to hold them together. The tape made a sticky sound—zip, press.

Pip brought a thin spoon from the edge of the table. “Cheep! A spoon can be the ramp!”

“Thank you,” said Miri. “Teamwork is kind.”

Down below, a beetle named Dot peeked out from under the chair. “I want to cross first,” Dot said, wiggling her antennae.

Miri smiled. “We will all cross. We will take turns. That is justice.”

“But I am big,” Dot said. “Big ones go first!”

Miri shook her head gently. “Big or small, we share. We make a fair list.” She showed her notebook. She drew simple shapes. Mouse. Sparrow. Beetle. Crumbs.

Pip chirped. “I see. Writing a list is fair.”

Miri taped the spoon to the straws. She tapped it with her paw. Tap, tap. The bridge felt steady. She added a napkin on top for soft steps.

“Time to test,” Miri said. She picked up a crumb. It smelled like butter. She placed it on the bridge. The crumb slid slowly down the spoon, then along the straws, and into the basket below. Plink! The basket made a tiny happy sound.

“It works!” Pip cheeped and fluttered.

Dot clapped her little legs. “My turn to cross!”

Miri looked at her notebook. “We follow our list. First: crumbs. Second: Dot. Third: Miri. Fourth: Pip.”

Dot huffed, but she waited and watched the crumbs roll safely into the basket. The room felt calm and kind. The sun warmed Miri’s ears.

Soon, a little breeze from the open window blew across the table. Whoosh! The napkin flicked up and tickled Miri’s nose. The bridge wobbled. A sugar crystal slid toward the edge.

“Oh no!” Pip cried.

Miri took a deep breath. “We will fix it. Builders fix and build again.” She pressed the napkin smoother and doubled the tape. Zip, press, press. She wrote in her notebook: add more tape when windy.

The bridge held steady. The sugar crystal rolled across. Plink!

“Now it is fair turn time,” Miri said. Dot stepped onto the soft napkin top. Her tiny feet made a quiet pat-pat sound. She crossed, smiling. “Thank you,” she said. “I like fair turns.”

Miri went next, and then Pip hopped very carefully. The bridge swayed a little, but it stayed strong.

From the doorway, the big people voices drifted in, warm like cocoa. “Clean table, clean hearts,” one said. The scent of lemon soap floated through the room.

Miri looked at her notebook again. She drew a star. “Our plan worked. We used building to help. We used writing to guide us. We used justice to share.”

Pip bobbed. “Cheep! What did we learn?”

Miri read aloud, her voice soft but bright:
“One: Builders make a plan.
Two: Writing helps us remember and be fair.
Three: Justice means everyone gets a turn and help.
Four: If something wobbles, we try again.”

Dot nodded. “And five: Crumbs are safe!” She giggled, and her giggle sounded like a tiny drum—dum-de-dum.

They packed up the tools. The tape felt smooth and warm. The pencil nub left gray marks on Miri’s paw. The basket of crumbs smelled sweet and toasty. Miri pushed it gently to the edge of her little nest, where everyone could share.

Pip fluffed his wings. “Today was a good day in the dining room.”

Miri smiled. “Yes. We built something helpful. We were fair. We wrote our plan. We can build again tomorrow.”

The sun made one last golden square on the rug. The room grew quiet and cozy. Miri curled up by her toolbox. Her heart felt full, like the crumb basket.

“Good night,” she whispered. “Good night, bridge. Good night, friends.”

The End.
