# Ollie Otter’s Attic Story

Ollie was a playful otter with shiny whiskers and bright, curious eyes. One sunny afternoon, Ollie padded up creaky wooden stairs into a quiet attic. The air smelled like dusty paper and vanilla. Sunbeams made soft, golden squares on the floor. It felt cozy and still, like the attic was holding its breath, waiting.

“Ooo,” Ollie whispered. “What a perfect place to write!”

Ollie loved stories. He loved splishy-splashy river tales, and drip-drop rain songs, and whooshy wind adventures. Today, he wanted to write his very own story. He wanted to use his voice. He wanted to practice and grow.

On a little table, Ollie found a big notebook, smooth and clean. A cup held fat, colorful crayons. There was a small clock that tick-tick-ticked, and a soft blanket folded on a chair. Ollie wiggled with joy. “I can write here,” he said. “I can plan. I can practice. I can be brave.”

Ollie made a plan, step by step.
“Step one,” he said, tapping his whiskers. “Think.”
“Step two: Plan.”
“Step three: Write.”
“Step four: Check.”

He drew a simple list with boxes. The crayon made a soft scritch-scratch sound on the paper. Ollie liked that sound. It felt like tiny footsteps.

Ollie closed his eyes to think. He listened to the attic. He heard the clock—tick, tick. He smelled the wood—warm and sweet. He felt the soft blanket under his paws. “My story will be about an otter who learns to glide,” he decided. “That otter is me!”

He drew a little otter with a big smile. “Hi, I’m Glide-Ollie!” he giggled.

Ollie began to write in big, careful letters. He said the words as he wrote them, slow and clear. “O-l-l-i-e g-l-i-d-e-s.” His crayon squeaked a friendly squeak. Sometimes his letters wiggled. Sometimes he had to stop and try again. That was okay. He was building a skill.

A flutter of wings made him look up. “Hello?” Ollie called.

A small gray mouse peeked from a box. “Hi, I’m Millie,” squeaked the mouse. “What are you doing?”

“I’m writing,” said Ollie. “This is my story. I am planning, and then I will check.”

Millie’s eyes shone. “Can I help?”

Ollie thought. He was happy to have a friend, but this was his voice, his practice. “Thank you, Millie,” he said kindly. “I will write my own words first. Then I can read to you, and you can listen and help me check. That is my plan.”

“That is good self-advocacy,” Millie said, nodding. “You said what you need.”

Ollie smiled and kept writing. He whispered his sentences. “Ollie wants to glide. He tries. He slips. He tries again. He plans. He learns.”

He drew arrows and boxes, like a tiny map. He put a star where Glide-Ollie practiced each day. “Practice helps,” he said softly.

A tall stack of papers wobbled nearby. On top sat a shiny sticker. It said, “Best Story!” Ollie’s whiskers twitched. “I want that sticker,” he thought. “But I will not take it. I will earn it.” He put his paws behind his back and took a little breath. “Integrity means being honest,” he told himself. “I will tell the truth in my story. I will do my own work.”

Ollie wrote more. “Ollie glides on a soft mat. He glides on a blanket. He glides on a sunny floor. He checks his plan.” He colored the sunbeams a warm yellow. He drew wavy lines for motion. The attic felt full of quiet energy. Tick, tick. Scritch, scratch. Swish.

“Time to check,” Ollie said. He read his story out loud to Millie. His voice was calm and proud.

“Ollie wants to glide. He plans. He asks for help when he needs it. He practices. He keeps trying. Ollie glides!”

Millie clapped her tiny paws. “I like how you planned,” she said. “I like how you kept your own words. I like how you told the truth.”

Ollie nodded. “Thank you for listening. Can you help me check tricky parts?”

Millie pointed gently. “This letter is upside down. This word needs a space.”

“Good catch,” said Ollie. He fixed the letters. He added the space. He smiled. “I did it!”

Just then, a warm breeze slipped through a small attic window. It smelled like leaves and sunshine. Ollie felt happy inside, like a soft glow. He put a tiny box at the end of his list and drew a check mark. “Done,” he whispered.

He did not take the sticker from the wobbly stack. Instead, he drew his own little star on the page and wrote, “I did my best.”

Millie squeaked, “You practiced. You planned. You used your voice. You showed integrity. You built your writing skill!”

Ollie’s tail swished. “Writing is like gliding,” he said. “You start small. You try. You plan. You practice. You keep going.”

He closed the notebook and hugged it gently. The attic was still quiet and kind. Tick, tick. The sunbeams were softer now. Ollie felt proud and peaceful.

“Tomorrow,” he said, “I will write again.”

The End.
