# Benny Bear’s Busy Garage Day

Benny was a friendly bear cub with soft brown fur and bright curious eyes. He loved to help. Today, he padded into the garage workshop where Mama kept tools and paint. The air smelled like pine wood and lemon soap. Sunlight made little dust sparkles dance.

“Good morning, tools!” Benny said with a giggle. He liked to say hello. It made him feel brave and kind.

Mama Bear wiped her paws on a cloth. “Good morning, Benny. I’m fixing our wobbly shelf. Would you like to be my helper?”

“Yes, please!” Benny’s ears perked up. He wanted to be useful. He bounced on his toes, then stopped and took a breath. He remembered: helpers listen first.

Mama showed him a small toolbox. “We need the short, safe screwdriver,” she said. “Can you find it?”

Benny looked carefully. He saw a long one, a tiny one, and a short one with a red handle. “This one?” he asked.

“Yes,” Mama smiled. “Thank you, helper.”

The shelf squeaked. Squeeeak. Benny held it steady with both paws. “I’m strong and careful,” he whispered. The wood felt cool and smooth. Mama turned the screws—twist, twist, twist. The shelf stopped wobbling.

“We did it!” Benny cheered.

“Your helping paws made it easy,” said Mama. Benny felt warm and proud.

Just then, a small sound came from the open garage door. “Peep?” A little bird hopped inside. Its wing drooped. The bird looked scared.

Benny’s heart felt soft. “Oh no,” he said. “Little Bird needs help.”

Mama nodded. “We can be gentle helpers. First, we make a safe space.”

Benny looked around. He found a clean box and a soft cloth. “This can be cozy,” he said. He placed it on the workbench, away from sharp tools. The lemon-soap smell was calm. He hummed a tiny tune so the bird would not be afraid.

“Peep,” said the bird, softer now.

Benny crouched low. “Hello, Little Bird. I am Benny. I will help.” He spoke slowly and quietly. His paws stayed still. Mama showed him how to hold a small cup of water. Benny set it near the box.

“Let’s wash our paws,” Mama said. “Helpers take care of themselves so they can help others.”

Benny washed with warm water. He rubbed his paws until they were clean and dry. “I feel ready,” he said.

Mama looked at the wing. “It looks tired, not broken. The bird needs rest, water, and a little food.”

Benny nodded. He remembered sunflower seeds in the pantry. He walked, not ran, so his steps were calm. Tap, tap, tap went his little claws on the floor. He brought a tiny pinch of seeds and placed them gently in the box.

The bird pecked once. “Peep!”

Benny smiled. “You are brave.”

While the bird rested, Benny helped Mama sweep the floor. Swish, swish went the broom. They put away the paint cans. Clink, clink went the lids. Benny even wiped the workbench with a damp cloth. “Helping makes things tidy,” he said. “And safe.”

Mama nodded. “Helping also means asking, ‘What do you need?’”

Benny looked at the bird. “Little Bird, what do you need?” he asked.

The bird hopped to the edge of the box and looked at the open door. A breeze carried the smell of fresh grass. The bird fluttered its wing. “Peep!” It wanted the sky.

“Maybe just a little more rest,” Benny said kindly. “And then you can try.”

They waited together. Benny took a sip of water and ate a small snack Mama brought. “Helpers need energy,” Mama reminded him.

Benny stretched his arms. He noticed his tummy felt full and happy. He noticed his paws felt strong. “I took care of me,” he said. “Now I can take care of others.”

After a while, the bird stood taller. Benny opened the big garage door a bit wider. Sunlight poured in like warm honey. The bird fluttered its wings—flap, flap—then flew to the doorway. It paused and looked back at Benny.

“Good luck,” Benny whispered. He lifted his paw and waved. “Be safe.”

The bird lifted into the blue sky. Up, up it went, circling once. “Peep!” it called, bright and cheerful. Then it soared away.

Benny felt a happy glow in his chest. He leaned into Mama’s hug. Her fur smelled like soap and cinnamon.

“I liked helping,” Benny said. “I listened. I was gentle. I kept the place tidy. I took care of me, too.”

Mama smiled. “That is true helpfulness—kind hands, kind heart, and kind care for yourself.”

Benny looked around the neat garage workshop. The shelf was steady. The tools were safe. The room was calm. He imagined the little bird finding a tree. He imagined it singing.

He picked up a small wooden block and drew a heart on it with chalk. “This is our Helper Heart,” he said. “We can look at it and remember.”

Mama placed the heart on the steady shelf. “Everyday helpers make the world better,” she said.

Benny nodded. “I can help at home. I can help friends. I can help me.”

He took a deep breath. The lemon and pine smells made him feel peaceful. “Helping feels good,” he said. “Helping is kind.”

The End.
