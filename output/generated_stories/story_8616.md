# Dippy the Dolphin Finds His Words

In a cozy, comfortable nursery filled with soft rugs and colorful blocks, a playful dolphin named Dippy splashed gently in a round, warm water tub. The tub bubbled softly. Blub-blub! The room smelled like fresh soap and oranges. Sunlight made little sparkles on Dippy’s smooth, gray-blue back.

Dippy loved to play. He clicked and chirped happily. “Eee-eee! Let’s play!” he squeaked, flipping his tail and splashing tiny drops that felt cool on his skin.

Around the nursery, friends were busy. Mia the mouse stacked rainbow blocks. Theo the turtle rolled a soft red ball. Lila the lamb cuddled a fuzzy blanket. Everyone was smiling.

Dippy had an idea. He wanted to play a new game: Pass-the-Ball-and-Sing. He bounced in the water and splashed. Splash! Splash! He reached his flippers toward Theo’s ball. “My turn! My turn!” he squeaked, eyes wide with excitement.

Theo blinked slowly. “I am playing now,” he said. “I will share soon.”

But Dippy felt so excited. He took the ball with a quick flip. Plop! The ball fell into the tub. “Whee!” Dippy chirped, rolling it with his nose. The ball squeaked—squeak, squeak!

Mia’s ears twitched. “Hey, Dippy,” she said softly. “Theo was using that.”

Lila hugged her blanket close. “It’s not fair to take,” she bleated, her voice gentle but sad.

Dippy stopped. His tummy felt tight, like a knotted string. He didn’t want to make his friends sad. He just wanted to play together.

Ms. Star, the kind teacher, knelt by the tub. She smelled like lemon and warm cookies. “Dippy,” she said in a calm voice, “I see you are excited. You had an idea. That is wonderful initiative. But we also use our words. We ask. We wait. That is fairness.”

Dippy looked down at the water. Ripples danced in little rings. He took a deep breath. Bubble-bubble. “I forgot to ask,” he whispered.

Ms. Star nodded. “You can fix it. Use your words. Try again.”

Dippy pushed the ball back to Theo with a gentle nudge. “I am sorry, Theo,” he said. “I was excited. I did not ask. May I please have a turn after you?”

Theo’s eyes brightened. “Thank you for asking,” he said. “Yes. I will roll it to you when I am done. Then we can play your game.”

Dippy’s heart felt warm, like sunshine on his back. “Thank you,” he said. He flapped his tail softly, making tiny waves that went shhh-shhh against the tub.

While Theo played, Dippy waited. He hummed a little tune: doo-doo, dee-dee. Waiting was hard. His flippers wiggled. He wanted to splash big, but he remembered fairness. He watched quietly. He counted the bubbles. One bubble, two bubble, three bubble… pop!

Mia came over with her blocks. “What is your game?” she asked.

Dippy smiled. “Pass-the-Ball-and-Sing,” he said clearly. “We pass the ball. We sing a little line. Then we pass again. We take turns. We listen. Everyone gets a turn.”

Mia clapped her tiny paws. “That sounds fun!”

Lila nodded. “I like singing.”

Theo rolled the ball one last time and hugged it close. “Okay,” he said. “Now it is Dippy’s turn.” He looked at Dippy’s eyes. “Thank you for waiting.”

Dippy took a deep breath and used his words. “Friends, let’s start the game,” he said. “I will show you. I will begin, then I will pass.”

He sang a soft, happy line. “Splash, splash, we sing and play, taking turns the friendly way.” His voice sounded like a bell in the water—ring-a-ring, gentle and bright.

He passed the ball to Mia. Mia sang, “Squeak, squeak, I pass to you. Sharing feels so happy too!” She giggled and passed to Lila.

Lila’s voice was warm and woolly. “Baa-baa, I wait and share. Talking shows we really care.” She passed to Theo.

Theo smiled and sang slowly, “Roll, roll, the red ball goes. Ask and listen, that’s how it flows.”

The nursery filled with soft music and laughter. The ball rolled, the water swished, and the friends’ voices blended like a sweet lullaby. Ms. Star tapped the beat with her finger on the floor—tap, tap, tap.

After many turns, Dippy looked at his friends. “I have another idea,” he said. He remembered to ask. “May we make a rule together? If someone has an idea, they say it out loud. We listen. We take turns. Is that fair?”

Mia nodded. “Fair.”

Lila bleated, “Fair.”

Theo lifted his flipper. “Fair.”

Ms. Star smiled. “That is strong communication,” she said. “You used your words. You listened. You showed fairness. You took initiative to make a plan. I am proud of you.”

Dippy felt proud too. His tummy felt light now, like a bubble floating up. He splashed just a little—plip-plip—then wiped a tiny drop from Mia’s whiskers. “Sorry for the splash,” he said kindly.

“It’s okay,” Mia giggled. “You asked and you told me.”

They played until the sunbeam moved across the rug and made the colors on the wall glow like rainbows. When it was time to clean up, Dippy took initiative again. “I will help,” he said. He used a net to lift the red ball and floated it to the toy bin. He told each friend, “Thank you for playing.”

At last, the friends cuddled on the soft rug. The room smelled like warm milk now. Dippy whispered, “Today I learned to use my words, to ask, to listen, and to share. That is fair.”

Ms. Star tucked a towel around him. “Yes,” she said softly. “Good communication makes playtime happy for everyone.”

Dippy closed his eyes, tail gently swishing. He felt safe, kind, and proud.

The End.
