# Joey Roo and the Fair Turn Treehouse

Joey Roo was a joyful kangaroo joey with a soft, bouncy tail and big, bright eyes. He lived near a tall treehouse that smelled like fresh pine and warm sunshine. The wooden steps felt smooth under his feet. The rope ladder swung and squeaked, “Creak, creak,” in the gentle breeze.

“Good morning, treehouse!” Joey Roo sang as he hopped up. “I’m ready to play!”

Inside the treehouse, friends were busy. Polly Possum stacked colorful blocks. Kiki Koala drew with bright crayons that smelled like berries. Benny Bilby balanced a beanbag on his nose. Everyone waited to play on the big spiral slide.

“I want the slide!” said Benny.

“I want the slide too!” said Kiki.

“I was here first,” said Polly, hugging her blocks.

Joey Roo felt the floorboards hum under his toes. He took a deep breath. “Hmm,” he said softly. “We all want a turn. Justice means fair. Fair means everyone gets a turn.”

Benny crossed his arms. “I want my turn now.”

Kiki pouted. “Me too.”

Joey Roo’s ears twitched. He wanted to help. He was small, but he was resourceful. He looked around and saw a little bell on a string, a sandy timer on a shelf, and a chalkboard with dusty white chalk.

“I have an idea!” Joey Roo said. “We can make a Turn Plan.”

“What’s a Turn Plan?” asked Polly.

“It’s a fair plan so everyone gets the same time,” Joey Roo said. “That is justice.”

Joey Roo set the sandy timer on the floor. The sand glittered like tiny stars. “When the sand runs out, it’s the next friend’s turn,” he explained. He rang the bell—ding!—so it sounded friendly and fun. He wrote names on the chalkboard with careful strokes: Benny, Kiki, Polly, Joey.

Benny’s nose twitched. “Okay. I can try.”

Kiki nodded slowly. “I can wait.”

Polly smiled. “I like the bell.”

Joey Roo felt proud, but he still needed patience. “I will go last,” he said. “That is fair, because I made the plan.” His tummy fluttered a little. He wanted the slide too, but justice meant doing what was right.

Benny climbed to the top of the slide. He wiggled his whiskers. “Ready, set—whoosh!” He slid down with a soft “swish-swoosh,” and his fur ruffled in the wind. The timer sand fell in a whispery hush. When it ended, Joey Roo rang the bell—ding!

“My turn!” said Kiki. She sat tall, gripped the sides, and went, “Weee!” Her fur smelled like eucalyptus leaves as she zipped by. Ding! went the bell when the sand ran out.

Polly went next. She was careful and a little slow. Joey Roo held the ladder steady. “Take your time,” he said. “Patience helps us do things well.” Polly slid with a happy giggle. Ding!

Now it was Joey Roo’s turn. He held the smooth wood with both paws. “Practice helps,” he told himself. “I can practice good balance.” He bent his knees. He felt the breeze on his cheeks. “Ready,” he whispered.

“Go, Joey!” cheered his friends.

Joey Roo slid down, swish-swoosh-swish, fast and light. He landed on his big feet with a soft thump. “I did it!” he laughed. His tail wiggled in joy. The sunlight felt warm on his back.

Just then, a little lizard named Lolly poked her head in. “I want the slide,” she said shyly. “But I am small.”

Joey Roo remembered justice. “Small friends get fair turns too,” he said kindly. He wrote Lolly’s name on the chalkboard. “We can help you.”

“How?” asked Lolly, eyes wide.

“We can practice together,” Joey Roo said. He showed Lolly how to hold the side rails. Polly stood by the ladder. Kiki counted, “One, two, three.” Benny held the timer and nodded. Everyone worked in coordination, like a team.

Lolly took a deep breath. “I can do it.” She slid down with a tiny, happy squeak. Her scales felt cool like smooth pebbles when she landed and smiled.

“Ding!” Joey Roo rang. “Great job!”

Soon, a new problem popped up. The chalk point broke—snap!—and they needed more names on the board. Joey Roo looked around. He was resourceful again. He found a small piece of charcoal near the grill and a leaf to wipe the board. “This will work,” he said. He wrote with the charcoal. It made soft, dusty lines.

All day, the friends took turns. They waited, they watched, and they cheered. The treehouse smelled like wood and crayons and picnic apples. The slide sang, “Swish, whoosh, weee!” The bell rang bright and sweet, “Ding, ding!”

At sunset, the sky turned peach and purple. Joey Roo gathered everyone. “Today we learned about justice,” he said. “Justice means fair. Fair means everyone gets a turn. We also used resourcefulness to make our Turn Plan, patience to wait, practice to get better, and coordination to help each other.”

“That felt good,” said Kiki.

“It felt right,” said Benny.

“It felt fair,” said Polly.

Lolly hugged Joey Roo’s paw. “Thank you for helping me.”

Joey Roo smiled. His heart felt warm and light. “When we are fair, everyone can have fun.”

They rang the bell one last time. Ding!

The End.
