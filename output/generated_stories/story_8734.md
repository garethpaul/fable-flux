# Kiki Koala’s Brave Study Corner

Kiki was a calm koala with soft, gray fur and a tiny pink nose. She had a cozy study corner by the window. Sunlight made warm squares on the floor. Her pencils smelled like cedar wood. Her books felt smooth under her paws. A small clock went tick-tock, tick-tock.

But one morning, Kiki looked at her study corner and whispered, “Oh no.” Papers were crinkled. Crayons were under the chair. Stickers were stuck to the rug. Her tummy felt fluttery, like tiny butterflies.

“I can’t do it,” Kiki said softly. “I am too small. I am not good at this.”

A gentle breeze came through the window. It made the curtains swish. Kiki closed her eyes and took a slow breath in and out. “I am a calm koala,” she said. “I can try.”

Kiki had a problem. She wanted to read her counting book and draw a picture. But she could not find anything. Her favorite blue crayon was hiding. Her counting book was lost under papers. Kiki’s ears drooped.

From the hallway, Mama Koala called, “Kiki, how is my brave helper?”

Kiki took another breath. “Mama, my study corner is messy. I feel sad. I think I can’t do it.”

Mama stepped in with a warm smile. She smelled like eucalyptus tea. “You can do it, little leaf,” she said. “Let’s make a plan. A plan is like a little map.”

“A little map?” Kiki asked. Her eyes got bright.

“Yes,” Mama said. “Step one: sort. Step two: place. Step three: praise. We will cheer for each small step.”

Kiki liked that. It felt simple. It felt kind.

“Step one: sort,” Kiki said. She picked up papers. “Papers here.” She put them in a neat pile. She picked up crayons. “Crayons here.” She dropped them into a tin cup. Clink! Clink! The sound made her giggle. She found stickers. “Stickers here,” she said, placing them in a little box.

“You are brave to start,” Mama said. “Brave means trying, even when it feels a little hard.”

Kiki’s cheeks felt warm with pride. “I am brave,” she said.

“Step two: place,” Kiki went on. She looked at the shelf. “Books on the shelf.” She put her counting book on the bottom shelf where her paws could reach. She put her drawing pad next to it. She lined up her pencils in a row. Tap, tap, tap. They looked like tiny soldiers standing tall.

Kiki found the blue crayon at last. “There you are!” she cheered. She sniffed it. It smelled waxy and sweet.

“You are organized,” Mama said. “Organized means everything has a home.”

Kiki smiled. “I like when things have a home.”

“Step three: praise,” Mama said. “What will we say to Kiki?”

Kiki stood tall. “Good job, me! I am calm. I am brave. I can do hard things.” Her voice sounded strong, like a little drum. Boom, boom.

The room felt different now. The window light looked brighter. The air felt fresh. Kiki’s tummy was still and happy.

Kiki sat at her clean table. The wood felt smooth under her paws. She opened her counting book. The pages went swish. She traced the number 1 with her claw. “One sun.” She traced the number 2. “Two leaves.” She giggled at the pictures.

Then she drew a picture. She used her favorite blue crayon. She drew a big sun, a tall tree, and a small koala—herself—smiling. She stuck a gold star sticker in the corner.

Mama clapped softly. Clap, clap. “How do you feel now?”

Kiki put a paw on her chest. She felt her heartbeat. “I feel proud,” she said. “I made a plan. I cleaned my space. I believed in me.”

Mama nodded. “That is self-esteem,” she said. “Self-esteem is the good feeling inside when you know you can learn and try.”

Kiki liked that word. “Self-esteem,” she repeated slowly. It tasted like sweet tea in her mouth.

Later, Kiki heard a tiny rattle under the chair. She peeked under. A dust bunny wiggled. “I feel a little scared,” Kiki said.

She took a calm breath. “I am brave. I can ask for help.” She called, “Mama, can you help me?”

Together, they slid the chair. The dust bunny rolled away. Kiki laughed. “It was just fluff!”

“See?” Mama smiled. “Bravery plus a plan helps.”

Kiki made a little sign for her study corner. She wrote: “Sort. Place. Praise.” She taped it on the wall with a star sticker. The tape made a soft rip sound. Rip, smooth.

Each day after that, Kiki looked at her sign. She kept her pencils in the tin. She put her books on the shelf. She praised herself after every try. “Good job, me,” she would say. “I am calm. I am brave. I can learn.”

Her study corner stayed cozy and clear. Her heart stayed cozy and clear, too.

And when a new problem came, Kiki smiled and said, “I have a plan. I believe in me.”

The End.
