# Gigi’s Tall Letter

Gigi was a thoughtful giraffe who loved the comfortable nursery. The nursery smelled like fresh crayons and warm blankets. Sunlight made golden squares on the soft rug. Little chairs and big pillows waited for playtime.

One morning, Teacher Bell said, “Today we will write a big thank-you letter to the library. We will use our words to show care.”

Gigi’s long neck stretched high with excitement. “I love writing,” Gigi said softly. “I have tall ideas.”

Her friends gathered around the round table. There was Max the mouse, Lolo the lamb, Pip the puppy, and Nia the newt. The table was covered with paper that felt smooth, and bright markers that clicked when you took off the caps.

Teacher Bell smiled. “We will work together. That means we will cooperate. We will take turns, listen, and help.”

Gigi wanted to begin right away. She picked up a blue marker. “I can write the first line,” she said. Her hooves gently held the marker, and it squeaked on the paper. “Dear Library, thank you for—”

Max squeaked, “Wait, I have an idea!” He tugged the paper toward him.

Gigi frowned. The marker made a zigzag line. “Oh no,” she said. The zigzag crossed her neat letters.

Pip wagged his tail so fast it thumped the chair. “I want to draw a book!” he barked, nudging the paper closer still.

Lolo bleated, “I can add sparkly stars!” She shook glitter, and a tiny twinkle fell on Gigi’s nose.

Nia whispered, “I want to write my name too.”

The paper wrinkled. The letters bumped into the drawing. The glitter stuck to the zigzag. Gigi’s heart felt tight, like a knot in a ribbon. She wanted the letter to be tall and tidy.

Gigi took a slow breath. She remembered Teacher Bell’s word: cooperate.

“Friends,” Gigi said in her gentle voice, “I feel upset. I wanted to write neatly. But we can work together. Can we make a plan?”

Teacher Bell nodded. “A leader helps everyone’s ideas fit. Gigi, you can lead by listening and sharing.”

Gigi’s ears twitched. “Let’s try this. First, we think. Then, we take turns. I will write one line. Max can write the next. Pip can draw at the bottom. Lolo can add stars at the end, not on the words. Nia can write our names together.”

Max rubbed his paws. “That sounds fair.”

Pip’s tail slowed. “I can wait,” he said. “I can try.”

Lolo tucked her glitter. “I will sprinkle last.”

Nia smiled. “I will write small and neat.”

Gigi looked at Max. “I am sorry I grabbed the marker first. I did not ask.”

Max squeaked, “I am sorry I pulled the paper. I should have waited.”

They both felt the knot in their hearts loosen. The nursery seemed brighter, and the paper felt smooth again.

Gigi tried again. “Dear Library, thank you for our story time. We love the soft chairs and the big pictures.”

The marker whispered neatly. Max wrote, “We learn new words. We feel brave and calm.”

Pip drew a book at the bottom. He kept his paw light, so the lines stayed in their place. “Look, a bone-shaped bookmark,” he giggled.

Lolo sprinkled tiny stars in the corners. “Twinkle, twinkle,” she sang softly.

Nia wrote all their names with careful strokes: Gigi, Max, Pip, Lolo, Nia. The letters looked like little friends holding hands.

They stopped and looked. The letter was clear and kind. It smelled a little like glue and a little like lemony wipes. The colors were bright. The words were big and friendly.

Teacher Bell clapped. “You did it! You cooperated. You were patient. You said sorry. You did not give up.”

Gigi felt warm inside, like sunlight on the rug. “We kept trying,” she said. “We listened.”

Max nodded. “We took turns.”

Pip wagged. “We waited.”

Lolo twinkled. “We shared.”

Nia beamed. “We finished together.”

Gigi lifted the letter with care. “I will carry it with my tall neck,” she said. “I can be a leader who helps, not a leader who grabs.”

They walked to the door. The nursery smelled like snack time now—sweet apples and crunchy crackers. Pip’s tail tapped a happy beat. Lolo’s bell collar jingled softly. Max’s tiny feet pattered. Nia’s tail swished like a ribbon.

At the mailbox near the cubbies, Gigi paused. “Friends, may I say one more thing?”

“Yes,” they said.

“Thank you for helping,” Gigi said. “Thank you for trying again and again. Cooperation makes our letter strong.”

They all touched the corner of the paper, like a team high-five. Then Gigi slid the letter into the mail slot with a soft shoop.

Back on the rug, they curled up on pillows. Teacher Bell read a story about a bridge that many animals built together. The pages rustled. The pictures shone. Gigi’s eyes felt cozy. She thought, Working together feels good.

That day, the comfortable nursery felt even more comfortable, because the friends knew how to cooperate, to keep trying, and to say sorry when needed.

And every time Gigi saw a marker, she remembered to share and to lead with care.

The End.
