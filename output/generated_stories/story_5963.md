# Roary Writes His Roar

The playful living room was full of color and cozy sounds. A green rug felt soft like grass. Sunlight made bright squares on the floor. Crayons clicked in a little cup. Paper smelled clean and new.

Roary, a brave little lion cub, swished his tufted tail. His fur was warm and golden. He liked to play with blocks, and he loved to roar. Today, everyone was making thank-you notes. Mama Lion put out paper and stickers. Papa Lion set a bowl of crunchy apple slices on the table. Crunch, crunch!

“We will write kind notes,” Mama Lion said. “Use your big feelings and your brave ideas.”

Roary looked at the paper. It was so white. It looked big and quiet. His tummy fluttered. “What if I make a mistake?” he whispered.

His sister, Lina, shook a glittery marker. “I can help,” she said. “I know how to make fancy letters.”

Roary’s ears perked up. He wanted help. But he also wanted to try. He felt brave, but a little wobbly. He picked a purple crayon. It felt smooth and cool in his paw.

“I am going to write by myself,” Roary said softly.

Lina blinked. “Are you sure?”

Roary nodded. “Yes,” he said. “I can try. It’s my note.” His voice was small, but steady. That was Roary’s brave roar on the inside.

He pressed the crayon. Scribble, scribble. He made a big, curvy R. Then a round O. Then… oh no! The A was crooked. The Y looked like a sleepy tree. Roary’s whiskers drooped.

“I messed up,” he said. His eyes felt hot. The paper crackled when he moved it.

Papa Lion sat near and breathed slow. In, out. “I like your brave try,” Papa said. “Every letter is a step.”

Roary sniffed. “But it’s not perfect.”

Mama Lion touched his paw. Her hand was warm. “Perfect is not the goal,” she said gently. “Your note tells your heart. Your heart is already perfect. Keep going.”

Roary looked at the purple crayon. He wanted to hide under the couch. The couch smelled like dust and old cookies. But he didn’t hide. He sat tall on the pillow. He took a crunchy bite of apple. Crunch! The sweet juice made him smile.

“I will try again,” Roary said, louder now. “I can do hard things.”

He turned the paper over. He breathed like Papa. In, out. He made slow lines. He wrote: “Thank you.” The T was a little tilted. The Y had a long tail. But the words were his. He added a big heart. He drew a tiny sun. He stuck a sticker of a star at the top.

Lina peeked. “Roary, that looks kind and bright,” she said. “Can I read it?”

Roary held the note close. His paws made little prints on the paper. He felt something new grow inside, like a warm light. “I want to read it,” he said. “It’s my voice.” He looked at Mama and Papa. “Please listen.”

Mama and Papa smiled and sat crisscross on the rug. The rug tickled Roary’s toes. Roary cleared his throat. “Dear Grandma,” he read slowly, “thank you for the soft blanket. It makes me feel safe. Love, Roary.”

His voice shook a little, but he kept going. When he finished, the room was quiet and glowy. Then Mama clapped softly. Papa clapped too. Lina tapped the beat on the table. Tap, tap, tap.

Roary’s cheeks felt bubbly with pride. His chest felt big and warm. He was still a small cub, but his heart felt tall.

Lina held out a shiny gold sticker. “For your brave writing,” she said.

Roary smiled. “Thank you, Lina,” he said. “Can I choose where to put it?”

“Of course,” Lina said.

Roary stuck the sticker next to his name. He pressed it firm. It made a soft crinkle sound.

Then he saw another paper. It had lots of swirls and sparkles. Lina’s letters were fancy. Roary liked them.

“Lina,” Roary said, “your letters are pretty. But I like my letters too. They look like me.”

Lina nodded. “Yes,” she said. “Your letters sound like your roar.”

Roary giggled. “Roooar!” He made a small roar so it wouldn’t scare the crayons. The crayons rolled anyway, clack, clack, clack, and everyone laughed.

Papa Lion asked, “What did you learn today, little lion?”

Roary sat up tall. He put his paw on his note. “I learned that my voice matters,” he said. “I can ask for space to try. I can say, ‘I want to do it myself.’ And when I try and try, I feel proud.”

Mama Lion hugged him. Her fur smelled like warm soap and sunshine. “That is true,” she said. “Your roar is in your words.”

Roary looked at the bright squares of sunlight. He felt brave from his whiskers to his tail. He picked up a blue crayon. “I’m going to write another note,” he said. “This one is for me.”

He wrote, “Dear Roary, you are brave. Keep going.”

He read it out loud. His voice was strong and sweet. Everyone cheered.

Roary grinned. He tucked both notes into a little envelope. It made a soft whoosh as it closed.

“I did it,” he said.

“Yes,” said Mama. “You did it.”

The End.
