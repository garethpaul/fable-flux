# Squeaky the Squirrel and the Tidy Shed

The garden was quiet and warm, and the garden shed smelled like wood, dry leaves, and a little bit like apples. Dust danced in the sunlight. A tiny whiskery nose peeked under the shed door. It was Squeaky the squirrel.

Squeaky loved the shed. There were shiny tools, soft twine, and baskets. But today, the shed was a mess. A broom lay sideways. A box of acorns had tipped over. Little nuts were everywhere, like crunchy brown raindrops on the floor.

“Oh acorns,” Squeaky whispered. “I need a safe place for my winter food. I promised myself I would do it today.”

Squeaky took a deep breath. He puffed his cheeks and looked around. “I can do this,” he said. “I can make a tidy, safe spot. I can do it by myself.”

Just then, his friend, Cricket, chirped from the window. “Squeaky! Want to play leaf-hop?”

Squeaky’s tail twitched. He loved leaf-hop. But he looked at the sunbeam on the floor and the messy nuts by his paws. He remembered his plan. He remembered the promise to himself.

“Hi, Cricket,” Squeaky said kindly. “I want to play. But first, I have a job. I need to make a safe acorn space. I told myself I would do it today. I will be honest with my time. I will do my job now and play later.”

Cricket nodded. “Okay! I’ll come back when the sunbeam reaches the wall.”

Squeaky smiled. “That’s a good time plan. Thank you!”

He rubbed his paws together. The air felt cool on his fur. He began to sort. “Big acorns in the basket,” he said. “Little acorns in the little jar. Broken shells in the bin.” Clink, clink, clack. The sounds were friendly.

He found a small blue box with a loose lid. “Hmm,” Squeaky said. “This won’t keep acorns safe.” He looked up at the tall shelf. On top was a sturdy tin with a snug cap. But the shelf was high.

Squeaky tried to climb the broom. Slide! He tried to jump from a crate. Boing—miss! He wiggled his whiskers and sat down. His tummy felt fluttery for a moment.

“I feel a little stuck,” he said softly. “But I can think. I can try again. I can use what I have.”

He looked around. Squeaky saw a short stool, some twine, and a wooden spoon. An idea bloomed, warm like sunshine.

“I will make a careful reacher,” he said.

Squeaky tied the twine to the spoon. He stood on the stool. He tapped the tin gently with the spoon. Tap, tap. “Come closer, tin,” he whispered.

The tin wobbled. “Oh!” Squeaky gasped. He steadied the stool with his toes and tried again, slower. “I will be patient. I will not rush.”

Tap, scoot, tap. The tin slid nearer. Squeaky stretched. His paws tingled on the cool metal. “Got you!” he cheered.

He set the tin on the table and patted it proudly. “I did it myself. I asked my brain for help.”

A soft voice floated from the doorway. It was Old Garden Cat, with gray whiskers and kind eyes. “Hello, Squeaky. Do you want help?”

Squeaky looked at the tin and the piles he had sorted. He felt honest words bubble up. “Thank you, Cat. I had trouble, but I tried and tried. I made a reacher and got the tin down. I think I can finish by myself. I promised myself I would.”

Old Garden Cat purred. “That is wise. You told the truth about your plan. You are using your time well. I will watch quietly.”

Squeaky washed the tin with a damp cloth. Swish, swish. He dried it. Pat, pat. He tested the lid. Snap! “Safe and snug!” he said.

He poured in the big acorns. Clatter, clatter, stop. He labeled the tin with a leaf and a tiny scratch mark. “Winter Food,” he read in a happy whisper.

Next, Squeaky swept the floor. The broom made a gentle whoosh, like wind in tall grass. He sang as he worked. “Sweep and sort, sweep and sort, I can do hard things of any sort.”

The sunbeam crept along the wall. Time was moving. Squeaky checked the light. “Halfway,” he said. “I will finish before the sunbeam touches the door. That is my plan.”

He stacked the baskets neatly. He placed the little jar on a low shelf. He hung the twine on a peg. He wiped the table. He put the broken shells in the bin. He checked the list in his head.

“Acorns safe? Yes. Floor clean? Yes. Tools tidy? Yes. Hands washed? Not yet!”

Squeaky washed his paws in a small bowl. Splash, splash. The water felt cool and tickly.

Cricket hopped back as the sunbeam reached the wall. “Squeaky! Are you ready?”

Squeaky looked around. The shed was calm and tidy. It smelled like clean wood and a little like fresh air now.

“I am ready,” he said, his eyes bright. “I kept my promise. I used my time well. I told the truth about what I could do. I tried again when it was hard. I did it myself.”

Old Garden Cat smiled. “You were brave and patient. That is real strength.”

Cricket chirped, “Leaf-hop time!”

Squeaky picked up one small acorn, shiny and smooth. He tucked it in his pocket fur. “This is my reminder,” he said. “I can be self-reliant. I can be honest. I can use my time wisely. I can keep trying.”

They stepped into the warm garden. The leaves rustled like soft applause. Squeaky’s heart felt light.

And then they played.

The End.
