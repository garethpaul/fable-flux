# Ellie’s Big Brave Breath

Ellie was a gentle elephant calf with soft gray ears and a tiny, swishy tail. Today, Ellie climbed into the family car with Mama, Papa, and her big sister, Lila. The car smelled like warm banana muffins, and the seats felt smooth and cool. The seat belt clicked—click!—around Ellie’s belly, snug and safe.

“We’re going to the Global Kids Fair,” said Mama, starting the car. “Children from many places will share songs and stories.”

Ellie’s ears flapped with worry. “But I am small,” she whispered. “What if my voice is tiny? What if I forget my song?”

Papa’s trunk gently patted her shoulder. “Your voice is your voice, Ellie. It is special.”

Lila smiled and handed Ellie a small map of the fair. “Look,” she said, pointing. “There will be drums from Africa, kites from Asia, maracas from Latin America, and stories from all over the world.”

Ellie watched the trees whoosh by the window. She felt her tummy flutter. The car hummed, mmmmmm, and the air through the window smelled like sunshine and cut grass. But Ellie’s thoughts buzzed, too. What if, what if, what if?

Mama glanced in the mirror. “Ellie, when I feel wiggly inside, I take a belly breath. Would you like to try?”

Ellie nodded.

“Feet on the floor,” Mama said. “Hands on your tummy. Breathe in slow like you are smelling a flower.” Ellie sniffed—sniff. “Now breathe out slow like you are blowing a bubble.” Ellie blew—whoooo.

Again. Sniff. Whoooo. The tight knot in Ellie’s tummy began to loosen.

“What do you hear?” asked Papa.

Ellie listened. “I hear the car, mmmmmm. I hear birds, tweet-tweet. I hear Lila tapping, tap-tap, on her map.”

“What do you see?” asked Lila.

“I see the big blue sky. I see a bright kite on a string. I see a bus with smiling children.”

“What do you feel?” Mama asked softly.

Ellie patted her chest. “I feel my heart, thump-thump. I feel my seat belt, click-snug. I feel my breath go in and out.”

“Mindful Ellie,” said Papa. “Here and now.”

Ellie smiled a tiny smile. The worry cloud felt a little lighter.

At a red light, a family crossed the street. The little boy wore a colorful hat. The little girl held a small flag with stars. Ellie waved. The girl waved back.

“People live in many places,” Lila said, “but we can all wave and smile.”

“That is global,” Mama said. “We all share one big world.”

Ellie touched the map. “I want to learn their songs.”

“You will,” Papa said. “And you can share your song, too.”

Ellie took another slow breath. Sniff. Whoooo.

When the car parked, Ellie heard drums, dum-dum-dum, and sweet singing, la-la-la. She smelled popcorn and spicy rice. Flags flapped in the breeze, flap-flap, bright like painted rainbows.

But the worry flutter came back. Ellie pressed her trunk to her chest. “What if my voice is too small?”

Papa knelt. “Let’s try a power plan. Step one: breathe. Step two: say kind words to yourself. Step three: take one small brave step.”

Ellie nodded. Step one: sniff. Whoooo. Step two: she whispered, “I am Ellie. I am gentle and strong. I can try.” The words felt warm, like a soft blanket.

“Step three?” asked Lila.

“One small brave step,” Ellie said. She took Papa’s hand and walked to a little stage where children took turns sharing. A boy played a drum, dum-da-dum. A girl twirled her kite. A child shook maracas, chika-chika.

A kind teacher smiled at Ellie. “Would you like to share a song from your family?”

Ellie’s heart thumped fast. She looked at Mama. Mama placed a hand over her own heart and took a slow breath. Ellie matched her. Sniff. Whoooo. She looked at the children. They smiled. She looked at the sky. Big, blue, calm.

Ellie stepped up. Her feet felt steady. “Hello,” she said, her voice small but clear. “My name is Ellie. I have a lullaby from my family.”

She sang the song her grandma sang at bedtime. It was soft and gentle, like moonlight. “La-lu-la, la-lu-la.” Her trunk swayed. Her ears fluttered. The car ride hum was still in her belly, a steady mmmmmm that made her brave.

When she finished, the children clapped, clap-clap-clap! The drums joined in, dum-dum-dum, like a happy heartbeat.

A girl with braids said, “I liked your soft song. It made me feel calm.”

A boy with a bright hat said, “Your voice is small and special.”

Ellie felt warm and tall inside. “Thank you,” she said. “Can you teach me your drum beat? And your kite dance? And your maraca shake?”

“Yes!” they all said.

They played together. Ellie learned the drum, dum-da-dum. She danced with the kite, swish-swoosh. She shook the maracas, chika-chika. She taught the lullaby, la-lu-la. They shared smiles, claps, and kind words. Ellie saw that the world was big, and also close, right here in their hands and hearts.

On the drive home, the car hummed, mmmmmm. The air smelled like popcorn crumbs and sweet mango. Ellie leaned back, seat belt snug.

“How do you feel?” Mama asked.

“Brave,” Ellie said. “My voice is mine. It might be small, but it is strong when I breathe and try.”

Papa smiled. “What did you learn about our world?”

“We are different and the same,” Ellie said. “We can share and learn from each other.”

“And what is your plan when worries visit?” Lila asked.

Ellie held her tummy and grinned. “Sniff, whoooo. Kind words. One small brave step.”

Everyone cheered softly, yay.

Ellie looked out at the sunset sky, pink and gold. She whispered to herself, “I am Ellie. I can do hard things, one breath at a time.”

The End.
