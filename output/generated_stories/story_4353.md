# Thank You, Little Hooves

In a cozy, playful living room, a gentle deer fawn named Luma stood on a soft rug with tiny white dots. The rug felt warm on Luma’s small hooves. Sunlight made little squares on the floor. A breeze whispered through a cracked window and smelled like wet grass after rain.

“Today is a big day,” Luma said softly. “I want to say thank you to the world.”

Luma’s friend, a round, jolly globe pillow named Glo-Glo, sat on the couch. Glo-Glo had bright blue seas and green lands stitched with tiny threads. When Luma pressed a hoof on the soft world, the pillow wiggled. “Hello, Luma,” Glo-Glo hummed. “Where shall we go? The world is wide.”

Luma smiled. “First, we get ready.” Luma gently put on a little scarf with tiny sunflowers. “Thank you, scarf, for keeping my neck warm,” Luma whispered. Luma put a small water bottle in a bag. “Thank you, water, for helping me drink.” Luma opened the door a little and peeked out at the yard. Fresh air brushed Luma’s nose. “Thank you, air, for filling my chest,” Luma said, taking a deep breath.

From the doorway, Luma heard soft footsteps. Mama Deer peeked into the living room. “Good morning, Luma,” Mama said. “Where will your little hooves wander today?”

“Just outside,” Luma said. “I want to play and say thank you.”

Mama nodded. “That sounds kind. Remember your boundaries: stay where I can see you, and if someone says stop, you stop too.”

Luma nodded. “I will.”

Outside, the yard was bright and bouncy with sounds. Chirp-chirp! A bird sang from the fence. Luma lifted a hoof and waved. “Thank you, Bird, for your morning song. It makes my heart dance.”

A butterfly drifted low, its wings like tiny painted fans. Luma watched, careful and still. “Hello, Butterfly,” Luma said softly. “May I watch you?” The butterfly fluttered close, then away. Luma did not chase. “Thank you, Butterfly, for showing me your colors. I will give you space.”

In the grass, Luma found a smooth stone, warm from the sun. “Thank you, Stone, for being round and calm,” Luma whispered, pressing it to a cheek. It felt cool under the warm top, and that made Luma giggle.

Glo-Glo rolled onto the porch step with a soft flop. “Luma,” the globe pillow sang, “let’s look at the big world. See the bright deserts and snowy tops? The world has many homes.”

Luma touched a green stitch. “So many places,” Luma said. “Thank you, world, for holding us all.”

From the side gate, a neighbor child named Mei peeked in with a red ball. “Hi, Luma!” Mei called. “Do you want to roll the ball?”

Luma looked at Mama Deer, who was watering the flowers. Mama smiled. “You may play near the flowers.”

Luma trotted to Mei. “Yes! Let’s roll.”

Mei placed the ball between them. “Ready?”

“Ready.”

They rolled the ball back and forth. Thump, roll. Thump, roll. The ball made a bouncy sound and felt a little sticky from morning dew. Luma giggled when it tickled a hoof.

A small squirrel scampered close, nose twitching. “Eek-eek,” the squirrel squeaked. It stared at the red ball. Mei reached out a hand, but Luma remembered Mama’s words. “Hello, Squirrel,” Luma said gently. “We will not touch you. This is your space.” The squirrel sniffed, flicked its tail, and scrambled up the tree. “Thank you, Squirrel, for showing us how to be quick,” Luma added.

After a while, Luma felt warm and thirsty. “Pause,” Luma said. Luma sipped water. “Thank you, water, for cool sips.” Luma placed the bottle back carefully. Mei wiped her forehead. “Hot!” she said with a laugh.

Glo-Glo wiggled again. “Luma, do you know that children in many places drink water too? In some places, water must be carried far. In some places, it is very cold. In others, it is very hot.” The globe pillow’s thread shimmered in the sun.

Luma’s eyes grew wide. “The world is big,” Luma whispered. “I am thankful for my water, and for the rain that fills rivers.”

Mei nodded. “I’m thankful for shade.”

“I am thankful for you,” Luma told Mei. “Thank you for rolling the ball with me.”

Mei smiled. “Thank you for asking the squirrel first. That was kind.”

Together they watched clouds drift like soft sheep. Luma pointed. “That cloud looks like a boat.” Mei pointed to another. “That one looks like a flower.”

Mama Deer clapped softly from the porch. “Time to come in, my explorers.”

Back in the playful living room, the rug felt extra snuggly. The room smelled like clean soap and leaves. Luma and Mei sat with Glo-Glo between them. Luma closed small eyes for a moment and spoke in a sweet, small voice.

“Thank you, home, for being safe and soft. Thank you, friends, for playing. Thank you, sky, for clouds. Thank you, water, for sips. Thank you, butterfly, for colors. Thank you, bird, for songs. Thank you, squirrel, for showing us space. Thank you, world, for many people and places. I will be gentle with you.”

Mama Deer listened. “What did you learn today, Luma?” she asked.

Luma cuddled Glo-Glo and smiled. “I learned to say thank you. I learned to play outside and give space. I learned the world is big, and I can be kind in my little spot.”

Mei nodded. “Me too.”

They all took one more breath of the sweet, leafy air from the open window. The sun warmed their faces. Their hearts felt full.

The End.
