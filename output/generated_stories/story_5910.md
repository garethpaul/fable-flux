# Ellie’s Gentle Laundry Day

Ellie was a gentle elephant calf with soft gray ears and a tiny, swishy tail. One sunny morning, she padded into the laundry room with her big, careful feet. The floor felt cool and smooth. The room smelled like clean soap and fresh air. The washing machine hummed, “Whirr, whirr,” like a sleepy bee. The dryer made a warm, whooshy sound.

Mama said, “Good morning, Ellie. We have a laundry adventure today.”

Ellie’s eyes sparkled. “An adventure? In here?” She touched the soft basket with her trunk. It was full of little socks and bright shirts from many friends in their building—tiny red socks, long blue scarves, and a rainbow towel with stars.

“Today,” said Mama, “we help wash the building’s play-clothes from the shared playroom. We share water and power carefully, so we help the Earth.”

Ellie nodded. “We take care of our planet,” she said. “That is kind.”

Just then, their friends peeked in. Pika the penguin waddled with a striped scarf. Tavi the tortoise wore a shiny bell. Lina the lamb bounced in with a fluffy sweater. Niko the tiny newt waved a spotted sleeve. They all looked different, and that made Ellie smile. “Everyone’s clothes are special,” she said.

“May I wash my scarf first?” asked Pika.

“Can I push the button?” asked Lina.

“Is it okay if I climb on the basket?” asked Niko, already hopping closer.

Ellie felt a flutter in her tummy. So many friends. So many questions. She wanted to help. She wanted to be kind. But the basket was close to her feet and trunk, and Niko was very near.

Ellie took a deep breath. The soap smelled like lemons. She remembered something Mama had said before: “Your body is your space. Your voice helps others know your space.”

Ellie raised her trunk and spoke softly, “Friends, I like you near me, but please give me a little space. My body needs room.”

Niko stopped and blinked. “Oh! Is this too close?” He wiggled back a tiny step.

“Thank you,” Ellie said, smiling. “That feels better.”

Mama nodded. “Great words, Ellie. You used your voice to share your boundary.”

“What is a boundary?” asked Tavi, tilting his head.

Ellie thought. “A boundary is like an invisible line that keeps our bodies safe and comfy,” she said. “We can say yes or no to touches and closeness.”

“Oh,” said Lina. “May I give you a hug, Ellie?”

Ellie looked at Lina’s open arms and her bright, hopeful eyes. She noticed her own tummy—did it feel calm? Yes. “Yes, Lina. A gentle hug, please.” Lina gave a soft, quick hug, and Ellie felt warm and happy.

Pika flapped his flippers. “I like that. May I stand by your side, Ellie?”

“Yes,” Ellie said, “by my side is good.”

With everyone settled, they sorted the clothes into piles. Ellie’s trunk touched each piece gently. “Lights here, darks there,” she said. “And we will wash full loads to save water. That is good for the Earth.” She showed the friends how to close the lid softly. “Clunk,” it said, not “slam.”

Niko pointed at a big bottle. “Is that soap?”

“Yes,” said Mama. “It is plant soap. It is gentle for us and gentle for rivers.”

Ellie held the small scoop. “One scoop is enough,” she said. “Too much soap is not kind to water.” The soap sprinkled like white snowflakes, and a lemony smell floated up. Everyone smiled.

“Can I push the start button now?” Lina asked again.

“Let’s take turns,” Ellie said. “We can ask and wait. Pika, would you like to go first?”

Pika blinked. “Thank you, Ellie. May I push the button now?”

“Yes,” Ellie said. Pika pressed it. The machine began to sing, “Whirr, whirr, swish.”

While they waited, Ellie laid a soft mat on the floor. “This is our waiting spot,” she said. “We can sit here and keep our bodies calm.” Tavi rang his bell gently, “ting-ting,” and everyone giggled.

Niko scooted close to Ellie’s trunk again, excited by the spinning clothes. Ellie felt that flutter in her tummy again. She put her trunk on her heart and said, “Niko, please move back a little. I need more space.”

Niko nodded. “Okay. Thank you for telling me.” He scooted back. Ellie felt safe and proud.

When the wash was done, they moved the clothes to the dryer. “Fluff on low,” Mama said. “That saves energy.” The dryer hummed warm air, “whoosh, whoosh.” Everyone took turns pressing buttons and saying, “May I?” and “Yes” or “No, not yet.”

At last, the clothes were dry. Ellie folded the rainbow towel by smoothing the star corners. It felt soft and warm. She showed Pika how to roll socks into pairs. “Like a little hug,” she said, “but only if the socks say yes.” Everyone laughed.

“Thank you for helping,” Mama said. “You were careful with water and power. You used kind words. And you respected each other’s boundaries.”

Ellie’s heart felt big. “I am grateful for my friends,” she said. “We are all different. We all have space. We all can ask. We all can say yes or no.”

Lina bleated, “I liked asking first.”

Pika said, “I liked waiting my turn.”

Niko said, “I liked when you told me your boundary. It helped me know what to do.”

Tavi nodded. “I liked saving water.”

Ellie wrapped the rainbow towel around all of them like a soft, cozy circle. “We can be close, and we can be comfy,” she said. “We listen to our bodies. We listen to our friends. That is kindness.”

They placed the clean clothes back in the basket, neat and tidy. The laundry room smelled fresh, and the humming faded into quiet. Ellie felt calm and proud.

Today’s lesson, Ellie thought, is this: My body is my space. I can use my voice. I can be kind to myself, to friends, and to the Earth.

The End.
