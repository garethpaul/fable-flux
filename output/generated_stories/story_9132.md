# Ollie Otter’s Car-Snack Mission

Ollie the playful otter buckled into his car seat with a happy wiggle. The family car smelled like warm sunshine and orange slices. The seat felt soft and snuggly. Mama Otter started the engine, and the car hummed like a sleepy bee.

“We’re going to the lake,” Mama said. “Please keep your things tidy, Ollie.”

“I can do it!” Ollie chirped. He patted his backpack. It was stuffed with toys, crayons, and snacks. He liked to bring everything.

The car rolled along. Trees whooshed by, and Ollie’s whiskers twitched with excitement. He pulled out his favorite blue crayon. “I want to draw a boat,” he said. The crayon made soft scratchy sounds on his paper. Scratch, scratch, swish.

Then Ollie wanted a snack. He opened a bag of crunchy crackers. Crunch, crunch! “Mmm,” he said, crumbs tickling his nose.

Soon, the blue crayon slid under his leg. The cracker bag tipped on the seat. His toy boat bounced to the floor with a little thump. Ollie giggled and reached for everything at once. The car bumped over a tiny hill—boop!—and the crackers rained like little stars.

Uh-oh.

“Ollie,” Mama said gently, peeking in the mirror, “look at your seat.”

Ollie looked. Crumbs everywhere. Crayons rolling. The toy boat stuck by the door. His tummy felt twisty. His paws felt busy and fidgety.

“I didn’t mean to,” he whispered.

“It’s okay,” Mama said in a calm, warm voice. “We can solve this. Let’s use our otter brains.”

“How?” Ollie asked, eyes wide.

“We’ll stop at the next safe place,” Mama said, “and make a plan.”

They pulled into a quiet rest stop. The car clicked to a stop. Ollie took a deep breath. In… and out. His shoulders softened.

Mama turned to him. “First, let’s see the problem. What do you notice?”

Ollie looked carefully. “Crumbs on the seat. Crayons rolling. My boat on the floor. I can’t find my napkin.”

“Good noticing,” Mama said. “Second, let’s think of ideas. What could help?”

Ollie tapped his chin. Tap, tap. “Maybe… we put crayons in a box? Crackers in a cup? Toys in a bag?”

Mama smiled. “Great ideas. Third, let’s choose and try.”

She handed him three small things: a little box with a clicky lid, a cup with a lid and straw, and a soft pouch with a zipper. Click, slurp, zip—Ollie liked those sounds.

Ollie started to work. He brushed the crumbs into his paw and then into a tiny paper bag. Swipe, swipe. He picked up the crayons and set them in the clicky box. Click! He placed the crackers in the cup and snapped the lid. Snap! He found his toy boat and tucked it into the pouch. Zip!

“Ollie,” Mama said, “how does your body feel now?”

Ollie paused. He wiggled his toes. “Calm,” he said. “My tummy is not twisty.”

“That’s self-regulation,” Mama said softly. “You used your breath and made a plan.”

Ollie’s whiskers perked up. “Self… regu… la-tion,” he tried, giggling. The word felt big and bouncy.

Mama showed him the car pocket on the door. “This pocket is for your pouch. The cup goes in the cup holder. The box sits by your seat belt. Everything has a home.”

“Oooh,” said Ollie. “Homes for my things!” He gently placed each item in its spot. Home, home, home.

Back on the road, the car hummed again. Ollie colored with one crayon at a time. When he finished, he dropped it in the clicky box. Click. He took a sip from his cup. Slurp. He put it back in the holder. Plunk. He played with his toy boat, then zipped it away. Zip.

A small bump came—boop! Nothing spilled. Ollie grinned so big his cheeks puffed.

“I did it!” he cheered.

“You solved the problem,” Mama said. “You organized your space.”

Ollie felt proud. His chest felt warm, like sunshine. He looked out the window at the blue, sparkly water ahead.

At the lake, Mama opened the car door. “Look,” she said. “No mess!”

Ollie nodded. “I like our plan. First, see the problem. Second, think of ideas. Third, choose and try.”

“And last,” Mama added, “check how it worked and keep going.”

Ollie clapped his paws. “I can use the plan again!”

They splashed at the lake. The water was cool and tickly on Ollie’s paws. When it was time to go, Ollie packed his towel in the bag, his boat in the pouch, and his crayon box in the backpack. Click. Zip. Pat.

On the ride home, Ollie hummed with the car. He felt calm, clever, and cozy. He whispered to himself, “Homes for my things. Calm breaths. Try my plan.”

Mama squeezed his paw at a red light. “What did you learn today?”

Ollie smiled. “When there’s a mess, I can stop, breathe, and make a plan. I can put things in their homes. That helps my body feel calm.”

“That’s problem-solving,” Mama said. “And that’s organization.”

Ollie’s eyes grew sleepy. The car hummed, and the sun painted golden dots on his fur. He yawned a tiny otter yawn.

“I’m a tidy, clever otter,” he murmured.

“Yes,” Mama said. “You are.”

The End.
