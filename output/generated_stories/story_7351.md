# Polly Paints the Bubbles

Polly was a creative parrot with bright green feathers and a rainbow tail that shimmered in the light. One morning, Polly hopped into the bathroom. The tiles were cool on her feet. Drip, drip, went the faucet. The room smelled like lemon soap. It was bath time.

“Polly, today we wash, rinse, and tidy,” said Mama’s voice from the hallway. “Then we play.”

Polly loved to make art and tell stories. She saw foamy bubbles in the tub, a soft blue towel, and tiny cups for rinsing. “Ooh!” she chirped. “The bubbles look like clouds. I can make a bubble painting story!”

She dipped a wing in the bubbles and swirled them into shapes. “Once upon a time,” Polly sang, “a bubble dragon chased a bubble moon!” She giggled as the bubbles tickled her feathers.

But bath time had a job: wash, rinse, and tidy. Polly forgot. She stacked cups like a castle. She tapped the soap like a drum. Tap-tap-tap! Splash! A little wave went over the edge. Water slid onto the floor with a cold splash.

Polly’s heart did a flop. Her tummy felt wiggly. She looked at the wet floor and the messy bubbles. “Oh no,” she whispered. Her feathers drooped. “I was supposed to wash, rinse, and tidy. I didn’t finish my task.”

She heard footsteps. Tap, tap. Mama peeked in. “Polly, what happened here?”

Polly felt her cheeks grow warm under her feathers. She wanted to hide behind the towel. She wanted to say, “The bubble dragon did it!” But Polly took a slow, deep breath, like a soft hoot. She put a wing on her chest.

“I feel worried,” Polly said quietly. “I made a mess. I forgot my job. I was making a story with bubbles.”

Mama knelt and smiled gently. “Thank you for telling me how you feel. That’s brave. Feelings are okay. Messes can be fixed.”

Polly’s worry loosened a little. “I want to make it right,” she said. “Can I fix it?”

“Yes,” said Mama. “Let’s use our plan. First, we name the feeling. You did that: worried. Second, we breathe. Third, we fix and finish the task.”

Polly nodded. “Breathe in like smelling a flower.” She sniffed the lemon soap. “Breathe out like blowing a bubble.” She blew, and a tiny bubble wobbled and popped. Pop!

“Now,” Mama said, “how can we make amends?”

“I can wipe the water,” said Polly. “I can tidy the cups. I can wash and rinse properly.”

Together they pressed a towel on the floor. Squish. The water soaked up. Polly stacked the cups neatly on the shelf. Clack, clack. She put the soap back in its dish. Plop.

“Time to wash,” Polly said. She made a little washing song. “Wash, wash, wings and toes. Rinse, rinse, beak and nose.” Warm water ran over her feathers. It felt like a hug. The bubbles smelled fresh and clean.

As she rinsed, Polly felt proud and calm. Her tummy was steady now. “Mama,” she said, “my worried feeling is smaller. It floated away like a bubble.”

Mama nodded. “You noticed your feeling, you told the truth, and you finished your task. That is wonderful.”

Polly looked at the shiny faucet. She saw her round eyes in the metal. “I want to make amends more,” she said. “I want to share a story that helps.”

Mama sat on the bath mat. “I’d love to hear it.”

Polly puffed up her chest and began. “Once upon a time, a bubble dragon zoomed too fast. It splashed the moon. The dragon felt worried. It said, ‘I’m sorry, Moon. I didn’t mean to splash you.’ The dragon took a big breath. Then it used a soft cloud to dry the moon and put all the stars back in rows. The moon smiled. The end.”

Mama clapped softly. Clap, clap. “That story shows making amends. That means fixing what went wrong and trying to do better next time.”

Polly nodded. “Next time, I will tell my story after I wash, rinse, and tidy. Tasks first, then tales.”

They looked around the bathroom. It was clean. The towel was hung straight. The cups were in a line. The floor was dry and warm now.

“Polly,” Mama said, “how do you feel?”

Polly placed a wing on her chest. “I feel proud. I feel calm. I feel ready.”

“Ready for what?” Mama asked with a smile.

“Ready for bubble story time—on a towel, with small splashes.” Polly giggled.

They spread a towel on the floor and made tiny, careful bubbles in a bowl. Polly whispered, “Once upon a time, a brave parrot listened to her feelings, finished her tasks, and made amends. She learned that feelings help us choose what to do.”

Mama added, “And she learned that telling the truth helps hearts feel light.”

Polly fluffed her feathers. “And now, we play!”

They laughed. The bubbles shimmered like little rainbows. Pop, pop, pop went the tiny bubbles, and Polly’s happy heart beat in a steady, gentle rhythm.

The End.
