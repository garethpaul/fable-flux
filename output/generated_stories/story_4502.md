# Twirl in the Guest Room

Toby was a shy turtle with a smooth green shell and gentle eyes. He moved slowly and liked quiet corners. One day, Toby visited his cousin Tilly’s house. Tilly showed him a cozy guest room where he could rest. The room smelled like fresh soap and clean blankets. Sunlight made warm squares on the soft rug. A small music player sat on a dresser, and a basket of scarves rested by the bed.

“Make yourself comfy, Toby,” said Tilly. “I’ll bring snacks soon.” She smiled and went to the kitchen.

Toby looked around. He touched a blue scarf. It felt silky and cool. He listened. He heard a tiny hum from the music player. His heart made a little thump-thump. Toby whispered, “New place, new sounds. I feel a bit wiggly inside.”

Toby remembered that when he felt wiggly, it helped to name his feelings. He took a deep breath. “I feel shy,” he said softly. “My tummy feels tight. My legs feel slow.” He put a hand on his shell and felt its smooth curve. “Hello, feelings.”

Just then, the music player clicked, and gentle, bouncy music filled the guest room. Ding-ding-ding went a tiny bell in the song. Tap-tap-tap went a soft drum. The sound was warm and bright, like sunshine.

Toby blinked. “I want to dance,” he said, “but I feel shy. What if I look silly?” His cheeks felt warm. He tucked his head a little into his shell.

He took another breath in and out. “I can try small,” he whispered. “Small is okay.” Toby looked for helpful things. He was a resourceful turtle. He saw the scarves, the rug, and a big mirror on the closet door.

Toby chose a yellow scarf. It smelled like lemon and felt like a cloud. He tied it gently around his neck. “This scarf can be my sunshine,” he said. He picked up a green scarf too. “This one can be my brave leaf.”

He stood on the rug, where his feet felt safe and warm. He wiggled one toe. Tap. He wiggled the other toe. Tap-tap. “Little steps,” he told himself. “I can start with little steps.”

The music dinged and tapped. Toby swayed side to side. Sway, sway. He made small circles with his hands. Swish went the scarves, like leaves in the breeze. He peeked at himself in the mirror and gave a tiny smile.

“I see you, shy Toby,” he said. “You can dance a tiny dance.”

The door opened a crack, and Tilly peeked in with a plate of apple slices. “Oh!” she said softly. “I love your tiny dance.”

Toby’s heart did another thump-thump. He felt shy again. He hid his face a little. “I feel scared, Tilly,” he said. “I don’t want to be silly.”

Tilly nodded. “Thank you for telling me,” she said. “Your feelings are welcome here. Would you like me to watch, or should I close the door a bit?”

Toby thought. He put a hand on his belly. It still felt tight, but not as tight. “Please close it a bit,” he said. “But can you listen? The music is nice.”

“Of course,” said Tilly. She set the apple slices on the dresser and closed the door to a tiny crack. “I’m right here.”

Toby took a slow breath. In. Out. He felt the soft rug under his feet. He heard the dinging bell and the tapping drum. He smelled the sweet apples and lemony scarf. He felt his shy feeling and his brave feeling together, like two friends holding hands.

“I will make a turtle dance,” he said. “A slow, steady dance.”

He bent his knees and swayed like a little boat. He stretched his arms like wide turtle flippers. Swish went the scarves, whoosh like wind. He turned in a small circle. Twirl. He peeked from his shell and smiled bigger. The mirror showed a careful, kind dancer.

Tilly’s voice came softly through the door. “I hear swishes! It sounds wonderful.”

Toby tried a new move. He tapped three times. Tap, tap, tap. He made a shape like a hill with his arms, then a tiny hop. “I did it!” he laughed. His shy feeling was still there, but it was not in charge. His brave feeling was sitting next to it, holding it gently.

When the song ended, Toby bowed to the mirror. He opened the door. Tilly clapped quietly. “Bravo, Toby. How do you feel now?”

Toby touched his belly. “Softer,” he said. “I felt shy. I named it. I breathed. I started small. I used scarves and the soft rug. I made a turtle dance.”

Tilly smiled and handed him an apple slice. “That is very smart and very brave,” she said. “You were resourceful. You found what helped you.”

Toby crunched the apple. It was sweet and loud. Crunch! He giggled. “Next time, will you dance with me?”

“I would love to,” said Tilly.

Together, they pressed the button. The music began again. This time, Toby took Tilly’s hand. Sway, sway. Tap, tap. Twirl. The guest room was full of soft light, happy sounds, and two turtles who knew their feelings and used them well.

The End.
