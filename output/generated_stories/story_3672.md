# Bubbles, Buttons, and Beaver Brains

Benny the busy beaver loved to learn. He wore a tiny red cap and carried a small blue notebook. Today, Benny needed clean towels. He padded into the laundry room with his big flat tail thump-thump-thumping on the floor.

“Wow,” Benny whispered. “So many buttons!” The washing machine hummed like a soft drum. The dryer door clicked like a beak. The air smelled warm and soapy, like bubbles hugging a cloud.

Benny set his basket down. “I can learn how to wash,” he said. “Learning makes my brain grow!” He tapped his head gently and smiled.

But the washing machine had words and symbols all over it. Some words were big. Some words were small. Some words were not words Benny knew. He squinted. “Cotton?” he read slowly. “Del-i-cate?” He tilted his head. “What does that mean?”

He pressed a button. Beep. The screen glowed with new words. “Oh dear,” said Benny. “This is a lot.” He felt his tummy wiggle-wobble with worry.

Just then, he heard a little chirp. Pip the parrot peeked in from the window. “Hola!” chirped Pip. “That means hello!”

Benny waved. “Hi, Pip. I want to wash my towels. But these words are tricky.”

Pip hopped closer. “We can learn together,” Pip said. “I know words from many places. Hola is Spanish. Bonjour is French. Ni hao is Chinese. Learning words helps us be good friends to everyone.”

Benny’s ears perked up. “Global friends,” he said. “I like that.”

Mrs. Badger shuffled in with a laundry cart. “Need a paw?” she asked kindly. “When I do not know, I read the pictures first.” She pointed to a little shirt symbol and a little feather symbol.

Benny nodded. “Pictures help.” He looked. The feather meant soft. The shirt meant clothes. He touched a towel. It felt thick and fluffy. “My towels are strong, not feathery.”

Pip fluffed his wings. “In Spanish, towel is toalla,” he chirped. “Can you say toalla?”

“Toh-AH-yah,” Benny tried. He giggled when the word tickled his tongue.

“Great try,” said Mrs. Badger. “That is a growth mindset! We keep trying, even when it is hard.”

Benny took out his blue notebook. He drew a little towel and wrote: toalla. He smiled. “Learning new words helps me help others,” he said. “If a friend says toalla, I will know!”

He checked the machine again. “I still don’t know which setting,” he said.

Pip pointed to the big word. “Cotton,” Pip read. “Cotton is strong. Towels are often cotton.”

Mrs. Badger added, “Use warm water for towels. Not too hot, not too cold.”

Benny pressed the warm button. Beep! He poured in soap. Glug, glug, glug. A sweet, clean smell floated up like tiny flowers.

“Uh-oh,” Benny said. The soap suds puffed up. They bubbled like little clouds. “Is this too much soap?”

Mrs. Badger tapped the cap. “A little capful, dear. Not the whole river!”

Benny laughed. “I got excited. I’ll remember: just a little.” He took a deep breath. “It’s okay to make a mistake. I can fix it.” He scooped some soap back into the bottle, slow and steady.

Pip clapped his wings. “Muy bien! That means very good!”

Benny pressed start. The washer swished. Swish, swish, swish. He watched the round window. The towels spun like a gentle dance. Benny felt calm. He listened to the hum-hum-hum and the water whoosh.

While they waited, Mrs. Badger showed Benny the care tag on a towel. “These little pictures tell a story,” she said. “See the square with a circle? That means dryer. See the triangle? That is about bleach.”

Benny traced the shapes. “Like a map,” he said. “I can read maps of clothes!”

Pip nodded. “Signs and symbols help everyone, no matter what language they speak. That’s good for global friends.”

When the washer beeped, Benny moved the towels to the dryer. They were heavy and wet. Drip, drip. He giggled when a cold drop splashed his nose. “Brr! Cold like a river,” he said.

He turned the dial to warm and pressed start. The dryer rumbled. Whoom. Warm air puffed out. It smelled toasty, like sunshine on a rock.

While the towels dried, Benny practiced words with Pip. “Hello! Hola! Bonjour! Ni hao!” Benny said, tapping his notebook. He drew little flags and big smiling faces. “I want to welcome friends,” he said. “Learning words is kind.”

The dryer beeped. Ding! Benny opened the door. A wave of soft heat hugged his cheeks. He pulled out a towel. It was fluffy and cozy. “Perfect,” he said. He folded carefully, corner to corner. His tail thumped happily.

Mrs. Badger smiled. “You solved the problem. You asked for help, you read the signs, you tried again.”

Pip chirped, “And you learned new words!”

Benny beamed. “I learned to read pictures and tags. I learned to use a little soap. I learned to say toalla. Learning helps me help myself and others. I can learn more every day.”

He put one towel aside. “This one is for our laundry room. For anyone who needs it,” he said. “We can share.”

“That’s global citizenship,” said Mrs. Badger. “Caring for our community and our world.”

Benny hugged his notebook. “My brain feels bigger,” he said. “When I don’t know, I can ask, look, and try. I can learn new languages to be a kind friend.”

He picked up his basket. “Thank you,” he told his helpers.

“De nada,” said Pip. “You’re welcome!”

Benny waved. “See you next wash day! I’ll bring more words.”

He trotted away, tail thump-thump-thumping, feeling proud, warm, and ready to learn again.

The End.
