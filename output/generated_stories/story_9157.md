# Big Breaths on the Front Porch

On a sunny morning, a gentle elephant calf named Emi sat on the peaceful front porch of her home. The porch boards felt warm under her soft feet. A light breeze tickled her ears. Emi could smell jasmine flowers and fresh mango slices from the kitchen. It was a quiet, happy day.

Emi had a small paint set and a big sheet of paper. She wanted to paint a rainbow for her Grandma. “I will make it bright and smooth,” Emi said. She dipped her brush. Swish, swish. Red, orange, yellow. The colors shone in the sun.

Her little trunk hummed a tune. “La-la-la,” Emi sang softly. She felt calm. She heard birds chirp, “Cheep-cheep,” from the porch rail. A cricket made a tiny “zip-zip” in the grass.

Then her friend Tiko, a curious turtle, climbed onto the porch. “Hi, Emi!” Tiko called. His shell tapped the step. Tap, tap.

“Hi, Tiko,” Emi said with a smile. “I am painting a rainbow for Grandma.”

Tiko peered close. “Wow! Can I try a brush?” he asked.

Emi’s tummy felt tight. She liked to do her own painting. But she wanted to be open and kind. She took a slow breath. In through the trunk. Out through the trunk. “Yes,” she said. “We can share. I will show you.”

Tiko dipped the brush, but he was too excited. He scooped up a big glob of blue paint. Drip! Drip! Splat! The paint splashed across Emi’s rainbow. It covered the yellow and the orange.

Emi’s eyes got wide. Her cheeks felt hot. Her heart thumped. Thump-thump. “Oh!” she cried. She wanted to stomp. She wanted to shout. Her body felt like a storm cloud.

Tiko’s face drooped. “I’m sorry, Emi,” he said. “I didn’t mean to splat. It was an accident.”

Emi could feel the big feelings rising like a wave. She remembered a song her Grandma taught her. “When you feel a storm inside, pause and take your breath ride.”

Emi sat back on the warm porch. She put her trunk on her belly. “I need a moment,” she said honestly. “I feel mad.”

“That’s okay,” Tiko whispered. He sat very still.

Emi took one slow breath in. She counted, “One… two… three.” She let the air out slow, like blowing a bubble. She did it again. In… two… three. Out… two… three. The birds kept chirping. The breeze kept kissing her ears.

The mad feeling got smaller. Emi felt the tightness melt, like ice in the sun. She hummed, “La-la-la,” very softly to help her calm body. “I can choose my calm,” she told herself. “I can use my tools.”

After three big breaths, Emi looked at Tiko. “Thank you for waiting,” she said. “I feel better now.”

Tiko nodded. “I’m still sorry,” he said. “I should have asked how to use the brush.”

“Thank you for being honest,” Emi said. “I also needed to say I was mad. Saying it helped.”

Emi touched the paper. The blue splat looked like a pond. She tilted her head. “Maybe,” she said slowly, “we can try something new.”

“What do you mean?” asked Tiko.

“Let’s be open-minded,” Emi said. “Let’s see the splat as part of the picture. We can turn it into something good.”

Tiko blinked. “Like… fish in the pond?” he asked.

Emi smiled. “Yes! And we can add green grass around it. The rainbow can curve over the pond.”

“That sounds fun,” Tiko said. “Can I try again, but carefully?”

“Yes,” Emi said. “First, we make a plan. We will use little paint. Dip, tap, and brush. And we take turns.”

Emi showed Tiko. “Dip a little. Tap the extra paint. Brush soft.” Swish. Swish. Tiko copied her. His brush made tiny fish. Wiggle-wiggle. Emi painted green grass with gentle strokes. The porch smelled like peaches now, as Mama set a bowl nearby. “Snack time,” Mama called. “And I see beautiful teamwork.”

Emi giggled. “Thank you, Mama.” She turned to Tiko. “How about we add a bridge?” she asked.

“Good idea,” Tiko said with a grin. He took initiative and drew a small brown line. Emi added tiny flowers with pink dots. Dot, dot, dot. They heard the wind chimes sing, “Ting-ting-ting.”

Soon, the picture showed a rainbow arched over a blue pond, with fish, grass, and a little bridge. It was even better than before. Emi felt proud and calm. Tiko looked proud too.

Grandma stepped onto the porch. Her scarf rustled like leaves. “Oh my,” she said. “What a lovely scene.”

Emi’s ears wiggled. “Grandma, at first I felt very mad when the paint splashed,” she said honestly. “I told Tiko how I felt. I took big breaths. Then we made a plan and used small careful strokes. We turned a mistake into part of the art.”

Grandma smiled. “That is wonderful self-regulation,” she said. “You noticed your feelings. You paused. You breathed. You chose kind words and a new idea.”

Tiko nodded. “And I learned to be careful and to ask. I said sorry and tried again.”

Grandma hugged them both. Her shawl felt soft like clouds. “This rainbow pond will always remind me of your calm hearts,” she said.

Emi looked at the picture. “When I feel like a storm,” she said, “I can stop, breathe, and choose.”

“And we can be honest and open to new ideas,” Tiko added.

They all sat on the peaceful front porch, eating sweet peach slices, listening to birds and wind chimes, and feeling the warm boards under their feet. Emi felt the calm breeze inside her, too. She knew she could find her calm again and again.

The End.
