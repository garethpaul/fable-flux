# Tiko Listens and Learns

Tiko was a gentle elephant calf with big soft ears and a curious heart. Today, Tiko stepped into the musical practice room for the first time. The room smelled like polished wood and lemon oil. Sunlight slid across shiny drums and golden cymbals. Little bells winked on a shelf. The floor felt smooth and cool under Tiko’s feet.

“Wow,” Tiko whispered. His breath made a warm puff in the quiet air. “So many sounds to learn.”

On a low table sat a drum with a round, tan skin. Tiko tapped it with one toe. Tap. Tap-tap. The drum answered with a friendly thump. Tiko’s ears wiggled. He liked that sound!

“I want to play everything,” Tiko said, eyes wide. He reached for a tiny triangle that hung from a hook. Ting! It rang like a star. Then he brushed a row of chimes. Shhh-shing! The notes shimmered like rain.

From the corner, a small mouse named Miri peeked out. She wore a blue ribbon. “Hello, Tiko,” Miri squeaked. “We share this room. We listen. We learn. We take turns.”

Tiko smiled. “Hi, Miri! I want to learn fast!”

Miri’s whiskers twitched. “Fast can be fun. But careful is kind. We can learn more when we listen.”

Tiko nodded, but his feet still wanted to move. He tried the cymbal. Clang! Then he patted the drum again. Thump-thump! He shook a maraca. Rattle-rattle!

Soon, all the sounds mixed together. The room felt too loud. Tiko’s ears drooped. “Oh,” he said softly. “It’s jumbly.”

Miri covered her ears with her tiny paws. “It is jumbly,” she agreed. “The sounds are fighting for space.”

Tiko’s tummy felt wiggly and unsure. “I didn’t mean to make a mess,” he said. “I just wanted to learn.”

Miri scampered onto the drum and looked up at him kindly. “You are curious. That is good,” she said. “Curiosity helps us learn. But learning needs a plan.”

“A plan?” Tiko asked. He liked the word. It felt calm.

“Yes,” said Miri. “We can choose one thing. We can listen. We can practice. We can finish our task. Then we can try another.”

Tiko took a slow breath. In… and out. His big ears relaxed. “Okay. Let’s make a plan.”

Miri pointed to the drum with her tiny paw. “Let’s start here. First, we learn soft and loud. Can you tap soft?”

Tiko touched the drum like a whisper. Tap… tap…

Miri smiled. “Good listening! Now loud?”

Tiko lifted his foot and made a bigger thump. THUMP!

The drum boomed, and then the room grew quiet again. Tiko smiled back. “I heard the difference!”

Miri nodded. “You learned it. Now a pattern. Try: soft-soft-loud, soft-soft-loud.”

Tiko tried. Tap, tap, THUMP. Tap, tap, THUMP. The pattern made a little song. Tiko’s tail swished with joy.

“This feels nice,” he said. “Calm and happy.”

Miri clapped her tiny paws. “You completed a task. That is learning.”

Just then, the door creaked. A raccoon named Rafi rolled in a tall stand with a shiny xylophone. “Practice time!” he said. “I need the drum too.”

Tiko froze. “Oh no. I am still learning. I don’t want to stop.”

Miri held up her paw. “We can solve this. We can talk. We can take turns.”

Tiko looked at Rafi. “I’m learning a pattern. Can I finish one more set? Then you can use the drum.”

Rafi blinked, then nodded. “That sounds fair.”

Tiko did his pattern one more time. Tap, tap, THUMP. Tap, tap, THUMP. He finished with a soft tap. The sound faded like a sigh.

“Thank you,” Rafi said. “Now I can practice my beat. You can try the xylophone.”

Tiko stepped to the xylophone. The bars were smooth and cool. He held the mallet gently. “What is my plan now?”

Miri smiled. “We listen first. Try one bar. Hear its voice.”

Tiko struck a blue bar. Plink. It sounded like a tiny bell. He listened. Then he tried the next one. Plonk. Lower and warm.

He made a little ladder of sounds. Plink, plink, plonk, plonk. He practiced twice. He finished his task. He felt proud.

Soon, the room was full of friendly sounds. Rafi’s drum went thump-thump like a heartbeat. Tiko’s xylophone climbed up and down. Miri shook a small bell—ting! ting!—between their turns. No sounds fought now. They took turns. They listened. They learned.

When they finished, the room was quiet again. The lemon smell was still sweet. Tiko’s heart felt calm and strong.

“I learned to make a plan,” Tiko said. “I learned to listen. I learned to finish a task.”

“And you solved a problem with words,” Miri added. “That is kind and smart.”

Tiko’s ears lifted with pride. “Learning takes time. I can be calm and curious. I can ask for turns. I can practice.”

Miri nodded. “Yes. Little steps make big skills.”

Tiko smiled. “Tomorrow, I will learn another pattern.”

“And I will cheer,” said Miri.

The End.
