# Tumba’s Tumble of Towels

Tumba was a gentle elephant calf with soft, flappy ears and tiny, careful steps. One sunny afternoon, Tumba followed the hum-hum-hum sound to the laundry room. The room was warm and smelled like soap and sunshine. The washing machine whirled. The dryer hummed. Baskets were filled with colorful clothes, soft towels, and neat little socks.

“Oooh,” Tumba whispered, his trunk wiggling. “So many colors! So many textures!”

He touched a fluffy towel with his trunk. It felt like a cloud. He nuzzled a striped shirt. It felt smooth and cool. In one basket, he saw different cloths folded in neat piles. One cloth had sparkly threads. Another had tiny elephants stitched in a circle. There was a bright scarf with red and gold swirls that shimmered when the light hit it.

“Hello?” Tumba said softly. “Does anyone need help?”

From the doorway, three friends peered in. Mei had a blue ribbon in her hair and a silk sash. Sam wore a cap with stars. Asha had bracelets that chimed like little bells. They came to pick up laundry for a family celebration later that day.

“Hi, Tumba!” Mei waved. “We need to sort the special clothes for our party.”

Tumba smiled. “I can help. I am very gentle.”

Asha lifted a scarf with tiny mirrors sewn in. “This is my dupatta. It sparkles when I dance.”

Mei held up a soft, shiny top. “This is my qipao. It has flowers like the garden.”

Sam pointed to a woven shirt with bright stripes. “This is my guayabera. It feels cool when it’s hot.”

Tumba’s eyes grew wide. “They are all so special.”

They began to sort the clothes. Tumba tried to fold the dupatta, but his trunk was a little clumsy. The mirrors clinked together, ting-ting. He forgot to be careful. The scarf slid to the floor.

“Oh no!” Tumba gasped. “I’m sorry!”

Asha quickly picked it up and smiled. “It’s okay. It’s delicate.”

Tumba’s ears drooped. He wanted to help, but he didn’t know the right way. He reached for Mei’s qipao. The fabric was smooth like water. He folded it once, but the fold was crooked. Sam’s guayabera slid off the stack and landed in a puddle of warm dryer air with a soft fwump.

Mei said kindly, “Our special clothes need special care.”

Tumba’s heart thumped. “I want to help. I just don’t know how.”

Sam scratched his head. “Maybe we can show you?”

Asha nodded. “We can teach and learn together.”

Tumba took a deep breath. The laundry room hummed like a quiet song. He felt the warmth on his cheeks and the smooth tile under his feet. “Can we make a plan?” he asked. “We can take turns and use gentle steps. I can learn the right folds.”

Mei smiled. “That sounds fair.”

Sam said, “Let’s make a sorting song!”

Asha clapped her hands. “And dance gently so nothing falls.”

They all agreed. This was their negotiation. They would share ideas, take turns, and speak kindly. They would listen to each other about what each cloth needed.

Mei showed Tumba how to hold the qipao at the shoulders. “We fold it like a hug,” she said. “Soft and even.” Tumba tried again. His trunk moved slow and tender. Fold, pat, smile.

Asha spread the dupatta across a clean towel. “We keep it flat, then fold the edges in. The mirrors don’t like to bump.” Tumba nodded, and his trunk tickled the corners into place. Ting-ting, very soft.

Sam held up the guayabera. “See the pockets? We fold so the pockets don’t crunch.” Tumba lined up the pockets and patted them smooth.

They made a little song together:
“Fold and hold,
Gentle and bold.
Listen, learn,
Take your turn.”

They sang and worked. Tumba felt proud and calm. He could smell the clean cotton and the sweet soap. He could hear the happy hum of the dryer and the tiny bell-chime of Asha’s bracelets. He could see the bright colors lined up like a rainbow.

Soon, the special clothes were tidy and safe in their baskets. Mei tied a ribbon around hers. Asha wrapped hers in soft paper. Sam placed his on top with care.

Mei said, “Thank you, Tumba. You learned about my qipao.”

Asha said, “Thank you, Tumba. You respected my dupatta.”

Sam said, “Thank you, Tumba. You cared for my guayabera.”

Tumba’s ears perked up. “I learned that each special cloth tells a story. We can celebrate by caring for each other’s things.”

Mei giggled. “We can celebrate our differences!”

Sam added, “We can ask, listen, and make a plan.”

Asha said, “That’s good negotiation.”

Tumba smiled his biggest, gentlest smile. “Differences make our laundry room bright,” he said. “And our hearts bright, too.”

They all laughed. Then Tumba had an idea. “May I show you something from me?” He picked up a small cloth from his own basket. It had little gray elephants marching in a line. “This is my blanket. My nana made it.”

Mei stroked it. “It’s soft.”

Asha admired the tiny stitches. “So careful.”

Sam grinned. “We will fold it with our song.”

They sang again, folding Tumba’s blanket with love. Fold and hold, gentle and bold.

When they finished, the laundry room felt warm and full of joy. The baskets were ready. The friends were ready, too.

Tumba said softly, “Today I learned to appreciate your clothes, your stories, and your ways. We listened, we shared, and we took turns. That is how we celebrate each other.”

Mei, Asha, and Sam nodded. “Together.”

And off they went, carrying their baskets, their song, and their happy hearts.

The End.
