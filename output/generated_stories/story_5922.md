# Ellie’s Quiet Office Day

Ellie was a gentle elephant calf with soft gray ears and big, careful feet. Today, Ellie visited Mama’s home office. The room was cozy and bright. Sunlight made warm squares on the floor. The computer hummed, zzzzz, like a sleepy bee. Papers rustled like leaves. A little fan whispered, whirr, whirr, whirr.

“Ellie, this is my quiet office,” Mama said softly. “I work here. Please be gentle and careful.”

“I can be gentle,” Ellie said. She tucked her tail and tried to walk tiptoe. Tip. Toe. Tip. Toe. Her feet still went thump, thump, but she tried her best.

On the floor, Ellie saw three boxes. One was blue, one was green, and one was brown. The brown one had snacks—crackly bags and a banana peel. The green one had a picture of a bottle and said, “Recycling.” The blue one had a picture of a paper and also said, “Recycling.”

“What are these?” Ellie asked.

“These are our sorting boxes,” Mama said. “We recycle to help the Earth. Paper goes in the blue box. Bottles and cans go in the green box. Trash goes in the brown bin.”

Ellie’s eyes grew wide. “I want to help the Earth!”

“You can,” Mama said with a smile. “But remember our quiet rule. And ask before you take things. We have personal boundaries in the office. That means we respect each other’s space and work.”

Ellie nodded. “I will ask first.”

Mama put on her headphones. The soft music tickled the air. Ellie sat on a round cushion. It felt squishy under her belly. She drew a picture of a tree with big leaves. She used a green crayon that smelled like apples.

Soon, Ellie saw a shiny bottle on the desk. It looked lonely.

“Is this for the green box?” Ellie whispered.

Mama didn’t hear. Ellie waited. She swung her trunk. She looked at the bottle again. Plink, plink, plink, went the drops inside.

“I want to help,” Ellie said to herself. She lifted the bottle with her trunk. Her trunk felt cool on the smooth plastic.

But oh! Her big foot bumped the paper pile. Whoosh! Papers slid like snowflakes. They covered the floor. A crunchy sound came from the shelf. A tiny pencil cup tipped over. Clatter, clink! Pencils rolled like little sticks.

Mama lifted her headphones. She looked surprised. “Oh, Ellie,” she said gently. “What happened?”

Ellie’s cheeks felt warm. Her ears drooped. She wanted to hide behind the cushion. She took a deep breath. “I wanted to put the bottle in the green box,” she said in a small voice. “I did not ask. I made a mess. I am sorry.”

Mama came close and touched Ellie’s trunk. “Thank you for being honest,” she said. “It is brave to tell the truth. I know you wanted to help. Next time, please ask first. That keeps our office safe and calm.”

Ellie nodded. “I will ask. Can I help clean up now?”

“Yes,” Mama said. “Let’s do it together.”

They picked up the papers with soft swish, swish sounds. Ellie stacked them carefully. The paper felt dry and thin. Mama showed her a tiny blue label on the stack.

“These papers are for recycling,” Mama said. “They go in the blue box.”

Ellie smiled. “Blue for paper.” She set the papers in the blue box. Plop.

Mama held up two bottles. “These are for the green box.”

Ellie took them. The bottles felt cool and a little wet. She dropped them in. Plink, plunk.

“What about this banana peel?” Ellie asked. It smelled sweet and mushy.

“That goes in the brown bin,” Mama said. “It’s trash today.”

Ellie wiggled her toes. “I want to make a sign,” she said. “A helpful sign.”

Mama nodded. “Great idea.”

Ellie drew three pictures on a small card. She drew a blue square with a paper. She drew a green square with a bottle. She drew a brown square with a banana peel. She added a little elephant with a speech bubble that said, “Ask first!”

They taped the sign above the boxes. Tape made a snick sound. The sign looked bright and friendly.

Mama smiled. “You improved the office, Ellie. You learned about recycling and about asking first. That is wonderful.”

Ellie felt light as a leaf. “Can we practice? I will ask.”

“Okay,” Mama said.

Ellie pointed to the shiny bottle on the desk. “Mama, may I move this bottle to the green box?”

“Yes, you may,” Mama said.

Ellie lifted the bottle slowly. She kept her feet steady. No bumping. No whoosh. She set the bottle in the green box. Plink.

“Good job,” Mama said. “You were careful and kind.”

Ellie took a deep, happy breath. The office smelled like paper, apples, and a little bit of lemon from Mama’s desk wipe. The fan went whirr, whirr. The computer hummed. It felt calm again.

Ellie hugged Mama gently. “I like helping the Earth,” she said. “Blue for paper. Green for bottles. Brown for trash. Ask first. Be honest. Say sorry. Try again.”

Mama hugged Ellie back. “That is a perfect plan.”

Ellie sat on her cushion and drew another tree. This one had birds and a little recycling sign. She colored a tiny green heart. It looked like hope.

The office was quiet and neat. Ellie smiled. She felt proud and peaceful. She had made a mess, told the truth, said sorry, and made it better. She had learned to recycle and respect boundaries. Helping felt good.

The End.
