# Squeak’s Basement Bargain

In a busy basement, a kind mouse named Squeak woke up to the soft hum of a washing machine and the warm smell of clean socks. The floor felt cool under Squeak’s tiny paws. Boxes were stacked like tall buildings. Pipes sang drip-drop music. It was a bustling, busy place.

Squeak loved the basement. He helped where he could. He nibbled crumbs to keep things tidy. He dragged tiny threads to make cozy nests. He was small, but he was brave and kind.

One morning, Squeak heard a clatter. “Clank! Clink!” A shiny red marble rolled across the floor. It bumped Squeak’s whiskers. “Oh!” said Squeak, his nose twitching. “Who lost this?”

Out from behind a toolbox came Grumble, a gruff gray rat with a loud voice. “That’s mine!” he said, stomping his feet. His tail thumped. His eyes looked hot like tiny coals.

Squeak took a deep breath. He felt a flutter in his belly. He wanted to run. But Squeak was brave. He stood tall, as tall as a mouse could. “Hello, Grumble,” Squeak said in a calm, gentle voice. “I found your marble. It rolled to me.”

Grumble huffed. “Give it back right now! And don’t touch my stuff!” His voice echoed under the pipes.

Squeak’s ears drooped. He did not like yelling. He felt a spark of anger too, like a hot pebble. He squeezed his paws. Then he remembered: when angry, stop, breathe, and use words. So Squeak stopped. He took a slow breath in. He let a slow breath out. He felt the spark cool down.

“I want to help,” Squeak said. “I will give it back. May we talk? We can make a plan.”

“A plan?” Grumble blinked. He did not expect a plan. The washing machine hummed. The light bulb buzzed softly.

“Yes,” said Squeak. “Your marble is special. But it rolls away. That can be scary. You sound upset. I hear you.”

Grumble’s tail stopped thumping. “It keeps rolling,” he mumbled. “I don’t want to lose it.”

Squeak nodded. “I want the basement safe and tidy. You want your marble. We both want peace.”

Grumble sniffed. “Peace?”

“Peace means calm,” Squeak said. “Let’s make a calm plan. I have an idea. Would you like to hear it?”

Grumble crossed his arms. “Maybe.”

Squeak pointed to a low wooden crate. It smelled like old apples. “What if we make a marble park in that crate? We can make soft walls with socks from the laundry basket. The marble can roll and play, but it will not run away.”

Grumble rubbed his whiskers. “Hmm.”

“Here is my offer,” Squeak said kindly. “I give your marble back. We build the park together. We can share time. You roll it for five turns. Then I roll it for five turns. Then you again, then me. We can count out loud.”

Grumble looked at the shiny red marble in Squeak’s paws. It flashed like a tiny cherry. He took a breath. “I still feel a little mad,” he said.

“That is okay,” Squeak said. “Let’s both do three calm breaths.” He showed Grumble: in, out… in, out… in, out. The air smelled like clean cloth.

Grumble’s shoulders softened. “Okay,” he said. “That plan sounds fair.”

Squeak smiled and handed over the marble. “Thank you for listening.”

Together they tugged a sock here, a sock there. They patted soft walls into the crate. They made a gentle ramp from a cardboard flap. The marble made a happy “r-r-rroll” sound as it went down. The pipes dripped a friendly beat. Drip, roll, drip, roll.

“Five turns for you,” Squeak said.

Grumble rolled the marble. “One… two… three… four… five!” He grinned. His eyes were not hot now. “Your turn.”

Squeak rolled it. “One… two… three… four… five!” He giggled when the marble bumped the sock wall. “Boop!”

Soon, a spider named Dot peeped down on a silvery thread. “What’s that fun sound?” Dot asked.

“Our marble park,” said Squeak. “We made a plan so no one gets upset.”

Dot clapped her tiny legs. “Good plan!”

Just then, the washing machine beeped. A big basket wobbled. Socks tumbled like soft snow. One sock flopped onto the crate, knocking the marble up and out. It rolled toward a drain.

“Oh no!” Grumble shouted. He felt his anger pop again. “My marble!”

Squeak felt fear, like a cold wave. But he stood brave. “We can fix this,” he said. “Let’s work together.” He ran fast. Pat-pat-pat! Grumble ran too. Thump-thump!

Squeak jumped and used his tail like a broom. Swish! He slowed the marble. Grumble stretched out his paw and caught it right before the drain.

They both froze. Then they laughed with relief. “We did it,” Grumble said, breathing hard. “You were brave.”

“You were brave too,” said Squeak. “And we stayed calm. We used words. We made a plan.”

Grumble hugged the marble gently. “Thank you for not yelling at me,” he said. “I don’t like feeling mad.”

“It’s okay to feel mad,” Squeak said. “We can breathe, use calm words, and make fair plans. That is called negotiation. It helps everyone.”

Grumble nodded. “Negotiation,” he said slowly, rolling the word like the marble. “Can we keep the turn-counting?”

“Yes,” said Squeak. “And next time, we can ask before touching. We can listen to each other.”

Dot lowered a tiny sign made of web. It said, “Calm Words, Fair Turns, Brave Hearts.”

They all smiled. The basement felt warm and friendly. The hum was like a lullaby. The marble park was safe inside the crate with sock walls higher and stronger than before.

Squeak waved his whiskers. “Five turns for you, friend.”

Grumble smiled. “And five for you.”

They played, shared, and helped each other, all afternoon in the busy basement.

The lesson was clear: when you feel mad, stop and breathe. Use calm words. Make a fair plan. That is negotiation. It takes courage, and it keeps hearts kind.

The End.
