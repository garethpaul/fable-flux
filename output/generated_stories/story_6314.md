# Tilly Turtle’s Treehouse Tune

Tilly was a shy turtle with a smooth, green shell. She lived near a big oak tree with a cozy treehouse tucked in its branches. The treehouse smelled like pine and sunshine. Inside was a small drum, a twinkly triangle, a bright yellow shaker, and Tilly’s favorite—a tiny ukulele with rainbow strings.

Tilly loved music, but she felt nervous when others were around. Her cheeks felt warm, and her tummy fluttered like a moth. “Plink, plink,” she would whisper to the ukulele when no one was watching.

One bright afternoon, her friends climbed up the wooden ladder. Tap-tap-tap went their feet. “Hi, Tilly!” sang Bella Bunny, shaking the yellow shaker. “Let’s make music!”

Sammy Squirrel wiggled his tail. “I’ll play the drum! Boomp boomp!” he said, patting the drum with his tiny paws.

Ollie Owl blinked his big eyes and lifted the triangle. “Ting!” it went, high and clear like a tiny star.

Tilly smiled a small smile. “I can play my ukulele,” she said softly. “Plink?”

The treehouse filled with sounds. Boomp, ting, shake-shake, plink-plink. It sounded happy and bouncy. Sunbeams danced through the window, and dust sparkled like little notes in the air.

Then the sounds got louder. Boomp BOOMP! Shake-shake-SHAKE! Ting-ting-TING! Tilly’s hands shook. Her ukulele strings buzzed. She stopped playing. Her shell felt like a safe blanket.

Bella hopped closer. “Did we scare you?” she asked gently.

Tilly nodded. “It feels too loud,” she whispered. “My heart is beating fast.”

Ollie tilted his head. “Let’s take a slow breath together,” he hoo-hooed softly.

They breathed in. They breathed out. The treehouse felt calm again.

Sammy put a paw on the drum. “I’m sorry, Tilly,” he said. “I got excited.”

“It’s okay,” Tilly said. “I like making music. Maybe we can make a plan.”

Ollie smiled. “A music plan!”

Tilly thought. She pressed one finger to a rainbow string. Plink. “We can take turns. Quiet to loud. Slow to fast. We can share the sounds.”

Bella’s ears wiggled. “We can use hand signals!” she chirped. “One finger means soft. Two fingers means medium. Three fingers means loud!”

Tilly’s eyes lit up. “Yes,” she said, a little braver. “And I will start soft.”

They tried again. Tilly held up one finger. Soft. She brushed the strings. Plink… plink… The sound was like warm honey.

Sammy tapped the drum gently. boomp… boomp…

Bella shook the shaker lightly. shhh… shhh…

Ollie touched the triangle once. ting…

Tilly smiled bigger. “Now two fingers,” she said. The sounds grew a tiny bit stronger—plink-plink, boomp-boomp, shake-shake, ting-ting. It felt like a sunny walk.

“Three fingers!” called Bella.

The music rose, bright and full. PLINK-PLINK! BOOMP-BOOMP! SHAKE-SHAKE! TING-TING! Tilly’s heart still thumped, but it thumped with joy. She felt brave. She was part of the band.

“Stop,” Tilly said kindly, holding up a flat hand. The sounds faded like a soft cloud.

“Wow,” Sammy said, eyes wide. “We followed the plan. It sounded great!”

Ollie nodded. “We can also try slow and fast.”

Tilly tapped her foot. “Slow first,” she said. They played slow. The sounds stretched like taffy. Then she said, “A little faster.” The beat grew bouncy, and everyone giggled.

“Let’s make a song,” Bella said.

“What should we call it?” asked Sammy.

Tilly looked at her friends, then at the sun painting gold on the wood floor. “Treehouse Tune,” she said. “It begins soft, then grows, then rests.”

They practiced the Treehouse Tune again. Sometimes Sammy went too fast. Sometimes Bella shook too loud. Sometimes Tilly’s fingers slipped and made a squeak. Squeak!

“It’s okay,” Tilly said, taking a calm breath. “We are learning.” They tried again, and again, and again. Each time, the song felt smoother, like a path swept clean.

Soon, a gentle breeze carried their music out the window. A chipmunk below clapped tiny paws. “Yay!”

Tilly felt proud. Her cheeks were warm, but not from worry. From happiness. She looked at her friends. “Thank you for listening,” she said. “Thank you for sharing and taking turns.”

Bella hugged Tilly softly. “Thank you for the plan,” she said.

Sammy handed Tilly the drumstick. “Want to try the drum?” he asked.

Tilly’s eyes sparkled. “Share?” she asked.

“Share!” they all cheered.

Tilly tapped the drum—boomp. She plucked the ukulele—plink. Her friends joined in, following the signals. Together, they played the Treehouse Tune once more: soft, then strong, then a gentle stop.

Tilly took a deep breath. “When we listen, when we practice, and when we share,” she said, “music feels good in our hearts.”

And it did. The treehouse glowed with sound, with kindness, and with friends.

The End.
