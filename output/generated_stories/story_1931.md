# Tiko the Wise Tortoise Rides Along

Tiko the wise tortoise rode in a little basket on the back seat of the family car. The seat was soft and warm. The car hummed like a friendly bee. The windows showed trees, shops, and a bright blue sky. Sunlight made tiny sparkles on Tiko’s smooth, green shell.

Mama drove. Ben and Mia sat beside Tiko. They wore seatbelts that clicked. “We are going to the park clean-up,” Mama said. “We will help our community.”

“I packed apples and carrots,” Mia said. The sweet smell of apples filled the car. “Yum,” said Ben.

Tiko smiled slowly. “Helping is good for our hearts,” he said. “And good food helps our bodies.”

They stopped at a light. Suddenly, a car beeped. “Beep! Beep!” The sound was loud. Ben’s face got tight. “That scared me!” he said. His hands balled up into fists.

Tiko saw. “Big feelings can feel like storms,” Tiko said. “When your body feels hot, stop and breathe.”

“How?” asked Ben. The light turned green. The car rolled forward, smooth and steady.

“Smell the apple,” Tiko said. He lifted his nose. “In through the nose.” Tiko took a long, slow breath. “Blow out the candle,” he said softly. He blew a little “whoooo.”

Ben sniffed the apple smell. He blew out slowly, like a candle puff. His hands relaxed. “I feel better,” he said.

Mia nodded. “I will try too.” She sniffed and blew. Her eyes looked calm.

“Good,” said Tiko. “We can write our feelings too. Writing helps our brains.”

Ben looked around. “We have crayons!” He pulled a small pad of paper from his bag. The paper felt smooth. The blue crayon felt waxy. “I will draw a beep,” he said. He made zigzag lines for the loud sound. “Zig! Zag!”

Mia drew a big heart. “This is my calm heart,” she said.

Tiko watched. “You are using your words and your art,” he said. “That is smart.”

They reached the park. Mama parked the car. The air outside smelled like grass and dirt. Children laughed. Birds chirped. The sun warmed Tiko’s shell.

“We are here to help,” Mama said. She gave them small gloves. “We will pick up trash and keep the park clean.”

Ben saw a broken cup. “I can pick this up,” he said. He felt proud. Mia used a grabber to pick up a crinkly bag. “It makes a crunchy sound,” she said. “Crunch, crunch!”

Tiko guided them. “Step slow. Look down. Stay safe.”

They found a picnic bench with papers on it. “Oh no,” said Mia. “These are letters. They blew away.”

A little boy ran over. He looked upset. “My letters! My class wrote notes for the gardeners,” he said. “The wind took them.” His eyes were wet. His cheeks were red.

Tiko moved close. “Hello,” he said in a calm voice. “Big feelings are okay. Let’s breathe. Smell the apple. Blow out the candle.”

The boy sniffed. He took a breath. He blew out slowly. “Whoooo.”

“Better?” asked Tiko.

“A little,” the boy said.

Ben and Mia helped gather the papers. “We found many,” Mia said. “But some are gone.”

The boy’s hands clenched. “I feel angry,” he said. “We worked hard.”

Tiko nodded. “Anger tells us something is not right. We can listen. Then we can choose what to do.”

“What can we do?” the boy asked.

“We can write new notes,” said Tiko. “We can make them even kinder.”

Ben held up his pad. “We have paper and crayons!” He smiled. “We can write now.”

They sat at the bench. The wood felt warm in the sun. Birds sang. Wind whispered in the trees. Mama brought slices of crunchy carrots and juicy apples. “Snack time helps our bodies,” she said. “Then we write.”

They ate. Crunch, crunch. Slurp, slurp. The sweet apple juice tickled their tongues. The carrots snapped.

Tiko spoke softly. “When we feel mad, we can:
- Stop and breathe.
- Use words.
- Make a plan to help.”

They all nodded.

They wrote together. Ben wrote, “Thank you, gardeners.” Mia drew a watering can with flowers. The little boy wrote, “We care about the park.” Tiko added, “Your work helps our community.”

More children joined. Soon, there was a colorful pile of kind notes. The wind blew gently, but the notes sat under a smooth rock.

A gardener walked by. He had dirt on his gloves and a smile on his face. “What is this?” he asked.

“Thank-you notes,” said the little boy. His voice was bright now. “We made more!”

The gardener’s eyes shone. “This makes my day,” he said. “Thank you, helpers.”

Ben’s chest felt warm. Mia clapped. The little boy laughed. “I turned my mad into help,” he said.

Tiko smiled. “That is the growth way,” he said. “We learn. We try. We grow.”

Back in the family car, the seats felt cozy. The car hummed again. Ben held his crayon pad. “Today I learned to breathe,” he said.

Mia patted Tiko’s shell. “I learned that writing can help feelings.”

“And I learned that healthy snacks help us think,” said the little boy, who rode home with his dad behind them.

Tiko nodded slowly. “When anger comes, we can be gentle. We can breathe, use words, and choose kind action. That helps us, and it helps our community.”

Everyone smiled. The sky looked soft and golden. The car rolled on, calm and steady.

The End.
