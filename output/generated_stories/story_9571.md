# Nutmeg Learns New Words

Nutmeg was a bright, resourceful squirrel who lived in a cozy nest near a balcony garden. The balcony smelled like sweet tomatoes and mint. Tiny bells rang softly when the wind blew, ting-ting, ting-ting. Sunlight warmed the wooden floor. Leaves whispered, shhh, shhh.

Every morning, Nutmeg visited the balcony. “Good morning, plants!” she chirped. She patted the soil with her small paws. She liked to help the plants. She carried little pebbles, found lost seeds, and checked the water with her whiskers. “Cool water today,” she said, feeling the gentle splash. “You will like that!”

One day, Nutmeg heard a soft voice. “Hello?” It was Lina, a kind child who cared for the balcony garden. Lina held a small book with pictures. The cover said, “My First Words.” Lina smiled and waved. “Hi, Nutmeg. I’m learning new words. Would you like to learn too?”

Nutmeg’s tail swished. “Yes! I love sounds. I love names.”

Lina sat on a cushion. “We can help the garden and learn words at the same time,” she said. “We can volunteer to be word helpers.” Nutmeg felt proud. “Volunteer means help because we want to,” she repeated. “I can do that.”

They started with a bright red tomato. Lina pointed and said, “To-ma-to.” She broke the word into soft pieces. Nutmeg sniffed. The tomato smelled sweet and sunny. “To-ma-to,” Nutmeg said slowly. “It is round and red. It shines.” Her whiskers twitched with joy.

They moved to a pot of mint. “Mint,” said Lina. “Mint smells fresh.” Nutmeg rubbed a leaf. The scent was cool, like tiny snowflakes in her nose. “Mint,” she said. “It tickles my nose.”

Lina giggled. “You are a great word helper, Nutmeg! Let’s make a word story for the plants.”

“A story?” Nutmeg’s eyes sparkled. “I can tell a story. I am good at stories.”

Lina nodded. “We will use our new words. Stories help us remember. Words are like seeds. We plant them in our minds.”

Nutmeg stood tall on the railing. She felt the warm wood under her paws and the soft breeze in her fur. She began, with a happy chirp, “Once upon a balcony, there was a red to-ma-to. It was round. It was shiny. It was brave. It said, ‘I am juicy and sweet!’”

Lina clapped. “Good story start!”

Nutmeg continued, “Next to the tomato was mint. Mint said, ‘I am cool and kind. I help your breath feel fresh.’” She made a tiny sniff. “Ahh.”

Lina showed the book again. There were pictures of a watering can and a trowel. “Wa-ter-ing can,” she said, tapping the picture. Water trickled as she poured. Drip, drop, drip. “Trowel,” she said, picking up the small shovel. “It helps us dig.”

Nutmeg tried the new sounds. “Wa-ter-ing can. Trow-el.” She helped by holding a string in her teeth to tie a tomato stem to a stick. “Tie,” she said. “This helps the plant stand tall.”

Soon a little ladybug landed on Nutmeg’s ear. “Hello, little dot friend,” said Nutmeg. “La-dy-bug,” Lina said. “It is a helper bug.”

Nutmeg added to her story, “The ladybug came to help. She was small, but brave. She said, ‘I keep the leaves happy.’”

They heard a flutter. A yellow bird sat on the railing. “Tweet-tweet!” it sang. Lina whispered, “Bird.” Nutmeg whispered back, “Bird.” The song was like a ribbon in the air. It made them smile.

A tiny alarm beeped from Lina’s watch. “Snack time,” she said. She held up a bowl. “Blue-ber-ry.” The berries were soft and cool. Nutmeg took one gently. “Blue-ber-ry,” she said, tasting the sweet burst. “Blue like the sky.”

Then Lina frowned. “Uh-oh,” she said. “A plant is droopy.” The small basil plant leaned to one side. Its leaves looked tired.

Nutmeg took a deep breath. “I can help.” She touched the soil. “Dry,” she said. “The word is dry.” Lina nodded. “Yes. The soil is dry. It needs water.”

Together, they fixed it. Lina poured from the watering can. Drip, drop, drip. Nutmeg fanned the leaves with a cardboard fan. “Fan,” she said. “Gentle.” The basil perked up slowly. The leaves lifted, like little hands waving.

Lina smiled wide. “We did it! We helped. You are so resourceful, Nutmeg.”

Nutmeg’s tail flicked with pride. “Re-sour-ce-ful,” she repeated. “That means I find ways to help.”

They sat quietly for a moment. The wind went whoosh. The bells sang ting-ting. The garden smelled like mint and earth. Nutmeg felt calm and glad.

“I learned many words today,” Nutmeg said. “Tomato, mint, watering can, trowel, ladybug, bird, blueberry, dry, fan, resourceful.” She tapped her head gently. “The words are seeds. My story is the soil. I will water them with practice.”

Lina nodded. “Yes! When we tell stories, we remember words. We can be kind, we can help, and we can learn. That is independence too. You chose to help and learn today.”

Nutmeg looked around the balcony garden. She stood up and spoke in a clear, happy voice, “Thank you, words. Thank you, garden. I will keep helping. I will keep telling stories.”

Lina gave Nutmeg a tiny high-five. “Let’s share our word story with others,” she said. “We can read to the plants tomorrow. We can volunteer again.”

Nutmeg grinned. “Yes! Tomorrow we’ll learn more words. We’ll make new stories. We will be good helpers.”

The sun felt warm. The mint smelled sweet. The bells sang. Nutmeg curled her tail and whispered, “Words are wonderful.”

The End.
