# Bubbles, Feelings, and the Little Race

In a cozy den by the tall pine trees, a friendly bear cub named Bubby padded into the bathroom. The bathroom was warm and steamy. The mirror was foggy. The soap smelled like sweet berries. Plip, plop, went the dripping faucet. Bubby’s soft paws felt the cool, smooth tiles.

Bubby had a big day. After his bath, he would go to the Cub Club for a little sports day. There would be a tiny race, a ball toss, and a tunnel crawl. Bubby loved to run, but today his tummy felt fluttery.

“Mama,” Bubby called, climbing into the tub. “My tummy feels wiggly like jelly.”

Mama Bear turned on the warm water. Swish, swish, it filled the tub. “Tell me more, Bubby,” she said in a gentle voice. “What does your jelly tummy mean?”

Bubby watched bubbles rise and pop. “Pop! Pop!” He poked them. “I think I feel… worried,” he whispered. “What if I trip in the race? What if I lose?”

Mama handed Bubby a soft, yellow washcloth. It felt squishy and warm in his paws. “Thank you for telling me your feelings,” she said. “That is brave. When we say how we feel, we understand our hearts.”

Bubby dipped the washcloth and squeezed it. Drip, drip. “But I don’t like this feeling,” he said. “I want it to go away.”

Mama nodded. “Feelings come to visit, like little birds. We can listen to them, and then we can choose what to do. Let’s make a plan in our bubbly bathtub.”

Bubby sat up, ears perked. “A plan?”

“Yes,” Mama said, smiling. “First, we name the feeling. What is it?”

“Worried,” Bubby said, louder now.

“Second, we breathe,” Mama said. “Smell the flowers, blow the bubbles.”

They breathed together. In through the nose—sniff, sniff—smelling the berry soap. Out through the mouth—whooo—blowing little bubble domes across the water. The bubbles bumped each other and made tiny mountains. Bubby giggled. His tummy felt a little less wiggly.

“Third,” Mama went on, “we think with our bear brains. What can we do if we trip? What can we do if we don’t win?”

Bubby tapped his chin. “If I trip, I can get up. I can try again. If I don’t win, I can clap for friends.” He imagined clapping with his big soft paws. Clap, clap!

“That’s smart thinking,” Mama said. “That is using your logic. You are being a problem solver.”

Bubby swished the water with his paw. “Could we practice in the bathroom?” he asked.

Mama laughed softly. “A tiny practice? Yes!”

She placed three bath toys on the tub edge: a red boat, a blue duck, and a green frog. “Here is our little race,” she said. “When I say go, we gently splash to move them. If a toy tips, we start again. Ready?”

Bubby’s eyes sparkled. “Ready!”

“Go!” said Mama.

Bubby made small splashes with his paw. The blue duck bobbed forward. Plip! The red boat tipped. “Uh-oh!” said Bubby. “The boat tripped.”

“What can we do?” Mama asked.

“Start again,” Bubby said. He set the boat straight and tried softer splashes. The toys moved together. “I can make small splashes. I can go slow and steady.”

When the blue duck reached the drain, Bubby cheered. “Yay, duck!” He opened his arms wide and said, “Good job, boat! Good job, frog!” His voice echoed in the steamy room. Echo, echo.

“How does your tummy feel now?” Mama asked.

Bubby put his paw on his belly. “Not so jelly,” he said with a smile. “More like… warm oatmeal.”

Mama chuckled. “Cozy and calm.”

After the bath, Bubby dried off with a fluffy towel. Rub, rub. He brushed his fur until it felt soft like clouds. He looked in the mirror. He made a worried face, then a brave face. “I can name my feelings,” he said. “I can breathe and think. I can try.”

At Cub Club, the tiny race began. The other cubs lined up. “Good luck,” Bubby said to his friends. “Let’s cheer for everyone!”

“On your marks,” said Coach Otter. The whistle chirped. Tweet!

Bubby started to run, paws pat-pat on the grass. He felt a tiny wobble. His paw slid on a leaf. Swoop! He stumbled. “Oh no,” he thought. “I’m tripping!”

He heard his own voice from the bathroom in his mind: “If I trip, I can get up.” Bubby took a big breath. Sniff, sniff. Whooo. He stood up and ran again, slow and steady. He did not win first place. His friend Tilly did. Tilly waved.

Bubby clapped with his big soft paws. Clap, clap! “Great running, Tilly!” he called. His chest felt proud and light.

Coach Otter smiled. “Bubby, you showed brave heart and smart thinking. You noticed your feeling, you breathed, and you kept going. That is being a strong sport.”

On the way home, Bubby told Mama all about it. “I felt worried, I breathed, I thought, and I tried,” he said. “I can be open to tricky moments. I can learn.”

Mama squeezed his paw. “Feelings are guides,” she said. “You listened. You used your mind and your kind heart.”

Back in the cozy bathroom that night, Bubby winked at the mirror. “Hello, feelings,” he said. “I will listen to you. Then I will use my bear brain and my brave paws.”

He made a tiny splash with his paw. Plip! The bubble popped. Pop! And Bubby giggled.

The End.
