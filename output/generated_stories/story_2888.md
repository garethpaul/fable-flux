# Momo’s Big Basement Cleanup

Momo was a courageous monkey with bright eyes and quick hands. One sunny morning, Momo clapped and said, “Today is a busy day! I will help in the busy basement.” The basement was full of boxes, bags, and bins. It smelled dusty and a little sweet, like old crayons. Light came in through a small window and made tiny dust sparkles dance in the air.

Momo took a deep breath. “I can do this,” Momo whispered. “I am brave. I am smart.”

Down the creaky stairs, Momo went. Step, step, step. The floor felt cool. In the corners, Momo heard soft rustles. “Hello, basement,” Momo giggled. “I’m here to help.”

On a big table, there were piles of things: paper, plastic bottles, cardboard tubes, and metal cans. There were also broken crayons and fuzzy socks with no matches. A clock on the wall went tick-tock, tick-tock. Momo looked up. “I will use my time well,” Momo said. “I will make a plan.”

Momo drew a simple chart on a sheet of paper with a blue crayon. “Plan time!” Momo cheered. “First, sort. Second, recycle. Third, make art. Fourth, clean up.”

Momo set a timer on a little kitchen bell. “Ding in ten minutes,” Momo said. “I can do ten minutes.” Momo puffed cheeks and started sorting.

“Paper goes here,” Momo said, putting books, newspapers, and drawings into a paper bin. Rustle, rustle. “Plastic goes here,” Momo said, placing bottles into a plastic bin. Clink, clink. “Cardboard goes here,” Momo said, flattening boxes. Crunch, crunch. “Metal goes here,” Momo said, dropping cans into a can bin. Clang, clang!

The bell went ding. Momo clapped. “Great job, me! I am trying my best.”

Then Momo found a torn coloring page with a rainbow. “Oh no,” Momo said softly. “This is ripped.” Momo’s tail drooped. “I make mistakes sometimes.” Momo took another breath. “I can fix it.” That was problem-solving time.

Momo got tape and gently put the rainbow back together. Smooth, smooth. “There,” Momo said. “It is still good. I can recycle some. I can reuse some.” Momo smiled and felt warm and proud.

Next, Momo saw a shiny, clean plastic bottle. “I can make art!” Momo said. The timer went ding again. “Art time now!” Momo put the bottle on the table. Momo added paper wings from an old flyer, a cardboard tube for a rocket body, and a cap for a nose. “Zoom, zoom!” Momo whispered, gluing pieces with safe glue that smelled a little sweet. Momo drew stars with a gold crayon. The rocket sparkled. “Recycling can be art,” Momo said. “We can make new things from old things.”

From bottle caps and buttons, Momo made a funny face. Click, click, stick, stick. The face had big round eyes and a smile. “Hello, Cap Friend,” Momo said in a silly voice. “Let’s clean more!”

Momo looked at the clock. Tick-tock, tick-tock. “Time to finish sorting,” Momo decided. “I can do five more minutes.” Momo set the bell. Momo stood tall, rolled shoulders, and hummed a little song: “Sort, sort, sort it right. Make the basement clean and bright.”

Soon the bins were full. The paper bin rustled like leaves. The plastic bin crinkled. The metal bin chimed softly when Momo touched it. The cardboard stack was neat like a tiny city of flat buildings. Momo wiped the table with a cloth. Swipe, swipe. The floor looked clearer. The dust sparkles floated higher.

Then Momo found a broken crayon box. Inside were small, stubby crayons. “Too small to color?” Momo asked. Momo thought. “Wait! I can melt them with a grown-up. Then we can make new rainbow crayons.” Momo placed the bits in a reuse jar and put a little label: “Crayon Bits for Later.” Momo smiled. “Good time management,” Momo said proudly. “I can save this job for another day.”

Momo took a big drink of water and wiped a tiny bead of sweat. “Cleaning is work,” Momo said, “and I can do hard things.” Momo looked around. The busy basement felt calm now. It smelled less dusty and more like paper and soap.

Footsteps sounded at the top of the stairs. Tap, tap. A friendly voice called, “Momo, how is it going?”

Momo waved. “Great! I sorted paper, plastic, metal, and cardboard. I made a rocket and a cap-face from recycled things. I used my time plan. I fixed a torn rainbow. I saved crayon bits for later.”

“Wow,” said the voice. “You are a courageous helper.”

Momo’s chest felt big and happy. “I tried my best,” Momo said. “Recycling helps the Earth. When we put things in the right bins, we make less trash. We can reuse and make art. We can solve problems and feel proud.”

Momo carried the bins to the door. The cans went for recycling pick-up. The paper and cardboard went to the curb. The plastic went to the big blue bin. Momo waved to the bins like old friends. “See you soon,” Momo laughed.

Back inside, Momo placed the rocket on a shelf. The gold stars shone in the light. “Tick-tock,” went the clock. Momo stretched and took one more deep breath.

“Today I learned,” Momo said, counting on fingers, “to sort things, to recycle, to reuse, to plan my time, and to believe in me.”

Momo smiled at the clean floor and the neat table. The busy basement wasn’t too busy now. It was bright, calm, and ready for new ideas.

“Good job, Momo,” Momo whispered.

“Good job, Earth,” Momo added softly.

The End.
