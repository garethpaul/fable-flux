# Brave Momo Washes and Wins

Momo was a small, courageous monkey who lived in a comfortable nursery. The nursery was warm and bright. Soft rugs felt fluffy under tiny feet. Colorful books sat on low shelves. The air smelled like clean soap and oranges.

Morning sunlight peeked through the curtains. “Good morning, friends!” called Miss Nia, the gentle teacher. She rang a tiny bell that sounded like tinkling stars.

Momo stretched his little arms. “I’m ready for a big day,” he said. His tail curled with excitement.

“First, we wash our hands,” Miss Nia said kindly. “We wash before we play, and before we eat. That keeps us healthy.”

Momo nodded bravely, but his tummy fluttered a bit. The sink made a loud whooosh sound. The water was shiny and splashy. Sometimes Momo felt wiggly and wanted to skip washing.

“I can do it,” Momo whispered to himself. “I am a courageous monkey.”

He walked to the sink with his friends. The soap smelled like sweet oranges. The water felt cool and tickly. Momo liked the bubbles. They looked like tiny moons.

“Remember our song,” said Miss Nia, smiling. “Rub fronts, rub backs, between little fingers, rub thumbs, rub nails, rinse, and dry. Twenty seconds!”

Momo took a deep breath. “Fronts, backs, fingers too,” he sang softly. “Thumbs and nails, that’s what we do!” He rubbed and rubbed. Bubbles grew big and foamy. He rinsed until his hands felt squeaky and clean. Then he patted them dry with a soft towel.

“Great job, Momo,” Miss Nia said. “You followed directions.”

Momo’s chest felt warm and proud. “I did it!” he said. His tail swished happily.

It was time to build towers. Blocks clacked like tiny drums on the rug. “Look!” said Luna, a giggly panda friend. “Let’s make a tall castle!”

Momo giggled. “Yes! A super tall monkey castle!” He stacked red, blue, and yellow blocks. Tap, click, tap. The castle grew tall and strong.

After playtime, Miss Nia rang the tiny bell again. “Friends, it’s snack time. But first, what do we do?”

“Wash hands!” the children cheered.

Momo looked at the castle. He wanted to keep building. His fingers felt wiggly. His thoughts felt bouncy. “Maybe I can skip washing,” he thought. “Just this once.”

He wiggled toward the snack table. But near the table, he saw something sticky on his fingers—a smudge of green paint from the art shelf. It smelled funny, like sour grapes.

Momo paused. He remembered Miss Nia’s words: “We wash before we eat. That keeps us healthy.” He also remembered his brave heart. Being brave wasn’t just about noise or jumping high. Being brave was making good choices, even when it was hard.

Momo stood up tall. “I can wait,” he told himself. “I can follow directions.”

He turned around and marched back to the sink. “Fronts, backs, fingers too,” he sang, louder now. “Thumbs and nails, that’s what we do!” The soap felt silky. The water whispered shhh as it rinsed the bubbles away. He dried his hands until they were cozy and warm.

Luna waddled over. “I like your song,” she said. “It helps me remember.”

“Let’s sing together!” said Momo.

They washed and sang. Soon the room was full of gentle voices and splishy-splash sounds. Miss Nia smiled. The nursery smelled clean and bright.

At snack time, crunchy apple slices and warm toast waited on little plates. The apples were juicy and sweet. The toast was toasty and buttery. “Mmm,” said Momo. He chewed slowly. His clean hands felt nice as he held his cup of water. The cup was cool and smooth.

After snack, Momo felt a tickle in his nose. “A-choo!” he sneezed into his elbow, just like Miss Nia taught. “Good catch,” said Miss Nia. “Now what comes next?”

“Wash again!” Momo said. He went to the sink, calm and steady. He didn’t rush. He didn’t whine. He breathed in and out and hummed his song. He was learning self-control. He was learning to care for himself.

Later, Miss Nia gathered everyone on the soft rug. “Today I saw brave choices,” she said. “Momo, you noticed sticky paint. You used self-control. You followed directions. That keeps everyone safe and healthy.”

Momo’s cheeks felt warm. His heart felt big. “I did it,” he said softly. “I can be brave and clean.”

Miss Nia handed Momo a bright, star-shaped sticker that smelled faintly like orange. “Brave Cleaner,” it said.

Momo put the sticker on his shirt. He patted it gently. The star felt smooth and proud. “Friends,” he said, “let’s remember our washing steps. Fronts, backs, fingers too. Thumbs and nails, that’s what we do!”

Everyone clapped. The tiny bell chimed. The nursery shone with smiles.

That night, when it was time to go home, Momo washed one more time. Soap, bubbles, rinse, dry. He felt calm and clean and ready for rest.

The lesson was clear in Momo’s brave heart: clean hands help us learn, play, and stay healthy. Following directions is a brave choice, and brave choices help everyone feel good.

The End.
