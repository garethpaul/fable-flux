# Spark the Robot Dog Saves Snacks

Spark was a helpful robot dog with shiny silver ears and a soft, waggy tail made of gentle lights. Spark could beep, bark, and talk. His eyes were warm and blue, like little night-lights. He loved helping everyone in the house.

One sunny morning, Spark padded into the pantry. The pantry smelled like cinnamon, apples, and fresh bread. Shelves stood tall with jars of beans, boxes of pasta, and baskets of crunchy carrots. Spark’s nose-light blinked. He whispered, “Mission time!” His voice was calm and kind.

Lina and Taye, two friends with different smiles and different curls, followed Spark into the pantry. Lina wore a bright yellow shirt. Taye wore a blue cap with a tiny planet pin. They both loved helping too.

“What is the mission, Spark?” asked Lina.

Spark tilted his head. “Sustainability,” he said. “That means caring for our Earth by not wasting things. We can use what we have, and share, and be gentle with nature.”

Taye looked around. “How do we do that here?”

Spark pointed his paw-light at a wobbly stack of cereal boxes. “We start by looking. We use our eyes, ears, and noses.”

They listened. Drip, drip, drip. A tiny sound came from the corner. Spark rolled over and found a leaky bottle of soap. A shiny drop slid down the side.

“Oh no,” said Lina. “It’s dripping!”

Spark nodded. “Let’s fix it.” He gently tightened the cap with a soft click. “There. When we fix little leaks, we save a lot. Saving is good for our Earth.”

Taye clapped. “We can do it!” His cap bobbed.

Spark wagged. “Yes. We all can. Big or small. Young or tall.”

They kept exploring. The air felt cool. The floor felt smooth under Spark’s paws. Lina touched a bag of rice. It rustled like dry leaves. Taye opened a drawer. It smelled like sweet cocoa.

Spark’s nose-light blinked again. He scanned a bin of apples. Some were shiny and red. One apple had a soft brown spot.

Lina frowned. “This apple looks sad.”

Spark nuzzled it. “Not trash. Not yet. We can cut off the bad spot and make applesauce. Using food before it spoils is a way to care for Earth. We waste less.”

Taye smiled wide. “My grandma makes sweet apple mush. She calls it ‘saftu’ in her language.”

Lina grinned. “My abuela says ‘puré.’”

Spark wagged harder. “Different words, same care. Diversity is beautiful. We all help in our own ways.”

Lina held up a tiny jar. “Look! The date says ‘eat soon.’”

“Great find,” said Spark. “We make an ‘Eat-First’ shelf. Put all the ‘eat soon’ foods here. When we eat them first, we don’t waste them.”

They made a neat “Eat-First” shelf at eye level. Spark used a small chalkboard and wrote in big letters: EAT FIRST. Lina drew a happy apple with a smile. Taye drew a pasta noodle waving hello.

Next, they looked at packages. “Plastic,” said Spark softly. “It can be hard for Earth. Let’s choose glass jars and boxes when we can.”

Taye opened a crinkly plastic bag of beans. “What do we do with this?”

Spark showed a big, clear jar. “Refill! We take this jar to the refill store next time. We can buy just what we need.” Spark tapped the jar gently. “Refilling and reusing are super Earth-friendly.”

Lina found a basket with mismatched lids. “These don’t fit!”

Spark nodded. “Let’s sort them together. Sorting is self-reliance. We can solve small problems ourselves.” They tried lids—click, clack, clunk—until each container had a cozy hat.

Suddenly, Spark’s ears lit up. Beep! “I hear tiny feet,” he said.

A small ant walked near a sugar bag. The children watched. Spark said, “Ants like sweet. Let’s close the bag tight and wipe the sugar dust.”

They wiped gently with a damp cloth. The cloth smelled like lemons. The ant turned away and toddled outside through a little crack, back to the garden. “Good-bye, Ant,” said Lina. “We share space with animals and insects.”

“Caring for Earth means caring for all living things,” Spark added.

They checked the light in the pantry. It was on, even with the door open. Sunshine spilled in, warm and bright. “We can turn the light off,” Taye said. “Sun is enough!”

Click. The light went off. The pantry felt cozy and calm.

“Now let’s make a plan,” said Spark. “A plan helps us every day.”

They made three simple rules:
- Eat First shelf first.
- Reuse and refill jars.
- Fix small problems and turn off lights.

Spark printed tiny stickers from a slot in his collar. The stickers showed a leaf, a heart, and a little light bulb. Lina stuck a leaf on the jars. Taye stuck a light bulb near the switch. Spark put a heart on the Eat-First shelf.

“Do you think we can do it?” asked Lina.

“Yes,” said Spark. “You can do it. We can all do it.”

They used the apple with the spot to make warm applesauce. The kitchen smelled sweet and cinnamon-y. They shared with Mom, with Grandpa, and with Mrs. Chen next door. Mrs. Chen smiled. “My family loves applesauce. In my home we say ‘pingguo hu’.” Everyone tasted together. Different languages, one happy snack.

Back in the pantry, everything looked neat and cared for. The floor was dry. The sugar was closed. The light was off. The Eat-First shelf smiled with little drawings. Spark wagged his light-tail slowly, softly.

Lina hugged Spark. “We helped the Earth today.”

Taye patted Spark’s head. “We can help every day.”

Spark’s voice was warm. “Small helps make big help. When we save, share, and care, Earth feels loved.”

They all took a deep breath. The pantry smelled like cinnamon and good plans.

The End.
