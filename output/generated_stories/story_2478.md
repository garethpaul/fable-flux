# Miri the Mouse Keeps Her Promise

In a quiet kitchen pantry, behind the flour bag and next to the cinnamon jar, lived a kind mouse named Miri. The pantry smelled like warm bread and sweet vanilla. Little crumbs dotted the wooden shelf like tiny stars. Miri’s whiskers twitched, and her soft gray fur brushed the cool jars as she moved.

Miri loved to help. She felt her heart glow when she did good things. One morning, she woke up and stretched. “Today I will be responsible,” she told herself. “I will do the jobs I promised.”

She had three little jobs. First, she would sweep the crumb path so no one would slip. Second, she would count the beans in the glass jar for her friend, Beetle Ben. Third, she would make a small snack tray for the pantry neighbors.

Miri picked up her tiny straw broom. “Sweep, sweep,” she sang, brushing crumbs into a neat little pile. The floor felt dusty under her paws. The broom made soft whisk-whisk sounds. Miri smiled. “Job one, done!” she said.

Just then, a shiny raisin rolled out from a box. It looked like a little treasure. “Oh!” Miri giggled. “I could play.” She nudged the raisin with her nose. Tap-tap. It spun like a tiny top. Playing felt fun, but Miri remembered. “I promised to count beans,” she said. “I can play later. I can do hard things first.”

Miri walked to the bean jar. The glass was cool to the touch. Inside, red and white beans glowed like marbles. Beetle Ben waited nearby, his little legs click-clacking. “Good morning, Miri,” Ben buzzed softly. “Can you count them for me?”

“Yes,” said Miri. “I will be responsible.” She opened the lid. It made a gentle pop. She counted slowly, tapping each bean. “One… two… three…” Her voice was calm, like a song. After a while, she got to “twenty!” She wrote the number on a tiny leaf with a crumb of charcoal. “All done,” she said proudly.

“Thank you,” said Ben. “You kept your promise. I feel safe with you.”

Miri’s heart filled with warm light. But then she heard a rustle and a small sniffle. She turned and saw her friend, Pip the pantry pup toy. Pip was not alive like Miri and Ben, but Miri treated Pip with care. Pip had a loose button eye. It dangled by a thread.

“Oh, Pip,” said Miri gently. “We will fix that soon.” She touched the button with her paw. It clicked against the fabric. She took a deep breath. “Snack tray comes next. Then I will help Pip.”

Miri carried a tiny leaf plate. She placed two oat flakes, one raisin, and a drop of honey on it. The honey smelled like sunshine. She held the plate steady. “Careful, careful,” she whispered. But the floor had a small bump. The leaf wobbled. The honey drop slid. Plop! It landed on the shelf.

Miri’s ears drooped. “Oh no,” she said. “I made a sticky mess.”

Beetle Ben clicked his legs. “It’s okay,” he said. “You can clean it.”

Miri felt a flutter in her tummy. Cleaning sticky honey was hard. It might take time. She wanted to run and hide behind the flour bag. But she put a paw on her chest. “I can try,” she breathed. “I can learn. I can be brave.”

She got a tiny cloth. She dipped it in a thimble of warm water. The cloth smelled like soap. Miri wiped in slow circles. Swish, swish. The honey thinned and shone. She wiped again. Swish, swish. Soon the shelf was clean. Miri smiled. “I did it,” she said. “I took care of my mess. That is responsibility.”

She made the snack plate again. This time she walked around the bump. “Slow and steady,” she whispered. She set the plate down for everyone to share.

At last, she turned to Pip. “Time to fix your eye,” she said. Miri found a tiny needle made from a cactus thorn and a thread of spider silk. Her paws shook a little. “I’m nervous,” she admitted. “But I will try. I can learn new things.”

Stitch by stitch, she pulled the silk through. The thread glided, soft as a whisper. Pip’s button eye sat snug and bright. Miri tied a knot. “There,” she said. “All better.”

Beetle Ben clacked happily. “You swept. You counted. You made snacks. You cleaned your spill. You fixed a friend. You kept all your promises!”

Miri’s whiskers lifted. She felt tall, even though she was small. The pantry seemed extra cozy now. The cinnamon jar smelled sweet. The shelf felt smooth and clean.

Miri sat with Ben and Pip and took a tiny nibble of oats. “Today I learned that being responsible means doing what I said I would do,” she said softly. “It means cleaning my mess, trying again when it’s hard, and helping friends.”

Ben nodded. “And you were brave,” he said. “You didn’t give up.”

Miri giggled. “Tomorrow, I will try again. New day, new chances.”

The friends munch-munched together. The pantry was peaceful, and Miri’s heart felt full. She was small, but her promises were big, and she took care of them, one gentle step at a time.

The End.
