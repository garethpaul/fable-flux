# Ollie Otter’s Kitchen Dance

In a warm kitchen, sunlight glowed on yellow walls. A yummy smell of cinnamon swirled in the air. Pots clinked softly. The floor felt smooth and cool. This was Ollie’s favorite place.

Ollie was a playful otter with bright eyes and a big, wiggly smile. He liked to slide on the floor in his fuzzy socks. He loved to twirl and clap. Ollie wanted to dance.

“Good morning, Ollie,” said Mama Otter, stirring oatmeal with a wooden spoon. The spoon made a gentle tap-tap sound on the pot.

“Good morning!” Ollie chirped. “Today I will learn a new dance. My body wants to wiggle!”

Mama smiled. “Dancing sounds fun. The kitchen is warm and cozy. It is a good place to practice. But remember: listen, look, and keep it safe.”

“I can do that,” said Ollie. He puffed his cheeks and nodded. “Listen, look, safe.”

Ollie took a deep breath. He felt the warm air fill his chest. He heard the quiet bubble of oatmeal. He smelled sweet cinnamon. His tail swished with excitement.

“Step, step, slide,” Ollie whispered. He tapped his toes—tap, tap—and slid—whoosh! His socks whispered on the floor. He spun in a slow circle and giggled.

Just then, his friend Pip the puppy padded into the kitchen. Pip’s ears flopped, and his little paws clicked on the floor. “Hi, Ollie!” Pip wagged. “I want to dance too!”

“Let’s dance together!” Ollie cheered. “We can be a team.”

They held their paws up and counted. “One, two, three!” Ollie tapped. Pip hopped. Their feet went tap, hop, tap. The wooden spoon went tap-tap on the pot. The kitchen felt like a song.

But then, uh-oh! Pip bumped Ollie’s tail. Ollie bumped the cabinet door. Clunk! Ollie’s whiskers drooped. Pip’s ears lowered.

“Ouch,” Ollie said softly. “That bump made me feel mad and sad.”

Pip looked sorry. “I didn’t mean to bump. I was excited. My hops went too fast.”

Ollie took a slow breath in and out. He put a paw on his chest. “I feel mad,” he said. “But I want to fix it.”

Mama Otter turned down the stove and came closer. Her voice was calm and warm. “I see two dancers who care,” she said. “When there is a bump, we can use our words. We can make a plan. That is how friends solve problems.”

Ollie nodded. “Pip, can we make a dance plan?”

“Yes,” Pip said. His tail made a soft swish-swish. “Teach me the steps.”

Ollie smiled again. “Okay. First, we make space.” He pointed to a sunny square on the floor. “This is your spot, Pip. This is my spot.”

They each stood in a square. “Next, we count together. One, two, three. We move on three.”

They tried. “One, two, three,” said Ollie. Tap-tap. “One, two, three,” said Pip. Hop-hop.

“Great!” said Mama. “You are building skills. You are listening, looking, and waiting.”

Ollie added a clap. “Step, step, slide, clap!” Clap! The sound was bright, like a little bell. Pip added a wiggle. “Hop, hop, wiggle, clap!” Wiggle! The wiggle felt silly and fun.

They laughed. Their feelings felt light again. The kitchen smelled extra sweet, like happy cinnamon.

But soon, Pip forgot to wait. He hopped on “two” and scooted near Ollie’s square.

Ollie noticed. His belly felt tight again. He paused and took a deep breath. “I can use my words,” he told himself.

“Pip,” he said kindly, “please wait for three. I need space to slide.”

Pip blinked. “Oops. Thank you for telling me. I will try again.”

They counted slower. “One… two… three.” Their feet and paws moved together. Slide, hop, clap! The sounds were gentle: tap, whoosh, clap. The dance felt smooth and safe.

“You did it!” Mama clapped with the wooden spoon. “You worked together. You solved the bump with words. You practiced. That is skill-building.”

Ollie beamed. “Dancing with a friend is fun. Dancing with a plan is safer.”

Pip wagged. “And it feels good to fix a problem.”

Ollie had an idea. “Let’s make a special kitchen dance. We can call it the Cinnamon Slide.”

They made steps:

- Step, step (soft like flour).
- Slide (smooth like butter).
- Wiggle (silly like noodles).
- Clap (bright like a bell).
- Freeze and smile!

They practiced their new dance. The floor felt cool under their feet. The air felt warm on their fur and ears. Their giggles bubbled like oatmeal. They danced the Cinnamon Slide again and again, counting together every time.

At last, Mama poured oatmeal into bowls. Steam curled up in happy swirls. “Dancers need breakfast,” she said.

Ollie and Pip sat at the table. The bowls felt warm in their paws. They took careful bites. “Mmm,” Ollie said. “Sweet and cozy.”

Pip nodded. “My body feels calm. My heart feels proud.”

Ollie looked at his friend. “Today I learned a big lesson,” he said. “When we dance, we listen, look, and make space. We use our words to fix bumps. We practice to build skills. That makes dancing safe and fun.”

Pip wagged again. “And friends who listen can dance together all day.”

They tapped their spoons in a gentle rhythm: tap-tap, tap-tap. The kitchen glowed with warm light, warm smells, and warm smiles.

The End.
