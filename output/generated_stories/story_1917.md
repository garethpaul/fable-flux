# Buckle-Up Bunny and the Calm-Down Plan

Benny was a determined rabbit. His ears stood tall like two soft spoons, and his whiskers wiggled when he had a big idea. Today, Benny was riding in the family car with Mama Rabbit, Papa Rabbit, and Baby Sis. The car smelled like fresh carrots and clean seats. The seat belts clicked—click, click!—and the engine hummed like a sleepy bee.

Benny held his little notebook. He loved writing. He wrote tiny letters with a short pencil that smelled like wood. “I’m going to write a story about the park!” he said. “I will make it just right.”

Mama Rabbit smiled. “That’s a good plan, Benny.”

The car rolled onto the road. Trees waved hello. Sunlight popped in and out, making gentle stripes on Benny’s paws. Benny opened his notebook. He took a deep breath. He wanted his story to be perfect. “I must start now,” he said.

But Baby Sis kicked her feet. Thump-thump!

Papa turned on the air. Whoosh!

A loud truck roared by. Rrrr-rrr-rrrr!

Benny’s ears drooped. His pencil slipped. His letters wiggled. “Oh no,” he said. “It’s not neat. It’s not perfect.”

His tummy felt tight. His paws felt pokey. He wanted to yell, “Be quiet!” He wanted to fling his pencil.

Mama looked back. “Benny,” she said in a gentle voice, “what does your body feel like?”

Benny put a paw on his chest. “Fast,” he said. “Like drum-drum-drum.”

Papa nodded. “That feeling is big,” he said. “What is your plan?”

Benny blinked. He was a determined rabbit. He had a plan! He had learned it in Bunny School. “Stop. Breathe. Choose,” Benny whispered.

He closed his eyes. He took a slow breath in. Smell the carrot soup. He held it. One, two. He blew out softly. Cool the carrot soup.

He did it again. In. Hold. Out. His tummy softened. His paws relaxed. “Okay,” he said, “I can choose.”

Benny looked at his things. He had his notebook. He had his pencil. He had a little pouch with a tiny trash bag, a small eraser, and a clip. He liked to keep his things neat. It was good for the Earth, too. He reused paper and put trash in the bag. “Organization helps me,” he told himself.

He clipped the pencil to the notebook so it would not fall. He put the eraser in the pouch. He tucked the trash bag in the side pocket. “There,” he said. “All set.”

The truck was gone. The air made a soft whoosh. Baby Sis tapped her toes, not so loud now. Benny opened his notebook again.

“First,” he said, “I will make a plan for my story.” He drew three little boxes.

In the first box, he drew the park gate. In the second box, he drew a big tree. In the third box, he drew a bench and a rainbow. “Now I know the order,” he said. “That helps me.”

He started to write. “At the park, we share,” he said as he wrote the words. His letters still wiggled a little. He frowned. He felt the tight tummy coming back.

He stopped. He put a paw on his chest. “Stop. Breathe. Choose.” Smell the carrot soup. Hold. Cool the soup. The tightness went away.

Mama said, “Benny, that was strong self-control.”

Benny smiled. “Self-regulation,” he said. He liked big words.

Papa laughed. “Yes, self-regulation. You used your plan.”

Baby Sis reached for the pencil. “Mine?” she chirped.

Benny looked at her. He thought about fairness. Justice means fair for everyone. He did not want to shout. He did not want to grab. He took a breath. “We can share,” he said. “I will write three lines. Then you can draw one picture.”

Baby Sis clapped. “Yay!”

Benny wrote three lines. He counted them. One, two, three. Then he passed the pencil. Baby Sis drew a round sun with a smile. The pencil made a soft scratch-scratch sound. It felt calm in the car.

Benny saw a tiny wrapper on the floor. He picked it up. “We keep the car clean,” he said. He tucked it into the little trash bag. “That helps our family and the Earth.” He felt proud.

The car turned and stopped. The blinker made a tick-tick-tick sound. Mama said, “We are almost home.”

Benny checked his boxes. Gate, tree, bench. He finished his lines. He added a small note: “We share. We care. We keep things clean.” He used his eraser gently. Rub-rub. No tearing. He clipped the pencil back. Snap.

Papa parked the car. The engine quieted. Benny read his story out loud. His voice was bright and steady.

“At the park, we share the slide. We wait our turn. We help our friends. We put wrappers in the trash. We use our words. We take deep breaths. The end.”

Mama and Papa clapped. Baby Sis cheered, “Story!”

Benny’s ears stood tall again. “When I felt big feelings, I used my calm-down plan,” he said. “I stopped. I breathed. I chose. I got organized. I shared. That is fair.”

Mama hugged him. “That is justice,” she said. “And that is kindness.”

Papa nodded. “And that is sustainability—taking care of our things and our Earth.”

Benny hugged his notebook. It felt smooth and warm from his paws. He smiled. “I can do hard things,” he said. “I can be calm.”

The family unbuckled. Click, click. The car smelled like carrots and clean seats and a little like victory.

Benny whispered, “I am a determined rabbit with a calm heart.”

The End.
