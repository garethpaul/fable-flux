# Tiko the Tortoise Tells the Truth

Under the creaky old house was a small basement lab. The air felt cool and smelled a little like lemons and books. Bright stickers from many countries covered the walls—patterns from Ghana, flowers from Japan, stars from Mexico, and tiny dancing elephants from India. Beep, beep went a friendly machine that counted steps. Whirr, whirr hummed a gentle fan.

In the middle of the room stood Tiko, a wise tortoise with warm eyes and a speckled shell. He wore a tiny blue scarf. “Good morning,” said Tiko. His voice was soft and slow. “Today is a day for learning.”

On the table sat bowls of colorful snacks. Red strawberries shone like little hearts. Green cucumber slices looked like moons. Orange carrots crunched when you pressed them. A small card by each bowl said “Share.”

Tiko looked around. “Friends, are you ready?” he called.

Down the wooden steps came his helpers. Mei from China wore bright red shoes. Samir from Egypt had a golden scarf. Lila from Brazil wore beads that clicked. Kofi from Ghana wore a cheerful kente wristband. They waved and smiled.

“We will make rainbow salad,” said Tiko. “Red, orange, green, and more. Good food helps our bodies grow strong.” He tapped his shell. “Crunch, crunch,” he joked. Everyone giggled.

They washed hands. Splash, splash. They smelled the fruits. Sweet. Fresh. They listened to the carrots. Snap! Tiko showed a safe, slow way to cut soft things with a child-safe cutter. “We are brave when we try new things,” he said, “and we are honest with our words.”

Mei placed strawberries in a big bowl. Lila added mango. Samir sprinkled chickpeas. Kofi squeezed a lemon. “Sour!” he laughed, making a silly face. They mixed the salad with a wooden spoon. Swish, swish.

On a lower shelf sat a shiny, round button. A small sign said, “Press only with a grown-up.” The button made the machine play music and count steps in many languages. Tiko noticed the button and nodded. “We wait for help with that,” he said kindly. “Rules keep us safe.”

While Tiko checked the recipe, the room was very quiet. Mei looked at the button. It was so shiny. She felt a tickle in her tummy. “I want to hear the music,” she whispered.

Lila looked, too. Her beads clicked. “We can ask Tiko,” she said.

Kofi held the spoon and watched the salad swirl. “Let’s wait,” he said. “We promised.”

Samir rubbed his scarf. He was curious. His finger wiggled. He reached toward the button, then pulled back. “Hmm,” he hummed.

Tiko turned around. “Almost ready,” he said. “We will—”

Click.

The button lit up. Lights flashed rainbow colors. The machine beeped very fast. Beep-beep-beep! A whirr grew loud. Whirr-rr-rr!

Everyone jumped. Mei’s eyes grew wide. Lila held her beads still. Kofi gripped the spoon. Samir’s cheeks felt warm.

Tiko blinked. He looked at the button. He looked at his friends. His voice stayed calm and kind. “Friends,” he said softly, “someone pressed the button. It is okay to make mistakes. It is brave to tell the truth.”

The room got very quiet again. Samir took a tiny breath. He put his hand on his heart. “Tiko,” he said, “I did it. I pressed the button. I was curious. I am sorry.”

Tiko’s eyes were gentle. “Thank you for your honest words,” he said. “That is brave.” He nodded to Samir. “We all feel curious. We all make mistakes. Telling the truth helps us fix things.”

Mei stepped closer. “I wanted to press it, too,” she said. “But I didn’t.”

“That is honest, too,” said Tiko. “Thank you.”

Tiko showed Samir how to hold the power switch. “I will help you,” he said. Together they turned the machine off. The lights dimmed. The beeps slowed. Beep… beep… quiet.

The friends let out their breaths. “We did it,” said Lila, smiling. “Teamwork!”

Tiko clapped his little feet. Tap, tap. “Now we know: ask first. Stay safe. Tell the truth.”

They gathered around the table. “In my home in Brazil,” said Lila, “we add lime to salad.” She squeezed a little and the room smelled bright and fresh.

“In my home in Egypt,” said Samir, “we use chickpeas.” He sprinkled more with care.

“In my home in China,” said Mei, “we like crunchy cucumbers.” She added extra circles.

“In my home in Ghana,” said Kofi, “we love sweet pineapple.” He popped in golden cubes.

Tiko smiled. “All our homes bring colors and flavors. That is cultural diversity. Different foods. Different ways. All together in one bowl.”

They tasted the salad. Crunch, munch. Sweet, sour, salty, and bright. “Yum!” they sang.

Tiko raised his spoon. “What did we learn today?”

Samir stood tall. “I learned that telling the truth is brave.”

Mei nodded. “I learned to ask before I press a button.”

Lila said, “I learned that friends help each other.”

Kofi added, “I learned that many foods make us strong.”

Tiko’s eyes shone. “Honesty keeps us safe. Honesty keeps our hearts light. We tell the truth, even when it is hard, and then we can fix problems together.”

They cleaned the table. Swish, wipe. They stacked bowls. Clink, clink. They waved to the flags and stickers on the wall. “Good-bye, colors!” they cheered.

As they walked up the steps, the basement lab felt warm and happy. The air smelled like lemon and mango. Tiko followed slowly, his scarf fluttering. He felt proud of his friends.

“Remember,” he said, “our words matter. Tell the truth. Be brave. Enjoy good food. Celebrate our different homes.”

Everyone smiled. Their hearts felt light as feathers.

The End.
