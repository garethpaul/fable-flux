# The Little Trunk Helper

Tipu was a gentle elephant calf with soft, flappy ears and a small, wiggly trunk. One sunny afternoon, Tipu climbed the creaky wooden stairs to the quiet attic. The attic smelled like old books and cinnamon dust. Sunbeams slid through a tiny window and made golden shapes on the floor.

Tipu’s friend, Nana, an old woven basket, sat by a stack of puzzles and buttons and shiny stamps. “Hello, Tipu,” creaked Nana in a friendly voice. “Today is a good day to practice a new skill.”

Tipu’s eyes grew wide. “A skill? What kind of skill?” He swished his little tail and his trunk made a soft whooo.

“Using your trunk with care,” said Nana. “Gentle lifting. Careful placing. You can learn, step by step.”

Tipu shuffled his feet. “I’m little. What if I can’t?” His ears drooped.

“You can,” said Nana. “Try one small thing.”

Tipu took a deep breath. He felt the cool attic air tickle his nose. He reached out with his trunk toward a soft, blue scarf. The scarf felt smooth and cool. Tipu picked it up and placed it in a small box. The box made a tiny tap when the scarf landed.

“I did it!” Tipu giggled.

“Good start,” said Nana. “Now try buttons.” The buttons were in many colors—red like apples, green like leaves, yellow like sunshine. They clicked together when Tipu stirred them with his trunk.

Tipu tried to pick up a red button. Plink! It slipped and fell. He tried again, slower. Plink… plop! It fell again. Tipu’s eyes felt watery.

Nana hummed softly. “Pause. Feel. Breathe in… and out. Skills grow when we practice.”

Tipu sniffled. He breathed in, and out. He felt the round edge of the red button with his trunk tip. He squeezed just a little, not too hard, not too soft. Lift… and place. Tap! The red button sat in a little cup.

“I did it!” Tipu clapped his feet. His ears went flap-flap.

“Try three,” Nana smiled.

Tipu made a tiny game. “Red, green, yellow,” he sang. Lift, place, lift, place, lift, place. Tap, tap, tap. The sounds were like happy raindrops. Tipu felt warm and proud inside.

On a dusty shelf, Tipu found a book of stamps from many places. The stamps showed bright flags, smiling children, tall trees, and big cities. “Who are they?” Tipu asked, pointing to a stamp with kids waving.

“They are friends from far away,” said Nana. “We share one big world. We care for each other. That is being a good neighbor to our planet.”

Tipu traced the stamp with his trunk. “I want to help our friends.”

“You can start right here,” Nana said kindly. “Let’s make a Sharing Box. We will sort things neatly. When someone needs something, we can give it.”

Tipu’s heart fluttered. “I can do that! I will use my careful trunk.”

Together, they made three little piles:
- Soft things: scarves and mittens, squish-squish.
- Bright things: buttons and stickers, click-click.
- Letters and stamps: rustle-rustle.

Tipu drew tiny pictures on labels: a mitten, a button, a stamp. He stuck the labels on the boxes, smooth and straight. The attic felt cozy and busy. Sunbeams warmed Tipu’s back.

Then Tipu saw a jigsaw puzzle with a picture of the earth—blue and green, swirly and bright. “Can we fix this puzzle?” he asked.

“We can try,” said Nana. “Find the edges first.”

Tipu tapped pieces gently. He looked for straight sides. “This one!” he said. Piece by piece, click-click, the puzzle grew. Sometimes a piece did not fit. Tipu frowned. “Maybe this one?” He turned it. No. He tried another. Yes! It clicked in. He smiled big.

“You are using your eyes and your trunk,” Nana said. “That is skill-building.”

Tipu whispered, “I can learn. I can try.”

At last, the earth puzzle was whole. Tipu touched the blue oceans. “Water,” he murmured. He touched the green shapes. “Land.” He smiled at the small stamps around the edges. “Friends everywhere.”

Tipu and Nana sat quietly. The attic window let in a soft breeze. It smelled like rain and old paper. Tipu listened. He could hear tiny roof birds cheep-cheep.

Nana broke the quiet. “Tipu, what did you learn today?”

Tipu lifted his chin. “I learned to use my trunk with care. I learned to sort and to try again. When I slow down and breathe, I can do hard things.” He patted the Sharing Boxes. “And I can help friends, near and far.”

Nana’s basket sides creaked with happiness. “You built skills. You grew confidence. You used your skills to care for our world. That is wise.”

Tipu’s ears wiggled. His heart felt steady and brave. “Tomorrow, I will practice again,” he said. “I will teach my friends how to make Sharing Boxes. We can help together.”

The sun made one last gold square on the floor. Tipu hummed his little song—lift, place, click, tap—and trotted down the stairs. His steps sounded light and sure.

He whispered, “I can learn. I can help. I belong to a big, kind world.”

The End.
