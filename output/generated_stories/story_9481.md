# Owl Hoots on the Balcony

On a small balcony garden, high above a busy street, a wise old owl named Oren lived in a cozy wooden nest box. The balcony had pots of mint, soft basil, bright tomatoes, and tiny strawberries that glowed like little red buttons. The air smelled like green leaves and sweet soil. Wind chimes tinkled like tiny bells.

Every morning, Oren opened his round golden eyes and hooted, “Hoo-hoo, good day!” He stretched his wings slowly, like a gentle hug, and looked over the plants. He loved caring for them. He loved the quiet rustle of leaves. He loved to share old owl traditions, too.

One sunny day, a little neighbor named Mia peeked through the railing. She had a watering can with a smiling sun painted on it. Her shoes were bright blue and squeaked when she walked.

“Hello, Owl,” Mia said softly. “Can I help?”

Oren blinked kindly. “Hoo-hoo, yes. Today is Balcony Garden Day. In my owl family, we have a tradition. We share helpful ways to care for our home. We sing the Watering Song, we count the leaves, and we always take a rest after work.”

Mia’s eyes sparkled. “A tradition? Can I learn it?”

“Of course,” Oren hooted. “Traditions are gifts we share.”

Oren fluffed his feathers and began the Watering Song in a warm, smooth voice:
“Little sip, not a flood,
Give the roots a gentle hug.”
He tapped the rim of the pot with a soft, rhythmic beak-tock.

Mia giggled and sang along, “Little sip, not a flood!” She tipped her can slowly. Water sparkled in the sunlight and made a soft shhh sound as it kissed the soil.

Oren nodded. “That’s responsibility, little one. We care for our plants every day, not too much, not too little.”

They walked along the balcony. The mint leaves smelled cool and fresh. The basil was spicy-sweet. A shy ladybug climbed a green stem. Mia held out her finger, and the ladybug tickled her skin.

“Another owl tradition,” Oren said gently, “is Leaf Counting. We count to check how each plant is growing. Counting helps us notice changes.”

Mia clapped. “I can count! One, two, three!” She counted the strawberry leaves. “Four, five, six!” She counted the basil leaves. Her voice was bright and bouncy.

“Very good,” Oren hooted. “You are being careful and kind.”

Mia touched a tomato. It was orange, not red yet. “Is it ready?”

“Not yet,” Oren replied. “Waiting is part of responsibility. We harvest when the fruit is ready. That keeps our plants strong.”

A warm breeze puffed along the balcony. The wind chimes chimed. Mia wiped a tiny bead of sweat from her forehead. “I’m a little tired,” she said.

Oren tucked his wings close. “Then it is time for the Resting Breath. In my family, we say self-care helps us help others. When we care for ourselves, we can care for our garden, too.”

He showed Mia how to breathe in slowly and breathe out softly. “Smell the mint,” he said. “Blow the wind chime.”

Mia sniffed the mint. It smelled like toothpaste and cool rain. She blew gently. The chimes ting-ting-tinged like friendly stars. She smiled. “I feel better!”

Oren looked at the strawberry pot. “Now for the Sharing Bowl,” he said. He placed a small wooden bowl on the railing. “In this tradition, we share what the garden gives. We take just enough, and we give thanks.”

He turned to Mia. “Would you like to pick one strawberry?”

Mia nodded. She chose a bright red berry and placed it in the bowl. Oren picked one, too.

“Before we eat,” Oren hooted softly, “we say thank you.” Together they said, “Thank you, garden. Thank you, hands. Thank you, sunny day.”

They each took a small bite. The strawberry was juicy and sweet, and red juice tickled Mia’s tongue. She giggled. “It tastes like a happy song!”

Oren’s eyes shone. “Now you know our balcony traditions: singing the Watering Song, Leaf Counting, the Resting Breath, and the Sharing Bowl.”

Mia stood tall. “I can help every day. I can be responsible. I can take care of me. And I can share!”

Oren fluffed his feathers with pride. “Hoo-hoo, that is the heart of tradition. We pass down good ways, we care for our home, and we care for ourselves.”

The sky turned soft and golden. Birds chirped in the distance. Mia waved. “Goodbye, Oren! See you tomorrow for Balcony Garden Day!”

“Goodbye, little gardener,” Oren hooted. “Remember: Little sip, not a flood; count and notice; rest and breathe; share with thanks.”

Mia skipped away with her squeaky blue shoes. Oren looked over the leaves one more time. The balcony felt peaceful and bright.

And the garden, and Oren, and Mia’s happy heart, all grew a little more.

The End.
