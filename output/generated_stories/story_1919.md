# Buckle-Up Bunny’s Big Car Trip

Benny Bunny was a small, determined rabbit with soft gray fur and bright, bouncy ears. Today, he was going on a car trip with his family. The family car smelled like warm crackers and lemon wipes. The seats felt smooth, and the sun made sparkly dots on the windows.

“I can do it myself,” Benny said, puffing his cheeks with pride.

Mama Rabbit smiled. “I know you can be independent, Benny. Let’s get organized.”

Benny hopped into his seat. His favorite carrot snack was in a little orange cup. His book was in a small blue bag. His water bottle sat in a holder that felt cool when he touched it.

“I want to buckle my seatbelt all by myself,” Benny said, wiggling his paws.

“Okay,” Mama Rabbit said softly. “First, take a calm breath. Then listen for the click.”

Benny grabbed the buckle. It felt hard and cold. His little paws were wiggly. He pushed. The buckle slipped. “Ugh!” he grunted. His ears drooped.

Papa Rabbit turned the key. The car made a gentle hum. “We have time,” Papa said. “Try again. Slow and steady.”

Benny squeezed his eyes shut. He felt hot frustration in his tummy, like a tiny fire. His paws wanted to squeeze and stomp.

“I’m mad!” he said, his voice a little loud.

Mama Rabbit nodded. “It’s okay to feel mad. Let’s use our calm tools.”

“What are calm tools?” Benny asked.

“Listening and breathing,” Mama said. “Smell the flower.” She sniffed in. “Blow the candle.” She blew out gently.

Benny copied. He breathed in. The car smelled like lemon and carrots. He breathed out. His tummy felt softer.

“Now,” Mama said, “let’s get organized. Seatbelt first, snack second, book last.”

Benny nodded. “Seatbelt first.” He placed his book back in the blue bag. He put the carrot cup in the holder. Click, clack, tidy and neat.

He tried the buckle again. He used both paws, just like Mama showed last time. He lined up the metal piece with the slot. He listened. Click? Not yet. It slid away. His ears twitched.

“I can’t!” he cried. Tears sparkled in his eyes.

Papa Rabbit turned and met Benny’s eyes. “You can’t yet. That means you are still learning. Let’s try a plan.”

“A plan?” Benny sniffled.

“Step one,” Papa said, holding up a finger, “wiggle your seat so the belt is flat.” Benny wiggled. The belt felt smooth now, not twisty.

“Step two,” Mama said, “hold the buckle with your steady paw. That’s your strong helper.” Benny used his left paw to hold the buckle still. It felt firm.

“Step three,” Papa said, “push the metal piece in with your other paw. Slow and straight.”

Benny took a calm breath. Smell the flower. Blow the candle. He pushed. The buckle slid in. He listened hard. The car was quiet except for a soft bird song outside.

Click!

Benny’s eyes went wide. “I did it! I did it myself!”

“You did,” Mama cheered. “Independent Benny!”

Benny’s chest felt warm and proud. He wiggled his toes in his shoes. “Snack now,” he said, “because snack is second.” He crunched a carrot. Crunch, crunch. It tasted sweet and salty at the same time.

As the car rolled along, the road made a gentle rumble. Trees wooshed by the window. Benny opened his blue bag and pulled out his book. He turned the pages. Flip, flip. The pictures were bright and happy.

After a while, the car stopped at a red light. Benny dropped his book by accident. It slid under the seat. “Oh no!” he said. His tummy squeezed again.

Mama Rabbit looked back. “Let’s use our skills.”

Benny nodded slowly. “First, calm tools.” He smelled the flower. He blew the candle. His shoulders relaxed.

“Second,” he said, “get organized.” He looked around. “I can use my long ears!” He lowered an ear and nudged the book closer. “Almost!” He used his paw to reach. The cover felt smooth under his fingers. “Got it!”

“Great listening to yourself,” Papa said. “You made a plan.”

Benny grinned. “I listened, I planned, I did it.”

Soon they reached the park. The sun felt warm on Benny’s fur. The air smelled like fresh grass. Birds chirped peep-peep!

Mama Rabbit unbuckled her seatbelt. Benny waited, then touched his buckle. “Can I do it myself?” he asked.

“You can try,” Mama said.

Benny pressed the red button. Pop! The belt slid back. He climbed out carefully. “Independent Benny!” he sang.

At the park, he carried his blue bag by the handle. He held his water bottle with the other paw. “I am organized,” he told himself. “Seatbelt first, snack second, book last.”

Mama hugged him. “You did big things today.”

Benny hugged back. “When I feel mad,” he said, “I can breathe. I can listen. I can make a plan. I can do it myself.”

“That’s right,” Papa said. “Being independent means trying, listening, and calming down when it’s hard.”

Benny’s ears wiggled in the sunshine. “I’m ready for more big tries!”

The End.
