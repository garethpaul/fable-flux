# Little Lark in the Dining Room

Little Lark was a small singing bird with bright blue feathers and a sunny yellow chest. One warm morning, she fluttered through an open window and landed gently on the dining room chair. The room smelled like warm toast and fresh apples. Sunlight made little squares on the tablecloth. The forks and spoons made tiny clinks as the house was waking up.

“Hello?” sang Little Lark in a soft trill. “Is anyone home?”

The dining room was quiet. A glass of water sparkled. A bowl of apples sat in the center of the table like a red and green mountain. Little Lark’s tummy gave a tiny rumble. She wanted a taste, but she also remembered: the apples were not hers.

“I can find my own food,” she told herself, tapping her little beak. “I am a clever bird. I can be brave and do things myself.”

She hopped to the windowsill and peered outside. The garden was full of green leaves and little seeds on the ground. But she was inside, and the window screen was closed. “Hmm,” she chirped. “I will wait and think.”

Little Lark sang a thinking song. “Tweet-a-dee, tweet-a-doo, I can figure out what to do.” Her song was bright like the sun, and it helped her feel calm.

Then, footsteps! Soft and slow. Mia, a small child with curly hair, peeked into the dining room. She wore a blue shirt with a star on it. She saw Little Lark and gasped, but not too loud.

“Hello, little bird,” Mia whispered. “Are you okay?”

Little Lark tilted her head. “I’m all right,” she sang gently. “I came in the window. I am hungry, but I want to use my own skills. The apples look yummy, but they are yours.”

Mia smiled. “Thank you for asking. These are my family’s apples. They are for breakfast. We can share, but let’s think first.”

Little Lark’s heart felt warm. “I like sharing,” she sang, “but I also want to learn to care for myself. I can find seeds. I can sip water. I just need a safe way out.”

Mia nodded. “That is a good plan. You want to do it yourself. I will not touch you. I will give you space.”

Little Lark fluttered her wings, relieved. “Thank you for understanding my boundary,” she sang. “I feel safe when I have space.”

Mia stepped back. She opened the back door with a gentle creak. Cool morning air moved into the dining room. It smelled like wet grass and flowers. Outside, Little Lark could see a patch of bright yellow sun and tiny seeds on the patio.

“Tweet! That looks perfect,” Little Lark said. “But the screen is still closed on the window.”

Mia nodded again. “The screen keeps bugs out. It also keeps birds in. I will use the door. I will stand far away so you can fly.”

Mia walked to the door and propped it open with a small mat. She pointed to the open space. “There you go,” she said softly. “You can try.”

Little Lark took a breath. She wiggled her tail. Her wings felt light and ready. “I can do it,” she told herself. “I can be strong and careful.”

She hopped to the edge of the chair. She looked at the open door. The sunlight made a golden path on the floor. Little Lark sang, “I am brave. I am kind. I can use my clever mind.” Then she flew—whoosh!—across the dining room, over the bowl of apples, past the sparkling glass of water, and out into the fresh air.

The garden greeted her with soft wind and the sound of leaves whispering. She landed on the patio and pecked at tiny seeds. Crunch, crunch. She sipped a drop of morning dew from a leaf. It tasted cool and clean.

Mia stood in the doorway and waved. “You did it yourself,” she said. “I’m proud of you.”

Little Lark chirped happily. “Thank you for helping by giving me space. You were kind and careful.”

Mia smiled. “We can share the world. You have the garden. I have the dining room. We can take care of both.”

Little Lark looked around. “Yes,” she sang. “I will not make a mess inside. I will take only what I need outside. That helps the earth. That is good conservation.”

Mia nodded. “I will keep the window screen closed so birds stay safe. And if a friend needs help, I will ask first and listen.”

Little Lark fluffed her feathers. She felt strong. She had found food. She had respected the home. She had used her own skills. And Mia had respected her boundaries and shown understanding.

“Tweet-a-dee, tweet-a-doo,” Little Lark sang. “I can do many things. I can ask for help, and I can do it myself, too.”

The sun climbed higher. The dining room glowed warm and bright. The garden swayed and shimmered.

Little Lark took one more sip of dew and called, “Good-bye, Mia!”

“Good-bye, Little Lark!” Mia called back. “Have a happy day!”

And they both felt proud—Mia for listening and caring, and Little Lark for being brave and self-reliant.

The End.
