# The Little Song in the Attic

In a quiet attic, where dust sparkled like tiny stars, a small singing bird lived by a round window. The bird had blue feathers, a bright eye, and a sweet voice that sounded like “twiddle-dee, twiddle-doo.” The attic smelled like old paper and warm wood. Sunlight made golden stripes on the floor. It was calm and still.

“Good morning, attic!” sang the bird. “Good morning, sun!”

The bird liked to hop along a wooden beam and look at boxes. The bird was very curious. “What is in this box?” the bird chirped. “What is that shiny lid?” The bird pecked gently and listened. Tap, tap. The sound echoed softly. The bird felt happy to explore.

One day, the bird found a big bag on the floor. The bag was full of crinkly, colorful things. The bird tugged at a strip. It stretched and snapped. “Oh!” said the bird, surprised. The strip was sticky and soft. The bird tilted its head. “This could help me build a cozy nest!”

The bird used its beak to pull a long piece of the stretchy strip. It felt smooth like rubber. The bird carried it to the window. But the strip fell and landed in a loop. The bird stepped into the loop. The strip grabbed the bird’s leg. “Eep!” peeped the bird. The bird pulled, but the loop pulled too. The bird’s heart thumped. The attic felt too big now.

“Help,” the bird sang, voice shaky. “Twiddle… please?”

Soft feet pattered on the stairs. A child named Mira peeked into the attic. She wore a green shirt with a tiny leaf on it, and her hair smelled like soap and sunshine. “Hello?” she whispered. She heard the worried peeps. Mira climbed up carefully. Creak, creak went the steps.

“Oh no,” said Mira when she saw the bird. “You’re stuck!” Her voice was gentle. “It’s okay, little singer. I’ll help.”

Mira knelt and spoke softly. “I won’t hurt you.” The bird trembled but looked into her kind eyes. Mira slowly loosened the sticky loop. It stretched and slipped free. The bird shook its leg. “Twiddle… thank you,” sang the bird. The bird’s voice fluttered like a soft ribbon.

Mira picked up the stretchy strip. She frowned a little. “This is trouble,” she said. “This strip is trash. Animals can get stuck. That’s not good for our world.”

The bird tilted its head. “Trash?” it chirped. “What is trash? What can I do?”

Mira smiled. “Trash is something we don’t use. But we must be careful. We must keep it out of nature. We can sort it. We can reuse. We can keep animals safe.” She showed the bird a small bin with a blue symbol. “This bin is for recycling. We clean and sort things so they can be used again. That helps the Earth.”

The bird ruffled its feathers. “I wanted a nest,” it sang softly. “But this strip was bad for me.”

Mira nodded. “Your curiosity was brave. You learned something new. That’s good. Let’s make a plan together. A compromise,” she said slowly, sounding out the big word. “That means we work together so we both feel good. You want a safe nest. I want a clean attic and a safe Earth. Let’s find safe things you can use.”

They looked around. The attic held many gentle smells: dry leaves, clean cotton, and old wood. Mira opened a box. Inside were soft wool scraps. She held one to her cheek. “So soft,” she said. She found a small basket made of twigs, a tiny rolled paper tube, and a handful of clean yarn pieces.

“Twiddle-dee!” sang the bird. The yarn felt light and not sticky. The wool felt warm. The twig basket was cozy. The bird hopped inside and turned in a circle. Swish, swish went its little feet.

Mira gathered the dangerous trash—the sticky strips, the loose rings, the sharp bits—and put them in a bag. She snipped the loops so nothing could trap a foot or wing. Snip, snip. She put clean, safe things in another box with a label: “For Bird’s Nest.”

“Curiosity helped us learn,” said Mira. “Conservation means we take care of our home—the Earth. We sort trash. We reuse. We keep animals safe.”

The bird sang a bright note. “I will help too,” it said. “I will not pull sticky strips. I will look for safe bits. I will sing when I see danger.”

From then on, the attic felt busy but calm. When the sun painted warm gold stripes, the bird sang, “Twiddle-dee, twiddle-doo, take care of our home, me and you!” When Mira visited, she checked the bins, snipped loops, and smiled. They shared the attic, and they shared the job of caring.

One rainy afternoon, they heard pitter-pat on the roof. The bird cuddled in its safe, soft nest. “Thank you,” it sang. “This nest is perfect.”

Mira patted the basket gently. “We did it together,” she said. “That’s compromise. That’s conservation. That’s kindness.”

The bird’s song filled the quiet attic. The dust sparkled. The air smelled like clean wool and warm wood. The world felt safer and loved.

And the little bird sang a promise: “I will care. I will share. I will be curious and kind.”

The End.
