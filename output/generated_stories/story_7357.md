# Poppy the Helpful Parrot

Poppy was a bright green parrot with a yellow belly and a big, curious beak. She lived on a sunny balcony garden with pots of mint, basil, strawberries, and tiny tomatoes. Every morning, Poppy hopped along the warm tiles and said, “Good morning, garden!” The air smelled like sweet strawberries and fresh leaves. Bees buzzed softly, and the wind tickled Poppy’s feathers.

Poppy loved to create things. She made a little bell out of a spoon and a string. She built a tiny flag from a leaf and a stick. She even painted with blueberry juice, dabbing dots on a smooth stone. Poppy chirped, “I am a creative parrot!”

One hot morning, Poppy noticed something wrong. The leaves looked droopy. The soil felt dry and dusty under her claws. “Oh no,” Poppy said. “The garden is thirsty.” She looked around. Her friend Mia, who lived in the apartment, would usually water the plants before school. But Mia had left early today. The watering can sat empty by the door.

Poppy fluffed her feathers and thought hard. “I want to help,” she said. “But how?” She listened. Drip, drip, drip. She heard water from the balcony tap. Poppy hopped closer and pecked gently. “I can’t twist the tap,” she said. “My beak is not a hand.” Poppy felt worried. “What can I do to help my garden?”

She looked at the things she had made. The spoon bell chimed when the breeze moved it. Ding, ding. Poppy smiled. “I can make a plan.” She remembered Mia’s routine: water, feed the fish, then brush teeth. Poppy said, “Routine helps us remember. I can make a garden routine, too.”

First, Poppy needed water. She used her creative mind. She found an old paper cup near the paint stones. She found a clean leaf shaped like a bowl. She flapped to the tap and held the cup under the slow drip. Drip, drip, drip. The cup filled a little. “It takes time,” Poppy said. “I can be patient.” She carried the small cup to the tomato pot and poured. Glug. The soil turned dark and soft. “Good job,” she told herself. “I can do this, one small cup at a time.”

Poppy made a watering song to keep her going. “Cup and drip, tip and sip, happy plants, don’t you slip!” She sang and danced. The bell chimed. The wind was warm on her wings. She watered the mint, the basil, and the strawberries. The leaves lifted, like smiles. Poppy felt proud. “Helping feels good,” she said.

But then Poppy saw a problem. The big sunflower still drooped. It needed more water than the little cup could give. Poppy took a deep breath. “When something is hard, I can try another way.” She looked around again. She saw her blueberry-painted stone. She saw the spoon bell. She saw the empty watering can. Poppy had an idea. “I can make a helpful tool!”

She flew to the kitchen window and called, “Mia! Mia!” No answer. Poppy spotted a clean plastic bottle on the counter. The window was open a crack. With careful pecks and gentle pulls, Poppy nudged the bottle through the opening. “I can do hard things if I try safely,” she told herself.

Poppy carried the bottle to the tap and pressed the bottle mouth to the drip. She used the string from her bell to tie the bottle to the railing so it would not fall. “Now the bottle can catch water while I rest,” she said. She waited, listening to the drip and to the city sounds below—cars humming, a dog barking, someone laughing. The bottle slowly filled. Poppy sipped some cool air and felt calm.

When the bottle was full, Poppy untied it carefully and poured water into the watering can. Swish! Then she pushed the can near the sunflower and tipped it with her shoulder and beak. Water flowed like rain. The sunflower perked up, tall and bright. Poppy clapped her wings. “We did it!”

Just then, the door opened. Mia stepped onto the balcony. She saw the wet soil, the tied string, the bottle, and the happy plants. “Poppy! Did you help water the garden?” Mia asked, eyes wide.

Poppy bobbed her head. “Yes! I made a plan. I used small cups, then a bottle. I kept a routine. Water, rest, water again!”

Mia smiled and petted Poppy gently. “You were so helpful. You were careful and clever, too. Thank you, Poppy.”

Poppy felt warm with joy. She rang the spoon bell. Ding! “Helping makes our garden strong,” she said. “And routine helps me remember.”

Mia nodded. “Tomorrow, let’s make a little chart. We can draw the steps: check soil, get water, pour slowly, tidy up.”

Poppy fluffed with pride. “I can do it by myself when you are busy,” she said. “I am a helpful, creative parrot!”

That evening, the balcony smelled like mint tea and sweet strawberries. The sunflower swayed in the breeze. Poppy sat on the railing, watching the sky turn orange and pink. She felt calm and brave. She had solved a problem. She had helped. She had followed a routine. She had used her own ideas.

Poppy whispered to the plants, “When we help, we grow. When we have a routine, we remember. When we try, we can do it.”

The End.
