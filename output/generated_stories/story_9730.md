# Tully the Shy Turtle Saves Water

Tully was a shy little turtle who loved quiet places. One sunny morning, Tully peeked into the bright, steamy bathroom. The tiles felt cool under his tiny feet. Drip, drip, drip went the faucet. Plink, plink, plink went the drops into the shiny sink.

“Oh,” whispered Tully, tucking his head a little. “That sounds like the bathroom is crying.”

He took a deep breath. “I can be brave,” he told himself. “I can help.”

Tully slid closer. He smelled soap—sweet and clean like lemons. He heard the soft whirr of the fan. He felt the warm steam on his shell, like a cozy hug. But he was still shy. He liked quiet, not drips.

“Hello, faucet,” Tully said softly. “Are you okay?”

Drip, drip, drip.

Tully looked around. There was a little toolkit on the shelf: a tiny wrench, some rubber bands, and a roll of tape. On the wall was a picture with words: “Turn it off. Save water.” The picture showed a happy fish swimming in a big blue lake.

Tully thought about the fish. He thought about trees and birds. He remembered a story from Grandma Turtle: “Water is a treasure. We share it with plants and animals.”

Tully nodded. “I will make good choices. I will help save water.”

Just then, his friend Pip the Mouse darted in. “Hi, Tully! What are you doing?” Pip squeaked, nose twitching. “It smells lemony!”

“The faucet is leaking,” said Tully. “I want to build a fix. But I’m shy. I don’t like loud noises.”

Pip smiled. “We can work slow and soft. We can build a water-saving plan.”

“A plan?” Tully’s eyes grew wide. “Yes. A plan.”

Together, they made three little steps.

Step one: Decide what to do.
Step two: Build a gentle fix.
Step three: Check and save.

Tully whispered, “We can do it.”

Step one: Decide what to do.
Tully touched the faucet. It wiggled. “It is loose,” he said. He felt brave for saying his idea out loud.

“That’s good thinking,” Pip said. “You made a smart choice.”

Step two: Build a gentle fix.
Tully took the tiny wrench. It was cold and smooth. He turned it very slowly. “Lefty loosey? Righty tighty,” he sang in a small voice.

“Righty tighty,” Pip sang back, tapping a happy beat on the counter, tap-tap-tap, not too loud.

Tully turned the handle. He found a little rubber ring inside, dry and cracked. “This ring is tired,” he said. “It needs a new one.” Pip handed him a fresh rubber ring from the kit. Tully pressed it in with careful turtle fingers.

“Good building,” Pip cheered.

Step three: Check and save.
Tully turned the faucet on, then off. He listened. He waited.

Silence.

No drip. No plink.

The bathroom felt peaceful. The fan whirred softly. The mirror fogged like a cloudy window. Tully smiled a small, shy smile.

“We did it,” he whispered. “We saved water.”

Pip clapped. “Teamwork! And you led the plan!”

Tully’s cheeks felt warm. “Thank you for helping me be brave.”

They looked around the bathroom for more ways to save. Tully read another sign: “Turn off the tap while brushing.”

“That is smart,” Tully said. He pictured a child brushing teeth. The water would not run and run. More water would stay in rivers and lakes. Fish would splash and say, “Thank you!”

Pip pointed to a big bucket by the tub. “We can catch extra water when the shower warms up,” Pip said. “Then we can water plants with it.”

Tully nodded. “Yes. We will conserve. We will share water.”

Together, they built a “Save Water Station.” They placed the bucket under the shower for warm-up water. They put a little cup by the sink to rinse after brushing, not the running tap. They added a soft towel sign that read: “Use one towel, let it dry, use again.” Tully sniffed the clean towel—sunny and fresh.

Pip asked, “What about soap?”

Tully said, “A little bit is enough. Use just what you need.” He made tiny bubbles with a tiny squeeze. Pop, pop went the bubbles. The bathroom sparkled with sleepy, happy light.

Just then, Bibi the Beetle crawled in. “Hi friends,” buzzed Bibi. “May I use the sink?”

Tully tucked his head, then peeped out. “Yes, Bibi. Please turn off the tap while you scrub.”

Bibi smiled. “Thanks for telling me. I will do that.” Bibi washed quickly, turned off the water, and dried hands. “I like your Save Water Station!”

Tully felt a warm, calm glow. He was shy, but he had spoken up. He had helped his friends. He had helped the earth.

Pip squeaked, “Tully, you made great choices. You built a fix. You saved water. That is sustainability!”

Tully touched the faucet one more time. It stayed quiet and kind. He whispered, “Thank you, water. We will take care of you.”

He picked up the toolkit and put it away. He looked at his friends. “I learned that small steps help,” he said. “Decide. Build. Check and save. We can all help, even if we are shy.”

Pip and Bibi nodded. “We accept each other,” said Pip. “Your quiet is part of you. Your care is strong.”

Tully smiled bigger now. The bathroom felt like a tiny world, safe and bright. And in that tiny world, Tully, Pip, and Bibi made a big, gentle change.

The lesson was clear: Use only what you need. Fix little leaks. Share and care. That’s how we help our home.

The End.
