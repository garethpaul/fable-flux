# Nutmeg’s Tick-Tock Treehouse

Nutmeg was a small, resourceful squirrel with bright eyes and a fluffy tail. She lived in a cozy treehouse high in a maple tree. The treehouse smelled like warm wood and acorns. Sunlight danced on the walls, and a soft breeze made the curtains whisper, swish, swish.

Nutmeg loved adventures. She also loved to help. But sometimes, she tried to do too many things at once.

One sunny morning, Nutmeg woke up and stretched her paws. “Today, I will do everything!” she said. “I will sweep the floor, fix the nut shelf, help Robin with her nest, and bring pinecones to the forest fair.” She made a long list on a leaf.

Tap, tap. Someone knocked on the treehouse door.

It was Robin, the red-breasted bird. “Nutmeg,” chirped Robin, “I lost my ribbon for my nest. Can you help me find it?”

“Yes!” said Nutmeg. “I will help. I have time!” She grabbed her little basket.

As they looked, Nutmeg heard another tap.

“Nutmeg!” called Tilly Turtle from below. “Can you clean the painted stones for the fair?”

“Yes!” said Nutmeg. “I have time!” She added it to her list.

Nutmeg and Robin searched under leaves and on branches. They found soft moss, shiny beetles, and one blue feather. The forest smelled like pine and earth. But no ribbon.

Nutmeg peeked at her leaf list. “Oh no,” she whispered. “So many things.”

Just then, Soot the squirrel, who lived in a nearby stump, appeared. Soot frowned and held a shiny acorn. “Someone took this from my pile,” Soot said. “It is my acorn with a swirl. I want justice.”

Nutmeg blinked. “Justice means fair,” she said softly. “We must be fair.”

Soot nodded. “Can you help, Nutmeg? You are smart.”

Nutmeg’s whiskers twitched. “Yes… but I need a plan.”

She scurried back to her treehouse and took a deep breath. She listened to the wind. Swish. She smelled the sweet sap of the tree. She put a paw on her fast-beating heart. “I have too much,” she told herself. “I need to manage my time.”

Nutmeg picked up a stick and drew three circles in a patch of dirt on the floor.

“This circle is Now,” she said. “This circle is Soon. And this circle is Later.”

She put little stones in each circle.

“Now: Find Robin’s ribbon. Soon: Help Soot with his acorn. Later: Clean Tilly’s stones and fix my nut shelf.”

Nutmeg smiled. “I can do each one. One by one.”

She turned a pinecone into a timer. She tied a tiny leaf to it and placed it on a string. “When the pinecone reaches the knot, time is done.” Snap, snap went the string as she tied it tight. “This is my time tool.”

Robin flapped her wings. “Let’s look again while the pinecone falls.”

They searched carefully. Nutmeg listened for a soft ribbon sound. Shhh. She looked for a glimmer. In the grass, near a dandelion puff, she saw a blue shine.

“I found it!” Nutmeg said. She held up the ribbon. It felt smooth and cool. Robin chirped with joy. “Thank you!”

The pinecone touched the knot. Tap. “Now, it is time to help Soot,” Nutmeg said.

They met Soot by his stump. “Let’s be fair and kind,” Nutmeg said. “We will ask, not accuse.”

Nutmeg used innovation. She made a little scale from two shells and a stick. She balanced Soot’s shiny acorn on one side and a regular acorn on the other. “See?” she said. “Soot’s acorn is heavier. It has the swirl and a tiny pebble inside. No other acorn feels like this.”

Benny Beaver waddled over, looking shy. He held two acorns. “I found these near the path,” Benny said. “I took one without asking. I am sorry.” His eyes were wet. “I thought no one wanted it.”

Soot’s tail drooped. “That was mine. But thank you for telling the truth,” Soot said.

Nutmeg nodded. “Justice means we fix what is wrong in a fair way. Benny, please return the acorn. And next time, please ask first.”

Benny gave back the shiny acorn. Soot hugged it. “I forgive you,” Soot said. “Please come to the fair with us.”

Benny smiled. “Thank you. I will ask next time.”

The pinecone timer tapped again. “Soon is done,” Nutmeg said. “Now for Later.”

Back at the treehouse, Nutmeg washed Tilly’s painted stones in a bowl of warm water. Swish, swish. The colors shined: red, blue, yellow. She set them in the sun. Then she fixed her nut shelf with a little bark brace. Tap, tap, tap went her wooden hammer.

By afternoon, the treehouse was tidy. The air smelled like soap and sunlight. Nutmeg’s list was all checked. She felt calm and proud.

At the forest fair, there was music, laughter, and sweet berry pies. Robin’s nest had the ribbon. Tilly’s stones made a bright path. Soot and Benny played a ring toss game together.

Nutmeg took a deep breath. The sky was pink and gold. “One by one,” she whispered. “Plan, do, and be fair.”

Robin fluttered beside her. “You helped us all.”

Nutmeg smiled. “I learned to manage my time. I used a plan and a timer. I used my mind to make a tool. I was fair, and I forgave. That made the day good.”

The friends shared pie. The crust was flaky, and the berries were sweet. Nutmeg’s heart felt warm.

And every morning after that, Nutmeg drew three circles: Now, Soon, Later. She used her pinecone timer. She helped with care. She made time for fun too.

Lesson: When we plan our day and do one thing at a time, we feel calm, do good work, and can be fair and kind to others.

The End.
