# Tortoise Tim and the Fair Game

In a cozy guest room with pale yellow walls, a wise tortoise named Tim lived in a soft basket by the window. The guest bed had a quilt that smelled like soap and sunshine. A tiny desk held crayons, paper, and a whistle. Outside the window, Tim could hear children laughing, “Ready, set, go!” They were playing a game inside the house on a rainy day.

Tim blinked slowly and smiled. “Good morning, room,” he said in a warm, gentle voice. “Today is a good day to learn.” His shell felt smooth under the lamp’s glow, and the carpet tickled his feet.

Knock, knock! Two friends, Mia and Leo, ran into the guest room with a soft ball and a stack of paper ribbons. “Tim! We’re playing indoor toss!” Mia said. Her cheeks were pink and excited. “We made a goal by the chair. First to five wins!”

Leo bounced the ball. Thump, thump. He grinned. “I will win for sure,” he said. “I am fast!”

Tim nodded. “Fast is good,” he said. “Fair is better.” He tapped the whistle on the desk. “Is there a referee?”

“What’s a ref… refa… what?” Mia asked.

“A referee is a helper for justice,” Tim said. He said the word slowly: “jus-tice.” “Justice means fair rules for everyone, and kind choices when we feel big feelings.”

Mia and Leo looked at each other. “Can you be the referee, Tim?” Leo asked.

Tim’s eyes twinkled. “Yes. But we will make the rules together. Then we will write them so we remember.”

They sat on the soft rug. Tim took a crayon in his careful, slow paw. “Rule One,” he said, writing big easy letters. “Take turns.” The crayon made a gentle scratchy sound on the paper. “Rule Two: No pushing. Rule Three: Count points out loud so we all know. Rule Four: If we feel mad, we stop, breathe, and try again.”

Mia clapped. “I like the breathing part. Sometimes I get mad.”

Leo nodded. “Me too. My tummy feels hot.”

Tim smiled. “We can cool our tummies with air.” He showed them, breathing in for three slow counts and out for three. “Like the wind through the window.”

The game began. “Mia first,” said Tim. Mia tossed the soft ball toward the chair-leg goal. It made a little thump and rolled in. “Point for Mia,” Leo said, and they marked a tiny star on the paper. Leo tossed next. It bounced off the leg and skittered away. “Almost!” Mia cheered.

Soon the score was four to four. The room felt buzzy with excitement. Rain tapped the window. The lamp hummed softly. Mia took a deep breath and tossed. The ball kissed the chair leg and slipped in. “Five!” she shouted.

But Leo’s face changed. “No! It hit my foot!” he cried. His fists squeezed. His cheeks puffed. “That point is not fair!”

Mia frowned. “It did not hit your foot,” she said. “I saw it.”

Tim raised his whistle and blew a tiny toot. Not loud, just enough to make them pause. “We have a justice problem,” he said calmly. “Let us check the facts.”

Leo crossed his arms. “I’m mad,” he said. He stamped once. The carpet felt squishy under his shoe.

Tim nodded. “It is okay to feel mad. Let’s use Rule Four. Stop. Breathe.”

Together they placed hands on bellies. In, two, three. Out, two, three. Leo’s shoulders softened. “Okay,” he whispered.

Tim walked, slow and steady, to the chair leg. He looked at the floor. “I see a little line in the carpet where the ball rolled,” he said. “And I see your foot over here, Leo. It looks like the ball missed your shoe.”

Leo looked down. He blinked. “Oh,” he said. “Maybe I was wrong.” His voice was small.

Mia’s eyes were gentle. “It’s hard to tell when we are excited,” she said. “It’s okay.”

Tim smiled. “Justice is not about winning. Justice is about truth and fairness, even when we feel hot in our tummies.”

Leo nodded slowly. “I want justice,” he said. “Mia gets the point.” He held out the paper and handed Mia a ribbon. “Good game.”

Mia tied the ribbon on the chair. “Thank you, Leo,” she said. “You were brave to change your mind.”

Tim picked up the crayon again. “Let us write what we learned.” He wrote: “We can be fair. We can calm down. We can check the facts.” The crayon’s scent was waxy and sweet.

“Can we play one more round?” Leo asked.

“Yes,” said Tim. “This time, let’s add a new rule: Rule Five. If there is a question, we pause and look together.”

They played again. The ball made soft thumps and happy rolls. They counted points out loud. “One for Leo!” “One for Mia!” When Leo felt mad for a moment, he put his hand on his belly and breathed. “I’m okay,” he said. The room felt peaceful and bright.

When the game ended, both had ribbons. They smiled and high-fived. Rain stopped, and the window showed a little rainbow. The guest room smelled like warm dust and crayons. Tim tucked the rule paper under the desk lamp.

“What did we learn today?” Tim asked.

Mia and Leo said together, “Justice means fair rules for everyone. We can calm down, check the facts, and do what is right.”

Tim nodded, proud and kind. “That is a win for all.”

The End.
