# Rooftop Ribbon Day

Binky was a determined rabbit with soft gray fur and bright, curious eyes. He lived in a small house with a flat rooftop. The rooftop was Binky’s favorite place. It was sunny, breezy, and smelled like warm tiles and fresh air.

One morning, Binky bounced up the stairs to the roof. “Today, I will make something special,” he said. “I will make a big, colorful wind ribbon that dances in the sky!”

The rooftop felt warm under Binky’s paws. The breeze tickled his whiskers. He set down a little basket filled with craft things: long ribbons, paper, string, and a safe, kid-sized pair of scissors. His friend, Mina the sparrow, fluttered down and chirped, “Tweet! What are you making, Binky?”

“A wind ribbon,” Binky said, standing tall. “But I have choices to make. I want to decide the best way.”

Mina tilted her head. “What will you decide first?”

Binky looked at the basket. “Color!” he said. He held up a red ribbon, a blue ribbon, and a yellow ribbon. The red looked bright like a strawberry. The blue looked cool like the sky. The yellow looked happy like sunshine.

Binky’s ears wiggled. “Hmm. I want them all. But I must choose a plan. I will think.” He closed his eyes. He listened to the wind whoosh and felt it brush his fur. He opened one eye and smiled. “I can twist the colors together! That way, I don’t have to pick just one. I can make a decision that uses them all.”

Mina clapped her wings. “Good thinking!”

Binky tied the red, blue, and yellow ribbons at the top of a stick. He tested the knot. Tug, tug. It held tight.

Next, Binky had to decide how long the ribbons should be. “Long ribbons will dance big,” he said. “But short ribbons won’t get tangled.” He raised one paw to the wind. The breeze was gentle, not too strong. “I will make them medium,” he decided. “Not too long, not too short.” He snipped carefully. Snip, snip, snip. The ribbons whispered against the tiles.

Just then, two more friends popped up from the stairway. Tiko the turtle slowly peeked out, and Lila the lamb trotted onto the roof. “What’s that?” asked Lila, nose wiggling.

“A wind ribbon,” Binky said proudly. “I am almost done.”

“I want a turn to tie a bow!” Lila said.

“I want a turn to hold the stick,” Tiko said, smiling slow.

Binky took a breath. He felt excited and a little worried. He wanted to keep crafting, but he also wanted to share. He thought, “I can make a kind decision.” He nodded and said, “We can take turns.”

He pointed to the stick. “Tiko, you can hold the stick first because you asked to hold it. Lila, you can tie the bow after Tiko holds it steady. Then I will tie the last knot.”

“Thank you,” said Tiko.

“Thank you,” said Lila.

“Thank you for helping,” said Binky. “I am grateful for my friends.”

They worked together. Tiko’s strong, careful hands held the stick still. Lila tied a soft yellow bow near the top. Binky tied one more knot to keep the ribbons safe. The breeze hummed and the ribbons rustled like a gentle song.

“Now we have one more choice,” Binky said. “Where do we put our wind ribbon? We can put it by the edge where the wind is strong. Or we can put it by the little garden where the wind is soft.”

Mina flew to the edge and felt the breeze tug her feathers. “It’s very windy here,” she chirped.

Lila nuzzled the small rooftop garden and heard the leaves whisper. “It’s soft and quiet here,” she said.

Binky thought again. He tapped his chin. “If we put it by the edge, it will dance big, but it might pull hard. If we put it by the garden, it will dance gentle, and we can watch it close.” He looked at his friends. “What do you think?”

Tiko blinked slowly. “I like gentle,” he said.

Lila nodded. “Me too.”

Binky smiled. “I understand. Gentle is safe for our ribbon and for all of us.” He made his decision. “We will put it by the garden.”

They tied the stick to a small post near the plants. The ribbons fluttered—swish, swish—like colorful fish in the air. The sun warmed their faces. The breeze smelled like tomato leaves and mint.

Mina chirped, “It’s beautiful!”

Binky felt proud and calm. “We made good choices,” he said. “We used many colors. We picked a good length. We took turns. We asked each other. We listened. We chose a safe place.”

Lila leaned against Binky. “Thank you for sharing.”

Tiko gave a slow, happy nod. “Thank you for deciding kindly.”

Binky’s ears lifted high. “I am grateful for your help,” he said. “When we think, listen, and choose with care, our day feels bright.”

They sat together on the warm rooftop, watching the ribbons dance and sparkle. The wind whispered, the tiles felt cozy, and everyone felt understood.

“Tomorrow,” Binky said, “we can make a wind chime. We will decide together again.”

“Tweet!” said Mina. “I can’t wait!”

The End.
