# Tully Tortoise and the Wiggle Machine

In a cozy basement lab, Tully the wise tortoise blinked behind tiny round glasses. The lab smelled like warm toast and lemon soap. Lights hummed softly. On the wall, colorful buttons glowed red, blue, and green. Tully loved to think and to move slowly, one careful step at a time.

“Good morning, buttons,” Tully said, smiling. “Let’s make a Wiggle Machine to help little bodies move!”

Tully tapped a pencil. Tap, tap. His shell felt cool, and the floor felt smooth under his feet. He drew a picture of a happy stick figure that stretched arms up high. He drew another that hopped like a frog. He wrote, “MOVE SAFELY” in big, neat letters.

He set parts on the table. There were soft springs, a gentle fan, and a silly speaker shaped like a star. Tully spoke out loud as he worked. “Step one: Look. Step two: Think. Step three: Try.”

He turned a knob. Click. The star speaker chirped, “Wiggle, wiggle!”

“Ha!” Tully laughed. “That’s a good start.”

He turned another knob. The gentle fan blew a breeze that smelled like fresh air after rain. A strip of paper fluttered. “That will feel nice when we move,” Tully said.

Tully pressed the red button. The Wiggle Machine blinked. A tiny screen lit up and showed a picture of a child raising arms. The star speaker sang, “Reach up high, touch the sky!”

Tully reached up high. He felt a long, warm stretch in his legs and neck. “Ahhh,” he said. “Movement helps our bodies and our brains.”

Suddenly, the machine buzzed. Bzzz! The screen flickered and showed a very fast dance. The star speaker shouted, “Spin, spin, spin!”

Tully froze. “Oh dear,” he said. “That is too fast for small feet. It might make someone dizzy.”

He took a slow breath. “Step one: Look.” He watched the screen carefully.
“Step two: Think.” He put a gentle paw on his chin. “What is the problem?”
“The problem is speed,” he said. “It is too fast.”

He turned the blue knob down. Nothing changed. He turned the green knob up. The fan blew harder, whoosh, whoosh, but the dance stayed fast.

Tully felt a little twist in his tummy. He looked at his notebook. There, in neat letters, he had written, “Use the blue knob for speed.”

He blinked. “Oh,” he said softly. “I told my own notes the blue knob is for speed. But I did not build it that way. I made a mistake.”

Tully took a deep, slow breath. “I will be honest,” he said. “I made the wrong choice for the knob.”

He looked around the cozy lab. The lights hummed. The gentle fan whirred. Tully stood up straight, feeling brave. “I can fix this.”

“Step three: Try,” he said, smiling.

He turned off the machine. Click. He opened the panel with a tiny screwdriver. He could smell warm metal and the lemon soap on his hands. He moved carefully, lefty-loosey, righty-tighty. He changed two tiny wires so the blue knob would control the speed.

“Now let’s test,” Tully said.

He pressed the red button again. The screen showed a child lifting knees like marching. The star speaker sang, “March, march, march! Lift your knees, not too fast!”

Tully tried it. Lift, step. Lift, step. He felt his heart go thump-thump in a happy way. “This feels safe,” he said. “This feels fun!”

He tapped the blue knob slowly. The marching got a little quicker but still gentle. He turned it back down. “Yes,” he said. “We can choose a comfy speed.”

The Wiggle Machine showed a new picture: reach to the side. “Stretch to the left, stretch to the right!” sang the star.

Tully stretched left. He smelled the lemon soap on his arm. He stretched right. He heard the fan whisper. His shell felt warm from the light.

Then the machine showed another move: jump like a bunny. The star speaker sang, “Boing! Boing! Soft knees!”

Tully smiled. “I am not a fast jumper,” he said. “But I can try small hops.” He bent his knees and did two tiny hops. Boing, boing. He felt proud.

Suddenly, the screen showed “Spin!” again. Tully blinked. “Oh my,” he said. “The spin is back.” He did not want to remove all spins. Some children like to turn once, slowly.

He thought hard. “Step one: Look.” He watched the spin picture. It showed three very fast spins.
“Step two: Think.” He asked, “How can I make spins safe and kind?”
He turned the blue knob down and pressed the yellow button labeled “Guide.” He spoke into the star speaker. “Let us change the plan. One slow turn. Feet apart. Eyes on a spot.”

He typed new words for the speaker. The star sang, “Turn once, very slow. Stop and breathe. Ready? Go!”

Tully tried one slow turn. He looked at a sticker on the wall, a shiny star. He turned once and stopped. He felt steady and calm. “That’s better,” he said.

Now the Wiggle Machine had a new game: Think and Move. The screen asked, “Which move is safe in a small space?” It showed three pictures: a giant leap, a gentle stretch, and a tumble roll.

Tully tapped his chin. “We must use critical thinking,” he said. He pointed to the gentle stretch. “This one is safe.”

The star cheered, “Yes! Stretch in small spaces. Save big leaps for big places!”

Tully clapped softly. “We did it,” he said. “We fixed the problem by being honest, thinking carefully, and moving safely.”

He wrote in his notebook: “Lesson: Be honest about mistakes. Think before you move. Choose safe, happy moves. Movement helps bodies and brains.”

He turned to the Wiggle Machine. “Thank you, friend,” he said. “Let’s help children move with joy.”

The machine blinked a happy light. The fan hummed a kind little song. Tully did one more stretch, one more march, and two tiny hops. He felt strong. He felt wise. He felt proud.

“We can all move,” Tully said, “slow or fast, big or small. We just need to think, be honest, and choose safe moves.”

The End.
