# Little Hooves, Big Notes

Tap, tap, tap. Soft hooves tapped on the wooden floor. A gentle deer fawn named Dala peeked into the musical practice room. The room smelled like fresh wood and shiny brass. Sunlight made warm squares on the floor. Drums sat in a circle. A big piano smiled with white teeth. Tiny bells rested in a basket and looked like stars.

Dala’s ears wiggled. Her heart went thump-thump. Today was music day. Today, she wanted to be brave.

“Hello?” Dala whispered. Her voice sounded like a small bell.

“Hello, Dala!” sang Ms. Lark, the music teacher. Ms. Lark wore a scarf with bright notes on it. It swished when she moved. “Are you ready to make music?”

Dala took a deep breath. “I… I think so.”

Her friends were already there. A bunny named Pip held drumsticks. A squirrel named Numi hugged a ukulele. A little bird named Tiko hopped on the piano keys and made tiny plinks.

Ms. Lark clapped softly. “Today we share songs from our homes. We share our traditions. We listen. We learn. And we are brave.”

Dala’s tummy fluttered. She had a family song. It was an old deer lullaby. Her grandma sang it at night. It sounded like wind in leaves. Dala loved it. But sharing it felt big.

Pip banged the drum with a steady boom-boom. “My grandpa sings this beat at parties!” Pip said, smiling. Everyone clapped.

Numi strummed the ukulele. “My auntie plays this at the harvest.” The notes were bright and bouncy. Dala swayed. Her hooves tapped again.

Tiko trilled and peeped. “My parents chirp this every morning!” The piano answered with gentle plink-plinks.

Ms. Lark looked at Dala. “Would you like to share your song, dear one?”

Dala’s ears drooped. “I… I’m scared,” she said. “What if I sing wrong? What if everyone laughs?”

Ms. Lark knelt so her eyes were kind and close. “Bravery does not mean no fear,” she said softly. “Bravery means you try, even with a wiggly tummy. We will listen with kind hearts.”

Pip hopped closer. “We won’t laugh,” Pip said.

Numi nodded fast. “We will cheer!”

Tiko chirped, “We love new songs!”

Dala took another breath. She felt the room. The drum’s warm boom still floated in the air. The piano smelled like lemon polish. The bells twinkled in the basket. She remembered Grandma’s soft voice and the safe rustle of tall grass.

“I can try,” Dala said. Her voice was tiny, but steady.

She closed her eyes. Her ears tilted forward. Dala sang:

“Rustle, rustle, little leaves,
Moon is peeking through the trees.
Little fawn, rest your eyes,
Stars will hum you lullabies.”

Her voice was gentle, like a breeze. At first, it shook. Then it grew smooth. The room felt quiet and cozy, like night. Dala could almost hear Grandma humming with her.

When she finished, no one moved for a moment. Then Ms. Lark’s eyes shone. “Beautiful. Thank you for sharing your tradition, Dala.”

Pip clapped his paws. “I love the rustle part!”

Numi wiped a happy tear. “It feels like hugs.”

Tiko fluttered. “Can we sing it together?”

Dala smiled. Warm pride bloomed in her chest. “Yes,” she said. “We can sing it together.”

Ms. Lark handed out tiny bells. “Let’s add a soft leaf sound,” she said.

They shook the bells gently: shhhh-shhh, like leaves. Pip tapped the drum like a quiet heart. Numi strummed a soft hum. Tiko peeped a sweet echo. Dala led the song again. This time, the room glowed with friendship. Sharing made her song even bigger.

When the song ended, Dala looked at Ms. Lark. “Thank you for helping me be brave.”

Ms. Lark nodded. “You helped yourself. You chose to try.”

Then a small bump happened. Pip, dancing, bumped Numi’s ukulele. It made a sharp twang! Numi squeaked and hugged it tight. The room went still.

Pip’s eyes went wide. “Oh no,” he said. “I didn’t mean to.” He looked at Numi. His ears drooped. “I’m sorry.”

Numi took a breath. He touched the strings. The ukulele was okay. He looked at Pip and smiled. “Thank you for your apology,” Numi said. “I forgive you. Please be careful.”

Pip nodded. “I will.” He set the drumsticks down and gave Numi a gentle pat.

Ms. Lark smiled. “That was brave too, Pip. Saying ‘I’m sorry’ takes courage. And Numi, forgiving is kind.”

Dala’s heart felt light. “We can be brave in many ways,” she said. “We can share. We can try. We can say sorry.”

The class made one more song. They mixed all their traditions. Boom-boom from Pip. Plink-plink from Tiko. Strum-strum from Numi. Shhhh-shhh like leaves from Dala’s bells. It sounded like friends. It sounded like home.

As they packed up, Ms. Lark said, “What did we learn today?”

Dala held her bells and answered, proud and clear. “Being brave means trying, even when scared. Sharing our traditions is special. And saying sorry helps hearts feel better.”

Everyone nodded. The practice room felt warm and bright, like sunshine inside.

Dala trotted to the door, hooves tapping a happy beat. Her song was in the air, and in her heart too.

The End.
