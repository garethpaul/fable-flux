# Dot’s Big Choice in the Quiet Attic

Dot was a gentle deer fawn with soft ears and tiny white spots. One rainy day, Dot tiptoed up creaky wooden stairs to a quiet attic. The air smelled like old books and warm dust. Light came in through a small round window, making a sunny circle on the floor.

“Oh,” whispered Dot, eyes wide. “Look at all the treasures!”

There were boxes with ribbons, baskets with buttons, and a big easel with clean paper. Paints, crayons, and glue sticks sat in a row, bright like candy. Dot’s heart felt bouncy. “I want to make art!” she said. “But… what should I make?”

Dot looked at the paints. “I can paint.” She looked at the crayons. “I can draw.” She looked at a box of soft yarn and a stack of shiny stickers. “I can make a collage!” Dot’s ears drooped. “I can’t do all three at once. How do I choose?”

Dot sat on a cozy rug and took a deep breath. She listened. Drip, drip, drip went the rain. Tick-tock went a tiny clock. In the quiet attic, Dot could hear her own small sigh. “I feel stuck,” she said. “I need a plan.”

Just then, a friendly moth fluttered past. Its wings were like tiny gray leaves. “Hi,” said Dot. “I’m Dot. I want to make art, but I can’t decide what to do.”

The moth circled gently. “Hello, Dot. I am Moth. When I can’t decide, I ask a question. What feels good right now? What do you want most?”

Dot put a hoof on her chest. “Right now, I feel wiggly and splashy,” she said. “I think I want to paint. But I also like stickers!”

Moth nodded. “You can choose one first. You can do the others later. One step at a time.”

Dot smiled. That sounded calm and kind. “Okay. I will choose painting first.” She walked to the easel. She opened the paint. Pop! The lid came off. Dot dipped a brush. Swish! Blue paint made a soft, smooth line. Dot felt happy. “This feels good,” she said.

But then Dot paused. “Wait,” she said to Moth. “What should I paint?”

Moth landed on the easel. “Try three ideas. Then pick one.”

Dot tried to imagine. She smelled the paint, cool and a little sweet. She touched the paper. It was smooth and crisp. “Idea one: a rainy window with tiny drops.” She closed her eyes. Plip, plip. “Idea two: a sunny field with flowers.” She pictured bright yellow petals. “Idea three: a family of stars that sparkle.”

Dot’s mind felt busy. “How do I pick?” she asked.

Moth flapped, soft as a whisper. “Use your words. Talk it out. Say why you like each one.”

Dot nodded and spoke slowly. “I like the rainy window because I hear the rain right now. I like the sunny field because it makes me feel warm. I like the star family because it makes me feel calm.”

Dot put a hoof to her chin. “What do I need most today?” She listened again: drip, drip, drip. Her body felt jumpy. “I need calm,” she said. “So I choose the star family.”

“Good choice,” said Moth.

Dot dipped the brush in dark blue. Swish, swish. She made a big night sky. She added tiny dots of white. Tap, tap, tap. “Hello, little stars,” Dot whispered. She painted a big star with a soft smile. “This is Mama Star.” She painted two smaller stars with round cheeks. “These are Baby Stars.”

Dot looked at the shiny stickers. “Can I add one sticker?” she asked herself. “I chose painting first, but maybe one sticker will help.” Dot thought. She took one deep breath. “I can set a rule,” she said. “One sticker only.” She picked a silver moon sticker and pressed it gently. Press. It glowed in the light from the window.

Dot stood back. The painting looked calm and bright. Dot felt calm too.

“Now what?” asked Moth.

Dot wiped her brush. “Now I will share,” she said. She spoke to the quiet attic, as if the boxes and buttons could listen. “I chose painting. I talked about my ideas. I picked the one that helps me feel calm. I used one sticker. I followed my plan.”

Dot smiled. “I can make more art later,” she said. “Next time, I can try crayons. Or I can make a collage.”

The rain became soft. The clock went tick-tock, tick-tock. The attic felt warm and safe. Dot signed her name at the bottom: Dot the Fawn. Squiggle, squiggle.

“Thank you, Moth,” Dot said. “You helped me decide.”

“You helped yourself,” said Moth. “You asked questions. You used your words. You made a plan. That is good decision-making.”

Dot’s heart felt big and bright. “I can do hard things,” she said. “I can choose.”

She picked up her painting and looked at the stars. “Hello, Star Family,” she whispered. “You are my calm.”

Dot tiptoed back down the creaky stairs, holding her art. She knew that next time, she could listen, talk it out, and choose again.

The End.
