# Tilly Turtle’s Little Lullaby

Tilly was a shy turtle who lived in a cozy bedroom with soft blankets and a small nightlight that glowed like a firefly. Her bed felt warm like a hug, and the air smelled like clean sheets and pine from her wooden toy shelf. On the dresser sat a tiny toy drum, a shiny triangle, and a small ukulele with bright blue strings.

Tilly loved music, but she was shy about making sounds. When she picked up the ukulele, her tummy felt wiggly, like jelly. “What if I play it wrong?” she whispered, pulling her shell a little closer around her.

Her friend, a fluffy stuffed rabbit named Ruffle, sat on the pillow and smiled with button eyes. “Try a tiny strum,” Ruffle seemed to say. Tilly took a deep breath and brushed one string. Ting! The sound was gentle and sweet. It tickled her ears.

“I want to make a lullaby,” said Tilly. “But I don’t know how.”

Mama Turtle peeked in the door with a warm smile. “Hi, little shell,” she said in a soft voice. “I hear music feelings.”

Tilly looked down. “I’m shy, Mama. What if my song is not good?”

Mama Turtle knelt beside the bed. She smelled like warm tea and honey. “Songs grow with practice,” she said. “Practice is trying again and again, even when it feels wiggly inside.”

Tilly touched the blue strings. “Practice?”

“Yes,” Mama said. “Small steps. Slow and steady, like a turtle.”

Tilly nodded. She wiggled her toes under the blanket. “Okay. I will try.”

She strummed one string again. Ting. Then two strings. Ting-ting. Her tummy still fluttered, but she felt brave. “Listen, Ruffle,” she giggled. “Ting-ting.”

“Beautiful,” Mama whispered. “How does your body feel now?”

Tilly closed her eyes. “My tummy still wiggles, but my heart feels warm.”

“That is a feeling,” Mama said. “You can name it. Maybe it’s nervous and proud at the same time.”

Tilly smiled. “Nervous and proud.”

They made a practice plan together:
- First, play one string.
- Next, play two strings.
- Then, hum a tiny tune.

“Slow and steady,” Tilly said.

She played one string. Ting. She played two strings. Ting-ting. She hummed, “Hmm-hmm,” like a sleepy bee. The sound felt soft in her mouth. Her nose smelled the bedtime lavender on her pillow. Her little room felt safe.

“Oops,” Tilly said, when a string buzzed. “That sounded funny.”

“That was a learning note,” Mama said kindly. “What can we try now?”

Tilly tried again, softer. Ting… ting… “Hmm-hmm-hmm,” she hummed. She wiggled her shoulders and took a deep breath. In. Out. Her tummy felt calmer.

She added the triangle. Ting! It sparkled like a tiny star. “Listen, Ruffle,” she told her stuffed friend. “It’s like twinkle rain!”

Ruffle sat very still, ears up.

Tilly giggled. “I think Ruffle likes it.”

Mama nodded. “I like it too. Would you like to keep practicing for three more tries?”

“Three is small,” Tilly said. “I can do three.”

Try one: Ting-ting. Hmm-hmm. Ting!
Try two: Ting-ting-ting. Hmm-hmm-hmm. Ting!
Try three: Ting… ting… ting… “La-la,” she sang softly, like a cloud.

Her voice was tiny, but it was there. Tilly felt a warm glow in her chest. “My shy is still here,” she said. “But it is smaller.”

“Shy can be a friend,” Mama said. “Shy keeps us gentle. Practice helps us grow brave.”

Tilly thought about this. “I can be gentle and brave.”

Mama smiled. “Yes, you can.”

They made a little beat on the toy drum: tap, tap. Tilly tapped with one finger. Tap. Tap. Tap. The drum felt smooth under her hand.

She strummed the ukulele. Ting-ting. She tapped the drum. Tap-tap. She rang the triangle. Ting! Then she sang, “La-la, la-la,” soft as a whisper.

The bedroom felt cozy and musical. The nightlight glowed. The moon peeked through the curtains. Tilly’s lullaby floated like a feather.

“I did it,” Tilly said. Her eyes felt sleepy and happy.

“How do you feel now?” Mama asked.

Tilly touched her shell. “Calm. Proud. A tiny bit shy. And… peaceful.”

Mama gave her a gentle hug. “That is a lot of feelings. You listened to them. You practiced. Your song grew.”

Tilly tucked Ruffle under her chin. “Tomorrow, I can practice again. Slow and steady.”

Mama kissed her head. “Yes. Practice makes skills grow, and hearts feel brave.”

Tilly sang one last time, “La-la, la-la,” and the sounds were warm like cocoa. She smiled, closed her eyes, and felt the soft blanket, the safe room, and the happy quiet after music.

Her shy was still a little there, but her brave was bigger. She knew that practicing a little each day could make her song strong.

“Good night, Ruffle,” she whispered. “Good night, music.”

The End.
