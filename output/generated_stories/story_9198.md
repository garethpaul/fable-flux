# Leo’s Thank-You Garden

Morning sunshine warmed the balcony garden. Tiny pots lined the rail like little drums. Mint leaves smelled cool and sweet. Basil waved green hands. Blossoms popped like small stars. A brave little lion cub named Leo peeked out, his soft paws pat-pat on the wooden floor.

“Today I water the garden all by myself,” Leo said, puffing his chest. “I can do it!”

He lifted the small watering can. It sloshed. “Woah,” he giggled, “it’s heavy.” He walked carefully past tomatoes on tall sticks and a shy strawberry plant hiding under leaves.

His belly rumbled. “Thank you, sun, for warming us,” Leo whispered. “Thank you, water, for helping plants drink.” He liked saying thank you. It made his heart feel big and warm, like a cozy blanket.

A busy bee buzzed near a purple flower. “Bzz-hello,” Leo said softly. “Thank you for visiting and helping the flowers.” The bee zipped away, and the air smelled like oranges and soil.

Leo held the can high. “Sprinkle, sprinkle,” he sang. Water sparkled like tiny diamonds. Mint leaves shivered happily. Basil said, “Mmm!” in leaf language.

Then Leo saw a snail inching along the strawberry pot. “Oh! Hello, Snail,” he said. “Please don’t nibble the strawberries. They are still babies.”

The snail kept sliding. Leo frowned. “I worked hard. Those berries are for sharing later.” He reached to move the snail, but—uh-oh! The watering can tipped. Splash! Water poured too fast. Soil tumbled over the pot’s edge. A baby strawberry plopped onto the floor.

Leo’s eyes grew wide. His ears drooped. “Oh no.” He picked up the berry. It was soft and red and a little muddy. “I’m sorry, little strawberry,” he whispered. His tail tucked low.

Grandma Lion padded out with a warm smile and soft steps. “I heard a splash,” she said. “How is my brave gardener?”

Leo blinked back tears. “I messed up, Grandma. I spilled. I scared the strawberry. And the snail didn’t listen.”

Grandma’s whiskers twitched. “Hmm. Our garden is a good teacher,” she said. “What did it teach you right now?”

Leo sniffled. He listened to the wind. He felt the sun on his fur. He smelled wet soil. “It taught me to go slow,” he said. “And to be gentle.” He looked at the snail. “And… to forgive. Snails are hungry too.”

Grandma nodded. “Yes. We can forgive the snail. We can accept that small mistakes happen. And we can be grateful for the chance to fix them.”

Leo took a breath. It felt like a fresh breeze. “Thank you, Grandma. Thank you, garden, for teaching me.”

Together they scooped the soil back. Pat-pat. Leo set the baby strawberry softly in place. He smoothed the leaves. “There you go,” he murmured. “I’m sorry for the splash. I will be gentle now.” He sprinkled just a tiny sip. Drip, drip. The plant seemed to stand taller.

“Self-reliant cub,” Grandma said proudly. “You fixed it.”

Leo peered over the balcony rail. Far below, the city hummed. Cars whooshed. A child laughed. A dog barked. Above, a bird flew by, carrying a twig. “Grandma,” Leo asked, “do other gardens grow in other places?”

“Oh yes,” Grandma said. “Gardens grow on rooftops, balconies, and in yards. People all over the world plant seeds. They say thank you to sun and rain, just like you.”

Leo smiled. He closed his eyes for a tiny moment. “Thank you, people everywhere, for growing food and flowers,” he said quietly. “We all share the same sun.”

Just then, a small wind pushed the snail closer to the strawberries again. Leo crouched, heart calm. “Hello again, Snail,” he said. “I forgive you for nibbling. These berries need time. I will give you a mint leaf instead.” He broke a small piece of mint and set it near the snail. The snail touched it with gentle feelers and paused there, happy.

“See?” Grandma whispered. “Acceptance and kindness can guide.”

Leo stood tall. “I am grateful for the sun, the water, the bee, the snail, and you,” he said. “I am even grateful for my spill. It helped me learn.”

They finished watering together, slow and steady. The balcony smelled like fresh rain and peppery basil. Leo’s paws felt cool. A cloud slid over the sun, then moved on, and warm light came back like a hug.

When they were done, Leo held up his watering can. “I did it,” he said. “I took care of our garden.”

“You did,” Grandma said, giving him a soft head bump. “What did you learn today, brave gardener?”

Leo thought. He touched the strawberry leaves. He listened to his happy heart. “I learned to be grateful for help and for chances to try again,” he said. “I learned to forgive, to accept, and to go slow. Thank you, garden.”

The bee buzzed a friendly goodbye. The snail munched mint. The city hummed gently. Leo curled his tail with pride and joy. He looked at the tiny pots, his little world, and felt big inside.

“Tomorrow,” he whispered, “I will say thank you again.”

The End.
