# Pebble the Penguin Finds His Voice

Pebble was a cheerful penguin who loved his comfortable nursery. The room was warm and bright. Soft rugs felt squishy under his feet. Sunlight made little sparkles on the fish-shaped mobile. Pebble wiggled his flippers and sang, “Waddle, waddle, wiggle!” His voice sounded happy in the cozy room.

Miss Lila, the kind teacher, smiled. “Good morning, Pebble. Time for our day!”

Pebble saw neat shelves with puzzles, chunky crayons, and a basket of toy boats. He flapped with excitement. “Boats!” he chirped. He also saw a sign with simple pictures: a cup for water, a hand for help, and a heart for feelings.

Miss Lila tapped the sign. “Today we practice using our voices. We ask for what we need. We say how we feel. That is self-advocacy.”

Pebble tilted his head. “Self-advo… advo…” He tried the new word.

Miss Lila nodded. “Self-advocacy means you can speak up kindly for yourself.”

First, it was clean-up time. Pebble wanted to keep playing with blocks, but the bell chimed softly, ding-ding. His tummy felt tight. He loved building tall towers. He took a slow breath. The room smelled like lemons and crayons.

Pebble waddled to Miss Lila. “Miss Lila,” he said, “I feel a little sad. Can I finish one more block wall and then clean up?”

Miss Lila crouched to his eye level. “Thank you for telling me, Pebble. That is great self-advocacy. Yes, one more wall, then we put blocks away together.”

Pebble smiled. “Thank you.” He finished one small wall. He felt proud. Then he used both flippers to put blocks back. “One, two, slide,” he whispered. He moved slow and steady, practicing coordination. The blocks made a soft clack in the bin.

Next, it was hand-washing time. The sink hummed. Warm water felt like a hug on Pebble’s flippers. “Scrub, scrub,” he sang. He rubbed soap until bubbles smelled like oranges. Miss Lila said, “That’s self-care! You are keeping your body clean.”

Pebble dried his flippers on a fluffy towel. The towel felt cozy on his feathers.

After snack, Miss Lila said, “Let’s try the obstacle trail!” Mats were like little blue waves. There were soft cones to go around, and a gentle balance beam. Pebble’s heart thumped with excitement. He loved to move, but he also felt a tiny worry. The beam looked narrow.

“Friends,” said Miss Lila, “remember safety. We move slowly. We ask for help if we need it. Using your voice keeps you safe.”

Pebble stepped onto the beam. His feet felt wobbly. The room was quiet except for soft music and the swish of feet on mats. Pebble took one step. His body wiggled. His feathers tickled with the breeze from the fan. He took another step. Then he paused.

He looked at Miss Lila and lifted his flipper. “I feel wobbly. Can I hold your hand?” he asked.

“Yes,” Miss Lila said warmly. “Thank you for asking. That’s self-advocacy and safety.”

Pebble held her hand. Together they went slow. Step, pause. Step, pause. Pebble felt his belly relax. At the end, he chirped, “I did it!” His voice sounded bright and proud.

Later, Pebble wanted the red boat from the basket, but his friend Tully the turtle had it. Pebble’s cheeks felt warm. He almost reached to grab it, but he remembered the heart on the sign.

He waddled close and said, “Tully, I feel sad. I would like a turn with the red boat. Can we take turns, please?”

Tully looked up. “Okay. Two more minutes, then your turn.” He set a timer shaped like a star. Tick-tick-tick.

Pebble nodded. “Thank you for listening.” When the star chimed, Tully handed him the boat. “Your turn,” Tully said.

“Thank you,” Pebble replied. He placed the boat in the water table. The water felt cool and splashy. Pebble practiced coordination, pushing the boat gently so it didn’t splash out. “Sail, boat, sail,” he hummed.

At rest time, the room grew quiet. Lights dimmed. Pebble lay on his soft blanket. He noticed his body. His feet felt tired. His eyes felt heavy. He whispered, “Miss Lila, I need my cuddle pillow, please.”

Miss Lila brought the small, squishy pillow. “You used your voice again. That is self-care too—knowing what helps you feel calm.”

Pebble hugged the pillow. It smelled like clean cotton. He felt safe and cozy.

When rest time ended, Miss Lila gathered everyone. “Friends, what did we learn today?”

Pebble sat up tall. “I learned to use my voice,” he said. “I can say how I feel. I can ask for help to be safe. I can take turns. I can care for my body.”

Miss Lila clapped softly. “Yes. That is self-advocacy, self-care, coordination, and safety. Pebble, you led the way.”

Pebble’s heart felt warm. He wiggled his flippers and sang, “Waddle, waddle, I can say what I need!”

He looked around the comfortable nursery—the soft rugs, the shiny mobile, the friendly faces. Pebble smiled. “I am brave. I am kind. I can speak up.”

The End.
