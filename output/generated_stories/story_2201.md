# Tiko Sings in the Attic

Tiko was a small singing bird with bright blue feathers. He lived in a quiet attic above a friendly family’s home. The attic smelled like old wood and cedar boxes. Sunlight came in through a tiny round window and made warm golden squares on the floor. Sometimes dust sparkled like tiny stars.

“Good morning, attic!” Tiko chirped. His song sounded like twinkle-twinkle, soft and sweet. He fluffed his feathers and looked around at the trunks and hats and shiny buttons. Tiko liked to sing. He sang when the sun woke up. He sang when the moon peeked in.

One day, Tiko wanted to do things on his own. “I can be independent,” he said. “I can plan my day! I can make my own choices with care.” He hopped onto a big red hat box like a little drum. Tap, tap! His tiny feet made happy sounds.

Tiko made a plan. “First, I will tidy my nest. Then, I will practice songs. After that, I will rest. Later, I will listen and learn a new song.” He drew a simple picture plan on a scrap of paper with a smudgy pencil he found: a nest, a music note, a pillow, and a big ear.

Just then, Grandma Mei came up the creaky stairs. Creak, creak. She held a soft scarf and a tin that smelled like tea. “Hello, little bird,” she said with a smile. “I brought jasmine tea for me, and clean cloth for you.”

“Hello, Grandma Mei!” Tiko chirped. “Thank you.” He bowed his head to show respect. The scarf felt smooth and cool. The tea smell was sweet and flowery. Tiko liked learning from Grandma Mei. She told stories from many places.

Grandma Mei listened to Tiko’s plan. “Oh, you have a schedule,” she said kindly. “That is good time management. You are being independent.”

Tiko puffed with pride. “Yes! I can do it.”

Tiko began to tidy. He picked up soft feathers and tiny strings. “Pick and place, pick and place,” he sang. The attic felt neat. He felt calm.

Next, he practiced songs. “La-la-la, tweet-tweet-tweet!” He tried a gentle lullaby from the family’s baby, a clapping song from the radio, and a new tune he made up himself. His voice rang like silver bells.

Then it was time to rest. He nestled in his clean nest and closed his eyes. The attic was quiet. He heard the hum of the house and the whisper of the wind. He took three deep breaths. In, out. In, out. In, out.

Later, Tiko hopped to the window. Outside, the world was bright. He saw flowers in many colors. He wanted to learn a new song, like he planned.

Grandma Mei opened the tin. It had tiny metal bells. “These are from India,” she said softly. “They jingle in dances.” She shook them. Jingle-jingle! “Would you like to listen?”

“Yes, please,” Tiko said. He listened with big, round eyes. He tried to sing along. “Jing-jing-tweet!” But his song was too fast. He felt a little frustrated.

“I want to do it myself,” Tiko said. “But it is hard.”

Grandma Mei nodded. “You can be independent and still listen and learn. We show respect when we learn carefully, and we thank the people and places the music comes from.”

Tiko took another deep breath. “I will try again. I will listen first.”

They sat in the warm sun square. Grandma Mei jingled slowly. Jingle... pause... jingle. Tiko listened. He watched how her hand moved. He felt the soft beat in his chest. He started to hum gently. “Mmm... tweet... mmm...”

Then he sang, “Jingle here, a gentle tweet. Jingle there, a careful beat.” He moved his wings like a dancer, slow and kind. He did not copy every sound. He added his bird voice with care.

“That is respectful,” Grandma Mei said. “You are learning the heart of the song, and you are saying thank you with your care.”

Tiko smiled. “Thank you, India, for this pretty sound,” he whispered. He bowed again. He felt happy and strong.

Tiko looked at his picture plan. He had one thing left. “I will share my song when the clock says four,” he said, pointing to the round attic clock. The hands moved, tick-tock, tick-tock. He waited. He drank a tiny drop of water. He stretched his wings.

When the clock said four, Tiko sang his new song. It had a soft jingle, a warm hum, and his own bright tweet. The attic seemed to glow. Even the boxes felt like they were smiling.

From downstairs, the family clapped. Clap, clap! The baby giggled. Grandma Mei smiled and tapped the bells. “You did it, Tiko,” she said. “You planned your time. You did your tasks. You learned with respect. You made a song that is yours.”

Tiko felt proud. “I can be independent,” he chirped. “I can take care of my things. I can listen and learn from many cultures. I can say thank you. I can make good choices and follow my plan.”

He fluffed his feathers and looked at the golden window again. The attic was quiet and cozy. Tiko sang a last soft note, like a kiss good night.

The End.
