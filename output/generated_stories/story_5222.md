# Miro the Mouse Tells the Way

Miro was a kind little mouse with soft gray fur and bright, careful eyes. He lived with a friendly family who always buckled him in a tiny, cozy seat in their family car. His seat was snug and safe, right next to the window. He liked to press his paws on the cool glass and feel the gentle hum of the engine. Vrrrmm. Vrrrmm.

One sunny morning, the family was going to visit Grandma. The car smelled like warm crackers and a hint of lemon from a clean wipe. Miro’s whiskers twitched with joy.

“Grandma’s house!” he squeaked softly. “I can’t wait!”

“Me too,” said Mia, the little girl, swinging her feet. “I drew a picture for Grandma.”

Dad smiled in the front seat. “All set? Seat belts on?”

“Click!” said Mom, buckling in. “We’re ready.”

The car rolled onto the road. Trees swished by. The sky was blue, and a breeze whispered through the tiny open window. Miro listened to the tires go roar-shhh, roar-shhh, like ocean waves.

Soon, the car came to a long line of cars. Red lights glowed ahead. Everything felt slow. The car made a small sigh. Hiss.

“Oh no,” said Mia. “Traffic.”

Miro felt a little wiggle in his tummy. “Will we be late?” he asked, in his soft mouse voice.

Mom looked back and smiled gently. “We might be a little late. That’s okay. Let’s stay calm and talk it through.”

Dad nodded. “When things are slow, we can use calm words to share how we feel. That helps us make a plan.”

Miro took a deep breath in and out. In, sniff. Out, puff. “I feel worried,” he said. “I really want to see Grandma.”

“Thank you for telling us,” said Mom. “I hear you. I feel a little worried too. I want to be there on time.”

Mia squeezed her crayon. “I feel squirmy,” she said. “I want to move.”

Dad said, “Let’s take turns. We can all say how we feel, and then we can tell a story together. Stories help us feel calm.”

Miro’s ears perked up. “I love stories!”

“The rule,” said Mom, “is one voice at a time, and we listen. Listening is being kind.”

“Okay,” said Mia. “I go first. I feel squirmy. I want to stretch.”

Mom nodded. “Thank you. We can stretch our fingers.” She wiggled her fingers, slow like little starfish. “Stretch, stretch.”

Mia giggled and copied. Miro wiggled his tiny paws. He felt his tummy settle a bit.

Now it was Miro’s turn. “I feel worried,” he said again, softer. “I want to know we’ll be okay.”

Dad’s voice was steady, warm like cocoa. “We will be okay. We can call Grandma to tell her we are on the way.”

“Good idea,” said Mom, tapping her phone. Soon, Grandma’s happy voice filled the car. “Hello, my darlings!”

“We’re in traffic,” said Mom. “We might be late.”

“That’s fine, sweet peas,” Grandma said. “Be safe. I can’t wait to see you. I’ll have cookies ready.”

Miro could almost taste the cookies—sweet and soft, like warm clouds. He smiled. His worry felt smaller.

Dad said, “Now, let’s make a story. Miro, you start.”

Miro sat tall in his tiny seat. He cleared his little throat. “Once upon a car,” he began, “there was a brave explorer mouse named Miro. He rode in a big, shiny car-ship that said vrrrmm, vrrrmm.”

Mia clapped. “The car-ship sailed on the road-ocean,” she added. “It saw red light islands.”

Mom joined in. “The explorer used calm words to ask for help. He said, ‘Captain, I feel worried. What can we do?’”

Dad said, in a deep captain voice, “We can breathe like whales. In, and hoo, out!” They all breathed together—hoo, slow and long.

Miro giggled. “Then the explorer mouse told a map story. He said, ‘When the lights turn green, we go. When they are red, we rest.’”

The car was quiet for a moment. They all watched the lights. Red. Yellow. Green. The car rolled forward, smooth and steady. Vrrrmm.

Mia peeked at Miro. “Your story helped me,” she said. “I feel calm now.”

Miro felt warm in his chest. He had used his voice. He had been heard. He had helped.

The road opened up. The air whooshed gently through the window. Miro smelled fresh grass and something sweet—maybe a bakery. His ears heard birds singing on a light breeze.

“Let’s make a calm plan for rides,” said Mom. “We talk about feelings. We take turns. We tell a story. We breathe.”

Dad added, “We also share what we need. Mia?”

“I need a wiggle break when we stop,” Mia said.

“Good sharing,” Dad said. “Miro?”

“I need to know the plan,” Miro said. “I like to hear what comes next.”

“I hear you,” said Mom. “Next is Grandma’s house. Then cookies. Then a hug.”

Miro’s whiskers twitched. “That sounds perfect.”

They turned onto Grandma’s street. The car slowed and hummed low. Grandma waved from the porch. The car door opened with a soft thunk, and warm, cookie smell wrapped around them like a blanket.

“You made it!” Grandma cheered.

“We talked and stayed calm,” Mia said proudly.

Miro nodded. “We used our words. We took turns. We told a story.”

Grandma crouched down and gave a gentle smile. “That is the best way. When we talk and listen, we help each other feel safe.”

Miro tucked into a hug. He felt light and happy. He had learned that when feelings get big, calm words make room. A story can be a soft boat that carries everyone through.

The End.
