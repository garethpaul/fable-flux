# Trunks and Turns in the Treehouse

High in a wide, leafy tree stood a cozy treehouse. The walls smelled like warm wood. Sunlight made golden spots on the floor. The wind made the leaves whisper, shhh, shhh, shhh.

Up the ladder climbed a gentle elephant calf named Tiko. His small gray ears flapped. His soft feet made careful thumps on the rungs. He liked to go slow and steady. Tiko carried a basket with bright rope, a soft ball, and crunchy apple slices.

“I’m here!” Tiko called. His voice was deep and sweet.

Inside the treehouse, Tiko saw his friends. Nia the nimble squirrel flicked her fluffy tail. Pip the parrot flapped green wings and clicked his beak. Momo the monkey swung from a rope and giggled.

“Hi, Tiko!” chirped Pip.

“Let’s play!” squeaked Nia.

Momo grinned. “Let’s do the rope swing first!”

Tiko set down the basket. The treehouse floor felt cool under his toes. He took a deep breath. He liked when everyone felt happy and safe.

“We can take turns,” Tiko said. “One at a time. Then everyone gets to swing.”

Nia nodded fast. “Okay! I go first?”

Pip bobbed his head. “I can count. One… two… three!”

Momo hopped. “Me next! Me next!”

Tiko smiled. “We will make a turn line. Nia, then Momo, then Pip, then me.”

Nia took the rope. She held it tight with tiny paws. Whoosh! She swung and squealed. The rope smelled like the tree bark, sweet and green. Nia’s tail brushed Tiko’s trunk. He laughed softly.

Pip clapped his wings. “Good turn, Nia!”

Momo hopped in place. “My turn!” he said. He grabbed the rope. Whoosh! His feet kicked the air. He swung too fast and bonked the wall—tap! He blinked. “I’m okay,” he said, then smiled.

Pip’s turn came next. He held the rope with his claws and swung gently. “Wheee,” he sang. The breeze tickled his feathers.

Tiko’s turn came last. He wrapped the rope around his trunk. It felt rough and strong. He swung slow and smooth. “I like this,” he rumbled. His heart felt calm. Taking turns worked. Everyone got a turn. No one had to push or shout.

After the rope swing, Tiko opened the basket. “Snack time,” he said. The apples smelled sweet and juicy. “One slice each. We can take turns picking.”

Nia picked first. Crunch! She chewed. Momo picked next. Crunch, crunch! Pip pecked his slice—peep, peep! Tiko picked last and took a careful bite. Crunch. The juice dripped. He wiped it with his trunk.

Momo looked at the soft ball in the basket. “Catch game!” he cheered. “Let’s toss in a circle.”

Tiko nodded. “We will say names and take turns. That helps our hands and feet work together. That’s called coordination.”

“Co-or-di-na-tion,” Pip echoed. He liked new words.

They stood in a circle. The treehouse felt snug and safe. Nia tossed the ball to Pip. “Pip!” she called.

Pip caught it with a soft thump and tossed to Momo. “Momo!”

Momo tossed to Tiko. “Tiko!”

Tiko caught gently. He felt proud. He tossed the ball back to Nia. “Nia!”

They went around and around. Their voices made a happy rhythm. Pip, Momo, Tiko, Nia. Pip, Momo, Tiko, Nia.

Then the ball rolled near a little wooden ramp. It looked like a tiny slide. Momo’s eyes sparkled. “Race the ball!” he shouted. He grabbed it and put it at the top of the ramp.

“Wait,” Tiko said kindly. “Whose turn is it?”

Momo froze. His cheeks went pink under his fur. “Oops,” he whispered. “It was Pip’s turn.”

Pip tilted his head. “It’s okay,” he said softly.

Tiko touched Momo’s shoulder with his trunk. “Thank you for listening,” he said. “We can fix this. Momo, can you be honest?”

Momo’s tail drooped. He looked at Pip. “I’m sorry,” he said. “I didn’t wait. I wanted it too much.”

Pip nodded. “Thank you for telling the truth,” he said. “That was honest.”

Nia smiled. “Let’s make a plan. We can count to three before each toss. That will help us wait.”

Tiko grinned. “Good idea. Counting helps with coordination, too.”

They lined up again. Pip held the ball. “Ready? One… two… three!” He tossed to Momo.

Momo waited until “three” to toss to Tiko. Tiko waited, then tossed to Nia. Around and around they went. The ball made a soft pat in their hands. Their feet shuffled, tap-tap. The wind hummed. The leaves clapped.

Soon, Pip said, “My wings are tired.”

Nia yawned. “My tail is tired, too.”

Tiko set the ball down. “We did a good job,” he said. “We took turns. We waited. We told the truth when we forgot.”

Momo’s eyes were bright again. “Taking turns helps everyone have fun,” he said.

Tiko nodded. “And being honest helps hearts feel safe.”

Pip bobbed. “And counting and waiting help our bodies work together. Co-or-di-na-tion!”

They all giggled. They packed the rope and the ball. They cleaned up the apple skins. Tiko looked around the warm treehouse. He felt proud and peaceful.

“Today,” Tiko said, “we learned that taking turns keeps play fair. Waiting and counting help our bodies. And honesty fixes mistakes.”

“Let’s do it again tomorrow,” Nia said.

“Yes!” Momo and Pip cheered.

The wind whispered, shhh, shhh, shhh, like the treehouse agreed.

The End.
