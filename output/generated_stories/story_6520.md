# Paco the Parrot and the Tidy Time Plan

Paco was a bright, creative parrot with rainbow feathers and a curious mind. He lived in a busy basement where the washing machine hummed, boxes rustled, and a tiny window let in a stripe of sunlight. The basement smelled like warm soap, dusty cardboard, and fresh wood. Drip, drip, went a pipe. Buzz, buzz, hummed the dryer. It was a busy place for a busy bird!

Paco loved to make things. He painted with his wings, he stacked blocks into tall towers, and he glued shiny buttons onto paper. “Squawk! I have big ideas!” he chirped, flapping with joy.

One day, Paco wanted to make a “World Parade” artwork. He wanted to paint flags from many places, build a tiny drum, and hang paper people holding hands. “I will make it all today!” he said. “Everything, everything, everything!”

He zipped around the basement. He pulled out paints—red, blue, green, and gold. He tugged out yarn and tape. He rolled out paper like a white river on the floor. But the basement was already busy. The washing machine went swish-swash. A box tower teetered. Buttons scattered and bounced like tiny pebbles. Tap-tap-tap, they went on the floor.

Paco tried to do everything at once. He dipped a wing in paint, then pecked at the tape, then reached for the drum pieces. He bumped a box. Thump! The box fell. “Oh no!” Paco squawked. “So many things! So little time!”

His friend Mina, a small mouse with soft gray fur, poked her head from behind a spool of yarn. “Hi, Paco,” Mina squeaked. “You look busy.”

“I want to make a World Parade,” said Paco, breathing fast. “Flags, drums, and paper friends from everywhere! But my feathers feel twisty. I cannot finish.”

Mina twitched her whiskers. “When I feel twisty, I make a plan,” she said. “A tiny plan, one step at a time.”

Paco blinked. “A tiny plan?”

Mina nodded. “Yes. We can use the big clock.” She pointed to a round clock on the wall. Tick-tock, tick-tock, it went. “We set times. We do a little, then a little more.”

Paco fluffed his feathers. “Okay,” he said. “Let’s make a Tidy Time Plan!”

Mina drew three boxes on a small card with a purple crayon.

Box 1: Paint flags.
Box 2: Build drum.
Box 3: Make paper friends.

“Each box is ten ticks,” Mina said. “Listen: tick-tock, tick-tock. When the little hand moves a bit, we switch.”

Paco smiled. “I can do that!”

First came Box 1. Paco dipped his wing in smooth paint. Swoosh! Red and white stripes. Swoop! A blue square with a tiny dot star. Swish! Green and yellow shapes. “These flags are from many places,” Paco said softly. “The world is big and different. Different is good.” He listened to the tick-tock and took deep breaths. The paint smelled sweet and a little like rain.

“Two more ticks,” Mina said kindly.

Paco added a small sun to one flag. “Stop!” he chirped when the time was up. He wiped his wing on a cloth. “Next box.”

Box 2 was the drum. Paco found a round tin and stretched paper over it. Tap-tap with tiny claws. Pat-pat with soft wings. He wrapped it with bright yarn. “This drum can play many beats,” he said. “Boom for big steps. Tap for tiny steps.” He tapped: boom-tap, boom-tap. The dryer buzzed, and Paco smiled. “I hear music in the basement!”

“Two ticks left,” Mina squeaked.

Paco tied a neat knot. “Stop! Next box.”

Box 3 was the paper friends. Mina cut little paper people holding hands. Snip-snip went the scissors. Paco colored each one. One wore a red hat. One wore a blue scarf. One had gold shoes. “We are all different,” said Paco. “We can still hold hands.”

“Time,” Mina said gently.

Paco looked around. There were still buttons on the floor, a tilting paint cup, and a small mess. He felt a little droop in his wings. “Oh,” he said. “I did not finish everything.”

Mina smiled. “Look at your plan,” she said. “You finished each box. That is success! Now we do Tidy Time.”

They made a new tiny plan: four ticks to clean. Paco pecked buttons into a jar. Click, click. Mina wound up the yarn. Whirr. Paco wiped paint drips. Swish. They sang a clean-up song: “Little steps, little steps, we can do hard things!”

When the clock went tick-tock to the last tick, the basement looked neat again. The flags dried in a bright row. The drum sat ready. The paper friends smiled from a string.

Paco’s chest puffed with pride. “I did it,” he said. “I did it by making a plan, doing one thing at a time, and taking little rests.”

Mina clapped her tiny paws. “You were brave and calm,” she said. “You used your time well.”

Paco hung the flags, tied the drum, and strung the paper friends. The basement felt warm and happy. The hum of the machines sounded like a parade. Boom-tap. Swish-swish. Tick-tock.

Paco called, “Friends, come see!” The old cat from upstairs peered down. The neighbor kid waved from the tiny window. Paco played the drum: boom-tap. “This is our World Parade,” he said. “We all belong.”

He looked at the clock and smiled. “Time helps me make dreams real,” Paco said. “I will use plans again when things feel big.”

Mina nodded. “One step, then the next. That is the way.”

Paco fluffed his rainbow feathers. “Squawk! I am ready for new ideas,” he chirped. “But first, snack time.”

They laughed, and the basement buzzed softly, calm and proud.

The End.
