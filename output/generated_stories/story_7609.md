# Milo Mouse and the Rooftop Garden

The sun was soft and golden. A tiny breeze wiggled the clotheslines. On the rooftop of a tall building, a kind mouse named Milo brushed his whiskers and sniffed the morning air. It smelled like wet soil, sweet tomatoes, and mint.

“Good morning, little leaves,” Milo said. He patted the small garden he had planted in old tin cans. “I will water you, but only a little. We must save water.”

Milo filled a thimble with water from a jar. Drip, drip, drip. The soil drank happily. A sparrow fluttered to the railing and tilted her head. “Chirp! What are you doing, Mouse?”

“I’m caring for my rooftop garden,” Milo said with a smile. “I’m careful with water. That’s called conservation. I share what I have and save some for later.”

Just then, a loud clatter echoed. Clang! Clunk! A big gray cat named Gus leaped onto the roof. His tail swished. His paws thumped on the boards. “This is my sun spot,” Gus said. “I like to nap right here.”

Milo looked at the sunny square where Gus stood. It was also where the cans of tomatoes liked to sit. The plants needed the warm sun. Milo’s ears twitched. “Oh,” he said softly. “I need sun for the tomatoes.”

Gus narrowed his eyes. “But I need sun for my nap.”

The sparrow fluffed her feathers. “Chirp! What will you do?”

Milo’s tummy felt tight. He didn’t want to argue. He didn’t want to be bossy. He wanted to be kind. He took a deep breath. He remembered a helpful plan: use words, not fuss. “Let’s talk,” Milo said. “Can we communicate?”

Gus blinked. “Talk?”

“Yes,” Milo said gently. “I feel worried. If the tomatoes don’t get sun, they will droop. They will be sad and soft.”

Gus sat, paws tucked. “I feel sleepy. If I don’t get sun, I will be grumpy.”

Milo nodded. “Thank you for telling me your feelings.”

The sparrow chirped, “What if you share?”

“Sharing is good,” Milo agreed. “But how?”

They all looked around the rooftop. The sun moved slowly. The shadows crawled.

Milo pointed to a patch of sun near a silver chimney. “Gus, could you nap here for a little while? Then we can switch. We can take turns with the sun.”

Gus squinted. “How long is a little while?”

Milo held up his thimble. “Until I count five slow drips of water. Drip… drip… drip… drip… drip. Then we switch.”

Gus thought. His tail stopped swishing. “Hmm. That sounds fair. But the boards near the chimney feel hard on my belly.”

Milo’s whiskers quivered with an idea. “I have a soft scrap of cloth. I can make a small pillow.” He ran to his nest, tugged a tiny square of blue fabric, and folded it carefully. He set it by the chimney. “Here, you can use this. I want to be generous.”

Gus sniffed the pillow. “It smells like soap and sunshine.” He smiled a little. “Okay, Mouse. I will try.”

Milo counted slow drips as he watered the mint. The water went plip, plip, plip, and the mint leaves smelled bright and cool. The sparrow sang a soft song. Gus stretched on the blue pillow and purred, a low rumbly sound like a tiny engine.

“Five drips,” Milo said kindly. “Would you like to switch now?”

Gus yawned. “Yes.” He stood and padded to the sunny square by the garden. “Your turn, tomatoes.”

Milo gently moved the tomato cans into the sun. “Thank you for listening, Gus.”

“Thank you for the pillow,” Gus said. “And thank you for asking, not shouting.”

The day moved on. The three friends used their plan again. Five drips for Gus’s nap. Five drips for the tomatoes. Five drips for the sparrow to warm her wings on the railing. They took turns, spoke kindly, and listened.

Later, a gray cloud slid over the sun. A cool wind brushed the rooftop. “Brr,” chirped the sparrow. “The air feels cold.”

Milo hugged his arms around himself. “The water jar is low,” he said. “We must save the last sips for the plants.”

Gus looked at the jar, then at Milo. His ears twitched. “I want a drink,” he said. “But the tomatoes look thirsty, too.”

Milo spoke softly. “Let’s communicate again. I feel worried about the plants. You feel thirsty. What can we do?”

Gus thought. The sparrow hopped near an old bucket. “Chirp! I see raindrops on the lid. Plip! Plop!” Tiny drops sparkled.

Milo’s eyes lit up. “We can collect the rain. That is conservation too!” He set out the bucket and a row of cups. The rain made music: pat, pat, pat. Soon, there was enough water for everyone.

Milo poured a small drink for Gus. “Here you go.”

Gus lapped and purred. “Thank you. You are generous.”

Milo gave the tomatoes a careful sip. He smiled. “We did it by talking, listening, and sharing.”

That evening, the sun peeked from the cloud and painted the sky pink and orange. The tomatoes looked bright. Gus purred on his blue pillow. The sparrow tucked her head under her wing.

Milo watched the soft colors and felt warm inside. “Today we learned: When we use our words and listen, we can find a way. We can compromise, be generous, and take care of our world.”

The friends nodded. Up on the rooftop, the garden, the cat, the sparrow, and the kind mouse all felt safe and happy.

The End.
