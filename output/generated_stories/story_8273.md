# Dippy the Rooftop Dolphin

One sunny afternoon, a playful dolphin named Dippy splashed out of a rooftop pool. The pool was on a tall, flat rooftop garden with bright flowers, soft grass, and a safe little fence all around. The air smelled like oranges from the trees. Wind tickled Dippy’s fins. He clicked and squeaked, “Eee-eee! Time to play!”

Dippy loved the rooftop pool. He loved jumping high and making rainbow sparkles. He wiggled his tail and shouted, “Big jump time!” But just then, his friends came up the stairs: Tessa the turtle, Benny the bunny, and Grandma Nene, a wise old seagull who wore tiny shell beads around her neck.

“Hello, little waves,” Grandma Nene said kindly. “Remember our rooftop rules. We take turns. We look with quiet eyes. We listen with calm ears. We keep each other safe.”

Dippy bobbed in the water. “I want to jump first!” he said. His flippers made happy splish-splashes.

Tessa peered over the pool edge. “I want a turn too,” she said, slow and gentle.

Benny bounced. “Me! Me! Me!”

Grandma Nene smiled. “We have a tradition,” she said. The beads clicked softly as she nodded. “On this rooftop, before we play, we share a ‘Calm Start.’ We breathe together, like the sea. In and out.”

She opened her wings. “In… two, three. Out… two, three.”

Dippy tried. He breathed in. He breathed out. The wind smelled like flowers. The sun felt warm and cozy on his back. He felt calm… a little.

“Thank you,” he said. “Now can I go first?”

Tessa and Benny looked at each other. “We can make a turn list,” Tessa suggested. “First Dippy, then me, then Benny.”

Benny’s ears wiggled. “Okay!”

Grandma Nene gave a soft caw. “And we wait behind the safety line,” she said, tapping a painted yellow line on the rooftop. “Wings, paws, and flippers behind the line.”

Dippy nodded and swam to the edge. He was ready. He bent his tail and leapt—whoosh! He flew up, up, up and splashed down, making tiny glitter drops. “Eee-eee!” he cheered.

“Great jump!” Tessa clapped.

“Sparkle jump!” Benny bounced.

Now it was Tessa’s turn. She stepped slowly to the line. But Dippy’s heart thumped fast. He wanted to jump again right now. He felt hot inside, like a bubble ready to pop. His flippers squeezed the water. “I want another turn,” he said, louder.

“It’s my turn,” Tessa reminded him gently.

“I can be quick,” Dippy said. “Just one more!”

Benny frowned. “We said we take turns.”

Dippy’s cheeks felt hot. His tail slapped the water—splash! His voice got bigger. “But I want it now!”

Grandma Nene watched with kind, steady eyes. “Dippy, your feelings are big like waves,” she said. “Let’s use our calm tools.”

“I don’t want calm tools!” Dippy snapped. His splash made Benny step back. Tessa’s eyes grew wide. The rooftop felt too bright. The wind felt too loud. Dippy’s tummy squeezed. He was mad.

Grandma Nene walked to the shade of an orange tree. The leaves made soft, sh-sh-sh sounds. “Dippy, come sit by me for a moment,” she said. “Everyone else, please stay behind the safety line.”

Dippy swam to the pool edge and rested his chin on the warm tile. He could smell oranges and wet stone. Grandma Nene hummed a low sea-song. “When we feel mad,” she said, “we can S.T.A.R.”

“What is STAR?” Dippy asked, still grumpy.

“Stop,” Grandma Nene said softly. “Take a breath. And Relax.”

Dippy blinked. “Stop. Take a breath. And Relax.”

“Let’s try,” said Grandma Nene. “Stop—pause your body.” Dippy held still. His tail floated. “Take a breath,” she said. They breathed in… two, three. Out… two, three. The air tasted sweet. The angry bubble in Dippy’s tummy got smaller.

“And Relax,” Grandma Nene whispered. “Let your fins feel heavy. Let your eyes get soft.”

Dippy closed his eyes. He heard bees buzz-buzz in the flowers. He felt the warm sun. He smelled the oranges again. His shoulders loosened. His voice got quiet. “I feel a little better,” he said.

Grandma Nene nodded. “You did it. Big feelings come and go, like tides. Your calm tools help you float.”

Dippy looked at Tessa and Benny. They waited by the yellow line, patient and still. “I’m sorry,” Dippy said. “I was mad, and I splashed too big. I will take turns.”

Tessa smiled. “Thank you.”

Benny grinned. “My ears got wet, but it’s okay!”

Grandma Nene fluffed her feathers. “Now, our tradition,” she said. She took off one tiny shell bead and held it up. It was smooth and cool-looking. “We share the Turn Bead,” she explained. “The one holding the bead is the one with the turn. The rest wait safely behind the line.”

She placed the bead by Tessa. “Tessa’s turn.” Tessa dipped a careful flipper in the water, then did a slow, gentle slide. “Wheee,” she said softly, making small ripples.

“Good slide!” Dippy clapped, feeling proud of his calm.

Then Grandma Nene gave the bead to Benny. Benny bounced to the line and did a little hop and a giggle. “Hop-splash!” Water sprinkled like tiny rain.

Finally, the bead came back to Dippy. He held it close. It felt cool and smooth against his fin. He smiled. “Thank you for waiting,” he said to his friends. He took his turn and made a big, careful jump—whoosh! Sparkles danced, but stayed inside the pool.

Grandma Nene clapped her wings. “You kept each other safe,” she said. “You took turns. You followed our rooftop tradition. And Dippy, you used STAR. How do you feel now?”

Dippy felt light and happy. “I feel calm,” he said. “When I get mad, I can Stop, Take a breath, And Relax.”

Benny nodded. “I can STAR too!”

Tessa added, “And we can share the Turn Bead.”

The wind softened, like a hug. The orange tree swayed. The rooftop felt friendly again. Dippy swam a quiet circle. “Thank you, Grandma Nene,” he said. “Thank you, friends. Taking turns is nice. Being safe is nice. And calming down helps everyone.”

They all smiled and played until the sun started to set, making the pool glow pink and gold. Together, they sang the sea-song and breathed in and out, a calm, happy team.

The End.
