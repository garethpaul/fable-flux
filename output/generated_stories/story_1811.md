# Ellie Tries Again

In a sunny garage workshop, a gentle elephant calf named Ellie stood on soft rubber mats. The air smelled like pine wood and orange soap. Sunlight made dust sparkle like tiny stars. Tools hung neatly on a pegboard. A big poster said, “We listen. We try. We learn.”

Ellie’s ears flapped with excitement. “I want to make art,” she said. “A colorful wind spinner!”

Grandpa Gus, who wore blue overalls with paint dots, smiled kindly. “We can do it together,” he said. “We will build and paint. We will follow directions. Step by step.”

Ellie nodded. “Step by step,” she echoed, feeling brave.

Grandpa took out a small box. Inside were wooden circles, a stick, some safe glue, and bright paint. The paint smelled a little like lemons. “First,” said Grandpa, pointing to a simple picture guide, “we sand the edges. Then we glue. Last, we paint. We must wait for things to dry.”

“I can do it!” Ellie said. Her trunk wiggled with joy.

Grandpa handed her a soft sanding block. “Gentle circles,” he said. “Like rubbing a sleepy pebble.”

Ellie tried. Swish, swish. The wood felt smoother. “It’s like giving the wood a hug,” she giggled.

“Perfect,” said Grandpa. “Now, we glue. Only a pea-sized dot.”

Ellie looked at the picture. It showed one small dot of glue. But Ellie felt excited. She wanted it to be strong and fast. She squeezed her glue bottle. SQUISH! A big blob popped out and oozed over the edge.

“Oh no!” Ellie squeaked. The glue was sticky and cold. It dripped onto the mat. Her big feet shuffled. The spinner pieces slid and tilted.

Grandpa Gus gently put a hand on her shoulder. “It’s okay,” he said softly. “Take a breath with me. In… and out.”

Ellie’s ears drooped. “I didn’t follow the directions,” she whispered. “I messed up.”

Grandpa nodded. “We all make mistakes when we learn a skill. We can clean and try again.”

He gave Ellie a warm cloth. Together they wiped the extra glue. The cloth felt damp and squishy. Ellie sniffed and blinked. “I’m sorry, Grandpa.”

“I forgive you,” Grandpa said. “And do you forgive yourself?”

Ellie thought. Her heart felt heavy, then lighter. “Yes,” she said. “I forgive me. I will try again. Step by step.”

Grandpa smiled. “That’s the spirit. Now, a pea-sized dot.”

Ellie squeezed slowly. Plip. Just a tiny dot. They pressed the pieces together. “Hold and count to ten,” said Grandpa.

“One… two… three…” Ellie counted in a sing-song voice. By ten, the pieces stayed put. “It worked!” she trumpeted softly.

“Next,” said Grandpa, “we wait for the glue to dry.”

Ellie’s eyes grew wide. “Wait?” Waiting felt hard. The paints were so bright—red like a juicy apple, blue like the morning sky, yellow like sunshine.

Grandpa showed her the picture guide again. “The directions say, dry first, paint last. Waiting is part of the skill.”

Ellie rocked on her feet. She hummed a small song. She watched a dust mote float and land on her trunk. It tickled. Giggle! Grandpa gave her a timer shaped like a duck. It quacked when time was up. Ellie listened to its soft ticking. Tock, tick, tock.

Quack! The timer went off. “Dry!” Ellie cheered.

Grandpa set out the paints and a short, soft brush. “Dip, dab, wipe,” he said. “That’s our painting rhythm.”

“Dip, dab, wipe,” Ellie echoed. She dipped her brush into blue. Cool paint kissed the wood. Dab, dab. Wipe on the edge of the jar. She switched to yellow. “It looks like sun on the ocean!” she said.

“Beautiful,” said Grandpa. “Try small strokes.”

Ellie moved the brush in tiny lines. She liked the scratchy sound on the wood. She added red dots, green swirls, and a purple stripe. A happy feeling grew in her belly, warm and bubbly.

Suddenly, her tail swished and bumped the paint jar. Oh no! The jar wobbled. Ellie froze. The jar settled. No spill. She took a slow breath. “Careful body,” she whispered. “Small moves.”

Grandpa nodded. “You followed your plan. Good self-control.”

At last, the spinner was painted. “Now we wait again,” said Grandpa gently.

Ellie looked at the duck timer. “Waiting helps paint stay pretty,” she said. She set the timer and sang her waiting song. When it quacked, the paint was dry and smooth.

Grandpa helped attach the stick. The spinner was ready. They opened the garage door. A soft breeze whooshed in, smelling like cut grass. Ellie held the spinner high. It caught the wind and turned—swish, swish, swish—colors dancing in the air.

Ellie’s eyes sparkled. “We did it! I learned a new skill. I followed directions. I fixed my mistake.”

Grandpa patted her shoulder. “You practiced patience, too. That’s a skill.”

Ellie nodded. “When I make a mistake, I can say sorry, get help, forgive myself, and try again.”

They hung the spinner by the door. It twirled gently, a rainbow wheel.

Ellie smiled big. “Tomorrow, can we make a picture frame?”

Grandpa chuckled. “Step by step, Ellie. We will.”

Ellie lifted her trunk. “Dip, dab, wipe,” she said, and they both laughed.

The End.
