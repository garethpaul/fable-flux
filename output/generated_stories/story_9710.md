# Benny Beaver's Quiet Plan

Benny was a busy beaver with bright teeth and a wide, wiggly tail. One rainy afternoon, he hurried into the musical practice room. The room smelled like polished wood. Soft lights glowed. Drums, flutes, and a shiny piano waited quietly. Benny loved to build, tap, and make rhythms. Tap-tap-tap went his paws on the floor.

“I want to play!” Benny said. His voice echoed a tiny bit. “I want to play now!”

But when he grabbed a drum and banged—BOOM, BOOM, BOOM!—the music stands rattled. A flute on the shelf wobbled. A little string on a small guitar went twang and snapped loose.

Benny’s eyes grew big. “Oh no,” he whispered. His cheeks felt hot. He wanted to bang more, but his tummy felt tight and twisty.

At that moment, Ms. Birdie, the music teacher, fluttered in. She was a small brown bird with a soft scarf and kind eyes. “Hello, Benny,” she chirped. “I hear big sounds. What is your plan?”

“My plan?” Benny blinked. “I don’t have a plan. I just want to play now. I feel mad and bouncy.”

Ms. Birdie tilted her head. “When we feel mad and bouncy, we can use a strategy. A strategy is a smart plan. We stop. We listen. We think. Then we act.”

“Stop. Listen. Think. Act,” Benny repeated slowly. He tapped the drum gently with one paw. tap… tap… “I can try.”

Ms. Birdie smiled. “First, stop the big banging. Put your paws on your tummy. Take a deep breath with me.” They breathed in… and out. Benny felt the air fill his cheeks like a balloon. The room sounded softer.

“Now listen,” Ms. Birdie said. They listened. Drip… drip… drip… went the rain on the window. Hummm… hummm… went the lights. The piano made a tiny ping when a drop touched the frame.

“I hear the room,” Benny said. His shoulders relaxed. “It’s like the room is talking.”

“Good listening,” Ms. Birdie chirped. “Now think. What is our goal?”

“I want to play music,” Benny said, “and not break things, and be kind to the room.”

“That is a good goal,” said Ms. Birdie. “We can make a gentle music plan. That is our strategy.”

Benny’s tail thumped softly. “A gentle music plan,” he repeated. “What do we need?”

“We use quiet first, loud later,” Ms. Birdie said. “We use soft sticks, not hard ones. We take turns. We also care for our instruments. We fix, not waste. That is called sustainability. We keep our things working so we don’t throw them away.”

Benny looked at the snapped string. His heart dipped. “I broke the guitar string.”

“We can fix it,” Ms. Birdie said kindly. “We will learn to mend. We will make it sing again.”

Benny breathed in and out. “Okay. I can try.”

They made a plan on a little chalkboard:

1) Stop, Listen, Think, Act.
2) Start soft: tap with fingers.
3) Choose tools: soft mallets.
4) Take turns and watch faces.
5) Care and mend: no waste.

Benny touched the drum with two fingers. tap… tap… tap… It sounded like raindrops. He smiled. “The drum says drip-drip.”

“Beautiful,” Ms. Birdie said. “Now try the shaker.” Benny picked a wooden shaker. shhh… shhh… It sounded like wind in trees.

“I like this,” Benny said. “I feel calm.”

“Let’s practice anger-management,” Ms. Birdie said. “When you feel mad, you can squeeze your paws, then open them. You can count to three.”

Benny squeezed. He counted, “One… two… three.” His tummy untwisted. “That helps!”

They listened again. The room seemed happy. Then Ms. Birdie gave Benny soft mallets. “Now, louder, but with control.”

Benny lifted the mallets high, then paused. He remembered: Stop, Listen, Think, Act. He looked at Ms. Birdie. She nodded. He played a steady beat: tum… tum… tum… The music stands stayed still. The flute did not wobble. The piano hummed along.

“Great strategy,” Ms. Birdie said. “You used your plan!”

Benny grinned. “I did it! I used a strategy. I listened. I thought. I acted.”

They moved to the little guitar. Together, they replaced the string. Ms. Birdie showed him how to wind it slowly. Whirr… click… twang… The guitar sang a warm note.

“We fixed it,” Benny said proudly. “We did not throw it away. That is good for our world.”

“Yes,” Ms. Birdie chirped. “That is sustainability. We care for what we have.”

Benny’s friends peeped in: a bunny with big ears and a mole with tiny glasses. “Can we play too?” asked the bunny.

“Yes,” Benny said. “We have a plan.” He pointed to the chalkboard. “Stop, Listen, Think, Act. Start soft. Take turns. Care and mend.”

They all tried. The bunny tapped the triangle: ting… ting… The mole brushed the cymbal: ssshhh… Benny kept the beat: tum… tum… tum… The music felt like rain, wind, and sunshine all together. Everyone smiled. The room did not shake. The instruments shone.

Benny’s heart felt light. “Strategy helps me play. Listening helps me learn. Caring helps our world.”

Ms. Birdie fluffed her scarf. “You solved your problem with a smart plan. You can use this plan any time—at music, at play, and at home.”

Benny nodded. “Stop, Listen, Think, Act,” he said again. He patted his tummy and took one more deep breath. He was still a busy beaver, but now he had a quiet, clever plan inside.

The End.
