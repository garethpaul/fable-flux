# Fawn’s Quiet Attic Concert

In a quiet attic, where soft dust danced in the sunbeams, a gentle deer fawn named Fawn peeked from behind a stack of old trunks. The attic smelled like cedar and warm blankets. Little motes of light floated like tiny stars. It was cozy and still.

Fawn had found a shiny tin pan, a wooden spoon, and a small bell. “I want to play a song,” Fawn whispered. “I want to make music for everyone.”

Fawn tapped the pan. Clonk. Fawn shook the bell. Ting-ting. “Hmm,” Fawn said. “It sounds messy.”

Fawn tried again, faster. Clonk-ting, clonk-ting, clonk—CLANG! The loud clang made Fawn’s ears twitch. The attic window rattled a little. Fawn’s heart did a fast patter. “Oh no. That did not sound like a song.”

From the rafters, a small brown bat peeked out, blinking. “Hello,” said Bat in a sleepy voice. “I am Nib. What are you doing?”

“I’m trying to play a song,” Fawn said, eyes big and hopeful. “But it’s not working.”

Nib stretched his wings. “Songs take practice,” he yawned gently. “Would you like help?”

Fawn nodded. “Yes, please.”

Nib fluttered down and landed on the tin pan. “First, we can plan,” he said. “Let’s choose a steady beat. Tap slowly. Tap softly. Count, too.”

“Plan, then practice,” Fawn repeated. “I can do that.”

Fawn took a deep breath. “One, two, one, two,” Fawn counted. Tap… tap… tap… tap… The wooden spoon made a soft clonk that didn’t scare the dust at all.

Nib smiled. “Good. Now add the bell on two. Like this: tap… ting!”

Fawn tried. Tap… ting! Tap… ting! The bell made a bright, happy sparkle in the air.

“Patience,” Nib said. “Slow is okay. Slow helps you learn.”

Fawn’s tail flicked. “I want to be fast. I want to be good right now.”

“I know,” Nib said kindly. “But good playing grows each time you practice. Just like a seed grows little by little.”

Fawn listened. The attic was quiet except for the soft taps and tings. The sun beam moved a tiny bit across the floor. Fawn kept going. Tap… ting… tap… ting…

Then the floor creaked. A gray mouse poked up from a small hole near a trunk. “Hello!” squeaked Mouse. “I’m Mima. Are you making a show?”

“I want to,” Fawn said. “But I must practice first.”

“That is fair,” Mima nodded. “May I clap along?”

Nib looked at Fawn. “Is it fair to let Mima join?”

Fawn thought. “Yes. But we will take turns. Everyone gets a chance.”

Mima smiled and clapped softly. Clap… clap… Fawn tapped. Tap… ting… Mima clapped. Clap… clap… Nib kept time with a tiny wing beat. Thrum… thrum…

Fawn felt happy. The rhythm felt smooth, like walking on soft moss. But then Fawn saw a shiny glass bottle on a shelf. “Maybe I can blow a note,” Fawn said. Fawn puffed into the bottle neck. Whooo… It made a low, windy sound. Fawn giggled. “That was nice!”

Nib nodded. “Good idea. But let’s plan. We will try the bottle after we finish ten good beats.” He held up a wing claw. “Counting helps.”

“Okay,” Fawn agreed. “That is fair for the song and fair for me.”

They counted together. “One… two… three… four… five… six… seven… eight… nine… ten!”

“Now the bottle,” Nib said.

Fawn took a slow breath and blew. Whooo… Tap… ting… clap… The attic filled with a soft song: wind, bell, beat, and tiny claps. It sounded gentle and bright, like rain on leaves.

Suddenly the wooden spoon slipped from Fawn’s hoof. Clatter! The pan wobbled. Fawn’s ears drooped. “I made a mistake,” Fawn whispered, cheeks warm.

Nib fluttered up and landed on Fawn’s head like a little hat. “Mistakes are part of practice,” he said. “We can try again.”

Mima nodded. “We can wait. Patience is fair for everyone.”

Fawn took another breath. “I will pick up the spoon. I will start slow.” Fawn picked it up carefully. Tap… pause… tap… ting… Fawn counted softly and kept going. The rhythm came back, smooth and steady.

Nib smiled. “You did it. You fixed it by practicing.”

Mima wiggled her whiskers. “Let’s have a concert now! But let’s invite everyone.”

Fawn looked around the quiet attic. “We will plan our concert,” Fawn said. “We will make a list.” Fawn used a bit of chalk on a board. “Plan: one, practice; two, invite friends; three, take turns; four, play slow and steady.”

Soon, a ladybug, a moth, and a sleepy cat from downstairs tiptoed up. Fawn explained the plan. “We will each have a turn. We will be patient. We will be fair. We will practice once before we play.”

Everyone agreed. They practiced the beat together. Then, the concert began. Fawn tapped the pan. Tap… tap… Mima clapped. Clap… clap… Nib shook the bell. Ting… ting… The bottle sang its whooo. The moth fluttered like a soft drum. The ladybug clicked tiny feet on a box. The cat hummed a purr that made the floor feel warm.

The music filled the attic like a hug. When they finished, everyone cheered in little voices. Fawn’s eyes shone. “We did it,” Fawn said. “We made a song.”

Nib bowed. “You practiced. You were patient. You made a fair plan. That is why it worked.”

Fawn nodded, heart calm and happy. “Practice helps me grow,” Fawn said. “I will practice a little every day.”

The quiet attic seemed to smile back, full of soft light and gentle sounds.

The End.
