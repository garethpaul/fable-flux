# Koala Kaya’s Smart Study Breaks

Koala Kaya lived in a cozy home office with a big, sunny window. The room smelled like fresh paper and a little like eucalyptus tea. Soft keys went click-clack on a keyboard. The chair went swish-swish when it turned. Kaya liked the quiet hum of the computer and the warm touch of the sun on her fuzzy ears.

Every morning, Kaya sat at a small desk beside a big desk. The big desk was where Auntie Kim worked. The small desk was Kaya’s learning spot. It had crayons, books, and a tiny clock with a tick-tick sound.

“Good morning, Auntie,” said Kaya in a calm voice.

“Good morning, Kaya,” Auntie Kim smiled. “Today is a learning day. We can have fun and learn, too.”

Kaya’s tummy felt fluttery. She loved learning. But sometimes, sitting too long made her legs wiggly.

Kaya took a deep breath. In goes the air. Out goes the air. Calm and cozy.

“What is our plan?” Kaya asked. “I like a plan.”

“A plan is a good strategy,” said Auntie Kim. “First, we read. Then, we count. Then, we move with an exercise break.”

Kaya clapped softly. “Yes, a plan!”

They began to read a book about shapes. The paper felt smooth under Kaya’s paws. “Circle,” she said, tracing a round circle. “It is like the sun.”

“Triangle,” said Auntie Kim, pointing. “Three sides.”

Kaya touched each side. “One, two, three,” she counted. “Like a little roof.”

Soon they counted buttons in a jar. Tap, tap, tap went the buttons. “One, two, three, four, five,” Kaya said. “I can count!”

A little later, Kaya’s legs started to wiggle. Her ears twitched. Her body whispered, Move, please.

Kaya put her paw up. “Auntie, I need a break,” she said in a calm voice. “My legs are wiggly.”

“That is good self-advocacy,” said Auntie Kim. “You told me what you need. Let’s use our strategy. Exercise break!”

They stood up. The rug felt soft under their feet. “Stretch up tall like a tree,” said Auntie Kim.

Kaya stretched. “I am a eucalyptus tree,” she giggled.

“Now hop like a kangaroo,” said Auntie Kim.

Boing, boing, boing! Kaya hopped three times. Her body felt warm and awake. She took a sip of cool water. Ahh.

Back at the small desk, Kaya felt ready. “What’s next?” she asked.

“Let’s learn new words,” said Auntie Kim. “We will make a word wall.”

Kaya chose bright sticky notes. They felt a little sticky on her paws. “Read,” Kaya said, writing slowly. “Plan. Break. Help.”

“These are strong words,” said Auntie Kim. “They help us learn.”

Kaya smiled. “When I need help, I will ask. When I need a break, I will say so.”

Tick-tick went the tiny clock. Kaya traced letters with her finger. She listened to the quiet hum. She smelled the tea. She felt calm and proud.

Soon, a pop-up sound went ping from the computer. Auntie Kim said, “I have a call in five minutes.” She looked at Kaya. “What is our plan?”

Kaya thought. She tapped her chin. “I can read the shape book again. Then I can draw shapes. If I need help, I will raise my paw. If my legs feel wiggly, I will do three slow breaths.”

“That is a smart strategy,” said Auntie Kim. “You made your own plan!”

Kaya sat with her crayons. She drew a big red circle, a blue triangle, and a green square. She whispered, “Circle, triangle, square,” and traced their sides. The crayons made a soft scritch-scratch sound.

After a while, Kaya felt a tiny wiggle. She put her paw on her chest. In goes the air. One. Out goes the air. Two. In, out. Three. The wiggle got smaller. Kaya smiled and drew a sunny yellow star.

The call ended. Auntie Kim turned her chair. “How did it go?”

Kaya showed her paper. “I used my plan. I read and drew. I took three breaths. I feel good.”

Auntie Kim gave a gentle hug. “You learned so much. You used education to grow your brain. You used self-advocacy to share your needs. You used strategy to make a plan. And you moved your body for exercise. That is a lot for one koala!”

Kaya’s tummy felt warm and proud. “Learning is like building,” she said. “Block by block.”

“Yes,” said Auntie Kim. “Every day, we learn a little and rest a little.”

Kaya looked at the word wall. Read. Plan. Break. Help. The words looked bright and brave.

“Can we add one more word?” Kaya asked.

“What word?” said Auntie Kim.

“Try,” said Kaya. She wrote it on a pink note and stuck it up high. “I will try.”

They both smiled. The sun shone on the notes. The room felt safe and kind. The click-clack was soft again, and the air smelled like tea and crayons.

Kaya whispered, “I can learn. I can ask. I can make a plan. I can move. I can try.”

And that day in the cozy home office, Kaya did all of that.

The End.
