# Buttons the Bear Cub and the Story Wrench

Buttons the bear cub padded into Grandpa’s garage workshop. The air smelled like pine wood and a little bit like oil. Sunlight shone in dusty stripes. Tools hung on the walls—hammers, brushes, and shiny wrenches like silver fish. The floor felt cool under Buttons’s fuzzy paws.

Grandpa Bear wiped his paws and smiled. “Good morning, Buttons. Want to help me fix the little wagon?”

Buttons nodded. “Yes! And… I want to tell a story while we work.”

Grandpa Bear’s eyes twinkled. “A story in the workshop? That sounds exciting.”

Buttons took a deep breath. He felt a fizzy bubble in his tummy. He loved stories, but sometimes the words got stuck. He picked up a tiny screw. It felt cold and smooth. “Once upon a time,” he began, “there was a… um… a brave… jam jar?”

Grandpa Bear chuckled softly. “A brave jam jar? I like it.”

Buttons frowned. “No, that’s not right. I wanted a knight. A knight bear. My words feel wiggly.” His ears drooped.

From the open window came a little chirp. “Cheer up!” said Cricket, Buttons’s small chirping friend. Cricket hopped onto a toolbox. “Can I listen? I love stories.”

Buttons shuffled his paws. “I want to tell it, but my mouth feels stuck. It makes me grumpy.” He squeezed the screw. It made a tiny squeak.

Grandpa Bear set down his wrench. “When I fix things, I go one step at a time,” he said. “Stories can be like that too. First, we choose a hero.”

Buttons puffed his chest. “The hero is a bear cub named… me! Buttons!”

“Great start,” chirped Cricket.

Grandpa Bear nodded. “Second, we choose a place.”

Buttons looked around at the workshop. He smelled sawdust and orange soap. “A garage workshop.” He smiled. “Just like this.”

“Third,” said Grandpa, “we choose a problem. What needs fixing?”

Buttons peeked at the wagon. One wheel wobbled. “The hero needs a special wrench to fix a wobbly wheel. But the wrench is hiding.”

Cricket leaned in. “Oooh!”

Buttons tried again. “Once upon a time, in a bright, busy garage workshop, there was a brave bear cub named Buttons. Buttons wanted to fix a wobbly wheel, but the special wrench was hiding.” He paused. The words felt softer now.

Grandpa Bear handed Buttons a small, smooth cloth. “When I feel stuck, I take a breath and wipe my paws. Want to try?”

Buttons nodded. He breathed in, smelled the fresh pine, and let the breath out slowly. He wiped his paws. His shoulders relaxed. “Okay,” he whispered to himself. “I can do this.”

Cricket said, “If you want help, you can ask. That’s good self-adv… self-advo—”

“Self-advocacy,” Grandpa Bear said kindly. “It means saying what you need.”

Buttons looked at Grandpa. “Grandpa, can you help me find the next part of my story?”

“I can listen,” Grandpa said. “Maybe ask a question. What did the hero try first?”

Buttons tapped a wooden box. Tap, tap. “Buttons looked under the paint rags.” He lifted a soft, smelly rag. “No wrench.”

Cricket chirped. “Then what?”

“Buttons listened.” Buttons tilted his head. He heard a tiny drip, drip, drip. “He followed the drip to the sink. He looked in the drawer by the sink.” Buttons pulled the drawer. Clatter! Spoons, bolts, and a blue ribbon jiggled. “Still no wrench.”

Buttons’s eyebrows squished together. “This is hard.” His tummy felt tight again.

Grandpa Bear’s voice was calm and warm. “Hard is okay. What helps when it’s hard?”

Buttons said softly, “I can take a break.” He set down the screw, shook his paws, and counted to three. “One… two… three.” He smiled at Cricket. “I can keep going.”

He climbed on a little stool and peered at the pegboard. Shiny shapes lined up like stars. “Buttons asked for help,” he said, telling the story and living it too. “He said, ‘Grandpa, can you point to the drawer for big wrenches?’”

Grandpa Bear pointed. “Top red drawer.”

Buttons pulled. The drawer slid open with a smooth whoosh. Inside lay a long, heavy wrench. Buttons’s paws curled around it. Cold, solid, just right. His eyes sparkled. “I found it!”

Cricket hopped with joy. “Hooray!”

Buttons finished his story in a proud voice. “Buttons the brave bear cub found the special wrench. He fixed the wobbly wheel with Grandpa. The wheel turned smooth and round. Whirr, whirr, whirr.”

Grandpa Bear tightened the bolt with Buttons. The wrench made a soft clink and a snug, happy sound. They gave the wagon a little push. It rolled straight. The air smelled like success—warm wood and sweet soap.

Grandpa Bear said, “How do you feel, storyteller?”

Buttons beamed. “I felt stuck, but I asked for help. I took breaths. I tried again. My story helped me fix the wagon!”

Cricket chirped, “Your story showed the steps. Hero, place, problem, try, help, and fix.”

Buttons nodded. “I can tell stories about anything, even hard feelings. Stories help me be brave and kind.”

Grandpa Bear gave Buttons a gentle hug. “And you can always speak up about what you need.”

Buttons looked at the wagon. “Let’s give rides and tell more stories!”

They rolled the wagon to the sunny door. As they took turns riding, Buttons told a new tale about a tiny screw who found its perfect home. Cricket chirped sound effects. Grandpa Bear laughed, deep and warm.

Buttons’s heart felt light. The words moved like a friendly river. He knew that stories, friends, and calm breaths could help on any day.

The End.
