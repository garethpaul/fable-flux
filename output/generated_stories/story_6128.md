# Pebble the Elephant Plans a Basement Parade

Pebble was a gentle elephant calf with soft gray ears and bright, curious eyes. One rainy morning, Pebble’s mama said, “We will play inside today.” Pebble’s feet went pat-pat down the stairs to the busy basement.

The basement was full of sounds and smells. The washing machine hummed, “Whirr, whirr.” The dryer puffed warm air that smelled like clean cotton. Boxes were stacked like tiny hills. There was a long table with crayons, paper, and glue. Pebble’s friends were there too—Tilly the tiny turtle, Buzz the fluffy bee puppet, and Nia the little neighbor with a kind smile.

“Hello!” Pebble trumpeted softly, careful not to be too loud. “Do you want to play with me?”

“Yes!” said Nia. “Let’s make a parade. We can plan it here.” She held up a shiny whistle. “We can go outside when the rain stops.”

“A parade!” Pebble’s tail swished with joy. “Let’s plan.” Pebble loved to plan. Planning made things feel safe and happy.

They spread a big paper on the table. Pebble drew a squiggly line. “This is our parade path,” he said. Tilly dipped a brush in blue paint. “I will make flags,” she whispered. Buzz the bee puppet wiggled on Nia’s hand. Nia buzzed, “Zzz! I will keep the beat.”

Pebble’s feet felt tingly with the hum of the machines and the fun of making. He loved the busy basement noises and the warm, soapy smell. He wanted to march right now.

“Boom-boom,” Pebble tapped a pot like a drum.

“Boom-boom,” Nia tapped gently.

Pebble lifted his trunk high. “I will be the leader!” he said, excited. His voice echoed a little in the basement. “Follow me! We will go fast and loud!”

Tilly tucked into her shell. Buzz stopped buzzing. Nia put a finger to her lips. “Pebble,” she said softly. “I like your idea. But we need to ask. We need to listen. We need to include everyone.”

Pebble blinked. “Ask?” He looked at his friends. “Oh.” He felt his ears get warm. He did not want to scare anyone. He wanted to be kind.

He took a slow breath. He listened to the “whirr” and the “whoosh” of the machines. He felt the cool floor under his feet. He tried again. “Friends,” he said gently, “would you like a loud parade or a quiet parade? Would you like to go fast or slow?”

Tilly peeked out. “Slow, please,” she whispered. “Loud makes me hide.”

Nia nodded. “Slow is good for Tilly. And Buzz likes a steady beat. We can all be in the parade.”

Pebble smiled. “We will go slow. I will step small. Like this.” He showed a tiny, careful step. Tap… tap… tap.

“Thank you,” Tilly said. She smiled and waved a blue flag.

They planned more. Pebble drew a stopping sign—a big red square. “This is where we rest,” he said. “And drink water.”

Nia clapped. “Good planning! That shows care.”

Pebble’s heart felt warm. He liked how it felt to ask and to listen. He liked that his friends felt safe.

The rain on the window went pitter-patter… then stopped. Sunlight slipped through the glass and made bright spots on the floor. “The rain is done!” Nia said. “We can go outside after we tidy.”

Pebble looked at the busy basement. There were crayons and paper and flags and tape. He wanted to run outside, but he remembered respect. “Let’s clean up together,” he said. “We can put the crayons back. We can fold the paper. We can be careful with the table.”

They sang while they cleaned. “Pick up, put away, thank you for our play,” Pebble sang in a soft trumpet voice. The basement hummed along.

When the floor was clear, they took their parade outside. The air smelled fresh, like wet grass and sunshine. Birds chirped, “Tweet! Tweet!” Pebble held the map. Nia held the whistle. Tilly carried the flags. Buzz buzzed a gentle beat.

“Are you ready?” Pebble asked. He remembered to ask. “Slow or fast?”

“Slow,” Tilly said.

“Steady,” Buzz buzzed.

“Slow and steady,” Nia agreed. “Let’s include everyone.”

Pebble took the lead with small, careful steps. Tap… tap… tap. He counted, “One… two… rest,” and they stopped at the red square sign he drew on the sidewalk with chalk. They sipped water. Pebble shaded Tilly with his big ear. “Are you okay?” he asked.

“I feel happy,” Tilly said. “Thank you for asking.”

They marched again, passing a puddle that sparkled like a mirror. Pebble wanted to stomp, but he looked at Tilly. “May I splash?” he asked.

Tilly smiled. “Yes, a tiny splash.”

Pebble made a tiny, gentle splash. “Plip!” Everyone giggled.

Neighbors waved. “What a kind parade!” said Mr. Lee. “Everyone fits!”

Pebble’s heart puffed with soft pride. He saw how planning and asking made room for his friends. He saw how respect made the day shine.

Back in the basement, they put the flags in a box and the whistle on a hook. The machines were quiet now. Pebble’s feet felt calm on the cool floor.

Nia said, “What did we learn?”

Pebble lifted his trunk. “Respect means asking, listening, and caring,” he said. “Respect means making space for everyone. When we plan with respect, we all have fun.”

Tilly nodded. “And we can go outside when we are ready and safe.”

Buzz buzzed, “Zzz-yes!”

Pebble smiled a big, gentle smile. “Let’s have more kind parades,” he said. “Slow, steady, and together.”

The End.
