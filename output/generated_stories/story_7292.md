# Leo Solves the Laundry Puzzle

One sunny morning, a brave little lion cub named Leo padded into the laundry room. His paws were soft and quiet on the cool floor. He sniffed the air. It smelled warm and soapy, like clean clouds. The washing machine hummed, Rrrr-rrr-rrr. The dryer tumbled with a gentle thump-thump.

Leo’s favorite red blanket was missing. “Hmm,” Leo said, tipping his head. “Where are you, blanket?” His whiskers twitched with worry. He loved that blanket for cozy naps.

He took a deep breath. “I can be brave. I can think and solve this.” His tail made a small swish.

First, he looked around with big, bright eyes. He used his senses like a detective. He listened. He looked. He sniffed. He even touched.

He spotted three baskets on the floor. One basket had white socks. One had blue shirts. One was empty. “Clues!” said Leo.

He talked to himself, step by step. “Step one: Look for red things,” he said. He remembered that good problem solvers take small steps. He padded to the shelf and pointed his paw to the bottles. “Red soap? No. Red towel? Maybe!”

He pulled the red towel down. It felt fluffy, but it wasn’t his blanket. “Not this,” Leo said. He put it back carefully. “I will try another idea.”

He heard the dryer go thump-thump again. “Maybe my blanket is inside.” He stretched up on his back paws, muscles working, to peek at the round window. “I see stripes,” he said. “But are they blanket stripes?”

He bent his knees and practiced a strong jump. “Jump, land. Jump, land.” He did it three times to get ready. On the fourth jump, he grabbed the handle. Click! The door opened when the dryer stopped. Warm air whooshed out. “Mmm, like sunshine cookies,” Leo giggled.

Inside, he found socks—plop, plop—one, two, three. He set them in the sock basket. He found a blue shirt—swish!—and put it in the shirt basket. He moved with care and practice. “Sort by kind,” he said. “Whites here. Blues here. Reds?” He checked again. No red blanket.

Leo sat on the floor and put a paw on his chin. “Let me think. When did I last have my blanket?” He closed his eyes to remember. “I had it at nap time. I was chilly. I put it around my shoulders. Then I came to help with laundry.”

His ears perked up. “Maybe I dropped it on the way!” He trotted to the door and looked at the floor. No blanket. He looked under a chair. He felt dust tickle his nose. “Ah-choo!” No blanket.

He stood tall and talked out loud. “New idea. Big machines first, small spaces next.” He checked the washing machine. Swish, swish. It was full of bubbly water. He pressed his ear to the glass. “Hello?” he whispered. No red.

He looked behind the tall hamper. “Heavy,” he grunted, pushing with both paws. He used his legs and counted, “One, two, three… push!” The hamper slid a little. Behind it, he saw a corner of something red.

Leo’s eyes sparkled. “A clue!” He kept pushing, slow and safe. He practiced, “Strong legs. Straight back. Small steps.” He moved, moved, moved. The hamper scooted just enough. There, crumpled and dusty, was his red blanket!

“I found you!” he cheered, wrapping his paws around it. The blanket felt cool and a little dirty. “You need a wash,” Leo said. “I can do that. I will think, move, and practice.”

He read the little tag out loud, sounding the words. “Warm wash. Gentle spin.” He was proud of himself for checking. He set the washer to warm. He put in a small scoop of soap. “Not too much,” he said. He placed the blanket in. Plop! He shut the door and pressed start. Beep!

While the washer swished, Leo practiced sorting more clothes. He made a game. “Find all the pairs!” he sang, tapping each sock. He crawled, reached, stretched, and bent, moving his body in lots of ways. “This is good practice,” he smiled. “When I move my body, my brain thinks better, too.”

Soon, the washer beeped again. Leo opened the door and hugged the wet blanket. “Drippy!” he laughed. He shook it gently, then put it in the dryer. He checked the heat. “Low,” he decided. “Gentle is best.” He pressed start. Whirr.

He danced a little while he waited, paws padding, tail swishing. “Think, move, practice,” he chanted softly. “Step by step, I can do it.”

When the dryer finished, Leo pulled out a warm, soft, clean red blanket. “Hello, cozy friend,” he purred. He folded it the best he could—fold, smooth, fold—then cuddled it close.

Leo sat with a happy sigh. “I used my eyes, my ears, my nose, and my paws,” he said. “I made a plan. I tried one idea, then another. I moved my body safely. I kept practicing. And I solved the problem!”

He carried the blanket to his favorite spot and curled up. “Being brave means thinking carefully,” he whispered. “When I have a problem, I can stop, make a plan, move my body, and try again.”

The red blanket smelled like fresh air. The laundry room hummed softly. Leo closed his eyes with a smile, cozy and proud.

The End.
