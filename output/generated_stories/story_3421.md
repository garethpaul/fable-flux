# Tilly Turtle Finds Her Shine

Inside a cozy garden shed, Tilly the shy turtle peeked out from her shell. The air smelled like fresh soil and sweet tomato vines. Sunlight made little dust sparkles dance. “Today I will peek a little longer,” Tilly whispered. Her voice was soft, like a leaf whispering.

In the shed, there were friendly tools and tiny animal neighbors. A broom leaned by the door. A watering can hummed a drippy song. A little mouse named Pip nibbled a crumb. “Good morning, Tilly!” squeaked Pip. “Do you want to play a sorting game?”

Tilly tucked her head in, then peeked out again. “Maybe,” she said. “What is the game?”

Pip pointed to a basket of colorful buttons, a pile of seed packets, and a jar of shiny screws. “We take turns. We sort by color, by shape, and by size. It’s fun!”

Tilly felt her heart go thump-thump inside her shell. She was shy when games were new. She worried, What if I get it wrong? What if I am too slow? She took a deep breath. She smelled mint leaves from a nearby pot. “I can try,” she said.

The broom wiggled and said in a raspy voice, “Sweep-sweep! I can draw a circle to help.” It swooshed a tidy circle in the dust.

“Thank you,” Tilly said. She felt warm gratitude. It felt like sunshine on her shell.

They began. Pip said, “My turn first!” He rolled a red button into the circle. “Your turn!”

Tilly gently nudged a blue button with her nose. Clink! It landed next to the red. “Blue,” she whispered. She smiled just a little. They took turns, one by one. The watering can dripped, plip, plop, plip, a gentle beat. The shed felt friendly and safe.

Then Pip held up a green triangle button. “Color or shape?” he asked.

Tilly blinked. “I don’t know,” she said.

“That’s okay,” Pip squeaked. “We can be open-minded. We can try both ways.”

Tilly thought. She looked at the circle, at the buttons, at the seed packets with pictures of peas and flowers. “Let’s sort by color first,” she said. “Then by shape.”

Pip nodded. “Good idea! Your turn to choose.”

Tilly’s heart felt bigger. Choosing felt brave. She said, “Thank you for waiting your turn.”

They kept sorting. Pip took a turn. Tilly took a turn. When they finished, the circle glowed with neat piles. Red, blue, green, round, square, triangle. The screws chimed, ting-ting, inside their jar, like tiny bells.

Just then, the door creaked open. A soft breeze brought the smell of grass and warm wood. Mrs. Finch, a kind bird with bright eyes, hopped in. She wore a tiny apron. “Hello, little helpers,” she chirped. “I need one brave friend to carry a note to the sunflowers. It’s a thank-you note for growing so tall.”

Pip bounced. “I can go!” Then he looked at Tilly and stopped. “We are taking turns,” he said. “Tilly, would you like this turn?”

Tilly’s tummy fluttered. The note was folded like a small square cloud. She had never carried a note outside the shed alone. She looked at the open door. She saw sunshine and heard bees humming, zzz-zzz. She felt shy and small.

Mrs. Finch tilted her head. “You can do it slowly,” she said. “Slow is fine.”

Tilly touched the note with her nose. The paper was smooth and warm from Mrs. Finch’s wing. She took a deep breath. She remembered sorting. She remembered choosing. She remembered being thanked. She whispered, “I can try.”

Pip smiled. “I believe in you.”

Tilly tucked the note under her chin. Step, step, step. Her little feet made soft taps on the wood floor. Tap, tap. She reached the door. The world was bright. The dirt felt cool. The sun felt soft and golden. She smelled sweet petals and peppery leaves. Bees hummed hello.

Tilly walked to the sunflowers. They were tall and friendly, with big brown faces and yellow petals like warm rays. “Hello,” Tilly said. “Thank you for the shade.” She held up the note.

A breeze lifted the note and rustled the petals. The sunflowers seemed to nod. Tilly stood very still. The note slid gently into a leaf’s curl. Safe.

“I did it,” Tilly whispered. Her shy heart felt proud now. It felt like a little drum playing a happy song: thump, thump, hooray!

She turned and walked back. Step, step, step. The shed welcomed her with cool shade and the smell of tomato vines again.

Pip clapped. The screws chimed. The broom swooshed a happy swirl. Mrs. Finch chirped, “Well done, Tilly! Thank you for helping. You were brave and careful.”

Tilly’s cheeks warmed. “Thank you for believing in me,” she said. “I am shy, but I can try. When I try, I learn I can do new things.”

Pip nodded. “And we can take turns, and be open to new ideas.”

Mrs. Finch added, “And we say thank you, because we are grateful.”

Tilly lifted her chin. “Today I found my shine,” she said softly. “Tomorrow I will try again.”

They all smiled. The shed felt bright, even in the shade.

The End.
