# Cub Benny Helps in the Workshop

Cub Benny was a friendly bear cub with soft brown fur and bright, curious eyes. He lived near a small garage workshop. The workshop smelled like fresh wood, warm oil, and a little bit of soap. Hammers tapped. A broom swished. The sun made a golden square on the floor.

One morning, Benny peeked inside. “Hello?” he called. His voice echoed. “I want to help!”

Mr. Pines, the kind fox who owned the workshop, smiled. He wore blue overalls and a red scarf. “Good morning, Benny. We can always use helpers. Today we fix bikes for neighbors. That is called volunteering. We help because it is kind.”

Benny’s ears perked up. “Vol-un-teer-ing,” he repeated slowly. “I can do that!”

Mr. Pines showed Benny a small red toolbox. “This box is for our visitors. It has tiny wrenches and shiny screws,” he said. The tools glittered in the light. “We share these with care.”

Benny nodded. “I will be careful.” His paws tingled with excitement. He loved shiny things.

Soon, neighbors came by with squeaky bikes and wobbly wheels. “Let’s help!” Benny said. He held a pump. He squeezed the handle. Pfft, pfft! Air whooshed into a tire. “Listen!” he giggled. “It sounds like a funny snake.”

A little turtle named Tilly rolled in a small blue bike. “My bell is quiet,” she said. “It does not ring.”

Benny crouched to look. “I can try!” He opened the red toolbox. A tiny golden screw winked up at him. It was the perfect size. He picked it up. It felt smooth and warm in his paw.

Mr. Pines looked busy helping a raccoon with a chain. The workshop hummed with friendly sounds—clink, clank, whirr. Benny felt proud. “I am a good helper,” he whispered.

He peeked at the golden screw again. It sparkled so nicely. “I could keep it,” he thought for a second. “No one will see.” His paw closed around it. His heart thumped fast.

Then Benny’s tummy felt a twist. He remembered Mr. Pines’s words: “We share these with care.” He looked at Tilly’s quiet bell. He looked at his closed paw. Benny took a deep breath. The air smelled like pine and soap. He opened his paw and set the screw back in the box.

“I will do what is right,” he said out loud. “That is called integrity. It means telling the truth and doing the right thing, even when no one is looking.”

Tilly blinked. “In-teg-ri-ty,” she said slowly. “I like that.”

Benny smiled. “Me too.” He chose a small silver screw from the shared box. “Let’s fix your bell.” He turned the screw gently. Twist, twist. He tested the bell. Ding-ding! The sound was bright and happy.

Tilly beamed. “Thank you, Benny!”

Benny’s chest felt warm. He liked this feeling better than hiding a shiny thing.

After lunch, a big rabbit named Ruby came with a red wagon. The wheel wobbled. Ruby looked worried. “Can you help?”

Benny nodded. “We can try.” He looked in the toolbox. The golden screw was gone!

“Oh no,” he whispered. “Did someone take it?”

Mr. Pines walked over, wiping his paws. “What is wrong, Benny?”

Benny held up the empty corner of the toolbox. His voice was small. “The golden screw is missing. I almost took it before. I wanted to. But I didn’t. I put it back. I’m telling the truth.”

Mr. Pines studied Benny’s face. His eyes were soft. “Thank you for telling me. That is integrity.” He looked under a rag and laughed gently. “Here it is! I moved it to clean the box.”

Benny’s ears twitched in relief. “Oh!”

Mr. Pines patted Benny’s shoulder. “I am proud of you for speaking up and for making a good choice.”

Ruby’s nose wiggled. “I thought only big foxes and rabbits could fix things,” she said. “But a bear cub can help too! I will be open-minded. I will try new ideas.”

Benny grinned. “Let’s work together.” He passed Ruby a small wrench. “You can hold this.”

Together they tightened the wagon wheel. Click, click. Benny pumped a little oil. Drip, drip. Ruby pushed the wagon. It rolled smooth and quiet. “It works!” she cheered.

Later, the sun dipped lower, and the workshop glowed. Everyone clapped for helpers. Mr. Pines rang a bell. Ding-ding! “Thank you for volunteering,” he said. “Thank you for open minds and honest hearts.”

Benny stood tall. “Today I learned three big things,” he said. He held up one fuzzy finger. “One: Integrity means doing the right thing, even when no one sees.” He held up a second finger. “Two: Volunteering is helping for kindness, not for prizes.” He held up a third. “Three: Being open-minded means trying new ideas and new friends.”

Tilly rang her bell. Ding-ding! Ruby tugged her wagon in a circle. The workshop smelled like warm wood and happy work.

Benny waved goodbye. “I will come back tomorrow,” he said. “I will help again.”

As he walked home, his paws felt light, like little drums on the path. He did not take the shiny screw. He did something better. He kept his promise to be honest.

The End.
