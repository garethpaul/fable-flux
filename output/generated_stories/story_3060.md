# Benny Beaver Says Sorry

Benny the busy beaver loved to fix things. Tap, tap, tap went his little wooden hammer. Scritch, scritch, scritch went his smooth beaver tail as he swept. Today, Benny sat on Grandma Maple’s peaceful front porch. The porch smelled like fresh pine and warm tea. The railing felt smooth under Benny’s paws. Birds sang softly in the maple tree, “Chee-chee-chee.”

“I will make the porch perfect!” Benny said. His eyes were bright. His paws were ready.

Grandma Maple rocked in her creaky chair and smiled. “Slow and gentle, Benny,” she said. “Listen and look first.”

“I’m busy! I can help!” Benny chirped. He hopped to the screen door. It squeaked, eeeeee.

“I will fix that squeak,” Benny said. He dabbed oil. Drip, drop. The hinges glistened.

Next, Benny saw a loose porch mat. He tugged it. “I will shake out the dust!” he said.

“Careful,” Grandma Maple called. But Benny was already shaking. Flap, flap, flap went the mat. Dust puffed into the air. Achoo! Benny sneezed. The dust floated and landed on Grandma’s tea.

“Oh dear,” Grandma said. “My tea got dusty.”

Benny blinked. “Oops.” His busy tail swished. He put the mat back fast, but now it was crooked. The corner stuck up like a little mountain.

At that moment, Pip the squirrel scampered in with a small basket. “I brought berries!” Pip squeaked. The berries smelled sweet and juicy. Pip’s tiny feet pattered on the boards.

Pip stepped on the crooked mat. Flip! Pip slipped and the basket flew. Plop, plop, plop! Red berries rolled everywhere. A berry bumped Benny’s paw. Splutch! Berry juice made a pink spot on the porch. Pip’s eyes filled with tears. “My berries…”

Benny’s tummy felt wiggly. He looked at Pip’s sad face. He looked at Grandma’s dusty tea. The birds went quiet. Even the wind seemed to hold its breath.

Benny took a slow breath too. He remembered Grandma’s words: “Listen and look first.”

He knelt beside Pip. “Are you okay?” Benny asked softly.

Pip sniffled. “My knee feels ouchy, and my berries are gone.”

Benny leaned closer. He saw a tiny scrape. “I can get a bandage,” he said. He saw Grandma’s tea cup. He smelled the dust. He saw the crooked mat mountain. He felt his cheeks grow warm.

“I am sorry,” Benny said. “I was too busy to be careful. I did not listen. I made the mat messy. I made your tea dusty. I made you slip.”

Grandma’s eyes were kind. Pip looked at Benny. “You said sorry,” Pip whispered. “That helps my heart feel better.”

Benny nodded. “I want to help your knee too.” He padded inside. The screen door went eeeeee, but softly now. He found a small bandage with a smiling acorn on it. He washed his paws with cool water. Swish, swish.

Back on the porch, Benny washed Pip’s scrape with gentle drops. Drip, dab. “Is the water too cold?” he asked.

“It’s okay,” Pip said. “It tickles a little.”

Benny put on the acorn bandage. “There,” he said. “Acorns are brave.”

Grandma Maple brought a fresh cup of tea. The steam curled like tiny clouds. She set two cups down. “One for me. One for you, Benny,” she said. “Pip, would you like a sip of berry tea?” She added a small splash of berry jam. The tea turned pink and smelled sweet.

Pip smiled. “Yes, please.”

Benny looked at the berry mess. He felt strong and calm now. “I will clean the porch. Slowly and gently,” he said. He picked up each berry. Plop. Plop. He wiped the juice with warm, soapy water. Swish, circle, swish. He straightened the mat flat. Pat, pat. He tested it with his paw. No more mountain.

Grandma Maple rocked and hummed. “What did you learn, Benny?” she asked.

Benny sat beside her. He listened to the birds sing again, “Chee-chee-chee.” He felt the smooth rail under his paw. He smelled warm tea and berries.

“I learned to look with my eyes,” he said. “I learned to listen with my ears. I learned to feel with my heart.” He touched his chest. “When I hurry, I can forget people. When I care, I move slow. I ask, ‘Are you okay?’ I say, ‘I am sorry.’ I help.”

Pip nodded. “That made me feel safe.”

Benny took a careful sip of tea. “Mmm,” he said. “Sweet and warm.”

Grandma Maple smiled. “Empathy,” she said. “That is feeling what someone else feels. You felt Pip’s ouch. You helped in kind ways.”

Benny’s tail thumped softly on the porch. Thump… pause… thump. “I want to practice empathy every day,” he said. “I can ask, ‘How do you feel?’ I can wait. I can help.”

Pip giggled. “You can practice with me!”

Benny grinned. “I will practice now.” He turned to Pip. “How do you feel?”

Pip held his knee and thought. “I feel better. I feel happy. I feel seen.”

Benny’s heart felt warm like the sun. The porch felt peaceful again. The mat was flat. The tea was fresh. The berries were washed and shared in a little bowl.

They sat together on the quiet porch—sip, munch, hum. Benny wasn’t just a busy beaver now. He was a caring beaver too.

The End.
