# Miko the Mouse Saves the Day

In a quiet house, under creaky stairs, there was a basement lab. The air felt cool. Lights hummed softly. Bottles clinked. Machines blinked with tiny green dots. On the floor, tiny paws pat-patted. It was Miko, a kind mouse with bright eyes and soft whiskers.

Miko loved the lab. He liked the smell of warm toast from the toaster-robot. He liked the gentle drip-drip from the water tubes. He liked the soft buzz of the air filter, like a sleepy bee.

One morning, Miko found a mess. Paper bits were everywhere. Plastic caps rolled and bonked into the wall. A fan blew dust into swirls. The floor felt crunchy under his feet.

“Oh no,” said Miko. “This is not kind to our home.”

Dr. Bea, the scientist, walked down the stairs. Her shoes squeaked. She wore blue gloves and a big smile. “Good morning, lab!” she sang. Drip-drip. Hum-hum. Bonk!

A rolling plastic cap bumped Miko’s tail. “Eep!” said Miko. He took a breath. He stood tall, even though he was small.

“Dr. Bea?” Miko called in his tiny voice. “Can I talk?”

Dr. Bea bent down. “Hello, Miko. Yes. I am listening.”

Miko held a little paper piece in his paw. “This mess makes the lab sad,” he said. “The paper and plastic can hurt our home. I want to keep the lab clean and safe. Can we fix it?”

Dr. Bea blinked. “You are brave to say that,” she said kindly. “Thank you for telling me. Let’s look together.”

They walked slowly. They listened. Crunch. Whirr. Drip. They looked into a big bin. It was full of mixed trash. Paper sat with banana peels. Plastic caps hid under sticky goo.

Miko wrinkled his nose. “Phew,” he said.

Dr. Bea nodded. “We did not sort our waste,” she said. “We can do better. We can reuse. We can recycle. We can reduce.”

Miko’s ears perked up. “Three R’s!” he cheered.

Just then, a little robot zoomed by. “Beep-beep! I like speed!” it chirped. It bumped the bin. Clatter! More mess spilled out.

“Wait, wait,” Miko said gently. “Robot, the mess makes trouble.”

“I did not know,” said the robot softly. Its lights dimmed. “I wanted to help fast.”

“It is okay,” said Miko. He put his paw on the robot’s metal side. It felt cool and smooth. “We can help each other. We can go slow and sort.”

Dr. Bea clapped her hands. “Team time!” she said. She got three clean bins. She put big pictures on them: a leaf for food scraps, a bottle for recycling, and a tiny trash can for what we cannot reuse yet.

Miko felt proud. “I can be the sorter,” he said. He was using his voice. That is self-advocacy.

“Beep-beep!” said the robot. “I can carry.”

Dr. Bea smiled. “I will wash and rinse.”

They made a plan. They sang a song:
“Leaf bin for peels,
Bottle bin for caps,
Tiny bin for little bits,
We can do it, claps!”

Clap-clap! Miko picked up a paper bit. It felt dry and light. “Recycle,” he said, plop. The robot rolled in a careful circle. “I move the bin,” it said. Dr. Bea rinsed a sticky cup. Splash-splash.

Soon, a spider crawled down on a silk thread. “Hello,” said the spider in a small voice. “I live here too. The dust makes me sneeze.”

Miko looked up with kind eyes. “We will make it better for you,” he said.

A ladybug flew in. Her tiny wings buzzed like a secret. “May I help?” she asked.

“Yes,” said Miko. “Everyone can help. We are all part of the lab family.” That is inclusion.

They worked together. They used soft brushes to sweep dust. They turned off lights they did not need. Click. The room felt calm and bright, not too bright. They fixed a drip with a twist. Twist-twist. The drip stopped. “We saved water,” said Miko. “That is good for our planet.”

But then, they found a box of old batteries. The box was torn. A little sparkle popped. “Tsss.” Miko jumped back.

“I feel scared,” he said. “We need a safe plan.”

Dr. Bea nodded seriously. “You are right to say how you feel,” she said. “Old batteries need special care.”

They put on tiny safety signs. The robot beeped for help. “Beep-beep, careful here!” The ladybug brought a little note that said, “Danger.” The spider made a tiny flag.

Dr. Bea put the batteries in a safe tub with a lid. “We will take these to a battery drop-off,” she said. “That is recycling too.”

At last, the lab felt clean. The air smelled fresh, like rain. The floor was smooth and warm. The machines hummed happy songs. Miko’s whiskers twitched. He felt cozy inside.

Dr. Bea knelt and looked at Miko. “Thank you for speaking up,” she said.

“Thank you for listening,” Miko said.

The robot blinked bright lights. “Thank you for teaching me,” it said.

The spider and the ladybug waved. “Thank you for making space for us,” they said.

They all smiled. Miko stretched his tiny arms. “We did it together,” he said. “We cared for our home.”

Dr. Bea wrote a new sign: “Reduce. Reuse. Recycle. Ask for help. Be kind.” She taped it near the stairs where everyone could see.

Miko read the words slowly. “We can do these every day,” he said. “We can make less waste. We can sort our things. We can fix drips. We can turn off lights. We can speak up when we see a problem. We can listen to friends.”

The lab glowed warm and gentle. The team shared a small snack: crunchy crackers for Miko, a drop of sugar water for the ladybug, a quiet corner for the spider, and a fresh charge for the robot. Crunch. Sip. Sigh. Beep.

Miko curled his tail into a neat loop. “Our planet is our home,” he whispered. “When we care for it, it cares for us.”

Everyone nodded.

The End.
