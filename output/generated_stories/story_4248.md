# Ruby Rabbit Shares the Guest Room

Ruby Rabbit was small, soft, and very determined. She had quick paws and a bright pink nose. Today, she had a big plan. Her Nana was coming to visit, and Ruby wanted the guest room to be perfect.

The guest room smelled like clean sheets and lemon soap. The window let in warm sunshine that made the curtains glow. A fuzzy blue rug felt cozy under Ruby’s paws. She wiggled her whiskers. “I can do this,” she said to herself. “I will make this room kind and comfy.”

Ruby hopped to the bed. “Pillow puff!” she said, patting the pillow. Poof! It rose up fluffy and high. She pulled the blanket tight. “Smooth blanket!” She ran her paws across the blanket. It felt soft, like a cloud.

Ruby looked at a basket full of toys—blocks, cars, and a tiny drum. “Hmm. Nana needs space for her bag,” Ruby said. “But I love my toys.” She felt a little tug in her tummy. She wanted to keep everything right where it was. She also wanted Nana to feel welcome.

Ruby took a deep breath. “I can share. Sharing is generous.” She moved the basket under the table and made a clear spot by the dresser. “There,” she said. “A special place for Nana.”

Her brother, Reed Rabbit, hopped in. “Ruby, can I help?”

Ruby’s ears flicked. “I want to do it all myself,” she said. She was determined to be the helper today.

Reed pointed to a heavy water pitcher. “That looks heavy. We can lift it together.”

Ruby looked at the pitcher. Her paws felt a little tired. She remembered Mom’s words: “Helping together keeps bodies safe.” Ruby nodded. “Okay. Let’s lift together. One, two, three!” They lifted slowly. “Good teamwork,” Ruby said. It felt nice to work side by side. That was coordination—moving together carefully.

They set the pitcher on the bedside table. It made a gentle clink. Ruby smiled. “Nana likes warm tea.”

Reed picked up the tiny drum. “Can we put the drum away? It’s loud.”

Ruby frowned a little. “But I love the drum.”

Reed said, “Nana might want quiet.”

Ruby’s tummy tugged again. She felt a bit grumpy. She wanted her drum out. She also wanted Nana to rest.

“I feel upset,” Ruby said softly. “I want my drum.” She took a slow, deep breath. In… and out. This helped her body feel calm. Then she tried a kind plan. “How about this? I will put the drum in the closet now. After Nana naps, we can play it outside.”

Reed grinned. “Good plan!”

They both put the drum away. The closet clicked shut with a gentle sound.

Ruby saw a small basket of Nana’s favorite tea bags on the shelf. She tiptoed to bring it down. The basket wobbled. “Oh!” she squeaked, and stepped back.

Reed said, “Maybe Mom can help with high things.”

Ruby nodded. “Asking for help is taking care of me.” She called, “Mom?”

Mom Rabbit came in, her paws warm and gentle. The room smelled sweet, like honey tea. “This looks lovely,” Mom said. “What do you need?”

“Can you get the tea basket, please?” Ruby asked.

“Of course,” Mom said, and placed the tea by the pitcher. “You are being very generous. You’re making space and thinking about Nana’s needs.”

Ruby felt proud. Her chest felt warm, like sunlight. “I want Nana to feel loved.”

Just then, their cousin Dot Rabbit bounced into the guest room. Dot loved to build. “Blocks, blocks!” Dot said, spotting the toy basket under the table. She pulled it out. Clatter, clatter!

Ruby’s ears drooped. “Oh no. I made that space for Nana.”

Dot frowned. “But I want to play now.”

Ruby took another slow breath. She remembered to use kind words. “Dot, I worked hard to make this room for Nana. I feel worried when the space gets messy. Can we play in the playroom instead?”

Dot hugged a block. “I don’t want to stop.”

Ruby thought. She wanted to be fair and also kind to Nana. “How about this? We can carry the blocks together to the playroom. I will help you. Then we can build a tall tower.”

Dot’s eyes lit up. “A tower?”

“A super tall one,” Ruby said. “I will even share my shiny red block.”

Dot nodded. “Okay!” Together, they scooped blocks, plop plop into the basket. Reed held the basket steady. Mom opened the door. Everyone moved like a team. Their paws made soft pat-pat sounds on the rug. This was good coordination—working together in a careful way.

Soon the guest room was tidy again. The bed was smooth. The pillow was puffy. The tea was ready. A tiny note on the table said, “Welcome, Nana! Love, Ruby.” She had drawn a small heart that smelled faintly of the cherry sticker she had placed on it.

The doorbell rang. Ding-dong! Ruby’s nose twitched with joy. Nana stepped in, her scarf smelling like lavender. “Oh my,” Nana said. “This room feels so cozy!”

Ruby’s heart fluttered. “I made space for your bag,” she said. “And we put noisy toys away so you can rest. We worked together.”

Nana hugged Ruby. “You were generous and careful. You took care of me and yourself.”

Ruby smiled wide. “Sharing can be hard, but it makes hearts happy.”

Nana nodded. “When we are generous, we show love. When we ask for help and use calm words, we solve problems.”

Ruby took Nana’s hand. “Tea time?”

“Tea time,” Nana said.

They poured warm tea, sip, sip. It tasted sweet and safe. Ruby felt calm. She had been determined, generous, and gentle. The guest room glowed in the soft light, and everyone felt welcome.

The lesson was clear: Be generous, take care of yourself, use kind words, and work together. That is how small rabbits solve big problems.

The End.
