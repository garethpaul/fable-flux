# Bramble Bear’s Colorful Feelings

Bramble was a friendly bear cub with soft, brown fur and bright, curious eyes. Today was Art Day in the creative art room. The room smelled like fresh paper and orange peel cleaner. Sunlight made little squares on the floor. Paint pots sat in a rainbow line. Brushes stood up like tiny trees. Sponges looked like fluffy clouds.

“Good morning, Bramble,” said Ms. Maple, the kind teacher with a paint-splattered apron. “We will make Feelings Posters today.”

Bramble’s paws clapped. “Yay! I like colors,” he said. His voice bounced like a happy drum.

Ms. Maple showed a big chart. “Red can mean excited or angry. Blue can mean calm or sad. Yellow can mean happy or brave. Green can mean peaceful or helpful. You can choose the color that matches your feelings.”

Bramble looked at his paper. He felt a little flutter in his chest, like a moth. He wanted to make the best poster. He wanted to help his friends too.

His best friend, Squeak the mouse, tugged on his sleeve. “I can’t open the glue,” Squeak squeaked.

“I can help!” said Bramble. He twisted the glue cap gently. Pop! “Here you go.”

“Thank you,” said Squeak with a grin.

Bramble dipped a brush into sunny yellow. He painted a big circle. “This is my happy,” he said.

At the next table, a fox kit named Fern spilled red paint. It splashed like tiny fireworks. “Oh no!” Fern cried. Her ears drooped.

Bramble felt a bump in his tummy. His paws wanted to keep painting. But he looked at Fern’s face. Her eyes were wet. Bramble took a deep breath, in and out, like Ms. Maple had taught. “I feel a little torn,” he said softly to himself. “I want to paint. I also want to help.”

He raised his paw. “Ms. Maple! May I get the cloth?”

“Yes, thank you for being responsible,” Ms. Maple said.

Bramble padded to the sink. He grabbed a soft cloth and a little bucket of water. The water sounded like tiny rain. Together, Fern and Bramble wiped the red splashes. Swish, swish. “It’s okay,” Bramble said kindly. “Spills happen.”

Fern smiled. “Thank you. I feel better now.”

Bramble felt a warm green feeling grow in his chest. “That’s my helpful green,” he whispered, and he painted a small green leaf on his poster.

Later, Squeak used the glitter. It sprinkled everywhere like shiny snow. Some landed on the floor. Bramble blinked. The glitter made the room sparkle, but it also made a mess.

Ms. Maple held up a little sign. “Clean as we go,” she said. “We care for our room. We care for our Earth.”

Bramble nodded. “We can be gentle with our supplies,” he said. He handed Squeak a tiny brush. “Let’s sweep the glitter into the bin. We can reuse the extra.” They giggled as the glitter made soft tink-tink sounds in the jar.

Bramble went back to his poster. He painted a blue swish. “This is my calm,” he said. He listened to the soft scratch of the brush. He smelled the sweet, safe paint. His shoulders relaxed.

Just then, the scissors table was full. A shy bunny named Dot hugged her ears. “I don’t have scissors,” Dot whispered.

Bramble looked at his scissors. He wanted to cut star shapes right now. He felt a little squeeze in his chest. He put a paw over his heart. “Hello, feeling,” he said quietly. “Are you worried?” He took a slow breath. “I can wait.”

“You can use mine first, Dot,” Bramble said. “I will draw my stars while I wait.”

Dot’s eyes lit up. “Thank you,” she said, and her voice sounded like a little bell.

Bramble drew stars with a crayon. It felt good to share. His squeeze feeling melted like ice in the sun. He painted a soft purple heart. “Purple is my generous feeling,” he said.

When it was time to show posters, Bramble’s paws tingled. He held his paper high. It had a yellow sun, a green leaf, a blue swish, and a purple heart. A tiny red dot sat in the corner.

“What is the red dot?” asked Ms. Maple.

Bramble smiled. “Sometimes I feel frustrated,” he said. “Like when I wanted to cut stars. I put my paw on my heart. I took a breath. I shared my scissors. The red dot got smaller when I helped.”

Ms. Maple nodded. “That is emotional awareness,” she said. “You noticed your feeling. You named it. You chose a kind action.”

The class clapped. The room sounded like soft rain and happy paws.

Bramble looked around. Fern’s poster had brave yellow. Squeak’s had peaceful green. Dot’s had a blue moon. Everyone had different colors. Everyone had different feelings. And that was okay.

“Let’s clean together,” said Ms. Maple. “We care for our room and our planet.” The children washed brushes until the water ran clear. They closed glue caps tight. They put paper scraps into the recycle bin. The room smelled fresh again.

Bramble felt tall inside. He had helped. He had shared. He had cared for the Earth. He had listened to his feelings.

On the walk out, Squeak squeezed his paw. “You are a good friend, Bramble.”

Bramble’s heart felt like a warm, glowing lamp. “I learned that feelings are like colors,” he said. “When I notice them and breathe, I can choose what to do. Helping and sharing make my colors bright.”

The End.
