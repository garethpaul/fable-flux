# Ollie Otter and the Pantry Plan

Ollie was a playful otter with bright eyes and soft, whiskery cheeks. One sunny morning, he slipped into the pantry with a happy wiggle. The pantry smelled like apples, warm oats, and a little bit of cinnamon. Shelves stood tall like friendly trees, stacked with jars, boxes, and baskets.

“Hello, pantry!” Ollie chirped. “I’m here to make my own lunch.”

His tummy gave a tiny rumble. “Glub-glub,” it sang.

Ollie looked around. On a low shelf sat a basket of brown and green apples. Next to it were oats in a big tin. A little jar held peanut butter. There were napkins, a small spoon, and a reusable plate made of bamboo.

Ollie took a deep breath. “I can do this myself,” he said. “I will be careful and kind to the Earth.”

Just then, his friends peeked around the doorway. Bibi the bear cub, Muna the mouse, and Jaz the blue jay watched with wide eyes.

“Do you need help?” asked Bibi in a gentle growl.

“I can try on my own first,” Ollie said with a brave smile. “You can cheer for me!”

“Cheep-cheep! Cheering!” sang Jaz. Muna squeaked, “We believe in you!”

Ollie made a plan. “First, wash hands. Then, choose food. Then, clean up.” He wiggled his whiskers. “That is my strategy.”

He turned the tiny faucet by the pantry sink. The water felt cool like morning dew. “Swish, swish,” went the water as he scrubbed with a small soap bar. His paws smelled like lemons. He dried them on a cloth towel. “No paper waste,” he said proudly.

Ollie picked up one green apple. It was firm and shiny. “Crunchy!” he giggled. He set it on the bamboo plate. He opened the oats tin with a soft “pop” and scooped some into a small bowl. “One scoop is enough,” he said. “We don’t waste food.”

He looked up at a jar of cinnamon on a higher shelf. It was too high. Ollie put his paws on his hips. “Hmm,” he hummed. “I will not climb. That is not safe.”

He spotted a little step stool in the corner. On the seat was a sticker that read, “Ask, plan, and be safe.” Ollie nodded. “Good idea.”

He pushed the stool carefully with slow, soft taps. “Tap, tap.” He set it under the shelf, checked that it was steady, and took one step. “I am brave and careful,” he whispered. He reached up and took the cinnamon jar with both paws. “Got it!”

His friends clapped their paws and wings. “Yay, Ollie!”

Ollie smiled. He stepped down, set the jar on the counter, and sprinkled a tiny bit on the oats. A sweet, cozy smell floated up. “Mmm,” said Bibi. “Smells like hugs.”

Ollie added a small spoon of peanut butter. “Just a scoop,” he said. “Enough is enough.” He stirred slowly. The oats made a soft “mush-mush” sound.

He looked at the apple. “I want slices,” he said. The knife was for grown-ups, so he chose a safe, round cutter shaped like a star. He pressed down with both paws, carefully and slowly. “Press, press.” The apple popped into star pieces. Juice tickled his whiskers.

“Do you want to share?” asked Muna.

“Yes,” said Ollie. “We can share and still be self-reliant. I made it, and I can also be kind.” He put three apple stars on the bamboo plate for his friends and three for himself. He saved the apple core in a small tin labeled “Compost.”

“What’s compost?” asked Jaz.

Ollie explained, “Compost is for peels and cores. They go back to the Earth and help new plants grow. That is sustainability!”

“Smart!” said Bibi. “You used what you had and made a plan.”

Ollie nodded. “I used a cloth towel, a bamboo plate, and only what I needed. No waste, and no mess.”

They all tasted the oats and apples together. “Yum!” said Muna. The cinnamon was warm. The peanut butter was nutty. The apples were crisp. Ollie felt proud and calm.

Then a little problem came. The peanut butter lid rolled off the counter. “Clink, clink!” It rolled under a shelf.

Ollie took a deep breath. “I can solve this,” he said. He lay flat and reached, but it was too far. He looked around and found a wooden spoon with a long handle. “I will try a tool,” he decided. He slid the spoon gently under the shelf and nudged the lid closer. “Nudge, nudge.” The lid rolled back into the light.

“You did it!” cheered Jaz. “That was brave and smart.”

Ollie put the lid on tight. “Twist, twist.” He washed the spoon and wiped the counter. He put the compost tin by the door to take outside later. He stacked the plate, bowl, and spoon to dry. The pantry was neat and calm again.

Bibi patted his shoulder. “You led the way today.”

Muna squeaked, “You asked for help when it was safe and shared your food.”

Jaz flapped. “You took care of the Earth!”

Ollie’s whiskers twitched with joy. “I can make a plan. I can try, fix little problems, and clean up. I can be self-reliant and still be kind and safe.”

They all smiled. The pantry felt warm and bright, and the air smelled like apples and cinnamon.

The End.
