# Tiko Tortoise Takes a Breath

In a sunny house with a squeaky door, there was a laundry room. The floor felt cool and smooth. The washing machine hummed, “Whirr-whirr,” and the dryer rumbled, “Rrmm-rrmm.” Warm, soapy smells filled the air.

This was Tiko Tortoise’s favorite place. Tiko was a wise tortoise with a soft green shell and gentle eyes. He liked to watch the clothes twirl like rainbows. He liked the warm air puffing from the dryer. He liked the quiet hum that made him feel calm.

One morning, Tiko discovered a problem. A big basket of laundry sat on the floor. Tiny socks were mismatched. Shirts were crumpled. A red towel was stuck under the washer door.

“Oh dear,” said Tiko. “Today, I will help fold. I will be careful. I will be kind.”

Just then, a little rabbit named Rufi hopped in. His feet went tip-tap on the tile. “I need my soccer shirt!” Rufi cried. He tugged at the basket. Clothes spilled. Socks flew like little birds.

“Rufi, please slow down,” Tiko said in a calm, low voice. “We can find it together.”

“I can’t wait!” Rufi stomped. The stomp made the dryer jiggle. “Rrmm—bump!” went the dryer. A button rolled across the floor and pinged into the corner.

Tiko felt a pinch in his tummy. His cheeks got warm. His shell felt tight. He did not like stomps. He did not like messes. His voice wanted to come out loud and fast.

He paused.

Tiko touched his shell and remembered. “Wise tortoises, take a breath,” he whispered. “Wise tortoises, take it slow.”

He closed his eyes and breathed in through his nose. In… one, two, three. He breathed out through his mouth. Out… one, two, three. His tummy softened.

“Rufi,” Tiko said gently, “I feel upset when the room gets messy. Let’s use soft feet and soft hands.”

Rufi’s ears drooped. “I’m mad,” he said. “I cannot find my shirt.”

“It is okay to feel mad,” said Tiko. “Feelings are like waves. They come and go. We can ride the waves. Would you like to ride them with me?”

“How?” asked Rufi.

“Watch,” said Tiko. He breathed in. “Smell the soap.” He breathed out. “Blow the bubbles.” The washer went, “Whirr-whirr,” like a song to match their breaths.

Rufi copied. He sniffed the warm, clean smell. He blew out, long and slow. His body felt lighter. His feet stopped stomping.

“Better?” Tiko asked.

“A little,” Rufi said.

“Good,” said Tiko. “Now we can be problem solvers.”

Together they started to sort. “Socks live with socks,” Tiko said. “Shirts live with shirts. Towels live with towels.” He spoke in a friendly rhythm. Rufi nodded and tapped his paw softly in time.

Tiko lifted the red towel free from the door. “Thank you for waiting,” he told the towel. He smoothed it with slow, kind hands. Swish, swish.

Rufi giggled. “You talk to towels?”

“I talk kindly to everyone and everything,” Tiko said. “Kind words keep the room calm.”

Rufi looked around. He saw a sock under the shelf. “I can help,” he said. He crouched and reached with careful paws. “Got it!”

“Teamwork,” said Tiko. “Thank you.”

They stacked shirts in neat little piles. They made a mountain of towels, soft and warm like a sunny hill. The dryer puffed a gentle “whoosh,” making their cheeks feel toasty.

“Do you see a green shirt with a soccer ball?” Rufi asked.

Tiko peered into the basket. “I see blue. I see yellow. I see… ah!” He held up a green sleeve. “Found you.”

“My shirt!” Rufi hopped, but then he remembered. He took a breath. In… smell the soap. Out… blow the bubbles. He reached with soft hands and big eyes. “Thank you, Tiko.”

“You’re welcome,” said Tiko. “You did a good job breathing and waiting.”

Rufi stroked the shirt. “I felt mad,” he said, “but I didn’t stomp again.”

“That is strong,” Tiko said. “That is wise.”

Just then, the washing machine beeped. Beep-beep. Tiko and Rufi opened it together. Warm steam drifted up, smelling like clean rain. They took turns. Tiko handed clothes to Rufi. Rufi placed them gently in the basket.

“I like gentle hands,” Rufi whispered. “They make the room quiet.”

“They make my heart quiet, too,” Tiko said. He smiled, and his eyes sparkled.

When they finished, the laundry room looked neat and happy. The basket sat tall. The floor was clear. The dryer hummed with a soft purr.

Rufi held his shirt to his chest. “I will say sorry to the room for my stomps,” he said. He patted the washer. “Sorry.” He patted the floor. “Sorry.” He looked at Tiko. “And thank you.”

“Thank you for learning,” Tiko said. “Next time you feel mad, what will you do?”

Rufi lifted his ears and spoke proudly. “I will smell the soap. I will blow the bubbles. I will use soft feet and soft hands. I will ask for help.”

Tiko nodded. “That is anger-management,” he said. “You showed respect. You learned. You are growing.”

Rufi grinned. “And we found my shirt!”

They both laughed. The laundry room seemed to laugh with them—whirr, rrmm, whoosh.

“Wise tortoises,” Tiko said, “know that calm helps solve problems.”

“And wise rabbits,” Rufi added, “can learn, too!”

They gave the basket a gentle push toward the door. Together, they stepped out, light and calm, ready for a bright day.

The End.
