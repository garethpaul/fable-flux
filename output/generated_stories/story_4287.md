# Bubbles Learns to Breathe

In a sunny town by the sea, a playful dolphin named Bubbles visited a garage workshop. The workshop smelled like fresh wood and a little like oil. It was full of sounds—clink, clank, whirr! Bright tools hung on the wall like shiny fish. A soft radio played gentle music, la-la-la.

“Hello!” Bubbles chirped, popping her head through the open garage door. “Is anyone here?”

Mr. Leo, a kind mechanic with big gentle hands, waved. “Hi, Bubbles! Come on in. We’re building a mini scooter for the kids’ beach games.”

Bubbles loved games. She loved to swim fast, jump high, and cheer for friends. “Can I help? I want to help!”

“You can help, yes,” said Mr. Leo. “But we use gentle hands and calm voices in the workshop.”

Bubbles nodded, her tail swishing. “Gentle and calm,” she echoed.

On a workbench sat the scooter frame, small wheels, and a bright blue bell. Ding-ding! The bell sounded sweet and tiny. Bubbles giggled. “It sings!”

“Let’s put it together,” said Mr. Leo. “We’ll use this soft wrench and these small screws.”

Bubbles watched closely. She liked the click of the screws and the scrape of the wrench. She liked the smell of pine wood from the scooter handle. She liked the feel of the smooth seat under her fin.

Just then, her friend Mia rolled in a soccer ball. “Hi, Bubbles! After the scooter, want to practice for the beach soccer game?”

“Yes!” Bubbles squeaked. She wiggled with excitement. “I love sports!”

They practiced in the driveway. Mia tapped the ball—tap tap—and Bubbles bounced it with her nose—boop! The ball rolled near a paint can.

“Careful,” Mr. Leo called kindly. “Slow and steady.”

Bubbles wanted to show a big trick. She pushed the ball hard—whoosh!—and the ball bumped the paint can. Clatter! The can tipped. A little blue paint splashed on the floor. Drip, drop.

“Oh no,” said Mia softly.

Bubbles’ face felt hot. Her tummy felt tight. Her tail slapped the floor—smack! She puffed air—huff huff. “I messed up!” she cried. “I’m mad at me!”

Mr. Leo stepped closer but kept his voice warm. “It’s okay to feel mad. Let’s use our calm tools.”

“Calm tools?” Bubbles blinked.

“Yes,” said Mr. Leo. “Language helps. We can say words to help our feelings. Try this with me: I feel mad. I can breathe. I can fix it.”

Bubbles repeated slowly, “I feel mad. I can breathe. I can fix it.”

Mr. Leo put his hand on his chest. “Smell the flower.” He breathed in through his nose—sniff. “Blow the candle.” He blew out—whooo.

Bubbles tried. Sniff. Whooo. Sniff. Whooo. Her tummy softened. Her tail stopped smacking. The tight feeling got smaller.

Mia smiled. “That was good breathing. I can help wipe the paint.”

“I can help too,” said Bubbles. “I made the mess. I can fix it.”

They got warm, soapy water. The bubbles in the bucket were shiny and tickly. Plop, swish, swish. Bubbles hummed with the radio, la-la-la, as she wiped the floor. The blue smudge faded. The garage smelled extra clean.

“See?” said Mr. Leo. “You used your words, your breath, and your helping hands.”

Bubbles’ cheeks felt cool now. “I feel better,” she said. “Thank you.”

They finished the scooter together. Click, click, ding! The bell sang again. Bubbles stroked the handle. “I like that sound. Ding means ready.”

“Ready for soccer practice?” Mia asked.

“Yes, but slow and steady,” said Bubbles. “And we keep the ball away from the paint.” She giggled.

They set up two small cones. The ball felt bouncy and fun under Bubbles’ nose. Tap, tap, pass. Mia cheered, “Nice pass!” Bubbles cheered back, “Go, Mia!” The sun warmed their backs. A sea breeze smelled salty and sweet.

A little crab waddled by the door, looking lost. “Click click?” it said softly.

Bubbles crouched low. “Hello, little friend. Do you need help?”

The crab pointed with a tiny claw at the curb. It was too tall.

Bubbles thought, then spoke kindly. “We can help. Teamwork!” She and Mia rolled a small plank. Mr. Leo held it steady. The crab walked up—tip tap tip tap—and waved its claws. “Click click!” It scurried toward the beach.

“Helping feels good,” said Bubbles. Her heart felt warm and light.

Mr. Leo nodded. “Words, breath, and helping—good tools for any day.”

Bubbles practiced the calm words again, like a song. “I feel mad. I can breathe. I can fix it.” She added a new line: “I can ask for help.”

Mia clapped. “And now, one more gentle goal?”

Bubbles smiled. “Gentle and calm.” She dribbled, then softly pushed the ball. It rolled between the cones. Goal! The bell on the scooter rang in the breeze—ding! Everyone laughed and clapped.

Bubbles looked around the bright, tidy garage workshop. She smelled clean wood, heard the happy bell, and felt the cool floor under her fins. “Today I learned,” she said. “When I feel mad, I won’t splash or smack. I will use my words, breathe slow, and fix what I can. I can help others, and I can play with care.”

“Beautiful,” said Mr. Leo.

“Teamwork!” said Mia.

“Ding!” sang the scooter.

Bubbles flipped her tail with joy. “I’m ready for our beach game,” she said, “calm and kind.”

The End.
