# Polly's Colorful Tradition

Polly the parrot loved the bright, cheerful art room at the community center. The walls were covered with rainbow paintings, and shelves held jars of sparkly paint in every color imaginable. The sweet smell of crayons and the soft sound of brushes on paper made Polly's feathers flutter with excitement.

Today, Polly wanted to teach the little children how to make traditional feather paintings, just like her grandmother had taught her. She had volunteered to help during art time, and her heart felt warm thinking about sharing this special tradition.

"Good morning, children!" chirped Polly as five little ones gathered around the art table. "Today I'll show you how my family makes beautiful feather paintings!"

But when Polly began to explain, the children seemed confused. "Why don't we just use brushes?" asked little Emma.

"Feathers are scratchy!" said Tommy, making a face.

Polly felt her colorful feathers droop. Her beak trembled a little. She had been so excited to share her grandmother's special way of painting, but the children didn't seem interested.

"Maybe we should just do regular painting," suggested the teacher kindly.

Polly's chest felt tight and frustrated. She wanted to squawk loudly or fly away, but instead she took three deep breaths, just like her grandmother had taught her. "One... two... three," she whispered softly to herself.

"Wait," Polly said gently. "Let me show you something magical first."

She picked up a soft, blue feather and dipped it carefully in golden paint. With gentle movements, she swept the feather across the paper. The paint flowed like silk, creating beautiful, swirling patterns that looked like clouds dancing in the sky.

"Ooooh!" gasped little Sarah, her eyes growing wide.

Polly smiled and continued. She dipped the feather in purple paint and made gentle dots that looked like tiny flowers. Then she used the tip of the feather to draw delicate lines that sparkled in the light.

"My grandmother taught me this when I was just a little parrot," Polly explained softly. "She said feathers can paint things that brushes cannot. They make soft, dreamy pictures that feel like hugs."

"Can I try?" asked Tommy, reaching for a green feather.

"Me too! Me too!" called the other children.

Polly's heart soared as she helped each child choose their own special feather. She showed Emma how to make gentle swirls, and helped Tommy create soft, puffy clouds. The children giggled with delight as their feathers danced across the paper.

"This feels so soft and tickly!" laughed Sarah, painting purple butterflies.

"Look! My feather makes the most beautiful trees!" said Emma proudly.

As the children painted, Polly told them stories about her grandmother and how feather painting had been passed down through many generations of parrots. The art room filled with happy chatter and the gentle swoosh of feathers on paper.

When art time ended, each child had created a magical feather painting. Their faces glowed with pride and joy.

"Thank you, Polly!" they called out together. "Can you teach us more traditions next week?"

Polly's feathers shimmered with happiness. She had learned that sharing traditions takes patience and understanding. When she felt frustrated, taking deep breaths helped her stay calm and find a better way to share her special gift.

"I would love to," Polly chirped cheerfully. "Traditions are meant to be shared with friends!"

The End.
