# Koala Kiri and the Whispering Attic

Koala Kiri was a calm, gentle koala with soft gray fur and big, round ears. One sunny afternoon, Kiri climbed the stairs to a quiet attic. The steps creaked softly—creak, creak—and the air smelled like cedar and old books. A tiny window let in a warm, golden light that made dust sparkle like little stars.

“This is a peaceful place,” Kiri whispered. “I will look, listen, and be safe.”

Kiri took a deep breath. He remembered the safety rules he learned: go slowly, keep paws to yourself, ask for help, and do not touch things you don’t know. Kiri nodded. “I can be careful.”

In the attic were many boxes. Some had labels in different languages. One said “Books.” Another said “Libros.” Another said “Bücher.” Kiri’s eyes grew wide. “These words say the same thing in different ways!” he said, smiling. “How wonderful.”

A soft rustle came from behind a trunk. Kiri’s ears perked up. “Hello?” he called gently. Something squeaked. Kiri stopped and thought. “I will not poke or scare. I will ask and be kind.”

He sat on a rug and spoke softly. “My name is Kiri. I am calm. I am careful. Are you okay?”

A small mouse peeked out. It had shiny eyes and whiskers that twitched. “Squeak,” it said, ears up.

Kiri smiled. “It is nice to see you.” He put his paws in his lap to show he would not grab. The mouse stepped closer. “Squeak-squeak.”

Kiri listened. “I don’t speak Mouse,” he said, “but I can listen. I can look at your face and learn.”

The mouse pointed its nose toward a tall shelf. A little bell sat at the top, near a string of tiny flags. The mouse squeaked and then placed its paws on its tummy. It looked worried.

“Oh,” said Kiri. “You want the bell, but it is too high, and you feel hungry or hurt?” Kiri tilted his head. “I must be sure.”

The mouse put its paws together like a plea, then pointed again to the bell. Kiri nodded. “I see. But climbing that shelf could be unsafe. It looks wobbly.”

Kiri looked around for help. He found a short step stool with rubber feet. He checked it. “Stable,” he said. “But I still need a grown-up.” He remembered another rule: ask for help when something seems tricky.

Kiri went to the top of the stairs and called softly, “Grandma Mei, may I have help, please?”

Grandma Mei came up, her steps slow and steady. She wore a bright scarf with patterns from her home far away. “Hello, Kiri,” she said kindly. “What do you need?”

Kiri pointed to the shelf. “The mouse wants the little bell. I want to be safe. The shelf is wobbly.”

Grandma Mei smiled. “Good noticing, Kiri. Safety first. We will not climb. We will move the shelf carefully together and use the stool, not the shelf.”

She held the shelf steady. Kiri climbed the stool slowly, one step at a time. “One, two,” he said. He kept three points of contact—two paws and one foot—just like Grandma taught him. He reached gently and picked up the bell. It was cool and smooth, with little stars on the side.

Kiri came down and placed the bell on the floor. The mouse tapped it with a tiny paw. Ting! The sound was bright and sweet, like a tiny sunbeam. The mouse’s eyes shined. “Squeak-squeak!” It made a happy circle dance.

Grandma Mei clapped softly. “Listen,” she said. “In my language, Mandarin, we might say, ‘Xie xie,’ for thank you.”

Kiri smiled at the mouse. “Xie xie means thank you,” he said. “In Spanish, we say, ‘Gracias.’ In German, we say, ‘Danke.’” He pointed to the labeled boxes. “Books, Libros, Bücher. Many words, same love.”

The mouse tried to copy. “Squee–xie,” it squeaked, then “Gra–squeak!” Everyone laughed gently. The attic felt warm with happy sounds.

Then the wind made the window rattle, clack-clack. Kiri looked up. “Is the window safe?” he asked.

Grandma Mei checked the latch. “It is a bit loose,” she said. “We will lock it and add a soft cloth so it stops rattling. Thank you for asking, Kiri. You kept us safe.”

They fixed the latch together. Kiri learned to turn the lock with two gentle paws and to tell a grown-up if something felt shaky. The mouse watched with big eyes, whiskers still.

To celebrate, Grandma Mei taught a little song. “In English, we sing, ‘Safe and slow, here we go.’ In Mandarin, we can sing, ‘Ānquán mànmàn, yīqǐ zǒu.’ It means the same.”

Kiri repeated the words slowly, feeling the shapes on his tongue. “Ānquán,” he said. The mouse made a tiny “an-chan” squeak. They all giggled.

The sunlight grew softer, and the dust stars danced slower. Kiri felt calm and proud. He had listened. He had asked for help. He had stayed safe. He had learned new words and showed kindness across languages.

Grandma Mei patted Kiri’s shoulder. “Today you used your eyes, ears, and heart. You kept yourself and others safe. That is very wise.”

Kiri rang the little bell once. Ting! “Thank you, Grandma Mei,” he said. He looked at the mouse. “Xie xie. Gracias. Danke.” The mouse squeaked back and waved its tiny paw.

As they went down the stairs, Kiri whispered his lesson: “Be calm. Go slow. Ask for help. Listen to words, even new ones. Safety is love.”

The quiet attic rested again, full of soft light, gentle sounds, and friendly words.

The End.
