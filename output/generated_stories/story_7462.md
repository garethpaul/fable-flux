# Tully the Turtle’s Treehouse Day

High in a friendly oak tree, there was a cozy treehouse. It smelled like pine and sunshine. Soft leaves brushed the windows with a whisper, swish-swish. Inside, a shy turtle named Tully lived and learned.

Tully liked quiet mornings. He listened to birds sing, “Tweet-tweet!” He felt the warm sun on his shell. He took slow breaths. In… and out. Being calm helped Tully feel safe.

“Good morning, Tully!” chirped Pip the sparrow, peeking in the round window.

“G-good morning,” Tully whispered, tucking his head in just a little. He was shy, but curious too.

Pip fluttered inside and looked around. The treehouse had shelves made from old wood, a tiny rainwater bucket, and a little broom made of twigs. On the wall hung a sign that Tully had painted. It said, “Care for our home.”

“What will we do today?” Pip asked.

Tully peeked out more. “Today, I want to take care of our tree,” he said softly. “We can keep it clean. We can save water. We can be gentle.”

Pip nodded. “Let’s do it!”

They heard a rustle below. “Hello up there!” called Squeak the field mouse, carrying a leaf bag. “Can I help?”

Tully smiled a tiny smile. “Yes, please,” he said. “We can all help.”

They climbed down the rope ladder, step by careful step. Tully felt the bark under his feet. It was rough and warm. He patted the trunk. “Thank you, tree,” he whispered.

On the ground, they saw little bits of paper and a shiny bottle cap near the roots. Pip’s feathers puffed. “Oh no! The tree could get hurt.”

Tully took a calm breath. “We can fix this,” he said in his gentle voice. “We will clean up.”

They sang a soft clean-up song as they worked. “Pick it up, tidy up, thank you, tree,” sang Pip. Squeak dropped the cap into the leaf bag with a clink. Tully used his twig broom. Swish, swish! It felt good to help.

Soon the ground was neat. The roots could breathe. Tully touched the clean soil. It felt cool and crumbly. “Better,” he whispered.

Back in the treehouse, Pip asked, “What next?”

Tully looked at his rainwater bucket. “We can save water,” he said. “We can use rain to wash our paws.”

Squeak peered in. “Is the rain clean?”

“Yes,” said Tully. “We catch it from the roof in a clean bucket. We take just what we need.” He dipped a cloth and gently washed his paws. Drip-drop. “We use the rest for the plants.”

They watered a little fern in the corner. The fern lifted its green fronds, happy and bright.

Just then, a gust of wind blew through the window. Flap-flap! A paper map slid off the shelf and tore. Squeak squeaked. “Oh dear! It’s broken!”

Tully stayed calm. He took a slow breath. “We can mend it,” he said. “We do not throw away what we can fix.”

He brought out a small pot of paste he made from flour and water. It smelled like warm bread. “We reuse,” he explained. “We make new from old.” With careful turtle hands, he smoothed the torn map. Pip held the corners. Squeak patted the edges. Together, they made it whole again.

“Wow,” Pip said. “You are so smart, Tully.”

Tully tucked his head a little, shy but proud. “We are smart together,” he whispered.

They heard another sound—crackle-crackle—from outside. Sunbeams showed tiny dust sparkles in the air. “The window is dusty,” Pip said.

“Let’s clean it with our cloth that we wash and use again,” said Tully. He dipped the cloth in rainwater and wiped the window. Round and round. The glass became clear. The world looked bright. They could see a ladybug on a leaf, shiny and red.

“Hello, ladybug,” Squeak giggled.

Tully looked around. The treehouse felt fresh. The air smelled like leaves and clean water. He felt a warm glow in his shell. “Caring feels good,” he said.

Pip fluttered to the painted sign. “Can we teach others?” he asked.

Tully nodded. His shy voice grew a little stronger. “Yes,” he said. “We can share simple steps.”

He made a tiny poster with big, friendly letters:
- Pick up litter.
- Save water.
- Fix and reuse.
- Be calm and kind.

They hung the poster by the door. Soon, friends came by—Bea the bee, Hoot the owl, and even a curious squirrel. Tully greeted them, slow and gentle. “Welcome,” he said. “This is our home. We care for it.”

Bea buzzed happily. “I will plant flowers.”

Hoot blinked. “I will watch at night and keep it safe.”

The squirrel chattered. “I will not drop shells on roots.”

Tully’s shy heart felt brave. He had used calmness and resourcefulness. He had learned, and he had taught. The treehouse hummed with happy sounds—buzz-buzz, tweet-tweet, rustle-rustle.

As the sun began to set, the sky turned peach and gold. The leaves whispered goodnight. Tully, Pip, and Squeak sat quietly, sipping cool water and listening.

Tully spoke softly. “Today we learned to care for our tree. We cleaned, we saved, we fixed, and we shared. When we care for our home, our home cares for us.”

Pip smiled. “We can do this every day.”

Squeak nodded. “Little steps, big help.”

Tully tucked into his shell, cozy and calm, and whispered, “Yes. Little steps.”

The End.
