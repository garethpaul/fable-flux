# Tika the Singing Bird and the Light Helper

Down a small set of creaky stairs, there was a cozy basement lab. It smelled like warm dust and lemon soap. Soft yellow lights hummed. Buttons blinked red, green, and blue. In the corner stood tall shelves with jars of screws that clicked and clinked. A little fan went whirr, whirr, whirr.

Tika the singing bird fluttered in through a tiny window. Her feathers were sky-blue with a bright orange chest. She loved to sing while people worked. Today, Tika wanted to help, too.

“Good morning, lab!” Tika chirped. “Tweet-a-dee! Let’s do a task!”

Mr. Leo, a gentle inventor with curly hair and round glasses, smiled. “Good morning, Tika. I have a big job. I want to make a Light Helper. It will turn on the lamp when it gets dark, so nobody trips.”

Tika tilted her head. “A Light Helper? That sounds smart. Can I help?”

“Yes,” said Mr. Leo. “We need to plan, build, and test. We will use understanding, and we will finish the task.”

Tika’s little heart beat fast with joy. “Plan, build, test! Tweet-a-dee!”

They spread paper on the table. Mr. Leo drew a lamp and a tiny switch. He drew a sensor with a smiley face. “This part feels the dark,” he explained. “When it is dark, it tells the lamp, ‘Turn on!’”

Tika tapped the paper with her beak. “What does the sensor need?”

“Good question,” said Mr. Leo. “It needs wires to carry the message. And we need a safe battery.”

Tika fluffed her feathers. “I will sort the wires!”

She flew to the shelf. Red wires. Black wires. Yellow wires. They smelled like new rubber. Tika sang, “Red to red, black to black, yellow for a happy track!”

Mr. Leo chuckled. “Great idea, Tika. A song helps us remember.”

They placed the pieces on the table: the lamp, the sensor, the switch, the battery, and the wires. Tika watched carefully. Her eyes were bright and curious.

“First, we connect,” said Mr. Leo.

“Connect means put together,” Tika said, nodding. “I understand.”

Click. A wire went into a little hole. Snap. Another wire fit tight. Tika listened. “I hear little clicks! That means it is snug.”

They built and built. But when Mr. Leo flipped the switch, nothing happened. The lamp stayed dark.

Tika paused. She felt a small flutter of worry. “Oh no. Did we do it wrong?”

Mr. Leo took a deep breath. “It is okay. Innovation means we try, we learn, and we try again. We understand by looking closely.”

Tika hummed softly to feel calm. “Try, learn, try again,” she sang. “Tweet-a-dee.”

They checked the wires. Tika peered with one bright eye. “This wire is loose,” she said softly. The yellow wire had slipped out.

“Good finding,” said Mr. Leo. “You understood the problem.”

He gently pushed the wire in until it clicked. “Let’s test again.”

He flipped the switch. The lamp glowed a warm, golden light. The room felt cozy and safe.

“Light!” Tika sang. “We did it! We did it!”

Mr. Leo smiled. “We are not done yet. We need to test the sensor. Let’s make it dark.”

Tika spread her wings and covered the sensor with a soft feathery hug. The sensor felt smooth and cool on her feathers. The fan went whirr, whirr, whirr. The lamp turned on all by itself.

“It works!” Tika chirped. “It works in the dark!”

When Tika lifted her wings, the lamp turned off. She giggled. “Peekaboo, little light!”

Mr. Leo clapped. “Task complete. We planned, we built, and we tested. We used understanding. We finished our job.”

Tika’s chest puffed with pride. “I helped with innovation. I used my eyes, my ears, and my song.”

Mr. Leo wiped the table and put the tools away. “Innovation means trying new ideas to help people. Today, our Light Helper will keep feet safe.”

They carried the Light Helper to the bottom of the stairs and set it near the steps. The air was cool there, and the cement floor felt smooth under Mr. Leo’s shoes. He turned off the main light. The stairs looked dark.

Tika covered the sensor again. Click—on came the lamp! The steps glowed softly like honey. Tika’s heart felt warm.

“Now no one will trip,” she said. “Our idea helps.”

Mr. Leo knelt so his eyes met Tika’s. “You asked good questions. You noticed little sounds. You stayed calm and kept trying. That is innovation.”

Tika sang a happy song. “Plan, build, test. Look, listen, rest. Try, learn, try again. Then we do our best!”

Mr. Leo laughed. “That’s the perfect lab song.”

They turned the main lights back on. The fan hummed. The jars of screws twinkled. The basement lab felt bright and friendly.

Tika fluttered to the window. “I will come back tomorrow,” she chirped. “We can make a Water Reminder or a Toy Picker-Upper!”

Mr. Leo waved. “We will innovate again. One task at a time.”

Tika flew out into the soft afternoon air, her song floating behind her like a ribbon. She felt brave, smart, and kind.

Lesson: Innovation means trying new ideas, asking questions, and testing until it works. With understanding and steady steps, we can complete our tasks and help others.

The End.
