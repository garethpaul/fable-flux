# Pebble Plans on the Porch

Pip was a cheerful penguin who loved to sit on the peaceful front porch. The porch smelled like fresh wood and warm tea. A soft breeze tickled Pip’s feathers. Birds chirped. The sun felt cozy on Pip’s beak.

Pip smiled. “Hello, world,” Pip said.

On the little porch table, Pip had a puzzle. It was a big picture of the Earth. The puzzle had many bright pieces—blue for oceans, green for forests, yellow for deserts, and white for snowy places like Pip’s home.

Pip wanted to finish the Earth puzzle today. But the box was very full. When Pip tipped it, the pieces scattered. Click, clack, patter! Pieces slid under the chair. Pieces fell near the flowerpot. One tiny piece bounced off the step and disappeared.

“Oh no,” said Pip. “I can’t see the whole Earth if a piece is missing.” Pip’s tummy fluttered. Pip wanted to hurry and finish, but hurrying felt messy.

Pip took a deep breath. In, out. The porch smelled like lemons from the tea. “I can think. I can plan,” Pip said. “I will use my penguin brain.”

Pip looked at the box. The picture on the box showed the whole Earth. Pip tapped the corners. “I will start with the edges,” Pip said. “Edges make a frame.” Pip found all the straight pieces and clicked them together. Click. Click. Click. “Good job, Pip,” Pip whispered.

A neighbor snail slid across the porch rail, leaving a silver line. “Hello, Pip,” said the snail, slow and kind. “What are you building?”

“I’m building our Earth,” said Pip. “But a piece fell. I need to find it.”

The snail nodded. “Let’s think. Where did it fall? What did it sound like?”

“It went pat-pat—plunk!” said Pip.

The snail smiled. “Plunk means it hit something hard. Maybe the step. Maybe the bucket.”

Pip looked around. Pip listened. The breeze rustled the porch plants. A wind chime sang, ting-ting. Pip crouched and peered under the chair. “Not here.” Pip lifted the flowerpot gently. “Not here.” Pip tapped the empty bucket. Clonk!

“Clonk is not plunk,” said Pip. “Hmm.”

Pip felt a squeeze in the chest. “I want to be done now,” Pip said. Pip’s flippers drooped.

The snail took a tiny sip of dew. “Being patient helps the brain,” the snail said. “Let’s try steps. Step one, look for clues. Step two, make a plan. Step three, try the plan. Step four, try again if needed.”

Pip nodded. “Okay. Clues. The piece was tiny and bounced. It was light. The wind is blowing toward the steps.” Pip looked at the porch mat by the steps. The mat had a little bump.

Pip lifted the mat. “Aha!” But under the mat was only dust and a small ant. The ant waved tiny feelers.

“Hello,” Pip said. “Did you see a blue piece?”

The ant waved again and pointed its itty antenna toward the porch plant. Pip followed the ant’s point and peered into the leaves. The leaves smelled green and wet. Pip saw a shimmer. “Just the snail trail,” Pip giggled.

Pip thought again. “Pieces bounce and roll. Where do round things roll?” Pip looked at the porch slope. The lowest place was the corner by the shoe rack.

Pip waddled to the corner and looked behind the shoes. “Hello, little piece?” Pip called. No piece.

The door opened with a soft creak. Pip’s friend, Mei, stepped out, holding a warm cup. “Hi, Pip,” Mei said. “What a lovely Earth puzzle!”

“Thanks,” said Pip. “But I’m missing a piece.”

Mei crouched. “Let’s use our senses,” she said. “Eyes to see, ears to hear, nose to smell, hands to feel. And let’s think like a team. The Earth is for all of us.”

Pip smiled big. “Yes! Team Earth!”

They both listened. A faint tap-tap came from under the porch bench. Pip lay on the porch. The boards felt smooth and warm. Pip slid a flipper under the bench. Swish. Pip felt something small and cool. “Got it!”

Pip pulled out the tiny blue piece. It had a curl like a wave. Pip held it up to the sun. The piece shone.

“Yay!” Mei clapped. The snail cheered in a slow, happy way: “Hoooray.”

Pip placed the piece where the oceans were. It clicked in. Click! The puzzle showed the whole Earth now—big blue seas, green lands, white ice at the bottom where Pip lived.

Pip and Mei sat quietly for a moment. They heard bees hum by the flowers. They smelled tea and leaves. The wind chime sang again.

Pip patted the puzzle. “We did it by thinking. We looked for clues. We made a plan. We used our senses. We were patient. We worked as a team.”

Mei nodded. “And we saw that every piece matters. Every place and every person is part of Earth.”

Pip smiled at the snail and the ant. “Thank you, neighbors,” Pip said. “I will care for our porch. I will care for our Earth. I will ask questions. I will listen. I will think before I act.”

The snail lifted its eyes on little stalks. “That is good citizenship,” the snail said slowly. “We all share this home.”

Pip poured a tiny drop of water for the plants. Pip picked up the puzzle box and put the extra pieces in carefully. Pip swept the dust.

Pip looked at the finished Earth and felt calm. The porch seemed even more peaceful now, like it was saying, “Well done.”

Pip whispered, “When I have a problem, I can stop, think, plan, and try. With patience and friends, I can find the missing piece.”

The End.
