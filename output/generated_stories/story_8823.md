# Ellie Paints a Plan

Ellie was a gentle elephant calf with soft gray ears and bright, curious eyes. She lived in a playful living room with colorful pillows, a bouncy rug, and shelves full of books and balls. Sunlight made golden squares on the floor. The air smelled like crayons and warm toast.

One morning, Ellie wiggled her toes on the rug and said, “I want to make art!” She set out paper, paints, and crayons. She put on a big, blue smock. “I can do it,” she whispered with a happy trumpet, “toot!”

But when Ellie dipped her brush, her trunk bumped the water cup. Splash! Water dripped on the paper. “Oh no,” Ellie said, her eyes wide. “My art is soggy.”

She tried again. Swish, swish went the brush. Her trunk tickled her ear. A streak of red went across the page, too wiggly and wild. “This does not look like the picture in my head,” Ellie said. Her tummy felt twisty. She wanted to cry.

Mama’s voice was warm and calm. “What is the problem, Ellie?”

“My trunk is big. My lines are wiggly. I can’t make my art,” Ellie sniffled.

Mama sat beside her on the soft rug. “We can make a plan,” Mama said. “Artists make plans. That is knowledge. We can learn and try.”

Ellie blinked. “A plan?”

“Yes,” Mama said. “Step one: breathe.” Mama took a slow breath. Ellie copied: in, out. Her tummy felt softer. “Step two: hold your tools in a way that works for you. Your trunk can be strong and gentle.”

Mama wrapped a ribbon around the brush to make a loop. Ellie slid the loop onto her trunk tip. It felt snug and safe. “Oooh,” Ellie said. “It feels steady.”

“Step three,” Mama said, “practice lines. Artists practice. Athletes practice too. Just like when you toss your soft ball into the basket.”

Ellie looked at her little basket by the couch and her fuzzy orange ball. “I practice toss, toss,” Ellie said. She wiggled her feet and tossed the ball. Plop! It landed in the basket. “I did it because I practiced.”

“That’s right,” Mama smiled. “You use your body in sports, and your mind and body in art. Practice helps both.”

Ellie dipped her brush again. The paint was cool and smooth. “Swish, swish,” she said. She made straight lines. She made tiny dots. She made slow curves. Some lines were still wiggly, but Ellie smiled. “I am learning.”

She listened to the brush whisper on the paper. She smelled the sweet paint and the woody crayon. She felt the paper bumpy under her trunk. “This is nice,” Ellie said.

After practice, Ellie sat back. “Now I want to paint my family,” she said. “But I do not know where to start.”

Mama nodded. “Artists look and think first. That is knowledge, too. Step four: look closely.”

Ellie looked at Mama’s kind eyes, at the round clock, at the sunny window. She noticed shapes. “Your ear is like a big leaf,” Ellie said. “The window is a square. The ball is a circle!”

“Good noticing,” Mama said.

Ellie drew a big circle for a head, big leaf ears, and a long, gentle trunk. She added her basket and ball. She added the sunny window. Her brush loop kept steady. Her heart felt warm and brave.

Then—whoops!—a drop of blue fell on the corner. “Oh no,” Ellie gasped.

Mama smiled. “Step five: turn mistakes into art. What can the blue drop be?”

Ellie tilted her head. She tapped her chin with her trunk. “A butterfly!” she said. She added wings and dots. “Hello, Blue!”

Ellie giggled and painted more butterflies. They danced by the window. She hummed a little song, “Swish and dot, I like my art.”

After a while, Ellie put down the brush. She held up her picture. “Mama, look!”

Mama’s eyes shone. “I see careful lines. I see your family. I see your basket and ball. I see butterflies. I see practice and patience.”

Ellie felt proud. Her cheeks felt warm. “I solved my problem,” she said. “I made a plan. I used my knowledge. I practiced like sports. I made art.”

“Art helps us share feelings and ideas,” Mama said. “Sports help our bodies grow strong. Books and plans help our minds grow wise. You used them all.”

Ellie nodded. She set the painting to dry. Then she picked up her fuzzy orange ball. “Can I toss while I wait?” she asked.

“Yes,” Mama said. “Let’s make a little game. Toss, then tell one thing you learned.”

Ellie tossed. Plop! “I learned to breathe.”

She tossed again. Plop! “I learned to look.”

She tossed once more. Plop! “I learned to turn oops into art.”

Mama clapped softly. “Those are big lessons.”

When the paint was dry, Ellie taped her picture to the wall. The room looked even more playful now, with color and joy.

Ellie hugged Mama. “Art makes me happy,” she said.

Mama hugged back. “Art, knowledge, and sports make a great team.”

Ellie smiled and gave a tiny trumpet. “Toot! I can learn and try. I can make a plan. I can make art.”

The End.
