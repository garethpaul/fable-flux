# Koala Kai Finds His Voice

Koala Kai was a calm koala with soft, gray fur and gentle eyes. One sunny morning, Kai climbed the creaky stairs to the quiet attic. The air smelled like old books and lemon polish. Dust danced in the light from a tiny window.

“Kai, can you help me tidy the attic today?” called Nana from below. “We need the box with the blue star.”

Kai took a slow breath. “I can try,” he said in his calm voice. “But I might need help.”

He stepped onto the old wooden floor. It made a little “creak, creak.” Kai listened. The attic was quiet, except for the soft hum of a fan and the faint chirp of birds outside.

Kai saw many boxes. Big boxes, small boxes, tall boxes, and tiny boxes. Some had labels. Some did not. He read, sounding out the words slowly. “Toys. Books. Winter hats.” But he did not see a blue star.

Kai felt a little flutter in his tummy. He wanted to do the job well. He wanted to help Nana. He hugged himself, just like a koala hugs a tree. “It’s okay to ask for what I need,” he whispered. “That is self-advocacy.”

He called to the stairs, “Nana, can I have a picture of the box? That would help me find it.”

Nana came halfway up with a smile. “Good asking, Kai,” she said. She showed him a small drawing of a box with a big, bright blue star. “It might be near the painting of the ocean.”

“Thank you,” said Kai. “I also need a cloth for dust, and a little snack, please.”

“Great job saying what you need,” Nana replied. She brought a soft cloth and a small bowl of apple slices. The apples smelled sweet. Kai took a crunchy bite. “Crunch!” He felt stronger.

Kai looked around. In the corner, a painting of the ocean leaned against a trunk. Waves splashed, blue and white. “Swish, swoosh,” he whispered, pretending he could hear the water. He checked the boxes nearby. No blue star.

He made a plan. “First, I will move the light boxes. Next, I will dust. Then, I will check under the table.” He said the steps out loud to help his mind.

“Step one,” he said. Kai slid a small box to the side. “Step two,” he gently wiped dust. The cloth turned gray. “Step three,” he peeked under a wobbly table. “Hello?” he said softly. Only a sleepy spider waved a thin leg.

Kai felt a little stuck. His ears got warm. He sat crisscross and took a deep, slow breath. In and out. In and out. He remembered his skill. “I can use my words. I can ask for help. I can say what I need.”

“Nana,” Kai called. “I am trying. I need a flashlight. It is a little dark under the table.”

“On my way,” said Nana. She handed him a small yellow flashlight. Click! Warm light made the shadows dance away.

Kai looked again. “Aha!” There, under a blanket with stars, was a box with a bright blue star sticker. “I found it!” he cheered.

He tried to pull the box out. It was stuck between two bigger boxes. Kai paused. “I need a safe way,” he told himself. He used his resourceful mind. He moved one big box just a little. Scoot, scoot. He used a soft towel to make the floor smooth. Then he pulled the star box slowly. “Slide, slide.” Out it came!

Kai hugged the box. It felt cool and bumpy. “Nana, I got it!”

Nana clapped. “Kai, you did it! You asked for what you needed. You made a plan. You kept going.”

Kai smiled. His heart felt warm. “Self-advocacy helped me,” he said. “I said what I needed: a picture, a cloth, a snack, and a flashlight. I made steps. I finished the job.”

They opened the star box together. Inside were family photos and a blue ribbon. The photos smelled like paper and a little bit like cinnamon from the box’s corner. “Memories,” Nana said softly. “Thank you for your careful work.”

Kai looked at the tidy corner. The attic felt brighter and friendlier. The fan hummed a happy “whirrr.” Kai felt proud from his nose to his toes.

He turned to Nana. “Next time, can we label more boxes?” he asked. “It will help me help you.”

“That is a smart idea,” Nana said. “You are speaking up. That helps both of us.”

Kai nodded. “When I use my words, I can get what I need to do my job.”

They placed a big, clear label on the star box together. Kai printed slowly: “Special Things.” He patted the box, then ate one last crunchy apple slice.

As they went downstairs, the steps went “creak, creak,” but now it sounded like a little song. Kai felt calm, strong, and ready for his next small adventure.

Lesson: It is good to speak up. You can say what you need. You can make a plan. You can finish your tasks. That is self-advocacy, and it helps everyone.

The End.
