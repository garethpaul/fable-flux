# Pebble Pockets on the Rooftop

Pip was a cheerful penguin with bright eyes and a wobbly walk. Pip loved to collect shiny pebbles. One sunny morning, Pip climbed the stairs, step by careful step, to the rooftop garden. The wind was gentle and cool. Pip felt it tickle the feathers. The sky was blue like a big soft blanket.

“Hello, rooftop!” Pip chirped. “I brought my pebbles.”

Pip set down a small backpack with a zip that went zzzip. Inside were many smooth, round pebbles—blue, white, and sparkly. Pip liked to sort them and make little paths for tiny toy boats to slide across. Pip hummed a happy tune, “La-la-la,” and tapped the pebbles together. Click, click, click.

Then Pip heard a flutter. “Good morning,” said a little gray bird hopping on the railing. “I’m Bree.”

“Hi, Bree! Want to see my pebble path?” Pip asked.

“Yes, please,” Bree chirped.

Pip unzipped the backpack, but the zipper stuck. Pip tugged hard. Zzz—rip! The backpack slipped. Pebbles spilled and scattered everywhere—tap, tap, tinkle—rolling across the rooftop floor. One pebble tumbled near the drain. Another slid under a flower pot. Another stopped by Pip’s feet.

“Oh no!” Pip yelped. Pip’s tummy felt tight. Pip’s face felt hot. Pip’s flippers shook. “My pebbles! My path!”

Bree tilted her head. “You look upset.”

“I am upset,” Pip said, with a wobbly voice. “I’m mad at my backpack. I’m sad about my pebbles. I don’t know what to do.”

Bree hopped closer. “Let’s name the feeling. Your tummy is tight. Your face is hot. Your flippers shake. That feels like big feelings.”

Pip put a flipper on the tummy. “Big feelings,” Pip whispered.

Bree nodded. “When I have big feelings, I do three calm things.”

“What things?” Pip asked, blinking back tears.

“First, I stop and be still,” Bree said. “Second, I take three slow breaths. Third, I talk kindly to myself.”

Pip sniffed. “I can try.”

Pip stood still. Pip planted feet on the warm rooftop. Pip felt the wind on the cheeks. Pip listened to a faraway car hum, mmm. Then Pip took a slow breath in. One. The air smelled like wet dirt and flowers. Pip let it out. Whoooo. Two. In and out. Three. In and out.

Pip put a flipper on the heart and whispered, “I can be calm. I can try again.”

“How do you feel now?” Bree asked gently.

“My tummy is softer,” Pip said. “My face is cooler. I still feel a little sad.”

“It’s okay to feel sad,” Bree said. “Feelings come and go, like clouds.”

Pip nodded. “I can name my feeling. I am sad and calm.”

“Now we can solve the problem,” Bree chirped. “We can use knowledge and make a plan.”

Pip’s eyes brightened. “A plan!”

Pip looked around the rooftop. The floor had lines, like squares on a big hop game. “We can check each square,” Pip said. “We can move the flower pots. We can listen for pebbles.”

Bree flapped. “We can also organize. Let’s make pebble pockets.”

“Pebble pockets?” Pip asked.

“Yes,” Bree said. “We can use little bowls for blue pebbles, white pebbles, and sparkly pebbles. That way, they won’t roll away.”

Pip clapped flippers softly. “Good idea!”

They searched the rooftop. Pip and Bree checked one square at a time. Step, look, pick. Pip moved a flower pot—scrape, scrape—and found two pebbles hiding in the shade. Bree pecked near the drain and heard a tiny clink. “Found one!” Bree sang.

Pip laughed. The laugh felt light. “Thank you, Bree.”

They set three bowls on the rooftop: blue in one, white in one, sparkly in one. Pip dropped pebbles in, plink, plink. The bowls made a sweet sound. Pip stood tall. “We are organizing!”

Sometimes a pebble would hide again, and Pip felt a small squeeze in the tummy. “Big feelings are back,” Pip said.

Bree nodded. “What can you do?”

Pip took three breaths. In. Out. In. Out. In. Out. “I can be calm,” Pip told Pip. “I can try again.”

They kept going, slow and steady. They found a pebble under a leaf. They found three by a shoe. They found one shining like a tiny moon in the sun.

At last, all the pebbles rested in the bowls. The rooftop felt neat and peaceful. Pip smiled so wide the cheeks felt warm again, but this time with happy.

“We did it,” Pip said. “We had a problem. We named our feelings. We calmed our bodies. We made a plan. We organized. We solved it!”

Bree chirped a little tune. “You learned a lot.”

Pip nodded. “I learned that feelings can be big. I can be calm and kind to myself. I can use my brain and make a plan. And I can keep my pebbles in bowls.”

Pip lined the pebbles into a small path, blue-white-sparkly, blue-white-sparkly. The path winked in the sun. Pip set a toy boat at the start. “Ready, set, slide!” The boat slid along the path—swish—and stopped by Bree.

Bree laughed. “Beautiful!”

Pip zipped the backpack gently. Zzzip. Then Pip put the bowls inside carefully. “Now my pebbles have cozy homes,” Pip said.

Pip looked at the sky and took one more soft breath. The air smelled sweet. The wind felt like a hug.

“I can feel, I can calm, I can think, I can organize,” Pip said. “I can try again.”

The End.
