# Tiko the Tortoise Says, “I’m Sorry”

In a cozy pantry at the back of a small, sunny house lived a wise tortoise named Tiko. The pantry smelled like warm bread and sweet cinnamon. Glass jars lined the shelves and made soft clinks when the door closed. A tiny window let in a stripe of light that moved across the floor each day.

Tiko loved the pantry. He loved the cool tile under his feet. He loved the shiny jars of beans and rice. And he loved to share stories with his friends who visited: Mimi the mouse, Pip the little chick, and Zoom the zig-zag beetle.

One morning, Tiko woke early. The light stripe was thin and golden. His tummy rumbled. “I would like some crunchy crackers,” he said. He saw a box high on a shelf. It was wrapped in crinkly paper. It looked yummy.

Tiko puffed his cheeks and thought. “I can do this by myself,” he said. He inched toward a wooden stool. The stool wobbled a tiny bit. Tiko was brave. He climbed one slow step. Tap. Tap.

“Careful, Tiko,” chirped Pip, peeking in. “That looks wobbly.”

“I am wise,” Tiko said kindly. “I can be careful too.”

Tiko reached up. His shell bumped a jar with a soft clink. The jar slid. Clink… clonk… CRASH!

The jar of beans fell and burst open. Beans rolled everywhere like tiny, hard rain. Tap-tap-tap, they danced across the floor. The sound was loud. The smell was warm and nutty. The pantry looked like a bean storm!

Mimi zipped in, whiskers twitching. Zoom buzzed in little zig-zags. Pip fluffed his feathers.

“Oh no!” said Pip.

“My beans,” squeaked Mimi. “I saved those.”

Zoom made a zig-zag circle. “It’s a bean parade,” he buzzed, but his eyes looked worried.

Tiko’s heart felt heavy. He tucked his head into his shell. “I didn’t mean to,” he whispered.

Mimi’s whiskers drooped. “Now there are beans everywhere. I can’t find my special blue bead either. It was on that shelf.”

Tiko took a slow breath. He felt sad. He felt sorry. He felt brave enough to say it. He lifted his head and looked at his friends.

“I made this mess,” said Tiko. “I knocked the jar. I am sorry, Mimi. I am sorry, everyone.”

The pantry was quiet except for the tiny tap of a bean rolling in a corner.

Pip stepped closer. “Thank you for saying sorry,” he chirped. “That is brave.”

Tiko nodded. “I want to make it better,” he said. “Let me help. We can clean together.”

Mimi blinked. “You will help?”

“Yes,” said Tiko. “I will sweep the beans. I will look for your blue bead. I will be careful with the shelves.”

Zoom zipped. “I can nudge beans into piles with my tiny legs,” he buzzed.

Pip puffed. “I can peck beans into a bowl.”

Mimi smiled a little. “I can hold a tiny broom.”

Tiko smiled back. “I will guide. Slow and steady.”

They made a plan. That was their compromise. Tiko would lead the cleaning because he was big and steady. Mimi and Zoom would gather small beans. Pip would carry them in a bowl. Everyone would take turns. Everyone could help.

Tap-tap, zoom-zoom, peck-peck, sweep-sweep. The pantry filled with soft sounds. The beans clicked into the bowl. The floor grew clear. The light stripe on the floor warmed their backs.

Tiko felt strong. He moved the stool carefully and set it far from the shelves. “Stools are for grown-ups,” he said. “Next time I will ask for help or use a safe step.”

“That is wise,” said Pip.

“I like the plan,” said Mimi.

Zoom zipped low. “Bead alert!” he buzzed. “Something blue!”

Tiko lowered his head. Under the shelf, near a tiny puff of flour, he saw a small blue bead. It glowed like a drop of sky. He smiled and nudged it toward Mimi with his nose.

“My bead!” squeaked Mimi. Her whiskers perked up. “Thank you, Tiko!”

“You are welcome,” said Tiko, feeling warm inside. “I am glad we found it.”

When the beans were all gathered, Tiko opened a new jar of beans, one he owned. He poured it gently into a clean jar for Mimi. The beans made a soft waterfall sound. Shhhh. He tied a ribbon around the jar. It was red and bright.

“For you,” he said. “To make amends.”

Mimi held the jar. “You did more than say sorry,” she said. “You made it right. That is kind.”

Pip clapped his wings. “We worked together,” he chirped.

Zoom zig-zagged a happy dance. “Team pantry!”

Tiko looked around. The floor was clean. The shelves were neat. The air smelled like cinnamon again. The pantry felt calm and friendly.

“I learned something,” Tiko said. “Being brave is not climbing a wobbly stool. Being brave is telling the truth, saying sorry, and making amends. It is asking for help and making a plan.”

Mimi nodded. “And we can compromise and share the work.”

Pip added, “We can include everyone. Big jobs feel small when we do them together.”

Tiko’s tummy rumbled again. He laughed softly. “Would you like crackers?” he asked. “We can sit on the rug and share. I will open the box on the floor this time.”

“Yes!” said Pip.

“Yum,” squeaked Mimi.

“Crunch crunch,” buzzed Zoom.

They sat in the stripe of light and ate crunchy crackers. Crinkle, crunch, munch. Tiko poured a tiny tea from a safe, low shelf. It smelled like apples. They sipped and smiled and felt together and warm.

Tiko looked at his friends. “Thank you for forgiving me,” he said.

Mimi patted his shell. “Thank you for making amends.”

In the cozy pantry, with crackers and tea and friends, Tiko felt wise and happy. He knew what to do when mistakes happen: be brave, say sorry, make it better, and include your friends.

The End.
