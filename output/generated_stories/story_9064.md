# Bun-Bun Says, "I Can!"

In a comfortable nursery with soft rugs and sunny windows, a little rabbit named Bun-Bun wriggled his whiskers. The room smelled like clean blankets and warm oatmeal. A mobile of silver stars spun slowly above a cozy crib, and blocks made gentle clacks as tiny paws touched them.

Bun-Bun was a determined rabbit. He liked to try things for himself. He liked to think with his big bunny brain. Today, he wanted to build a tall tower of blocks. He wanted to do it his way.

“Good morning, Bun-Bun,” said Nana Bear, the gentle grown-up who watched the nursery. Her voice was warm like a blanket. “What would you like to do?”

Bun-Bun stood tall on his little toes. “I want to build a tower,” he said. “I can do it.”

“Wonderful,” said Nana Bear. “Use your calm body and your thinking ears.”

Bun-Bun hopped to the block basket. The blocks smelled like pine wood. They felt smooth and cool. He picked a big square and set it on the rug. “One,” he said. He tapped it to make sure it did not wobble. Tap-tap. Steady.

Along came Squeak the mouse, fast and busy. “I will build for you!” Squeak squeaked. He grabbed a triangle block.

Bun-Bun took a soft breath. He put his paw on his chest to feel it rise and fall. He remembered Nana Bear’s words: calm body, thinking ears.

“Thank you, Squeak,” Bun-Bun said in a clear voice. “But I want to try by myself. You can watch.” His voice was kind and firm. His ears stood tall.

Squeak blinked, then smiled. “Okay! I will watch.”

Bun-Bun set a rectangle block on top. He looked left. He looked right. He used his thinking. “Big blocks first,” he said. “Then small ones.” He pressed the corners with a soft push. Press-press. Still steady.

The tower climbed higher. Click. Clack. The blocks made happy sounds as they met. Bun-Bun’s nose twitched with each little victory.

Pip the parrot fluttered down from a perch. “Let me do it! Let me do it!” squawked Pip, flapping bright green wings.

Bun-Bun felt a wiggle in his tummy. He liked friends. He liked help sometimes. But this was his try. He took another gentle breath. In through the nose. Out through the mouth. He put his paw up like a stop sign.

“Pip,” Bun-Bun said, “I am building this tower right now. I can play with you after. You can cheer for me.”

Pip paused, then nodded. “Cheer, cheer! Go, Bun-Bun, go!” He fluffed his feathers and hummed.

Bun-Bun picked a long, thin block. He pressed it on top. The tower wobbled. Wibble-wobble! His tummy felt tight.

Nana Bear walked over, slow and soft. “What does your thinking brain say?” she asked in a calm voice.

Bun-Bun looked at the base. He touched the bottom block. “The bottom must be wide,” he said. “Wide is strong.” He smiled a little. “I can fix it.”

He took the thin block off. He put a big, flat block on the bottom instead. Then he stacked two medium blocks. He used his paw to line up the edges. Straight and even. He listened to the tiny scrape as wood slid on wood. Scritch-scritch. The tower stood taller, and the wobble went away.

“Smart thinking,” said Nana Bear. “You were calm. You used logic.”

Bun-Bun’s ears felt warm with pride. “I did it,” he said softly.

Just then, Squeak zoomed too close. His tail brushed the tower. The top block tilted and fell with a soft thump. Bun-Bun’s eyes went wide. His whiskers drooped.

Squeak squeaked, “Sorry! Sorry!”

Bun-Bun felt tears tickle his eyes. He took a big breath again. He put words to his feeling. “I feel sad,” he said. “I worked hard.”

He looked at Squeak with kind eyes but kept his voice firm. “Please walk slowly near my tower.”

Squeak nodded fast. “I will be slow.”

Bun-Bun wiped his eyes and set the top block back with careful paws. He checked the base. He checked the middle. He checked the top. Tap-tap. Press-press. Still and strong.

Pip sang, “Steady tower, steady tower!”

Bun-Bun put one last block—a little blue square—right on top. It made a tiny click. He stepped back. The tower was as tall as his ears. Sunlight made the colors glow: red, yellow, blue, and green.

“I did it!” Bun-Bun said. He felt proud and brave. His heart felt like a warm drum. Bum-bum, bum-bum.

Nana Bear clapped softly. “You were assertive,” she said. “You said what you needed in a clear, kind voice. You were independent. You tried by yourself. You used logical thinking to solve a wobble.”

Bun-Bun beamed. “I said, ‘I want to try by myself.’ I fixed the base. I asked friends to be careful.”

Squeak lifted a tiny thumbs-up. “You did great, Bun-Bun!”

Pip flapped once. “Now play with me?”

Bun-Bun nodded. “Yes. Now I am ready to play together.” He turned to Squeak and Pip. “Let’s build a small tower for you!”

They made a new tower beside the first one, this time together. Bun-Bun used his strong, clear voice when he needed space, and he listened when his friends spoke. The room filled with happy clacks, soft songs, and the warm smell of afternoon snacks.

Bun-Bun looked at both towers and smiled. “I can say what I need. I can think. I can try,” he said.

And the nursery felt even more comfortable, with friends who understood.

The End.
