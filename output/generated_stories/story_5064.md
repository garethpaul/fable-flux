# The Fawn in the Quiet Guest Room

A gentle deer fawn named Dapple tiptoed into a quiet guest room. The room smelled like fresh soap and clean sheets. Sunlight made soft squares on the floor. The bed was big and fluffy, and a tiny bookshelf stood in the corner. A sign on the door said, “Guest Room. Please keep tidy.”

Dapple’s ears flicked. “This room is special,” she whispered. Her hooves made soft tap-tap sounds on the wooden floor. On the bed lay a small suitcase, neatly closed. Beside it was a little basket with folded towels. Everything looked calm and still.

Dapple saw a round puzzle on the table. It had bright colors and big pieces. “Ooh, a puzzle!” she said. She loved learning. She loved figuring things out. “I can use my brain,” she said proudly. “I can use knowledge.”

She gently nosed open the puzzle box. The pieces smelled like new cardboard. “Let’s learn,” she giggled. She put red with red and blue with blue. Click! The pieces fit. She hummed a happy tune, “Do-do, think it through!”

Just then, her tail brushed the little basket. The towels slipped. Flump! They fell to the floor in a soft white cloud.

Dapple’s eyes grew wide. “Oh no,” she whispered. “This is not my room. These towels are for a guest.” She felt a wiggly worry in her tummy. “I need to make amends. I need to fix this.”

She took a deep breath. “I can use knowledge to help,” Dapple said. She looked around the room. On the wall was a chart with pictures. It showed a bed, a book, a brush, and a towel. Under the towel picture were words: “Fold in thirds.”

Dapple tilted her head. “Fold in thirds… What does that mean?” She thought and thought. She tried once. The towel was lumpy. She tried again. Still lumpy. Her ears drooped.

Then she had an idea. “I can find more knowledge,” she said. She peeked at the little bookshelf. There was a small picture book called “How to Help at Home.” Dapple opened it carefully with her hoof. The paper felt smooth. The pages showed simple steps with big drawings. One page showed a towel: fold side, fold side, smooth, then place.

Dapple smiled. “I can do that!”

She laid a towel flat on the bed. “Side,” she said, folding the first side. “Side,” she said, folding the second side. “Smooth,” she whispered, running her hoof over the edges. She listened to the soft swish, swish. The towel looked neat like in the picture. She folded all the towels the same way. Soon, the basket was tidy again.

Knock, knock. The door opened a little. Kind Mrs. Wren, the housekeeper, peeked in. She wore a blue apron and smelled like lemon and sunshine. “Oh! Hello, Dapple,” she said with a gentle smile. “Are you visiting the guest room?”

Dapple’s cheeks felt warm. “Yes,” she said softly. “I was learning a puzzle, and I bumped the towels. I am sorry. I made a mess. But I used a book and the chart to learn how to fold. I tried to fix it.”

Mrs. Wren stepped in and looked at the basket. “What careful folds,” she said. “You used your knowledge and your heart. That is helpful and respectful.”

Dapple’s ears perked up. “I wanted to make amends,” she said. “I wanted to be helpful.”

Mrs. Wren nodded. “When we make a mistake, we can learn, fix it, and do better. That is smart and kind.”

Dapple swished her tail. “Can I help more?” she asked. “I can use the book.”

“Thank you,” said Mrs. Wren. “Could you please place the towels on the shelf? The book says heavy ones at the bottom.”

Dapple read the page again. “Heavy at the bottom,” she repeated. She gently pressed each towel to feel its weight. “This one is thicker,” she said. She stacked them from heavy to light. The shelf looked safe and neat.

“Wonderful,” said Mrs. Wren. “You were resourceful. You looked for steps. You used your eyes, your hooves, and your brain.”

Dapple beamed. “I like learning,” she said. “Knowledge helps me know what to do.”

Mrs. Wren pointed to the puzzle. “Would you like to finish your puzzle now?”

“Yes, please,” said Dapple. “But first, may I ask? Is it okay to play in the guest room?”

“That is a respectful question,” said Mrs. Wren. “Yes, as long as we keep it tidy and put things back. Asking first shows care for others.”

Dapple nodded. “I will ask next time,” she said. She clicked the last puzzle piece into place. Click! The picture showed a bright sun and a smiling house. It made Dapple feel warm and proud.

Mrs. Wren clapped softly. “You solved the puzzle and the problem,” she said.

Dapple took a happy breath. The room smelled like lemon and clean towels again. The basket was neat. The shelf was safe. The puzzle was complete. Her heart felt light.

Dapple whispered, “When I don’t know, I can learn. When I make a mess, I can fix it. When I help, I show respect.”

“Exactly,” said Mrs. Wren.

The End.
