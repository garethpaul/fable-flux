# Kiki the Calm Koala Solves the Color Puzzle

In a sunny creative art room, brushes stood like tiny trees in jars. Paper smelled crisp and clean. Soft music hummed from a little speaker. Kiki the calm koala took a deep breath. She loved the warm, painty smell and the gentle light.

Kiki’s fur was soft and gray. Her round ears twitched when she listened. She spoke in a slow, kind voice. “Hello, friends,” she said. “I am ready to make art.”

Around the long table sat many friends. Mateo wore a bright poncho. Amina had a scarf with golden stars. Haru had a tiny paper crane on his shirt. Jaya wore bangles that jingled. The teacher, Ms. Rosa, smiled. “Today we make a Friendship Quilt,” she said. “We will paint squares that show our homes, our families, and our favorite songs. Then we will fit them together.”

Everyone clapped. Kiki clapped too, but softly. She felt happy. She also felt a little nervous. The table had one big tray of paints, but the labels were missing. There were many colors, and some looked very close—two reds, two blues, two greens.

Ms. Rosa said, “Please share the paints and brushes. Everyone gets to use the colors they need.”

Kiki dipped a brush into water. Splash, swish. She tapped-tap-tapped it on the cup. She reached for a red, but there were two reds: one bright like a strawberry, one deep like a sunset. She paused. “Which red is which?” she wondered.

Across the table, Amina reached for blue. “I need a blue like the night sky,” she said.

Haru said, “I need a blue like the sea waves.”

They both touched the same little pot. “This one!” Amina said.

“No, this one!” said Haru.

Their hands stopped. The room got quiet. The soft music kept humming, but everyone looked worried. Jaya frowned. “It’s not fair if only one person gets the color.”

Kiki took a slow breath. She put both paws on the table, very calm. “We can solve this,” she said in her gentle voice. “Let’s use thinking steps.”

Ms. Rosa nodded. “Kiki, please lead us.”

Kiki smiled. “Step one: Ask questions.” She turned to Amina. “Amina, what do you mean by ‘night sky’ blue? Is it very dark?”

Amina touched her scarf. “Yes. Deep and calm.”

Kiki turned to Haru. “Haru, what do you mean by ‘sea waves’ blue? Is it bright?”

Haru nodded. “Yes. Like splashes in sunlight.”

Kiki nodded slowly. “Step two: Look for clues.” She opened both blue pots. One blue looked deep and cozy. The other blue looked bright and shiny. She dipped a brush into each and painted two little dots on a scrap paper. “Look,” she said. “This one dries darker. This one stays bright.”

Amina and Haru leaned in. “The darker one is like the night sky,” Amina said.

“The brighter one is like the sea,” Haru said.

Kiki smiled. “Step three: Make a fair plan.” She said, “Amina can use the dark blue first. Haru can use the bright blue first. Then you can switch. While you wait, you can paint other parts.” She pointed to their papers. “There is so much to paint.”

Amina and Haru looked at each other. “Okay!” they said together. Their faces relaxed. Jaya’s bangles jingled as she clapped softly. “That was fair,” she said. “Thank you, Kiki.”

Everyone began to paint again. Swish went the brushes. Tap tap went the cups. The room filled with color. Mateo painted a square with a rainbow bridge from his abuela’s village. Amina painted a crescent moon and tiny twinkling stars. Haru painted blue waves with white foam. Jaya painted bright flowers and a little drum.

Kiki looked at her blank square. She thought about her eucalyptus tree. She thought about the gentle wind and the soft leaves. She also thought about her friends’ art. She wanted her square to show that everyone belongs, and that fair choices help us stay happy.

She took a deep breath and began. She painted a big eucalyptus tree in the center. Then she painted small windows in the trunk. In each window, she painted a tiny picture from her friends—a moon, a wave, a flower, a rainbow bridge. She added a small balance scale at the roots. One side had a heart. The other side had a little paintbrush. The scale was even.

Ms. Rosa walked by. “Kiki, that is beautiful,” she said. “What does it mean?”

Kiki smiled. “It means we all live in the same tree. We share. We use our minds to be fair. Hearts and tools balance.”

At last, Ms. Rosa helped the class put the squares together on a big board. The colors glowed. The quilt told many stories—soft songs, bright dances, quiet prayers, and happy games. It felt like a hug for the whole room.

A small voice piped up. It was Leo, who had been very quiet. “I only had yellow left,” he said. “Is that okay?”

Kiki knelt beside him. “Yellow is sunshine,” she said. “Sunshine touches everyone.”

Everyone nodded. Mateo added, “My village loves yellow marigolds.”

Jaya said, “In my home, yellow means joy.”

Haru said, “And in mine, it means new day.”

Leo’s smile grew. “Then I am part of the quilt too.”

Ms. Rosa clapped. “Class, what did we learn?”

Kiki lifted a paw. “When there is a problem, we can stay calm, ask questions, look for clues, and make a fair plan. That is logical thinking. It helps everyone feel included. It is just and kind.”

The class cheered. The quilt shone like a little sunrise on the wall. Kiki felt warm inside. The art room smelled like paint and hope. She closed her eyes for a moment and listened to the soft music and happy voices.

“Thank you,” Kiki whispered to the room. “We did it. Together.”

The End.
