# Tiko the Turtle Finds a Place for Everything

Tiko was a shy turtle. He liked quiet corners and soft lights. One rainy afternoon, Tiko slid down the steps into the busy basement. The basement was full of sounds and smells. Drip, drip went the pipes. Clink, clank went a toolbox. The air smelled like old books, bread crumbs, and a little bit like paint.

“Hello?” Tiko whispered. His voice was small, like a tiny bell.

“Hi, Tiko!” called out Zara from the bright craft table. She wore a blue scarf with stars from her grandma’s country.

“Hey, Tiko!” said Mateo, who had a map on the wall with stickers from places his family visited.

“Come play drum!” boomed Arjun, tapping a hand drum from his auntie. Boom-ba, boom-ba.

The basement was a busy place. Shelves were full of boxes. Bins were full of blocks, beads, books, and balls. But the bins were mixed up. A train track sat on a puzzle. A sock slept in a snack bowl. A paper fan from Mei’s Spring Festival was under a pile of crayons.

Tiko’s eyes got big. “Oh,” he said. “It looks messy.”

“It is!” said Zara, giggling. “We can’t find anything.”

“I can’t find my drum beater,” Arjun frowned.

“And I can’t find my stamp from Peru,” Mateo said.

Tiko felt his shell get warm. He liked things to have a home. He took a slow breath, like Mama Turtle taught him. In and out. In and out. This helped him feel brave and calm. That was self-regulation.

“I… I have an idea,” Tiko said softly. “Maybe we can… organize?”

“Organize?” Zara tilted her head.

“It means we make places for things,” Tiko said. “So we can find them.”

“How?” asked Mei, holding the paper fan.

Tiko looked around. He listened to the drip, drip, and the boom-ba. He smelled the old books. He touched a smooth marble and a rough cardboard box. He thought.

“First,” Tiko said, “we sort. Let’s make little mountains.”

“Sorting mountains!” Mateo cheered.

They made a mountain of blocks. A mountain of books. A mountain of music. A mountain of art. They put the paper fan in art. They put the drum in music. They put the train track with the vehicles. Tiko hummed a tiny tune as he moved, scrape-scrape along the floor.

Arjun found his drum beater under a scarf. He smiled. “Thank you, mountains!”

“Next,” Tiko said, “we give each mountain a basket or a box.”

They looked at the shelves. There were empty bins, but no labels. Zara waved her scarf. “We can make labels with pictures!”

“Yes!” Tiko said, a little louder now. “Pictures help everyone.”

Mei drew a drum and a shaker for the music bin. Arjun drew a bright red bus for vehicles. Mateo drew a book with a yellow star. Zara drew a glue bottle and a rainbow brush for art. Tiko drew a tiny turtle for “friends”—the plush toys that always fell over.

They colored the labels with crayons that smelled like wax and apples. They taped them on the bins with sticky tape that went rippp.

“Now,” Tiko said, “we put things in their homes.”

Clack went the blocks in the block bin. Swish went scarves in the dress-up bin. Thump went the balls into a basket that went boing when it moved. Everyone laughed.

Mei unfolded the paper fan. “This fan is from my family’s Spring Festival,” she said. “It belongs in art. And we can use it for dance too.”

“That’s cool,” said Mateo. “My stamp from Peru goes in ‘treasures.’ We can make a treasure bin from everywhere.”

Arjun nodded. “From India, I have bells. They can go in music and treasures.”

Zara twirled. “My scarf can go in dress-up and treasures.”

Tiko smiled. “We can share and learn. Our things tell stories.”

They made one more label. Mei drew a little globe with hands around it. The treasure bin was for cultural treasures. They placed the fan, the stamp, the bells, and a small wooden turtle from Tiko’s grandma. Tiko touched it gently. It felt smooth and warm.

Suddenly, a loud crash! A tower of blocks tumbled. Tiko jumped and pulled into his shell. It was noisy. He felt a little scared.

He took another slow breath. In and out. He peeked out. “It’s okay,” he told himself. “Blocks fall. We can rebuild.”

“You okay, Tiko?” Arjun asked.

Tiko nodded. “Yes. Let’s try a rule. One tower at a time. And after we play, we put things back.”

“That’s being assertive,” Zara said kindly. “You said what helps you.”

Tiko felt brave. “Can I make a cleanup song?” he asked.

“Yes!” everyone said.

Tiko sang softly, tapping the floor: “Home, home, find your home. Blocks in blocks, books in books. Music sings in the music box. Treasures rest, no need to roam.”

They all sang. Their voices were different—low, high, fast, slow—but they fit together like puzzle pieces.

Soon the busy basement looked calm. The shelves looked neat. The labels smiled with bright colors. It smelled like crayons and clean wood. It sounded like soft humming.

“Now I can find my drum,” Arjun said.

“And my stamp,” Mateo said.

“And my scarf,” Zara said.

“And my fan,” Mei said.

Tiko blushed. “And I can find my brave.”

They played a song. They danced with the fan and the scarf. They stamped a map with stars. They read a book from the shelf. And when they finished, they followed the cleanup song and put everything home.

Zara wrapped her scarf around Tiko for a second. “Thank you for helping us organize.”

“You showed us a discovery,” Mei said. “We found new places and old treasures.”

“You taught us a plan,” Arjun added. “Sort, label, put away.”

Tiko’s heart felt big and warm. “We did it together,” he said. “When things have homes, we can find joy faster.”

Lesson: Being organized helps everyone share, learn, and play. We can be brave, say what we need, and make room for all our special things from many places in the world.

The End.
