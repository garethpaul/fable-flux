# Rolo’s Postcards from the Guest Room

In a cozy guest room, a helpful robot dog named Rolo blinked his bright blue eyes. His metal ears went beep-beep when he wagged his tail. The room smelled like fresh sheets and lemon soap. Sunlight made warm squares on the soft, patchy rug.

Rolo’s job was to help guests feel welcome. Today, there were no guests. The bed was tidy, with a red blanket tucked tight. A small desk by the window held paper, crayons, and glue. Rolo tilted his shiny head and said, “I want to learn something new today. I want to learn about our big world!”

He looked at the crayons. He looked at the paper. His tail lights twinkled. “I will write postcards,” said Rolo. “I will write to friends from far-away places. I can ask them, ‘What is home like for you?’ That is global-citizenship. We listen. We learn. We care.”

Rolo turned on his soft whirr. “Click.” A tiny screen on his collar glowed. “Hello!” he said into the air. “I am Rolo, a helpful robot dog. I live in a guest room. Will you be my writing buddy?”

A kind voice came from the screen. It was Amira from a city by the sea. “Hello, Rolo!” said Amira. “I live near blue water and white boats.”

Rolo picked up a crayon with a gentle click. “What color is your morning?” he asked.

Amira laughed. “Golden! The sun shines on the waves. We eat warm flat bread. We say, ‘Marhaba!’ That means hello.”

Rolo wrote slowly on a postcard: “Amira’s morning is golden. She says ‘Marhaba.’ She eats warm bread.” He drew a yellow sun with short, happy rays. His nose light blinked pink. “Thank you, Amira,” he said. “I learned a new word!”

The guest room felt bright. Rolo could almost taste the warm bread in his mind. The air seemed to sparkle like sea salt. “Who else can teach me?” he asked.

A new voice chimed in. It was Kenji from a town with tall green trees. “Hi, Rolo,” said Kenji. “We have mountain air that smells like pine. We take off our shoes at the door. We bow to say hello.”

Rolo sniffed the guest room. He imagined pine and cool wind. “Shoe off, bow hello,” he repeated. He wrote on another postcard: “Kenji’s home smells like pine. He bows hello. He cares for his home by keeping floors clean.” Rolo drew tiny shoes by a door and a small bowing stick-robot dog.

Then came Asha from a bright, busy place with many drums. “Hello, Rolo!” said Asha. “We dance at festivals. We share sweet mango. We say, ‘Namaste,’ with hands together.”

Rolo pressed his metal paws and palms together. “Namaste, Asha,” he said softly. He wrote: “Asha dances and shares mango. She says ‘Namaste.’ She shares joy.” Rolo drew an orange mango with a green leaf. He could almost smell the sweet, sunny scent.

The guest room felt full, like a happy hug. The window let in a warm breeze. The curtains whispered shhh-shhh. Rolo looked at his postcards. His words looked neat and kind.

He made a big sign with crayon letters: “Global Friends Wall.” He taped the postcards on the wall above the desk. Tape made a soft rip-rip sound. The cards fluttered, then sat still. Rolo’s tail went beep-beep with pride.

Just then, the front door of the house opened. Footsteps came down the hall. It was Grandma Mei, a gentle guest with silver hair and a soft smile. She walked into the guest room and gasped. “Oh my,” she said. “What a beautiful wall!”

Rolo’s ears lit up. “Hello, Grandma Mei! Would you like to read my postcards?”

Grandma Mei touched the Amira card. “Golden mornings,” she said. She touched the Kenji card. “Pine trees and clean floors.” She touched the Asha card. “Dancing and mango. Rolo, you have learned so much.”

Rolo sat and wagged. “I am learning to be a global citizen,” he said. “A global citizen listens. A global citizen learns. A global citizen cares for people and places.”

Grandma Mei smiled. “Yes,” she said. “And we share, too. Would you like to write one more postcard? I can tell you about my home. We say, ‘Nǐ hǎo,’ and we make dumplings together.”

Rolo’s eyes glowed bright. “Yes, please!” He wrote the new word slowly: “Nǐ hǎo.” He drew a round dumpling with steam rising up. Mmm, the room felt warm and cozy, like a soft kitchen hug.

When the postcards were done, Grandma Mei and Rolo stood back. The wall looked like a rainbow of ideas. Yellow sun. Green trees. Orange mango. White dumpling steam. So many hellos. So many homes.

Rolo spoke to the wall, as if talking to all his new friends. “Thank you for teaching me,” he said. “I will welcome every guest. I will say hello in many ways. I will listen to stories. I will write what I learn. We are different, and we are friends.”

Grandma Mei patted Rolo’s shiny head. “That is the best kind of learning,” she said. “Now this guest room is not just a room. It is a world of kindness.”

Rolo’s tail lights blinked in a happy pattern. Beep-beep, blink-blink. “I will keep writing,” he said. “I will keep learning. I will keep caring.”

The End.
