# Tiko’s Turn on the Balcony Garden

Tiko was a wise tortoise who lived in a cozy clay pot on a sunny balcony garden. The garden smelled like wet leaves and sweet mint. Bees hummed softly. A tiny wind chime sang, ting-ting, when the breeze came by.

Every morning, Tiko peeked over the pot. “Good morning, sun,” he said. “Good morning, plants.” He liked to watch the tomatoes blush red, the basil wave, and the strawberries hide under green hats.

On this day, the children who lived in the apartment came outside. Mia had a bright red watering can. Ben had a small blue one. Their cat, Pepper, curled up and flicked his tail.

“Let’s water the plants!” Mia said.

“I want to water first,” Ben said. He stamped his foot. Thump.

Mia hugged her can. “I got here first.”

Tiko blinked slowly. He felt the warm sun on his shell. He could hear the drip-drop of yesterday’s watering on the railing. He smelled fresh soil. He knew something important: today was a day to learn about fairness.

“Friends,” Tiko said in his soft, calm voice. “Let us take turns.”

“But I want the red can!” Ben cried. His face got hot and scrunchy. “It pours faster.”

Mia frowned. “That’s not fair. It’s my can.”

Pepper lifted his head and said, “Mrrrp?” The wind chime went ting-ting again.

Tiko took a deep breath. In… and out. He showed Mia and Ben. “Smell the flowers,” he said, breathing in. “Blow the wind,” he said, breathing out.

Mia and Ben copied him. In… out. Their faces softened.

Tiko smiled. “Fairness means everyone gets a turn. Fairness means we share chances, not just things.”

Mia tilted her head. “What does that mean?”

“It means,” Tiko said, “we can make a plan. A fair plan. A plan that lets both of you help.”

Ben looked at the red can. “But I still want the fast pour.”

“We can be independent and still be fair,” Tiko said. “Independent means you can do your part by yourself. Fair means you let others do their part, too. Let us make a watering line.”

Mia and Ben listened.

Tiko tapped his shell, thinking. Tap, tap. “First, we count plants. One tomato, two tomato, three tomato. One strawberry, two strawberry. One basil, one mint.” He nodded. “There are eight plants.”

Mia clapped. “I can count to eight!”

“Me too,” Ben said.

“Good,” said Tiko. “Mia waters four plants with the red can. Ben waters four plants with the blue can. We switch after each plant row. The red can does not make you more important. The blue can does not make you less. The plants just need a drink.”

Ben’s cheeks were still pink. He pouted. “What if Mia waters the best plants?”

Tiko chuckled. “All plants are best when they are thirsty.”

Mia looked at Ben. “We can trade after two plants.”

Ben blinked. “We can?”

“That is very fair,” Tiko said. “And very kind.”

They began. Drip, drip, drip. The water smelled cool. The soil made a soft, squishy sound. Pepper followed, sniffing, whiskers twitching.

Mia poured slowly. “I give each plant a little sip,” she said.

Ben poured slowly too. “Me too.” He smiled a tiny smile.

When they reached the strawberries, they traded cans. Mia held the blue can. Ben held the red one. Ben grinned wider. “It is fast,” he said. But he tried to pour gently, like Tiko showed, hand steady and slow. Drip… drip… drip.

The garden looked happy. The basil leaves lifted up. The tomatoes glowed. The mint sent out a minty whisper. The wind chime sang, ting-ting, ting.

Suddenly, a little bird landed on the railing. Chirp! It hopped near the mint.

“Hello, Bird,” Tiko said. “We are being fair today.”

The bird chirped again, as if to say, “Good job.”

Then a small problem came. Ben saw a fat, red tomato. “I want to pick it now!” he said. He reached out fast.

Mia reached too. “I want to pick it!”

Their hands bumped. Plop! The tomato fell into Tiko’s pot.

Pepper jumped. “Mrrrp!”

Ben’s face scrunched again. He felt mad. His tummy felt hot. He made a tiny growl sound.

Tiko looked up and spoke softly. “Anger is a big feeling. It is okay to feel it. Let us cool it down.”

He showed them again: “Smell the flowers.” In. “Blow the wind.” Out.

Mia and Ben breathed with him. In… out. In… out. Their bodies relaxed.

Tiko nodded. “Fairness can help with anger. We can make another plan. Who picked the last strawberry?”

Mia looked at her shoes. “Me.”

Ben nodded. “Then I can pick the tomato,” he said, voice small.

Mia looked at Ben. “Okay. You can pick it. And I can pick the next one.”

“That is fair,” Tiko said. “Thank you for being tolerant, Mia. Thank you for using calm, Ben.”

Ben carefully lifted the tomato from Tiko’s pot. He held it like a treasure. Mia smiled and touched the smooth red skin. They both giggled.

Pepper rubbed against Tiko’s pot. The wind chime sang one more ting-ting.

They sat together and tasted the tomato. It was warm from the sun and sweet like a tiny summer kiss. “Mmm,” Mia said. “We did it together.”

“Yes,” Tiko said. “You were fair. You were kind. You were calm. And you did your parts all by yourselves.”

Mia and Ben nodded. Their eyes were bright. The garden seemed brighter too.

Tiko tucked into his shell, cozy and proud. “Fairness helps everyone grow,” he whispered. “Plants, friends, and even wise tortoises.”

The End.
