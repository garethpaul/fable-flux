# Rocco’s Attic Band

Rocco was a musical raccoon with soft paws and a striped tail. He loved to make music in a quiet attic. The attic smelled like old books and warm wood. Dust danced in the sunlight, like tiny stars. Rocco tapped a tin can drum. Tap-tap! He strummed a rubber-band guitar. Twang-twang! He shook a jar with buttons. Shaka-shaka!

“Hello, attic!” said Rocco. “Are you ready for a song?”

The attic did not talk, but it held many sounds. A window creaked. Creak. The floorboards hummed. Mmmm. The wind gave a whisper. Whooosh.

Rocco smiled. “I can make a band,” he said. “I can make a happy song for everyone.”

But there was a problem. The attic was quiet for a reason. Down below, Mrs. Finch, the librarian, was reading stories to little kids. Rocco heard her soft voice float up through the floor. “Once upon a time…”

Rocco stopped tapping. “Oh no,” he whispered. “I want to play music, but I don’t want to disturb the story. What should I do?”

He sat on a cozy blanket and thought. He took a deep breath. He listened to the soft sounds. Tap-tap? No. Twang-twang? Not yet.

“I will use my creativity,” Rocco said. “I can solve this.”

He looked around the attic and found a fluff of wool. He wrapped the drumsticks with the wool. “Soft drumsticks!” he said. He found a scarf and tied it around the tin can. “Quiet drum!” He stretched the rubber band over a box with a cloth on it. “Soft guitar.” He put a lid on the button jar. “Gentle shaker.”

Rocco tried a tiny beat. Tap… tap… twiiing… shh-shh. The sounds were soft like a whisper. He smiled.

Still, Rocco worried. “Maybe even soft music is not okay right now,” he said. “I should ask. I can practice kindness. I can practice negotiation.”

Rocco tiptoed down the stairs. The air smelled like crayons and tea. Mrs. Finch looked up with warm eyes. The children sat on a colorful rug.

“Excuse me,” Rocco said in a small voice. “I am Rocco. I love music. I want to help. I can volunteer my music for story time. But I do not want to be too loud. Can we make a plan?”

Mrs. Finch smiled. “Hello, Rocco. Thank you for asking. That is very kind. We are reading a quiet part. Can you play very soft sounds after each page? Like a little breeze?”

Rocco nodded. “Yes! Soft like a breeze.”

A little child raised a hand. “Can you make a twinkle sound for the stars?”

“I can try,” said Rocco. “Twinkle-twinkle!”

Mrs. Finch said, “Let’s make an agreement. You play soft music only after I turn a page. If it is too loud, we will stop and practice again. Is that okay?”

Rocco took a deep breath. “Yes. That is fair. Thank you.”

They shook on it, paw to hand. This was their negotiation, their plan.

Rocco hurried back to the attic. He felt proud and calm. He practiced softly. Tap… tap… twiiing… shh-shh. He listened and fixed his sounds. He did it again. Tap… tap… twiiing… shh-shh. Practice made his paws steady and gentle.

When story time began again, Rocco listened through the floor. Mrs. Finch read, “The stars came out at night.” She turned the page. Rocco played a soft twinkle: ting-ting-ting, like tiny bells. The children gasped. “Ooooh!”

Mrs. Finch read, “The wind sang a lullaby.” Page turn. Rocco played a whisper beat: shh-shh, whooosh. The children swayed. “So cozy,” one child said.

But then Rocco got excited. He played a big tap. TAP! The floorboard squeaked. Eeeek!

Rocco froze. “Oh no,” he whispered. He stopped and took a slow breath. In the quiet, he heard Mrs. Finch say, “Let’s try again.”

Rocco nodded to himself. “I can fix it. I will practice again. Soft and gentle.” He wrapped an extra cloth around his drum. He held his sticks like feathers.

Page turn. Tap… like a tiny raindrop. Twiiing… like a long smile. Shh-shh… like kitten paws. The children smiled. Mrs. Finch gave a thumbs up.

At the end, Mrs. Finch said, “Thank you, Rocco. You helped us feel the story.”

The children clapped soft claps. Pat-pat-pat. Rocco peeked down the stairs. “I am happy I could help,” he said. “I like to volunteer my music.”

“Would you like to join us every week?” asked Mrs. Finch. “We can plan the sounds together.”

“Yes!” said Rocco. “We can be a team. We can create new sounds for each page. We can practice before we play. And we can listen to each other.”

Rocco went back to the attic. The sunlight was warm on his fur. He felt proud, and he felt calm. He made a tiny song for himself. Tap… twiiing… shh-shh… whooosh. It was a thank-you song.

“Creativity helped me,” Rocco said. “I asked. I listened. I practiced. I helped. That is the music of kindness.”

And the attic, quiet and happy, seemed to hum back, Mmmm.

The End.
