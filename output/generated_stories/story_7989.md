# Whiskers Fixes It

Whiskers was a clever cat with bright green eyes and whiskers that twitched when he thought. He loved to help in the garage workshop. The garage smelled like fresh wood and orange soap. Sunlight made warm stripes on the dusty floor. Tools hung on the wall: shiny hammers, tiny screws, and a big blue toolbox that clicked when it closed.

“Good morning, Whiskers,” said Nana June, tying on her polka-dot apron. “Today we will build a little birdhouse. We must listen, be safe, and clean as we go.”

Whiskers purred. “I can help! I know everything,” he said proudly. His tail swished with excitement.

Nana June smiled. “We listen first. Ears open, mouth quiet.” She tapped her ear. Tap, tap.

Whiskers nodded, but he was already bouncing. He loved to do things quickly. He loved to do things his way.

Nana June set out the wooden pieces. Tap, tap, tap went the little nails. “We use the small hammer for small nails,” she said. “And we wash our paws before we start.”

But Whiskers wrinkled his nose. “I don’t need a wash. My paws are fine!” He grabbed the big hammer with both paws. It felt heavy and exciting. Clonk! Clonk! The hammer sounded loud. The birdhouse roof wobbled.

“Whiskers,” Nana June said gently. “Please listen. Small hammer.”

Whiskers flicked his ears. “I can do it,” he said, clonk, clonk. He didn’t look at the small hammer at all.

Suddenly, the big hammer slipped. Clonk! It bonked the blue paint jar. The lid popped off. Plop! Blue paint splashed everywhere. It splashed on the wood, on the floor, and on Whiskers’ paws. Splat! Splot! His paws were sticky and blue.

Whiskers froze. His tail drooped. “Oh no,” he whispered. The garage smelled like paint now, sharp and wet. His paws felt cold and gooey. He saw a tiny trail of blue paw prints across the floor.

Nana June sighed softly. “Oh, Whiskers.” She did not shout. “Let’s stop. We will clean first. Clean paws, clean space.”

Whiskers blinked and felt his chest wobble. “I didn’t listen,” he said in a small voice. “I’m sorry, Nana June.”

“Thank you for saying sorry,” Nana June said kindly. “Saying sorry is the first step. Now, let’s make amends. That means we fix what we can.”

“How do I make amends?” Whiskers asked, sniffing. The paint smelled strong, like the ocean in a can.

“We wash up, we wipe up, and we try again,” Nana June said. She turned on the sink. Whoosh, whoosh! Warm water rushed out. She pumped orange soap into Whiskers’ paws. Squish, squish. Bubbles grew like soft clouds and smelled bright and clean.

Whiskers rubbed his paws. “Squishy bubbles!” he giggled. “This feels nice.” The blue paint swirled away, down, down the drain. He dried his paws on a towel with yellow stripes. Rub, rub, rub.

Next, they cleaned the floor. Shh, shh went the sponge. Swipe, wipe, wipe. The blue dots disappeared. Whiskers took a deep breath. He felt lighter.

“I made a mess,” he said, looking up. “I will help fix it. That is making amends.”

“That’s right,” Nana June said. “And we listen next time.”

Whiskers nodded. “Ears open, mouth quiet,” he said, tapping his ear. Tap, tap.

Nana June pointed to the small hammer. Whiskers picked it up. It felt just right in his paws. “Small hammer for small nails,” he repeated. Tap, tap, tap. The sound was soft and tidy. The roof sat straight. The wood smelled like a fresh pencil.

“Good listening,” Nana June said. “Would you like to add a perch?” She held up a little dowel rod.

“Yes, please,” Whiskers said. He dabbed a tiny bit of glue, then pressed the rod in place. He waited. “We wait and watch,” he whispered. “Patience.”

Nana June smiled. “And we build with care.”

When the glue dried, it was time to paint again. Nana June set the paint jars far from the hammer. “We keep tools tidy,” she said. “And we wash before we touch paint.”

Whiskers washed his paws again. Squish, squish. He liked the orange smell. He put on a tiny apron Nana June had made just for him. It had a little fish on the front. His eyes shone.

They painted the birdhouse sky blue. Swish, swish went the brush. The wood turned smooth and bright. Whiskers added little white dots. “Snow for the birds,” he giggled. He listened when Nana June said, “Only a little paint on the brush.” He wiped the extra on the tray. Drip, drip, not splat!

Soon the birdhouse was done. It gleamed in the sunlight. Whiskers’ whiskers twitched with pride.

“Nana June,” he said, “thank you for helping me make amends.”

“You did the work,” she said, giving him a gentle pat. “You said sorry. You cleaned up. You listened. You tried again.”

Whiskers purred. “I learned: When I make a mistake, I can say sorry and help fix it. I can listen with open ears. I can keep my paws clean and my tools tidy.”

They hung the birdhouse on a low branch outside. A breeze fluttered by, soft and sweet. A tiny bird chirped, “Peep!” as if to say thank you.

Whiskers stood tall. He felt calm, kind, and clever.

The End.
