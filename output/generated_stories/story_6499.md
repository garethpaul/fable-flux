# The Song in the Quiet Attic

In a quiet attic, a little singing bird named Pip lived by a round window. Sunlight made warm spots on the wooden floor. Dust sparkled like tiny stars. The air smelled like old books and pine. Pip loved to sing in the morning. He fluffed his soft feathers and chirped, “Tweet-tee-tee! Ta-da-dee!”

One morning, Pip started a big new song. “Laa-laa-laaa!” he sang. His voice was bright and sweet. But then, a small thump came from a cardboard box.

“Shh!” said a tiny voice. “I am sleeping!”

Pip blinked. “Who said that?”

A little brown mouse peeked out. Her name was Mima. She had bright eyes and a tiny pink nose. “I did,” she squeaked. “Your song is too loud. My ears are tired. I worked all night. I need rest.”

Pip’s chest felt tight. He loved to sing. Singing made his heart feel big and happy. He puffed his feathers. “But singing is my favorite,” he said. “This is my home too.”

Mima crossed her tiny paws. “I understand,” she said, yawning. “But I need quiet. I get cranky when I cannot sleep.”

Pip felt a rush of hot feelings. He fluttered to the window and looked at the sky. He wanted to sing more. He felt frustrated. His tail feathers twitched. “What can I do?” he whispered to himself. He took a deep breath. In and out. Like wind under wings. It helped a little.

Down below, an old clock ticked. Tick. Tock. Tick. Tock. The attic was still. Pip listened to the sound and felt his body calm. He hummed softly, “Hmmmmm,” like a bee, not a trumpet. The quiet hum felt kind.

Pip flew to Mima’s box and spoke gently. “Mima, I hear that you need sleep,” he said. “I feel frustrated because I want to sing. Maybe we can make a plan. Can we try a compromise?”

Mima tilted her head. “Compromise?” she asked.

“It means we both give a little,” Pip said. “We both get a little. We both feel okay.”

Mima thought. She rubbed her nose. “Maybe,” she said. “Can you sing soft in the morning and sing loud later?”

Pip hopped on a beam. “I can sing soft like a whisper in the early morning,” he said. “I can also sing by the window, so the sound goes outside. And I can save my big songs for when the big clock says noon.”

Mima’s whiskers wiggled. “That could work,” she said. “I can nap in the morning. At noon, I will be awake. I can even clap my tiny paws for your big song.”

Pip smiled. His eyes shone. “We have a plan!” he chirped. “Thank you for helping.”

Mima smiled back. “Thank you for listening.”

Pip began a soft morning song. It was gentle, like a cloud. “Teet, teet, teet,” he sang. The sound moved out the round window and into the trees. A breeze came in, cool and sweet. It smelled like leaves. The attic stayed quiet and kind. Mima curled up on a soft rag and slept. Her tiny breaths went in and out, in and out, like the clock.

As the sun climbed higher, Pip felt his big song waiting inside. He felt the push again. He remembered his breath. In and out. He looked at the clock. Almost noon. He waited and hummed, “Hmmmm,” to keep calm. Waiting was hard, but he could do it.

While he waited, Pip noticed a drip, drip, drip from the roof into a rusty tin. “Oh no,” he said. “Water is dripping. We must not waste water.”

He fluttered over. He found three clean jars in a box. He set them under the drip. Plink. Plink. Plink. The jars filled with clear drops. Pip felt proud. “I am saving water,” he said. “We can use it to water the attic fern.”

Mima woke and stretched. “Good idea!” she said. “That is conservation. We are caring for our home.”

The clock struck twelve. Ding! Ding! Ding! Mima clapped her tiny paws. “Ready, Pip!”

Pip opened his beak. Out came his big, bright song. “La-la-laa! Tee-ree-ree! Ta-da-dee!” The sound was full and happy. It filled the attic and slipped out the window. Sunbeams danced. Mima swayed and smiled.

When the song ended, Pip’s feathers lay smooth. His heart felt light. “Thank you for waiting with me,” he said.

Mima nodded. “Thank you for making a compromise. You were patient when you felt frustrated. You took deep breaths. You found a good plan.”

Pip and Mima carried one jar at a time to the fern. Plip, plip, plip went the water onto the green leaves. The fern looked fresh and bright. “We shared the attic,” Pip said. “We saved water. We helped the plant.”

Mima giggled. “We were resourceful,” she said. “We used what we had.”

Pip chirped softly, “We can do it again tomorrow.”

“Yes,” Mima said. “Soft in the morning. Big at noon. And we save water when it drips.”

Pip and Mima bumped noses and paws. They both felt safe and happy in the quiet attic. They had learned that listening, breathing, and sharing made room for both of them.

And that is how a singing bird and a little mouse learned the power of compromise, the calm of coping with frustration, and the joy of caring for their home.

The End.
