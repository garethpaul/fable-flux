# Bunny's Balcony Plan

Benny was a small, determined rabbit with soft gray fur and bright, curious eyes. He lived with Nana on a sunny balcony high above the busy street. The balcony garden was Benny’s favorite place. It smelled like mint and warm soil. The wind tickled the leaves. Bees hummed gently, and tiny bells on the railing jingled.

One morning, Benny stretched his ears and whispered, “Today, I will make a Garden Art Show!”

He hopped to the edge and looked at the pots. He saw red tomatoes, tiny carrots in a box, and flowers that looked like little suns. He felt a flutter in his tummy. “I can do it,” he said. “I am determined!”

Nana came out with a watering can. “Good morning, Benny,” she said. “What is your plan today?”

Benny blinked. “Um… I want to make art in the garden. A show! But… I don’t have a plan yet.”

Nana smiled. “Planning helps dreams grow, just like water helps plants grow.”

Benny’s whiskers wiggled. “Let’s plan!” he said.

They sat at the tiny table. The table was cool and smooth. Benny could smell fresh basil. Nana put a notepad down. “First, we make a list,” she said. “What do we need?”

Benny tapped the pencil. “We need things to make art,” he said. “We need a place to show it. We need signs so friends know where to go.”

“And we need time,” Nana added. “Let’s plan the steps.”

Benny drew big simple boxes. Step 1: Collect safe, clean things for art. Step 2: Make the art. Step 3: Set up the show. Step 4: Invite friends. Step 5: Clean up.

Benny grinned. “I can do that!”

He hopped to the recycling bin by the door. It smelled a little like orange peels. “Recycling can be art!” he said. He found a smooth pizza box lid, a shiny soda can, and some colorful paper scraps. Nana helped wash the can, and they dried it with a soft towel.

“Honesty time,” Benny said, holding up a child-size glue stick he found in a drawer. “This is yours, Nana. May I use it?”

Nana nodded. “Thank you for asking and telling the truth, Benny. Yes, you may.”

Benny felt proud. His ears stood tall.

On the warm floor, he began to plan again. “I will make a wind chime from this can,” he said. “I will make a picture on the pizza box. I will make a sign that says ‘Art Show.’”

He sorted the pieces into neat piles. The sun made little sparkles on the can. The mint leaves smelled sweet and cool.

Benny poked holes in the can with Nana’s help. “Ping!” said the can. He tied old buttons with string, and they made soft clinks. “Listen,” Benny whispered. The wind tugged, and the buttons sang, “Ting-ting-ting.”

Next, he made a collage on the pizza box lid. He planned the picture first. “A garden bunny!” he said. He placed green scraps for grass, a cotton ball tail, and a round brown button nose. He glued each piece with careful little pats. “Pat, pat, pat.” He hummed while he worked.

Benny made a sign with big friendly letters: “GARDEN ART SHOW.” He drew a smiley sun. He planned where to hang it on the railing so friends could see.

“Time to set up,” Nana said.

Benny looked around. “I will put the chime by the beans. I will put the picture near the carrots.” He checked his list. Step 1, done. Step 2, done. Step 3, setting up now!

Just then, a little voice called from the next balcony. It was Pip, a pigeon with shiny feathers. “Coo! What are you doing, Benny?”

“I planned a Garden Art Show,” Benny said. “Would you like to come?”

“Coo! Yes!” Pip flapped happily.

Benny gulped. He remembered the tomatoes. He had taken one to make red paint, but he had not asked. His paws felt heavy. “Nana,” he said softly, “I need to be honest. I squeezed a tomato to make paint. I did not ask.”

Nana crouched down. Her eyes were kind. “Thank you for telling me, Benny. That was brave. Next time, we plan for paint and ask first.”

Benny nodded. “I’m sorry. Next time, I will ask. Can I use beet water instead?”

Nana smiled. “Yes. Let’s use beet water and a tiny brush.”

They mixed beet water in a little jar. It smelled earthy. Benny painted rosy dots on the collage. “Dot, dot, dot!”

Soon, more friends peeked in. A ladybug landed on the railing. A neighbor child, Mia, waved from her balcony. “Can I see?” she asked.

“Yes!” Benny said. He led them around. “Here is the wind chime. Listen.” The buttons sang. “Here is my collage bunny.” The cotton tail looked fluffy. “Here is the sign I planned and made.”

Mia clapped. “It’s beautiful! How did you do it?”

Benny lifted his list. “I planned the steps. I used clean recycling. I asked before using things. I was honest when I forgot.”

Pip cooed, “Coo-ool!”

They all laughed. Nana brought cups of cool water with mint leaves. It tasted fresh and sweet.

When the show was over, Benny checked his list again. Step 4, done. Step 5: Clean up. He gathered scraps into the bin. He wiped the table. He put the glue away. “Clean and tidy,” he said.

Nana hugged him gently. “You made art. You told the truth. You cared for the garden. Your plan helped you do it.”

Benny’s heart felt light. The evening breeze made the chime sing one more time. “Ting-ting-ting.”

He smiled at his friends. “Planning makes big dreams small and easy,” he said. “And honesty keeps our hearts happy.”

Everyone nodded. The sun slid low, warm and orange, and the garden smelled like basil and good work.

The End.
