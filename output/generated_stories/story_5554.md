# Benny Bear Finds His Sound

In the musical practice room, soft lights glowed on shiny drums and bright xylophones. The room smelled like fresh wood and lemon polish. Benny, a friendly bear cub, stood on his tiptoes and touched a tiny triangle. “Ting!” it sang.

Benny’s ears perked up. “I want to play a song for my friends,” he said. “I want it to sound happy and brave!”

His teacher, Mr. Finch, a gentle bird with a warm scarf, nodded. “You can do it, Benny. Try your ideas. Listen with your heart.”

Benny took a deep breath. He tried the drum. Boom. Boom-boom. His paws felt the bouncy beat. He smiled. “This is fun!”

He tried the xylophone. Plink-plonk-plunk. The colors on the keys were red, orange, yellow. Benny saw the colors and heard the sounds. The room filled with tiny rainbow notes.

But when Benny tried to put the sounds together, the drum and the xylophone did not match. Boom-plink-plonk-boom—CRASH! The mallet slipped. The notes jumbled. Benny’s shoulders drooped. “Oh no,” he sighed. “My song sounds messy.”

From the corner, he heard quick, smooth notes. Zip-zip-zip! It was Riley Raccoon practicing a fast song. Benny’s eyes grew wide. “Riley’s song is perfect,” he whispered. “Maybe I should copy it.”

He lifted his mallet and started to make the same sounds. Zip-zip—clang! His paws stopped. Benny felt a tug in his tummy. It was his honesty feeling. He shook his head. “That’s Riley’s song. It’s not my song. I want my own sound.”

Mr. Finch fluttered closer. “What did you notice, Benny?”

Benny rubbed his paws. “Riley’s song is good. I thought I could copy it. But it didn’t feel right. I want to make my own song. I just don’t know how.”

“That is integrity,” said Mr. Finch softly. “You listened to your inside voice. Now let’s use your brain and your heart. What kind of story do you want your song to tell?”

Benny closed his eyes. He smelled the lemon polish. He felt the smooth drum skin. He heard the tiny hum of the lights. In his mind, he saw a tall green tree, a blue stream, and a bright morning. “My song is about a brave morning walk,” he said. “The sun peeks. The water splashes. My paws go step, step, step.”

“Great thinking,” said Mr. Finch. “Let’s match sounds to your story.”

Benny tapped the triangle for the sun. Ting… ting… gentle and bright. He nodded. “Sun sounds.”

He used soft fingers on the drum for paws. Tap… tap-tap. Like walking on a forest floor. He grinned. “Paw steps!”

He slid the xylophone mallet from low to high for water. Wooooo—plink! Like a splash and a sparkle. His eyes shone. “Water!”

He tried the parts together. Ting… tap-tap… woooo—plink. It was better, but the middle felt empty, like a missing leaf.

Benny frowned and thought hard. “What could fill the middle?” He looked around the room. He saw two wooden sticks in a basket. Click-clack. He tried them. The sticks made a bright, friendly sound. Click… clack… click-clack. He smiled. “Bird steps!”

Riley Raccoon peeked over. “Hey, Benny,” Riley said. “That sounds cool! How did you think of that?”

Benny puffed his chest a little, then paused. He wanted to say, “I made it fast!” But he remembered his honesty feeling. “I tried Riley’s song first,” Benny said. “It didn’t feel right. So I made my own plan. I thought of a story and matched sounds to it.”

Riley nodded. “That’s smart!”

Benny tried again. He counted softly, “One, two, ready, go.” Ting… tap-tap… click-clack… woooo—plink! The sounds lined up like friends holding hands. The song flowed like a sunny stream.

He played it once more, a little louder. Ting… tap-tap… click-clack… woooo—plink! The room seemed to smile. Mr. Finch clapped his wings. “Beautiful, Benny! You used creativity to make your own sound. You used critical thinking to solve the missing-middle problem. And you showed integrity by not copying.”

Benny felt warm and proud. “I didn’t give up,” he said. “I kept trying.”

Mr. Finch nodded. “That is resilience.”

Benny looked at Riley. “Do you want to add a soft shaker for wind?” he asked.

Riley shook the little shaker. Shhhhhh. It whispered like leaves. Benny listened and giggled. “Yes! The wind says hello.”

Together they played: Ting… tap-tap… click-clack… woooo—plink… shhhhhh. The song sounded happy and brave, just like Benny wanted.

Benny turned to his friends who had gathered to listen. “You can make your own sound too,” he said. “Think of a story. Find the sounds. Be honest. Keep trying.”

His friends cheered. Benny’s heart felt big and bright, like the triangle’s ting.

The End.
