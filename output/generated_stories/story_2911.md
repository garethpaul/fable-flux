# Penny Penguin and the Helpful Shed

Penny was a cheerful penguin with bright eyes and a warm scarf. One sunny morning, she waddled to the garden shed at the edge of the village park. The shed smelled like fresh wood and clean soil. The door was a little squeaky. Squeeeak! The sun made a golden square on the floor, and tiny dust sparkles danced in the light.

Inside the shed, there were rakes, little shovels, seed packets, and a big box of paper and markers. The community used the shed to care for the park. Today, Penny wanted to help. She also loved to write. She liked to write simple notes and kind signs to help others know what to do.

“Hello, shed!” Penny chirped. “I am here to help!”

Her friend, Mia Mouse, peeked out from a flower pot. “Hi, Penny! We need signs so everyone knows where things go,” Mia said. “Can you write them?”

Penny clapped her flippers. “Yes! I can write helpful signs.”

She sat at a little wooden table. The table felt smooth under her flippers. She opened a marker. Sniff, sniff. It smelled like berries. Penny smiled and began to write in big, clear letters: RAKES, SHOVELS, GLOVES, SEEDS. She drew small pictures too, like a tiny rake with smiley teeth.

Just then, Toby Toad hopped in. “Boing, boing! I want the blue marker!” he croaked, reaching across the table.

Penny pulled her flippers close to her chest. Her tummy felt tight. The marker was in her hand. “Um, I am using this one,” she said softly.

Toby grabbed for it again. Penny’s heart thumped. The blue marker slipped and made a big scribble across her SEEDS sign.

“Oh no,” said Mia Mouse. “We need clear signs.”

Penny took a deep breath. In through the beak, out slow. She remembered her mom’s gentle words: “Your body and your work are important. You can use your words to say stop.”

Penny looked at Toby. “Toby,” she said in a calm, brave voice, “Please stop grabbing. This is my body and my marker. I don’t like it when you grab. You can ask first.”

Toby blinked. “I was excited,” he croaked. “I didn’t mean to make you upset.”

“It’s okay to be excited,” Penny said. “But I need a bubble of space. My body is mine. My tools are mine right now. You can say, ‘May I use it when you’re done?’”

Toby nodded slowly. “May I use it when you’re done?”

Penny smiled. Her tummy felt soft again. “Yes. When I finish this sign, I will share.”

Mia Mouse clapped her tiny paws. “Good using your words, Penny! Good listening, Toby!”

Penny made a new SEEDS sign. She drew a happy seed with little sprout arms. She taped it up. Rrip! The tape made a funny sound. She wrote more signs: KEEP DOOR CLOSED, PLEASE ASK BEFORE TAKING TOOLS, and THANK YOU FOR HELPING THE PARK. She added a special sign by the table: PLEASE ASK BEFORE TOUCHING MY WORK.

Soon, Mr. Owl floated in, quiet and wise. “Hoo-hoo! What tidy signs,” he said. His feathers rustled like paper. “Penny, may I borrow a marker to sign today’s list?”

Penny smiled. “Yes, Mr. Owl, thank you for asking. Here you go.” She passed him the green marker.

Toby waited, hands behind his back. “May I have the blue marker now?” he asked.

“Yes,” Penny said. “Thank you for waiting.” She gave him the blue marker. Toby grinned and wrote a tiny note: I WILL PUT THINGS BACK. He drew a little toad stamp at the end.

As the day went on, neighbors came to the shed. A breeze smelled like mint from the garden. Bees buzzed softly outside. Lily Lamb came to get gloves. “May I take the small gloves?” she asked.

“Yes,” Penny said. “Please put them back when you’re done.”

Ben Bunny hopped to the shovels. “May I pick a shovel?” he asked.

“Sure,” said Penny. “Please use the small one if you are small.”

Everyone spoke kindly. Everyone asked first. The shed felt calm and happy. The tools went to the right places. The park looked cleaner and greener.

Later, Toby bounced over. “Penny, I am sorry I grabbed your marker,” he said. “I will remember to ask. Your signs help me remember.”

Penny nodded. “Thank you, Toby. I like it when you ask. It helps me feel safe.”

Mia Mouse smiled. “Penny, your writing helped the whole community. You used your words to set a boundary, and you used your writing to help everyone.”

Penny felt warm and proud. She wrote one last sign in big letters: KIND WORDS, KIND HANDS. She drew a little heart with a penguin and a toad inside, with a bubble of space around them.

Before leaving, Penny looked around the shed. Everything was tidy. The door creaked shut. Squeeeak, click. The garden smelled sweet. The sky was pink like strawberry milk.

Penny waddled home, humming. “My body is mine. My tools are mine. I can say stop. I can ask first. I can help my community with my writing.”

And in the quiet evening, the whole park felt safe, cared for, and full of kind voices.

The End.
