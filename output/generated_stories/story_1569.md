# Roary’s Calm-Down Click

Every morning, the home office smelled like warm paper and lemon cleaner. The big desk hummed softly from the computer. A brave little lion cub named Roary padded in on soft paws. His tail flicked with energy.

“Good morning, Roary,” said Mama Lion, tapping the keyboard. Click, click. “It’s work time for me and play time for you.”

Roary nodded. He knew the routine. First, quiet play on the rug. Next, snack. Then, story time. The routine felt safe, like a cozy blanket. He set out his blocks and his blue toy truck. The rug felt fluffy under his paws.

“I’m building a tall tower,” Roary whispered to himself. He stacked one block, then another. Clack. Clack. He added a red block on top. His whiskers twitched with pride.

Mama Lion answered a call. “Hello! Yes, I’m here,” she said gently. Her voice was calm like a soft drum.

Roary’s friend, Tiko the tiny turtle, scooted from his little basket by the bookshelf. “Hi, Roary,” Tiko said in a small, squeaky voice. “Can I play with the truck?”

Roary’s ears perked. He hugged the truck close. “I’m using it,” he said. His chest felt tight.

“Maybe we can share?” Tiko asked, blinking slowly.

Roary looked at his tower. He wanted to keep everything just the way he liked it. He put the truck down for a second to reach for another block. Tiko touched the truck gently.

“Hey!” Roary roared—just a little roar. The sound bounced off the books and the window. His paws felt hot. His face felt hot. Inside, a storm was starting.

Mama Lion looked up and met Roary’s eyes. She put a paw on her heart and took a slow breath. In. Out. Roary watched her chest move like a calm wave. She did it again and pointed to a little sign on the wall with pictures: a lion breathing, a lion counting, a lion squeezing a soft ball.

“Roary,” Mama whispered kindly, “use your calm-down clicks.”

Roary knew the routine. He tapped his claws softly together. Click, click, click. He breathed in through his nose. He breathed out through his mouth. “In… out,” he said in a tiny voice. The warm storm inside started to cool. His paws felt less hot.

“I feel mad,” Roary said. Saying it out loud made the room feel lighter.

Mama nodded. “Thank you for telling me. Do you want the glitter jar?” She held up a jar filled with blue water and shiny stars.

Roary nodded. He shook the jar. Swish! The glitter swirled like tiny fish. He watched it slowly fall. His shoulders lowered. His roar had turned back into a purr.

Tiko pulled the truck back a little. “I’m sorry, Roary. I didn’t mean to grab.”

Roary looked at Tiko’s careful eyes. “I didn’t like it,” he said, “but I can share.” He tapped his calm-down clicks again. Click, click. “Let’s take turns. I will build three blocks. Then you can drive the truck around the tower two times.”

Tiko smiled. “Okay! I can wait.”

Roary stacked three blocks. Clack. Clack. Clack. The tower was taller than his nose now. “Your turn,” he said, pushing the truck to Tiko. The wheels whirred softly against the rug. Whirr… whirr…

Tiko drove around the tower. “Beep-beep,” he said, giggling. Roary giggled too. His tail swished in a happy S-shape.

Mama Lion gave a thumbs-up and whispered, “You used your tools. I’m proud of you.”

After a while, the computer dinged. Ding! “Snack time,” Mama said. Roary and Tiko washed their paws with warm, soapy water in a small bowl. The bubbles popped. Pop! Pop! Pop! They sat by the sunny window and crunched apple slices. Crunch. Crunch.

“I was mad,” Roary said, nibbling carefully. “My tummy felt tight. I did my calm-down clicks.”

Tiko nodded. “I waited my turn,” he said. “We shared.”

After snack, it was story time. Mama read with a low, smooth voice. “Once upon a time…” Roary curled up, his fur warm from the sun. The home office felt peaceful. The printer purred. The curtains swished.

When the story ended, Roary looked at the sign again: Breathe. Count. Click. Share. He traced the pictures with his paw.

He turned to Tiko. “Do you want to build a bridge together?” he asked.

“Yes!” Tiko cheered.

Roary pushed a few blocks to Tiko. “You can start,” he said. His chest felt big and brave. He had been a strong lion, not with a loud roar, but with calm and kind choices.

They built the bridge side by side. When Tiko’s block wobbled, Roary held it steady. When Roary needed the blue block, Tiko passed it over without waiting. They worked in a happy rhythm, like humming bees.

Mama Lion smiled and whispered, “What good teamwork.”

As the day went on, Roary followed the routine: play, snack, story, clean-up. Each time he felt a little grumble in his belly, he remembered: click, breathe, share. The home office stayed cozy and bright.

That evening, Roary patted the glitter jar. “You helped me,” he said. He looked at Tiko and Mama. “When I feel mad, I can slow down. I can use my calm-down clicks. I can share.”

Mama hugged him. Her fur smelled like sunshine and paper. “That’s a brave lion,” she said.

Roary purred and smiled. “A brave, calm lion.”

The End.
