# Mabel Mouse Makes a Plan

Mabel was a kind little mouse with soft gray fur and bright, curious eyes. She lived in a busy basement. The basement hummed and buzzed all day. The washing machine rumbled. The pipes dripped, plink, plink. Boxes were stacked like tall brown mountains, and old brushes and paints sat on a shelf that smelled like lemons and wood.

Mabel liked to make art. She used tiny bits of thread, shiny buttons, and soft paper scraps. She painted with a teeny brush made from a feather. She hummed as she worked. “La, la, la,” she sang. “Art makes my heart feel warm.”

One morning, Mabel found a long cardboard strip under a box. It was smooth and perfect. “I can make a big picture,” she said. “I will make a rainbow garden!”

She set the strip on the cool floor. She touched her paint pots. Squish, squish. She dipped her feather brush and began to paint red strawberries. She giggled. “They look juicy!”

Just then, a loud rumble shook the floor. “Vroom-vroom!” Benny Beetle drove his bottle-cap car around the boxes. Benny loved speed. He zoomed and spun in circles. His tiny wheels clicked on the floor. Click-clack-click!

“Hi, Benny!” called Mabel. “I’m making a big art piece.”

“Hi, Mabel!” Benny zoomed closer. “I want to race right here. This is the best track!”

Mabel blinked. “But I put my picture here. The paint is still wet. See?” She sniffed. “It smells like lemons.”

Benny revved his bottle-cap car. “I want to race now!”

Mabel felt a little twist in her tummy. She looked at her wet rainbow. She looked at Benny’s shiny car. She wanted to be kind. She wanted to keep painting too.

Just then, Pip the Pill Bug rolled by. “Hello,” Pip said in a small, round voice. “What is the trouble?”

Mabel sighed. “I want to paint my big rainbow garden. But Benny wants to race right where I’m painting.”

Benny clicked his wheels. “This track is smooth and fast!”

Pip nodded and listened. The washing machine thumped, thump-thump, like a drum. The basement smelled like soap and dust.

Mabel took a deep breath. “We have a problem,” she said softly. “We need a plan.”

Pip smiled. “Maybe we can make a compromise.”

“Com-pro-mise?” Benny asked, tilting his head.

“It means we share and we take turns,” said Mabel. “It means we both get a little of what we want. We meet in the middle.”

Benny’s car wheels stopped clicking. “Hmm.”

Mabel looked around the busy basement. She saw a broken wooden ruler, a bent straw, some string, and a big shoebox lid. Her eyes lit up. “I have an idea! We can make a race track that goes around my picture. We can use the shoebox lid to make a bridge. And the straw can be a tunnel!”

“That sounds fun,” said Benny. His eyes sparkled. “Vroom under a tunnel!”

Pip clapped his tiny hands. “I can help.”

Mabel smiled. “I will move my rainbow garden to this side.” She carefully slid the cardboard strip a little to the left. The paint shivered but did not drip. “I can wait for the red to dry while we build.”

Mabel dabbed the wet parts with a soft cotton puff. Dab, dab. She set the picture on two small blocks so air could move under it. “This helps it dry faster,” she said.

Benny brought the shoebox lid. “Where does the bridge go?”

“Here,” said Mabel. “Over this little bump in the floor.” They tied the bent straw to the lid with string to make a tunnel. Pip taped the broken ruler down to make a ramp. The tape went rip-riiip!

Soon they had a race track that curved around Mabel’s art. It went up the ruler ramp, over the shoebox bridge, and through the straw tunnel. It made a big, gentle loop.

Benny tested it. “Vroom! Up the ramp! Over the bridge! Through the tunnel!” He laughed. “This is the best track!”

Mabel checked her painting. The red strawberries were dry. She painted orange marigolds, yellow sunflowers, and a big green leaf. She dipped her brush. Swish, swish. She added blue raindrops and a purple butterfly.

Pip watched with happy eyes. “It looks beautiful.”

Benny slowed his car and stopped beside Mabel. “I like your rainbow garden,” he said. “Can I add a tiny road in the corner? Just a small gray line?”

Mabel thought. She smiled. “Yes. A tiny road would be nice.” She handed Benny a gray crayon. He drew a small curved road at the edge of the garden. It did not touch the flowers.

When Mabel finished, the washing machine clicked off. The basement felt calm and warm. The rainbow garden glowed. The race track shined.

Mabel held up her painting. “We did it,” she said. “We solved our problem.”

Benny nodded. “We made a compromise. You painted. I raced. We shared the space.”

Pip added, “You were both helpful and smart. You used what you had.”

Mabel’s heart felt full. “This is our busy basement art day,” she said.

They all smiled. They all took turns. Benny let Mabel try the bottle-cap car. “Vroom!” she said, giggling. Pip added two tiny pebble seats to the bridge. “For resting,” he said.

At the end of the day, Mabel put her rainbow garden on the wall. She wrote, “We can make a plan. We can share. We can compromise.”

Then she looked at her friends. “When we listen and work together, everyone can have fun.”

The End.
