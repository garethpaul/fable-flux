# The Busy Beaver’s Shed Stories

In the cozy corner of a garden, there stood a small wooden shed. It smelled like clean wood and sweet straw. Sunlight peeked through a tiny window and made dust sparkle like gold.

Inside lived a busy beaver named Berta. Berta liked to fix things with her clever paws. Tap, tap, tap went her little hammer. Scritch-scratch went her brush. She hummed a soft song as she worked.

“Good morning, Shed,” Berta said. “Let’s make things neat!”

One bright day, Berta found a dusty, old box under a bench. “Hmm?” she murmured, twitching her whiskers. The box had carved patterns like waves and leaves. It smelled like old paper and pine sap. Berta lifted the lid. Inside were funny tools, small wooden signs with painted letters, and a big book tied with ribbon.

Berta blinked. “What is this?” she whispered. The ribbon felt silky. The book felt heavy and important.

Knock, knock! Someone tapped on the shed door.

Berta opened it. A robin hopped in, a snail slid in, and a tiny mouse peeked in. “Hi, Berta!” they chirped and squeaked. “We heard your tap, tap, tap! Can we help?”

“Hello, friends,” Berta said kindly. “I found a box with a book. It looks special. Do you want to see?”

“Yes!” said the robin. “I love stories.”

“Me too,” said the mouse, ears wiggling.

The snail smiled slowly. “I like slow stories that last.”

Berta untied the ribbon and opened the book. The pages smelled like cinnamon and leaves. Inside were drawings of beavers building dams, families sharing food, and a big round fire. There were words written in neat lines.

Berta traced the words with her paw. “These are my grandbeaver’s stories,” she said softly. “My family used to gather and tell these stories every week. We shared words and songs. We used these little signs to teach letters.” She lifted a painted sign that said B. “B is for beaver.”

The robin tilted her head. “Will you share them with us?”

Berta’s eyes grew warm. “I am busy,” she said. “I fix hinges. I paint shelves. But... traditions are important.” She took a breath. “Yes. Let’s share.”

Berta swept a clean circle on the floor. She put a soft rug down. She set the wooden signs like a small fence around the circle. She placed the book in the middle. The shed felt cozy, like a hug.

“Welcome to Shed Stories,” Berta said. “This is our sharing time.”

The mouse clapped. The snail nodded. The robin fluffed her feathers.

Berta opened to the first page. “In my family, we start with a greeting,” she said. “We say, ‘Nibble, nibble, neighbor!’ It means ‘Hello, friend!’ in old beaver talk.”

She smiled at her friends. “Repeat after me: Nibble, nibble, neighbor!”

“Nibble, nibble, neighbor!” they sang together. The words felt round and bouncy in their mouths. Everyone giggled.

Berta read the first story. “Long ago, a little beaver learned to listen to the river. The river said, ‘Slow, slow, smooth.’ The beaver built carefully and shared tools with all.”

As she read, she tapped the wooden signs. “B is for beaver. R is for river. S is for slow.” The mouse traced the S with a tiny paw. “It feels like a snake,” the mouse whispered.

The robin closed her eyes. “I hear water in the words,” she said. “Ssslow.”

The snail smiled. “Slow is my favorite.”

Berta paused. “In our tradition, we take turns telling a part. Who wants to add a line?” She looked around with open eyes.

The robin chirped, “The sky turned pink, like a peach, and the river sparkled.”

The mouse squeaked, “The beaver shared a shiny nail with a friend.”

The snail said softly, “The beaver waited, and waiting made the work kind.”

Berta felt her heart glow. “That is beautiful,” she said. “We are sharing our words. We are sharing our story. This is how traditions live.”

She closed the book gently. “Now we end with a thank-you in old beaver talk. We say, ‘Tap-tail thanks.’ It means, ‘I am grateful.’”

“Tap-tail thanks,” everyone said.

Berta smiled. “Tap-tail thanks.”

The friends looked around the shed. They noticed the wooden signs, the pictures, and the warm light. The shed sounded peaceful, with rain beginning to patter on the roof. Pitter-patter. Pitter-patter. It smelled like rain and wood and something sweet, like apples.

The mouse asked, “Berta, will you share again tomorrow?”

Berta nodded. “Yes. Tradition is not just one day. We share, we listen, and we learn. We can bring our words too. Robin, you can teach us a birdsong. Snail, you can teach us patient words. Mouse, you can teach us quiet squeaks that say big things.”

The robin beamed. “I know a song that says hello in three bird languages!”

The snail chuckled. “I know a word that means ‘waiting together.’”

The mouse grinned. “I can show a sign for M!”

Berta clapped her paws. “Tomorrow, we will add your traditions to ours. We will tell a story made of many voices.”

She put the book back in the special box and tied the ribbon. Then she hung the wooden signs on a string like bright flags. The shed looked proud.

As the rain slowed, Berta said, “Today I learned to make time for sharing. Stories and words are tools too. They build bridges in our hearts.”

Her friends nodded. The robin sang a soft note. The snail made a happy hum. The mouse patted the rug.

Berta closed the door with a gentle click. “See you for Shed Stories,” she said. “Nibble, nibble, neighbor!”

“Nibble, nibble, neighbor!” they replied.

And in the quiet shed, the new tradition began, full of stories, kind words, and open ears.

The End.
