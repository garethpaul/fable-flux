# Tully the Turtle Tries a New Way

Every morning, Tully the shy turtle peeked out from his cozy shell bed. He lived in a quiet home office with a sunny window, a big desk, soft carpet, and a friendly potted plant named Fern. The air smelled like warm paper and lemon cleaner. The keyboard clicked like tiny raindrops. The printer hummed like a sleepy bee.

Tully loved his routine. He stretched. He took three slow breaths. He counted his pencils. “One, two, three,” he whispered. Then he cleaned his tiny glasses. “Just right,” he said softly.

Tully liked quiet and same. New things made his tummy wiggle.

One day, the desk lamp flickered. “Blink, blink,” it went. The room looked different. Shadows danced on the wall like little puppets.

“Oh,” whispered Tully. “I don’t like different.”

Fern rustled her leaves and said, “Different can be okay.”

Tully shook his head. “I just want the lamp to stay the same.” His voice was small and shy.

Just then, the computer gave a soft “ding!” A note popped up on the screen. It read, “Try something new today.”

Tully blinked. “Try something new?” He tucked into his shell a little.

A tiny spider named Dot waved from the corner. “Hi, Tully,” said Dot. “New can be fun! Want to build a paper bridge with me?”

Tully felt his heart go thump-thump. “I don’t know,” he said. “I like my routine.”

Dot smiled. “We can keep your routine and try one new step. Just one.”

“One step,” Tully repeated. He took a slow breath and peeped out. “Okay. One.”

The printer hummed again, “Bzzzz.” A page slid out with big letters: BRIDGE. The paper felt smooth and cool under Tully’s little feet. “This is new,” he said. “I can try.”

Dot showed him how to fold. “Fold, press, fold,” she sang.

Tully copied, slow and careful. “Fold, press, fold,” he whispered. His shy smile peeked out. He liked the simple song. The paper made gentle crinkle sounds. It smelled like fresh books.

They set the little paper bridge between two pencil cups. “Ta-da!” said Dot.

“Will it hold?” asked Tully.

“We can test it,” said Dot. “Use the small eraser first.”

Tully nodded. He was being resourceful, using what he had. He placed the tiny eraser on the bridge. The bridge stayed strong. “It works!” Tully clapped his little turtle hands, very soft.

Fern swished happily. “See? Open mind, open door,” she said.

Tully giggled. “Open mind, open door.”

Then the lamp flickered again. Blink. The shadows made the bridge look like a mountain. Tully felt a wiggle in his tummy.

Dot noticed. “Want to move the lamp closer?” she asked. “We can try a different way.”

Tully thought. “Trying a different way is okay,” he said. He slowly pushed the lamp. The light felt warm on his shell. The shadows softened. “Better,” he said. He felt proud.

The keyboard clicked, click-click, like tiny feet tapping. The room felt busy, but safe. Tully took three slow breaths. “I can do new things,” he told himself.

A soft breeze came through the window. It smelled like rain. A paper clip fell with a little “tink.” Tully looked at the clip. “Maybe the clip can be a gate for our bridge,” he said.

“That is clever!” Dot cheered. “You are using what you have.”

Tully blushed, happy-shy. “I am being resourceful,” he said. He set the clip on the bridge. It became a tiny gate. “Open,” he said, lifting it. “Closed,” he said, setting it down. He giggled.

A ladybug named Roo landed on the desk. “Hello,” Roo said. “May I go over the bridge?”

Tully’s shy voice was soft. “Yes, you can. We share. We accept friends.”

Roo walked slowly over. Tap, tap, tap. The bridge did not wobble. “Thank you,” Roo said. “This bridge is kind.”

Tully felt warm inside. “We made it together,” he said to Dot. “We tried a new thing. We used what we had. We let friends join.”

He looked at his pencils. “One, two, three,” he counted again. The same counting felt good. Then he looked at the bridge. The new thing felt good too.

Dot smiled. “You can have routine and you can try new. Both can fit.”

Tully nodded. “I can be open-minded,” he said, tasting the big word. “Open-minded means I listen, I look, and I try.”

The lamp glowed steady now. The printer was quiet. The room felt calm and bright. Tully took three slow breaths and put the little eraser friend across the bridge again.

Fern whispered, “Every day can have a tiny new.”

Tully tucked into his shell, not to hide, but to rest. “Tomorrow,” he said, “I will try a new song. Fold, press, fold… and maybe wiggle-wiggle.”

Dot laughed. Roo clapped tiny wings. The home office hummed a happy hum.

Tully smiled. “I am shy,” he said, “and I am brave. I can try new things and keep my cozy routine.”

He closed his eyes, feeling safe, smart, and kind.

The lesson was clear in Tully’s heart: When I keep an open mind, I can learn, I can help, and I can make room for friends and new ideas.

The End.
