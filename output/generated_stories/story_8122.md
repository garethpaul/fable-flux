# Tilly Turtle Tidies the Attic

Tilly was a shy turtle with a soft, green shell. She liked quiet places. One sunny morning, Tilly climbed the creaky stairs to the quiet attic. The attic smelled like old books and warm dust. Sunlight made golden squares on the floor. It was calm and still. Tilly felt safe.

But the attic was messy. Boxes were open. Buttons and ribbons were scattered. A tiny bell rolled and dinged. Tilly blinked. “Oh,” she whispered. “This looks like a big job.”

Tilly took a deep breath. In, out. In, out. “I can do small steps,” she said softly. “Small steps are brave steps.”

She made a plan. “First, I will make a ‘Keep’ box. Then a ‘Share’ box. Then a ‘Trash’ bag.” Tilly found empty boxes and a bag. She drew little pictures on them with a blue crayon: a heart for Keep, two hands for Share, and a tiny bin for Trash.

Her tummy made a small rumble. “I need a snack and water,” she told herself. “Healthy turtles have healthy habits.” Tilly went downstairs, washed her hands with warm, soapy water—swish, swish—and dried them on a soft towel. She drank cool water. She ate apple slices, crunchy and sweet. “Crunch, crunch,” she giggled. Then she stretched her legs and rolled her shoulders. “Now I feel ready.”

Back in the attic, Tilly picked up the buttons. “Click, clack,” they went in a jar. She lifted ribbons—smooth and silky—and wrapped them into neat circles. “Ribbons go in Keep,” she said. She swept tiny dust bunnies into the Trash bag. “Good-bye, little fluff.”

A soft voice floated up the stairs. It was her friend, Pip the mouse. “Tilly? Are you in the attic?”

“Yes,” Tilly answered, shy but brave. “I am cleaning. It is messy.”

Pip peeked in with bright eyes and twitchy whiskers. “Wow,” he said, “So many things! Can I help?”

Tilly felt her cheeks warm. She liked help, but she liked quiet, too. She took another calm breath. “Yes, you can help,” she said. “Can we keep our voices soft? The attic is quiet, and I feel good when it’s calm.”

Pip nodded. “Soft voices,” he whispered. “How can I help?”

Tilly showed him the boxes. “Keep, Share, Trash. And we wash hands before snacks. And we drink water when we’re thirsty.”

Pip smiled. “Good plan.”

They worked together. Pip found a tiny red kite. “Share?” he asked.

“Share,” Tilly said. “Someone else can play.”

They found a book with a shiny moon on the cover. Tilly opened it. The pages smelled like paper and stars. “Keep,” she said. “We can read later.”

Soon it was time for a break. Tilly listened to her body. “My shell feels heavy. My tummy is empty again,” she said. “Break time.”

Pip nodded. “Break time!”

They washed hands, swish, swish. They ate carrot sticks—crunch, crunch—and hummed a little tune. Tilly gently stretched her neck and arms. “Stretching helps me feel calm and strong,” she said.

When they went back to the attic, the room already felt better. The floor made a soft thump under Tilly’s feet. Sunlight warmed her shell. “Thank you for helping,” she told Pip. “I feel shy sometimes. I like to go slow. If I need quiet, I will say so.”

“That’s smart,” said Pip. “Telling friends what you need is brave.”

They sorted the last things: a small drum (Share), three crayons (Keep), and a broken feather (Trash). Tilly taped labels on the boxes. She lined them up: Keep on the left, Share in the middle, Trash by the door.

“Let’s put things away,” Tilly said. “Everything has a home.” She stacked books on a low shelf. She put the button jar next to the ribbons. She folded a blue blanket—soft like clouds—and placed it in a basket.

At last, the attic felt open and cozy. The air smelled fresh. The floor was clear. Tilly felt a warm glow in her chest. “We did it,” she whispered.

Pip clapped softly. “It looks wonderful!”

Tilly smiled. “Now, I will make a little chart,” she said. She drew simple boxes on a paper: Drink water, Wash hands, Stretch, Clean up toys, Ask for quiet. She put a tiny star by each one they had done today.

Pip pointed. “You are very organized.”

Tilly nodded. “Small steps helped. Breaks helped. Asking for what I need helped.”

They curled up on the blue blanket and read the moon book. The pages rustled. The attic felt like a gentle hug. When they finished, Tilly stood tall. “Time to share the Share box,” she said. They carried it downstairs together.

At the door, Tilly looked back at the tidy attic. She took one more slow breath. In, out. “I learned something today,” she said. “Healthy habits—like washing hands, drinking water, taking breaks, and cleaning up—make big jobs feel small. And it’s okay to ask for what I need.”

Pip grinned. “That is a very good lesson.”

Tilly’s heart felt brave and light. “Tomorrow,” she said, “we can make a snack plan and a reading time.”

“And we can keep our chart,” Pip added.

“Yes,” Tilly said. “We will keep our chart.”

The attic was quiet and happy as they went down the stairs, step by gentle step.

The End.
