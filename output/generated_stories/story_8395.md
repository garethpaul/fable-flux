# Cubby’s Careful Fix-It Day

Cubby the bear cub woke up to sunshine peeking through the curtains. He stretched his little paws and wiggled his fuzzy ears. “Good morning, me!” he said with a smile. His tummy gave a tiny rumble. “First, self-care,” Cubby reminded himself. “I eat, I wash, I brush!”

He padded to the kitchen, poured a small bowl of crunchy oats, and added sweet berries. Crunch-crunch! The berries tasted juicy and bright. After breakfast, Cubby washed his paws with warm, soapy bubbles. The bubbles smelled like lemons. He brushed his teeth until they felt squeaky clean. “Shiny smile!” he giggled at the mirror.

Today, Cubby wanted to help in the garage workshop. He loved the clink-clank sounds and the gentle smell of wood and pine. Outside, birds peeped, and a soft breeze tickled his nose. Cubby took a deep breath. “Fresh air feels good,” he said. “Outdoor time is part of caring for me.”

In the garage, shelves held jars of buttons, screws, and colorful bottle caps. A big cardboard box sat by the door with a sign that said, “Recycling.” Cubby’s eyes sparkled. “I can fix and I can sort!”

“Hi, Cubby!” called River, a raccoon friend, peeking in with bright eyes. “Want to play tag?”

“I want to work in the workshop first,” Cubby said kindly. “Then we can play outside. I am practicing self-care, and that means I take my time and take good care of me.”

River nodded. “Okay! I’ll help.” He tugged on some gloves. “We will be safe.”

On the workbench was an old wagon with one squeaky wheel and a broken handle. Cubby touched it gently. “Poor wagon. We can help you.”

“Let’s check our bodies first,” Cubby said. “Are we hungry? Are we thirsty?” River listened to his tummy. “I’m okay, but I feel a little thirsty.”

Cubby poured cool water into two cups. They sipped. Ahhh! The water felt fresh and crisp. “Self-care is listening to our bodies,” Cubby said. “Now we are ready.”

They got to work. Cubby held the wagon while River found a smooth stick to make a new handle. Clink! Clank! The tools made tiny songs on the wood. Cubby used a small screwdriver. “Turn, turn,” he whispered. He dabbed a little oil on the squeaky wheel. Squeeeak… then quiet. “Better,” Cubby said, smiling.

River found a stack of old bottle caps. “Trash?” he asked.

Cubby shook his head. “Let’s recycle or reuse! We can turn these into bells for the wagon.” He showed River the big box with the recycling sign. “Paper here, plastic there, metal over here.” They sorted caps and cans, hearing plink, plonk, plunk as each piece dropped in the right place. “Recycling helps the Earth,” Cubby said. “We keep it clean and green.”

They made a string of shiny caps and tied them to the wagon. Chinkle-chinkle! The sound was cheerful and soft.

Soon Cubby felt his paws getting tired. His ears drooped a little. He stopped and took a breath. “I need a break,” he said.

River looked worried. “Should we hurry?”

“No,” Cubby said gently. “Self-care means rest. When we rest, we do our best.” They stretched their arms, shook out their paws, and took three slow breaths. In… and out. In… and out. In… and out. Cubby smiled again. “I feel calm.”

River grinned. “Me too!”

They finished the wagon by rubbing the wood with a tiny bit of safe, lemony wax. The garage smelled bright and clean. Cubby wiped the bench, then washed his paws. “We work, we wash,” he sang softly. “We care for our space, and we care for our bodies.”

“Now can we play?” River asked.

“Yes!” Cubby wheeled the wagon outside. The grass felt cool under their feet. The sky was big and blue. A butterfly fluttered by. The wagon rolled smoothly, and the cap-bells went chinkle-chinkle-chime. They took turns pulling and riding. “Whee!” giggled Cubby. The breeze whooshed in his fur.

After a while, Cubby noticed the sun felt warm, and his mouth felt dry. He stopped. “Water break!” he said. They sat in the shade, drank water, and munched on apple slices. Crunch! “Playing outside is fun,” Cubby said, “and taking care of ourselves keeps it fun.”

River pointed to a crumpled paper cup near the path. “Litter,” he frowned.

Cubby picked it up and walked to the bin. “We throw trash away and recycle what we can,” he said. They checked the cup. “Paper! Recycle.” Plunk! Into the right bin it went. “Little acts help our big world.”

Soon, the day felt just right. They rolled the wagon back to the garage and put tools in their places. Click, click, click. Everything was tidy and safe.

River waved. “Thanks for the fun, Cubby!”

Cubby smiled. “Thank you for helping,” he said. “Today I learned that self-care is food, water, rest, washing, and gentle words to myself. It helps me fix things, play outside, and take care of Earth with recycling.”

River nodded. “Self-care helps me, too!”

Cubby gave a happy bear cub hug. His heart felt warm. “When I care for me, I can care for others,” he said softly.

The sun began to set, and the air smelled like pine and lemon. Cubby brushed his teeth, washed his paws, and snuggled into bed. “Good job today, me,” he whispered. “Good job caring.”

The End.
