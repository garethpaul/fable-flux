# Moonlight Buckets on the Rooftop

A kind mouse named Miri lived under the tiles of a tall rooftop. At night, cool breezes tickled her whiskers. The moon made the roof look silver. Crickets sang chirp-chirp, and the tar smelled warm from the day.

Miri loved her home, but one summer was very dry. The garden below looked thirsty. The leaves were dusty. The little birds squeaked, “Water, please!” Miri felt a soft squeeze in her heart.

“I want to help,” Miri said. She held a tiny tin cup. “But I am small. What can I do?”

Miri sat on the edge of the roof. The city hummed low. A drop of dew slid over her paw. It was cool and clean.

“Oh!” Miri’s eyes got bright. “Drops are small, but many drops can fill a cup.”

She made a plan. She would save water. She would not waste even one drop.

Miri pulled out her Rooftop Kit. She had little bottle caps, a string, a thimble, and three tiny buckets made from old acorn tops. She lined them up in a neat row. Tap, tap—she set each one under places where dew liked to gather.

“This one is for catching,” she said. “This one is for washing paws. This one is for the thirsty plants.” She tied simple labels with string. Her tail curled with pride. “Being tidy helps me remember.”

A breeze shushed by. The night felt soft. Miri whispered, “Buckets, do your best.” She practiced walking slowly so she would not spill. Step, balance. Step, balance. “Practice makes me steady,” she said, giggling.

In the morning, the sky was pale pink. Miri peeked into her buckets. Plip, plip—tiny pools shimmered.

“It worked!” she cheered.

She washed her paws in the washing bucket. Rub, rub. “Clean paws mean healthy paws,” she sang. She didn’t use all the water. Just a little. She shook her paws dry and saved the rest.

Then she carried the plant bucket along the ridge. The roof tiles were warm on her feet. Far below, the garden waited. Miri found a crack where moss grew. She dripped water on the moss. The moss looked brighter. The smell turned fresh and green.

A raven landed nearby. His feathers shone purple in the sun. “Caw! Little mouse, why so many tiny buckets?” he asked.

Miri smiled up. “I am saving water. Drop by drop.”

The raven tilted his head. “Why not use a big bowl?”

“I am small,” said Miri. “Small buckets are safe for me. And small saves can grow big.”

“Caw,” said the raven, impressed. “Smart.”

Day by day, Miri kept going. She set out her buckets every night. She practiced carrying them every morning. Step, balance. Step, balance. She made a chart with chalk on a flat tile: Catch, Wash, Water. She drew little stars when she used just the right amount. She hummed, “Catch, wash, water. Save, share, order.”

One hot day, dark clouds rolled in. The air smelled like metal and wet dust. Boom! A soft thunder grumbled. Miri felt a raindrop on her ear. Plop!

“Oh!” Miri clapped her paws. “Rain!”

She ran to her kit and set out more things to catch the water—bottle caps, thimbles, a clean jar she found stuck in a gutter. She wiped the jar carefully. “Clean jar for clean water,” she said. “Hygiene keeps us safe.”

Rain began to tap on the roof. Tap-tap. Then it grew steady. Tappa-tappa-tap! Miri’s buckets filled fast. She moved them under the drip, one by one, in a neat line. She whispered, “We can do this. We can save.”

The raven flapped back. “Need help?”

“Yes, please,” Miri said. “Let’s work together.”

The raven picked up the jar with his beak. Miri pushed a bottle cap into place. Together they filled their little store: one row for washing, one for plants, one for drinks. The smell of rain was sweet and clean. The wind whooshed gently around them.

When the clouds passed, the sky turned bright again. Miri and the raven looked at all the water they had saved.

“Caw! So much!” the raven said.

Miri nodded. “We didn’t waste. We were careful. We kept things tidy. We practiced. We used what we had.”

They carried water to the garden below. Drip by drip, they shared with the dry leaves and the dusty flowers. The plants lifted their heads. Tiny birds fluttered and sipped. “Thank you,” they chirped, bright and happy.

Miri washed her paws again, just a little. She put the rest back on the shelf. She stacked the buckets. She tied fresh labels. “When we organize, we remember. When we practice, we get better. When we save, we have enough,” she said softly.

The sun slid down. The roof grew cool. Crickets began to sing. Miri curled up by her tidy rows and felt warm inside.

She smiled at the moon. “Small paws can help a big world,” she whispered.

The End.
