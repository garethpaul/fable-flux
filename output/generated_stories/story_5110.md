# Ollie Otter and the Balcony Garden Book

On a sunny morning, a playful otter named Ollie padded onto the balcony garden. The air smelled like mint and wet soil. Tiny bells of purple flowers danced in the breeze. Bees hummed. Leaves sparkled with drops of water.

“Hello, garden!” Ollie giggled, waving his paw. His whiskers tickled in the sunlight. He loved this sweet, small world high above the street.

Ollie had a big idea. “I will write a book about our garden,” he told the plants. “A real book! With words and pictures!” He bounced on his paws. He loved to splash and play, but today he wanted to write. He wanted to tell a story.

He got his little notebook and a short, stubby pencil. The paper smelled like clean wood. He licked his paw and turned the page. “Ready,” he said.

But when Ollie tried to write, the pencil slid. Scribble, scribble. The words looked messy. His paws were muddy from watering mint, and dirt stuck to the page.

“Oh no,” said Ollie. “My writing is all wiggly.”

He tried again. Scribble. Smudge. The page tore a little. Ollie’s tummy felt squirmy. His tail drooped. “Maybe I can’t write a book,” he whispered.

Just then, a ladybug landed on the railing. A small snail peeked from a pot. A sparrow hopped close and chirped, “Cheep?”

Ollie sniffed. He noticed the smudges. He saw the mud on his paws. He remembered a rule Mama Otter always said: “Clean paws, clear words.”

Ollie took a deep breath. The air tasted cool and minty. “I can fix this,” he said softly. “Problem first. Then solution.”

He set the muddy notebook aside. He got a small bowl and filled it with soapy water from the pitcher. Bubbles bloomed like tiny white clouds. They smelled like lemons. He washed his paws. Swish, swish. He scrubbed under his claws. He rinsed and dried with a soft towel hanging on the chair.

“Clean paws,” Ollie smiled. “Clean tools too!”

He gently wiped his pencil. He shook the dirt from the eraser. He found a fresh page. He felt calm now. “I can try again.”

Ollie looked around with careful eyes. He listened with careful ears.

“Cheep, cheep,” sang the sparrow.

“Crunch,” went the snail on a dry leaf.

“Shhh,” whispered the wind in the rosemary.

Ollie pressed his pencil and said the words as he wrote. “The mint smells sweet.” Line by line, he wrote simple sentences. “The flower is purple.” “The bee goes buzz.” He drew small dots for ladybug spots. He drew a tiny smile on the snail.

His letters were straighter. The page stayed clean. Ollie’s heart felt warm and steady.

Suddenly, he saw a problem. The snail looked thirsty. The soil in the snail’s pot looked dry and dusty. The snail’s shell was dull.

“Oh dear,” said Ollie. “You need help.”

He remembered another rule: “Clean and kind, help and find.” Ollie wrote in his book, “I see a thirsty friend.” Then he put down the pencil and got to work.

First, he washed his paws again. Swish, swish. He didn’t want to carry dirt into the snail’s home. Next, he fetched the small watering can. Drip, drip, drip—he poured just enough water to make the soil dark and soft. He placed a wet leaf near the snail. The snail touched it with gentle feelers and drank. The sparrow chirped a happy “Cheep-cheep!”

Ollie felt proud. “Helping others is part of my story,” he said.

He dried his paws and returned to his book. He wrote, “I helped the snail. The snail is happy.” He drew a bright little drop on the leaf. He added simple labels: leaf, water, snail.

Then Ollie made a discovery. On the railing, behind the rosemary, he spotted tiny silver lines. They shone in the sun like a secret map. “Wow,” he breathed. “Snail trails are shiny!” He leaned close. They smelled like clean rain.

Ollie wrote, “I discover shiny trails.” He slowly sounded out the word “shiny.” Shhh-iiii-nee. He smiled at his careful letters.

The ladybug flew in a lazy circle. The breeze tickled Ollie’s nose, and he giggled. He kept writing simple, kind words. He listened. He looked. He washed his paws when they got dusty. Wash, write, watch. Over and over, like a soft song.

Soon, his little book had many pages. There were sentences, pictures, and labels. There were notes about smells, sounds, and colors. There was a story about seeing a problem and helping.

Ollie read his pages out loud to the garden. “The mint smells sweet. The flower is purple. The bee goes buzz. I see a thirsty friend. I help the snail. I discover shiny trails.”

The sparrow fluffed its feathers. The snail waved its feelers. The ladybug tapped the cover like a tiny clap.

Ollie tucked the pencil behind his ear. He closed the book with clean paws. He felt brave and bright.

“I did it,” he said. “Writing helps me see. Writing helps me help.”

The sun slid a little lower. The mint leaves rustled. Ollie put the watering can away. He washed his paws one more time and dried them on the soft towel.

Then he hugged his book. “Thank you, balcony garden,” he whispered. “You helped me learn.”

And the garden seemed to whisper back, “Cheep, buzz, shhh,” like a happy song.

The End.
