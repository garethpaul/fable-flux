# Ellie on the Rooftop

Ellie was a gentle elephant calf with soft gray ears and a bright blue ribbon on her tail. One sunny morning, Ellie climbed the stairs with her mama to the rooftop garden. The air felt warm. The sky looked big and blue. Ellie could smell mint leaves and sweet flowers. She heard birds singing, “Cheep, cheep!”

“This is high,” Ellie whispered. Her tummy felt fluttery. The rooftop was safe, with a sturdy fence and colorful planters. But it was still very high.

Mama’s trunk touched Ellie’s shoulder. “I’m here,” Mama said softly. “We will have a rooftop picnic. We will listen. We will follow directions. We will be brave.”

Ellie took a deep breath. “I can be brave,” she said. Her voice was small but strong.

They set the picnic blanket. It was red and soft. Ellie helped Mama place mango slices, crunchy carrots, and peanut butter sandwiches. The mango smelled sweet like sunshine. The carrots snapped when Ellie bit them. “Crunch!” she giggled.

On one side of the rooftop, there was a little windsock that looked like a happy fish. It fluttered and flapped. “Swish, swish,” went the wind. Ellie watched the fish-sock dance. The wind made her ears sway. It felt cool and tickly.

A sign near the fence showed three pictures with words:
1. Walk, don’t run.
2. Stay with your grown-up.
3. Listen to directions.

Mama pointed and smiled. “These are our rooftop rules,” she said. “Can you tell me the rules?”

Ellie looked at the sign. She said slowly, “Walk, don’t run. Stay with my grown-up. Listen to directions.”

“Wonderful listening,” Mama said. “That is being brave and smart.”

They played a gentle game. Mama said, “Touch your nose.” Ellie touched her nose. “Touch your toes.” Ellie touched her toes. “Hold my trunk.” Ellie held Mama’s trunk and felt safe.

Then Ellie heard a new sound. “Clink, clink.” It was a watering can by the garden bed. Mama said, “Would you like to water the flowers?”

Ellie’s eyes grew wide. “Yes, please.”

Mama showed Ellie how to hold the can with both little paws. “Two paws,” Mama said. “Slow and steady. Pour only on the soil.”

Ellie listened. She followed the directions. The water made a soft “pssshh” sound and the soil smelled rich and wet. Tiny drops sparkled like stars. Ellie smiled. “I did it!”

Just then, the wind blew a bit stronger. “Whoooo,” it sang around the fence. The windsock fish swished fast. Ellie felt her tummy flip-flop again. She stepped back and her ears curled in.

Mama knelt and spoke calmly. “Ellie, do you feel wobbly?”

“A little,” Ellie said.

“Let’s use our brave breath,” Mama said. “In through the nose, out through the mouth. Like this.” Mama showed her. “In… and out.”

Ellie copied. “In… and out.” The wind still went “whoooo,” but Ellie felt her feet again. The rooftop felt steady.

“You listened so well,” Mama said. “You followed my directions. That is courage.”

Ellie stood taller. “I am brave,” she said. Her heart felt warm, like sunshine on her back.

They decided to fly a tiny kite shaped like a red heart. Mama gave gentle directions. “Walk, don’t run. Hold the string with both paws. Watch the wind.”

Ellie nodded. She listened closely. The kite tugged a little. “Tug, tug,” it said. Ellie kept her feet slow. She watched the wind. The kite lifted and floated. “Up, up!” Ellie laughed. Her giggle sounded like little bells.

A small bird landed on the fence and chirped, “Cheep-cheep!” Ellie whispered, “Hello, little bird.” The bird tilted its head, as if it was listening too.

After some time, Mama said, “Now we will bring the kite down. Pull in slowly. One, two, three.” Ellie followed each step. The kite came down soft as a feather. Mama clapped softly. “You did it. You were careful. You were brave.”

Ellie felt proud. The roof did not feel so high now. It felt like a safe place where she knew the rules and could be strong.

They packed the basket. Ellie carried the light napkins. “I can help,” she said.

“Yes,” Mama said. “Helping is brave, too.”

Before they went downstairs, Ellie looked at the sky one more time. The clouds were fluffy like cotton. The wind felt gentle again. Ellie smiled and said, “Thank you, rooftop.”

On the stairs, Ellie said to Mama, “I learned something.”

“What did you learn?” Mama asked.

Ellie counted on her toes. “I learned to breathe when I feel wobbly. I learned to listen to you. I learned to follow directions. That is courage.”

Mama hugged her with her trunk. “Yes, Ellie. Courage is listening, following directions, and trying again, even when you feel a little scared.”

Ellie nodded. “I can be brave every day.”

“Yes,” Mama said. “Every day.”

They walked down, step by step, safe and proud. Ellie’s heart felt big and steady. She could still hear the birds singing, “Cheep, cheep,” like a happy song just for her.

The End.
