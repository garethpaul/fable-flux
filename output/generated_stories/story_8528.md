# Mango on the Rooftop

Sunlight warmed the rooftop tiles, making them feel like toast. A soft breeze carried the smell of sweet mangoes from a basket near a chiming bell. Miko, a courageous little monkey with bright eyes and quick hands, bounced on his toes and looked around.

“I love the rooftop,” Miko said, patting the warm tiles. “It is high and breezy. It is a good place to think.”

He peeked into the mango basket. “Oh! A big mango,” he whispered. It was golden and smooth, with a sweet, juicy scent. “I will share this mango with my friends,” Miko decided. He felt happy and brave.

Just then, the bell rang, ding-ding, and a gust of wind puffed across the roof. The wind rolled the mango right to the edge. “Oh no!” cried Miko. The mango bumped a loose tile and slipped over the side.

Miko ran to the edge and looked down. The mango had landed on a tiny ledge below the rooftop. It sat there, wobbling. Far below, the street looked like a patchwork of colors. Miko’s tail twitched.

“I must think,” he said softly. “I can solve this.” He took a deep breath, feeling the warm sun on his back and the wind in his fur.

His friend Pip, a small pigeon with shiny feathers, landed with a flutter. “Coo! What’s wrong, Miko?” Pip asked.

“My mango fell,” Miko explained. “It is on that ledge. I want to share it with our friends. But it is far. I need a plan.”

Pip tilted his head. “Coo, coo. I can fly down, but the ledge is narrow. My feet might push the mango off.”

Miko nodded. “Thank you, Pip. We need another idea.”

He looked around. He saw a long clothesline, some soft scarves drying in the sun, a wooden broom, and the basket. He tapped his chin. “Hmm. What can we do with these?”

He tried first with the broom. “I will reach,” he said. He lay on his tummy, stretched the broom handle toward the mango, and wiggled it. The mango wobbled more. “Oh! Too shaky.” His heart thumped. He pulled the broom back. “That plan did not work. I will try again.”

Pip cooed kindly. “You can do it, Miko. Try, try again.”

Miko smiled. “Yes. I am brave, and I can keep trying.”

He picked up a long scarf. It was soft and smelled like soap and sunshine. He tied one end to the broom handle. The scarf dangled like a tail. “If I make a loop,” Miko said, “I can catch the mango.”

He tried to toss the scarf loop down. Whoosh! The wind blew it aside. “Oh dear,” he sighed. “The wind is strong.” He stood up, felt the breeze on his face, and listened to the bell ding-ding-ding. “The wind has a pattern,” he noticed. “It blows, then it rests.”

Miko watched carefully. Blow. Rest. Blow. Rest. He nodded. “I will toss the loop when the wind rests.”

He waited. He breathed slow. The bell grew quiet. The air felt still. “Now,” he whispered. He tossed the loop gently. It floated down, soft and slow, like a sleepy butterfly. The loop landed around the mango.

“I did it!” Miko cheered.

“Coo! Good thinking!” Pip flapped his wings.

Miko pulled the scarf very gently. “Slow and steady,” he said. The mango slid toward the roof. The ledge creaked a little. The scarf rubbed his fingers. “Almost there.”

Suddenly, the wind puffed again. Whoosh! The mango slipped sideways. Miko’s heart jumped. “Oh no!” He stopped pulling and held still. “Wait for the rest,” he told himself. He felt the fear, then took a deep breath. He remembered his plan. “I am resilient. I can keep going.”

When the wind rested, Miko tugged again, steady and small. The mango rose over the edge and plopped into the basket with a soft thump.

“We did it!” Miko laughed. He clapped his hands. Pip fluffed his feathers. The bell rang as if it were cheering, ding-ding-ding!

Soon, friends gathered on the rooftop. Lila the lizard clicked her tongue. Tutu the turtle smiled slowly. “It smells yummy,” said Lila. “It looks so bright,” said Tutu.

Miko held up the mango. “We will share,” he said. “Pip helped me. And the scarves and broom helped, too! We had a plan. We tried. We watched. We waited. We did not give up.”

He peeled the mango. The skin was smooth and cool. The fruit inside was juicy and golden. Juice dripped down his fingers. Everyone took small bites. “Mmm!” they said together. Sweetness filled the air.

Pip pecked a tiny bit and cooed, “You solved the problem, Miko.”

Miko nodded. “I learned three things,” he said. He held up his fingers. “One: Stop and think. Two: Try, try, and try again. Three: Share with friends.”

Tutu smiled. “That is a good lesson.”

Lila licked a drop of juice. “And it is kind to be generous,” she added.

Miko wiped his hands and looked at the sky. The breeze felt friendly now. “When we have a problem,” he said, “we can be calm, make a plan, and keep going. We can help each other. We can share what we get.”

Everyone cheered. The rooftop felt warm and safe. The bell chimed softly, like a happy heart.

The End.
