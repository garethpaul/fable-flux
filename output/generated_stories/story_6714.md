# Fawn’s Calm-Down Countdown

In a sunny garage workshop that smelled like fresh wood and warm soap, a gentle deer fawn named Fawn peeked inside. The floor felt cool on her tiny hooves. Tools hung on the wall like shiny fish—hammers, wrenches, and bright red screwdrivers. A little fan hummed, and a radio played soft music.

“I want to build something all by myself,” Fawn said. Her big ears wiggled. “I can be independent.”

On a low workbench lay a small wooden kit with simple blocks, stickers, and a tiny wooden wheel. It was a rolling duck toy. Fawn’s eyes sparkled. “Quack, quack! Let’s build you,” she giggled.

She read the picture instructions slowly. Step one: line up the blocks. Step two: add the wheel. Step three: decorate with a happy beak and wings.

Fawn nudged the blocks into a row. They clicked like puzzle pieces. “So far, so good,” she said, feeling proud.

Then the tiny wheel rolled away. It went tick-tick-tick across the table and plink! down to the floor. Fawn’s heart thumped. “Oh no!”

She bent to look under the bench. Dust smelled like old leaves. “Wheel? Where are you?” she whispered.

She found it and tried again. But the wheel didn’t fit. It wobbled and fell off. Clink! Clatter!

Fawn’s cheeks felt hot. Her breath got fast. Her tail flicked. “This is hard,” she said. Her hooves tapped, tap-tap-tap, on the floor. She wanted to stomp. She wanted to cry.

Just then, a kind voice floated from the doorway. It was Barn Owl, the wise helper in the workshop. “Hello, Fawn. I see a brave builder. What’s your plan?”

“I want to do it myself,” Fawn said, eyes watery. “But the wheel won’t stay. I feel all twisty inside.”

Barn Owl nodded slowly. “I hear big feelings. Big feelings are okay. When feelings get big, we can get small and calm. Would you like a tool for your feelings first?”

“A tool for feelings?” Fawn sniffled. “Like a tiny screwdriver?”

Barn Owl chuckled softly. “Almost. Try the Calm-Down Countdown. Would you like to try it by yourself?”

Fawn stood tall. “Yes. I’ll try.”

She put a hoof on her chest. “Five slow breaths.” She breathed in through her nose—sniff—smelling wood and soap—and out through her mouth—whooo. One. Two. Three. Four. Five.

“Four things I can see,” she said. “Red screwdriver. Blue tape. Yellow duck sticker. Silver wrench.”

“Three things I can feel,” she whispered. “Cool floor. Smooth wood. Soft air from the fan.”

“Two things I can hear,” she listened. “Fan hum. Radio song.”

“One kind thing to tell myself,” she said. “I can learn. I can try again.”

Fawn’s body felt softer, like warm grass. Her heart slowed. She gave herself a little smile. “I did it by myself,” she said.

Barn Owl blinked happily. “That’s self-regulation. You noticed your feelings, and you helped yourself calm.”

Fawn looked at the wheel. “I’m ready now.” She checked the picture again. “Oh! The little ring goes on first.” She had missed it before.

She slid on the little ring. The wheel clicked into place. Click! She gave it a gentle spin. It rolled smooth and quiet, like a whisper. “It works!”

Fawn stuck on the yellow duck wings. She pressed a tiny orange beak. The sticker smelled like sweet glue. “Quack,” she laughed.

Then her hoof bumped a tray of screws. Rattle! Tiny silver screws danced to the floor. “Oh no!” Fawn gasped.

Barn Owl tilted his head. “What can you do now?”

Fawn took one breath. “I can be kind to myself. I can fix it.” She knelt and picked up the screws, one by one. “Plink. Plink. Plink.” She put them back in the tray.

A little mouse peeked from a toolbox. “Squeak! Did you mean to drop those?” he asked.

Fawn shook her head. “It was an accident. I’m sorry.”

The mouse smiled. “I forgive you. Thank you for cleaning up.”

Fawn’s chest felt warm. “Thank you for forgiving me,” she said. “Next time, I will move the tray away before I decorate.”

Barn Owl hooted softly. “That’s independence and good planning.”

Fawn held up her duck. The wheel rolled across the workbench—smooth and steady. The radio played a happy tune. The workshop smelled like success and a little like stickers.

“I built it myself,” Fawn said. “I calmed my body. I fixed my mistake. I was kind to myself and others.”

Barn Owl nodded. “When we feel twisty, we can pause, breathe, and choose. That is how we grow.”

Fawn whispered to her duck, “When I feel big feelings, I’ll do my Calm-Down Countdown.”

She packed her duck in a small box, folded the picture plan, and cleaned the table with gentle swipes. Swish, swish. She stood in the doorway and looked back at the bright tools and the humming fan.

“I can try hard things,” Fawn said. “I can be calm. I can forgive. I can be me.”

The End.
