# The Little Rooftop Fawn

Tap, tap, tap went the soft hooves of a gentle deer fawn on the flat, warm rooftop. The sun made the roof feel toasty, and a breeze smelled like fresh rain and flowers. The fawn’s ears twitched. Her name was Lila, and she had big, bright eyes and a tiny white spot on her nose.

“Oh!” said Lila, peeking over the edge. “I’m high up!”

She had followed a line of crunchy leaves, step by step, up a ramp by the garden shed. Now she was on the rooftop, above the street, above the trees. She could see birds riding the wind, and she could hear a soft hum from the town below. But when she looked for the ramp, she couldn’t find it.

Her little heart went thump-thump. “How do I get down?” she whispered.

A tiny sparrow landed nearby. “Chirp, chirp! You look worried,” said the bird.

“I am,” said Lila. “I climbed up. But now I don’t know the way down.”

The sparrow tilted its head. “I can fly down,” it said. “But you can’t fly.” It hopped in a circle. “Maybe look, listen, and try. That is what I do.”

Lila took a slow breath. She smelled the warm tar of the roof. She felt the breeze against her cheeks. She listened. In the distance, a dog barked. Close by, a small metal door clicked and rattled when the wind blew.

Click. Rattle. Click.

Lila stepped closer. The door had a little handle and a shadowy stair inside. She set one hoof on the top step. It wobbled a little.

“I’m scared,” Lila said softly.

The sparrow chirped, “You can be brave and careful at the same time. Little steps.”

Lila nodded. “Little steps,” she repeated.

She tried one step. Tap. Then another. Tap. The stair felt cool and smooth. But it was dark inside. Lila’s ears listened. Drip… drip… drip went a faraway pipe. She took one more step, then stopped.

“This feels too dark,” she said. “My body says no.”

“That’s okay,” said the sparrow. “Kindness to yourself is smart.”

Lila walked back out to the warm sun. She shook her shoulders and felt better. She looked around again. A gust of wind lifted a line of colorful flags that fluttered and flapped. Under the flags, she saw a wide wooden box filled with soil and herbs. Next to it was a long, gentle ramp she had missed before, covered with soft rubber mats.

“My ramp!” Lila smiled.

But when she put her hoof on the ramp, she saw a broken plank near the middle. The plank wiggled and made a squeaky sound.

Squeak… squeak…

Lila pulled back. “I don’t want to fall.”

A gray cat stretched in a sunny patch. “Mrrrow,” the cat purred. “You look like you need a plan.”

“I do,” said Lila. “I want to go down, but the ramp is wiggly.”

The cat blinked slowly. “Test it. Fix what you can. Ask for help when you need it.”

Lila’s ears perked up. “I can try that.”

She pressed her hoof gently on the first part of the ramp. It felt firm. “Good.” She looked at the broken plank. Next to the herb box, she found a short piece of strong wood, and a coil of soft rope. “Maybe I can make it better,” she said.

Lila nudged the wood over the wiggly spot. Her nose wrinkled at the sharp, green smell of the herbs. She used her teeth and hooves to push the rope under and over, under and over, tying a simple knot the way she had seen children tie a swing. Her knot was messy, but it held the board in place.

She tested it with one hoof. No squeak.

“Kind and clever,” purred the cat. “But go slow.”

Lila looked at the sparrow. “Will you watch me?”

“Chirp! I will,” said the bird. “I can fly ahead and tell you if the way is clear.”

Lila took a breath. “Little steps,” she said. Tap. Tap. Tap. Her hooves made quiet sounds on the ramp. The wood was warm under her feet. When she reached the fixed plank, she paused. The sparrow chirped, “You’ve got this!”

Lila put her hoof down. The plank stayed steady. She smiled. Tap. Tap. Down she went, slow and sure, breathing in and out. The breeze grew softer as she descended. She could smell grass now, and hear leaves whisper.

At the bottom, Lila stepped onto the ground. It felt safe and cool. She touched her nose to the ramp. “Thank you, ramp,” she said. “Thank you, me.”

The cat came down the last step with a little hop. “You listened. You tried. You fixed what you could. That’s resilience,” the cat said.

“What is re-sil-ience?” Lila asked, sounding out the big word.

“It means you keep trying, even when it’s hard,” the cat purred. “You are kind to yourself, and you find a way.”

The sparrow chirped, “And you asked for help when you needed it. That is smart and brave.”

Lila’s tail flicked with happy pride. “I was scared, but I tried little steps. I looked, listened, and fixed a problem. I can do hard things.”

She nuzzled the cat and thanked the sparrow. Then she trotted to the grass, the sun warm on her back, the breeze soft on her ears. Lila lifted her head and smiled. “I can try again tomorrow,” she said. “I can be kind, and I can be strong.”

And the rooftop? It stayed high and sunny, a place where a small fawn learned a big lesson.

The End.
