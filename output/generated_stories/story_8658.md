# Pip's Plan at the Dinner Table

Pip was a small, singing bird with bright blue feathers and a warm, wiggly tummy. Pip lived by the window near a cozy dining room. In the dining room, there was a big wooden table, shiny spoons, and a bowl of sweet, red apples. In the morning, sunlight made the spoons sparkle. In the evening, the room smelled like warm bread.

One sunny morning, Pip chirped, “Tweet! Today I will sing a big song for my friends.” Pip loved to sing all day. But sometimes, Pip got carried away and sang too loudly, too close, and too long. Friends liked Pip’s songs, but they also liked quiet time.

Pip puffed up, ready to sing right away. Then Pip saw the clock and the tidy table. Pip paused. “Hmm,” Pip whispered. “I should make a plan.” Pip remembered: Planning helps things go well.

Pip hopped onto a placemat and laid out a plan:
- First, practice softly.
- Next, ask friends if they want to listen.
- Then, set a time to sing.
- After, take a quiet rest.

Pip took a deep breath. In, slow. Out, slow. “I can do this,” Pip said. That felt mindful and calm.

Pip practiced a gentle note. “Twee-tee-tee…” The note was as soft as warm butter. Spoons did not rattle. The apples did not wobble. Pip smiled. “Soft is nice.”

Just then, Daisy the kitten tiptoed under the table. Her paws were quiet, like little clouds. “Hi, Pip,” Daisy purred. “Are you singing now?”

Pip remembered the plan. “I am planning,” Pip said, holding a tiny feather to her heart. “Do you want to listen later?”

Daisy blinked her big eyes. “Yes, but only a little. I like quiet after my milk.”

“Thank you for telling me,” said Pip. “Your quiet time is important. I can sing two songs, not ten.” Pip drew two tiny circles in the dust with a claw. “Two songs.”

Mr. Spoon clinked gently as Grandma set the table. “Tap, tap,” went the forks. “Mmm,” went the fresh bread smell. Grandma smiled at Pip. “Hello, little singer. We will eat soon. Please keep the table clear.”

Pip nodded. “I will stay on the placemat. That is your space. This is my space.” Pip liked to respect personal boundaries. It felt kind.

Pip looked around. Where could Pip put a tiny song list? Pip spotted a folded napkin by the fruit bowl. “Aha!” Pip chirped. “I can use the back of the napkin to write my plan.” Pip used a berry to make purple dots. Dot for Song One, dot for Song Two. Resourceful Pip!

Daisy sniffed the berries. “They smell sweet.”

“They are sticky,” Pip giggled, shaking little feet. “But they work!”

Soon, Finn the goldfish swished in his bowl on the sideboard. The water made quiet shhhh sounds. “Hello, Pip,” Finn bubbled. “Is it story time or song time?”

Pip checked the napkin list. “Song time is in five big breaths,” Pip said. “Let’s count together.”

Everyone breathed with Pip. In and out, soft and slow:
“One,” whispered Daisy.
“Two,” bubbled Finn.
“Three,” smiled Grandma, laying the last spoon.
“Four,” chirped Pip.
“Five,” they all sighed.

Pip fluttered up and asked, “Is everyone ready for two soft songs?”

“Yes,” said Daisy. “But please not near my ears.”
“Yes,” bubbled Finn. “Please not too loud. Water makes sounds big.”
“Yes,” said Grandma. “And please keep your feathery toes off the plates.”

Pip listened carefully. “Thank you. I hear your needs.” Pip tapped the napkin plan. “I will sing from the chair, not the plates. I will be soft. I will stop after two songs. Then I will rest.”

Pip began the first song. It sounded like honey on toast—warm and sweet. “Twee-tee, twee-tee, good morning, good day.” The dining room felt cozy. Sunlight warmed Pip’s feathers. Daisy’s whiskers twitched happily. Finn’s bubbles made tiny pops.

Pip paused and checked in. “Is this okay?”

Daisy nodded. “Yes. My ears feel calm.”
Finn wiggled his fins. “Good volume.”

Pip sang the second song. It sounded like rain tapping the window—plip-plop, soft and gentle. “Plip-plop, breathe and smile, stay a little while.” Pip ended with a quiet “twee.”

Pip looked around. “All done. Two songs.” Pip folded the napkin plan and tucked it under the fruit bowl.

Daisy stretched. “Thank you for asking first,” she purred. “I like when you plan.”

Finn swished. “Thank you for being mindful,” he bubbled. “You made space for my needs.”

Grandma set a small dish of crumbs for Pip, beside the placemat. “Thank you for respecting the table,” she said. “You kept the space tidy.”

Pip felt proud and calm. The dining room smelled like bread and apples. The spoons were still and shiny. Pip took a quiet rest, as planned, with wings folded like a blanket.

Pip whispered, “Planning helps me sing well. Planning helps friends feel good. I can ask, I can listen, I can choose a good time.” Pip smiled and hummed a tiny, tiny tune, just for Pip.

The End.
