# Rocky Raccoon Finds the Right Beat

Rocky was a musical raccoon with soft gray fur and bright curious eyes. He wore a tiny blue scarf and carried two shiny drumsticks. Tap tap! Tap tap! Rocky loved to make music. Today, he padded into the musical practice room at the community art center. The room smelled like fresh wood and a little like crayons. There were drums, a small piano, a rainbow xylophone, and a big box of colorful scarves for dancing.

“Hello, music room!” Rocky said with a happy swish of his striped tail. “I’m ready to play!”

His friends were there too. Mia the mouse held a tiny violin. Bo the bunny had a small drum. Lila the ladybug sat on a paint cup, ready to make art after the music. The room hummed with soft sounds. Plink from the piano. Tinkle from the xylophone. Thump-thump from the drum.

“Let’s make a song,” said Mia, her whiskers twitching. “A soft song.”

“I want a loud song!” Bo thumped his drum. THUMP! THUMP!

Rocky tapped his drumsticks on the edge of a drum. Tap tap. He smiled. “Maybe we can do both,” he said. “Soft and loud.”

Lila waved her tiny paintbrush. “I can paint the music,” she buzzed. “Soft can be light blue. Loud can be bright orange!”

They all liked that idea. But when they began, the sounds got mixed up. Mia played soft, peep-peep, like a gentle breeze. Bo pounded louder and louder, THUMP THUMP THUMP, like thunder. Rocky tried to keep a beat, tap tap, but the sounds crashed together.

“Too loud!” squeaked Mia, covering her ears.

“Too soft!” Bo said, frowning. “No one hears my drum.”

Rocky’s ears drooped. His chest felt tight, like a drum pulled too hard. The room didn’t feel friendly anymore. The bright scarves looked messy. The xylophone sounded like clinks and clanks.

“This is a problem,” Rocky said softly. “We have a conflict.” He used a big word he had heard in music class. “That means we want different things.”

Mia nodded. “I want soft.”

Bo crossed his arms. “I want loud.”

Lila put down her brush. “I want to paint a happy song.”

Rocky took a deep breath. He smelled the wood and the paint. He felt his drumsticks smooth and cool in his paws. “We can fix this,” he said. “We can listen. We can take turns. We can find a plan.”

“Okay,” Mia whispered.

“Okay,” Bo said, still a little grumpy.

Rocky smiled. “First, let’s try soft. We all play soft together. Then we try loud together. Then we mix them, soft then loud.” He tapped the drumsticks together. Click-click. “We will use a signal. When I lift one stick, we play soft. When I lift two sticks, we play loud. Ready?”

Mia squeaked, “Ready.”

Bo took a breath. “Ready.”

Lila brightened. “I’ll paint blue for soft and orange for loud.”

Rocky lifted one stick. Mia played peep-peep. Bo tapped his drum gently, thip-thip. Rocky brushed the drum with the side of his stick, shhh-shhh, like rain on leaves. The room felt calm. Lila painted soft blue waves.

Then Rocky lifted two sticks. Bo smiled and played THUMP-THUMP. Mia drew her bow boldly, vee-vee! Rocky tapped strong, tap-TAP, tap-TAP! Lila painted bright orange dots that made the page sparkle.

Rocky lowered his sticks and wiggled with joy. “Now we mix!” he said. “Soft, then loud, like a surprise.”

He raised one stick. Soft. Blue waves. He raised two sticks. Loud! Orange dots. One stick again. Soft. Blue waves return. Two sticks. Loud! Orange dots dance. The music felt like a story. The sounds matched. The room glowed with happy colors.

Mia’s whiskers trembled with a smile. “I like the soft parts.”

Bo’s ears perked up. “I like the loud parts!”

Rocky laughed. “And I like that we worked together.”

Lila held up her painting. It showed a blue river with bright orange suns. “Our song looks beautiful,” she said.

They played the mix again. Soft, loud, soft, loud. The xylophone chimed like bells. The piano went plink-plonk in time. Rocky kept the beat. Tap-tap. Tap-TAP. He watched his friends’ faces. Mia looked calm. Bo looked proud. Lila hummed as she painted little stars.

When they finished, the room was quiet for a moment. Rocky’s heart felt warm and full, like a drum that had found the right beat.

Mia set down her violin. “Thank you for the plan,” she said to Rocky. “I felt heard.”

Bo nodded. “Me too. I got to be loud, but not too loud.”

Rocky patted the drum. “We listened. We took turns. We made space for each other.” He pointed to the painting. “And we made art from our music.”

Lila smiled. “That’s conflict-resolution,” she said in a tiny voice. “We had a problem. We talked. We tried ideas. We found a way.”

Rocky wagged his tail. “That is our lesson today,” he said. “When we have a conflict, we can breathe, listen, and make a plan together.”

They cleaned up. The scarves were folded in a rainbow stack. The instruments felt cozy in their places. The room smelled like paint and a little like happy.

“Good job, band,” said Rocky.

“Good job, leader,” said Bo.

“Good job, friends,” said Mia.

“Good job, art!” buzzed Lila.

Rocky tapped his sticks one last time. Click-click. “We found the right beat,” he said.

The End.
