# Otter Taps to the Beat

Otter was a playful little otter with bright eyes and quick paws. Today, Otter padded into the musical practice room. The room smelled like fresh wood and clean strings. Sunlight made warm squares on the floor. Drums, a piano, a shiny flute, and a big round drum called a djembe sat waiting. Soft scarves and small bells lay in a basket.

Otter wiggled with joy. “I want to play everything!” Otter said.

Mr. Kim, the kind music teacher, smiled. “Hello, Otter. Today we will practice listening and following directions. We will also learn a song from another place.”

He pointed to a poster on the wall. It showed a world map with tiny instruments. “This djembe comes from West Africa,” Mr. Kim said. “It makes deep, friendly sounds.”

Otter clapped. “Boom-boom-boom!”

“Let’s try,” said Mr. Kim. “First, we warm up. We take slow breaths. In and out.” He breathed in through his nose. “Smell the flowers.” He breathed out through his mouth. “Blow the bubbles.”

Otter tried. In. Out. Smell. Blow. It felt calm and gentle.

“Now, listen,” said Mr. Kim. He tapped the djembe: boom, boom... boom. The sound was like a heartbeat. “We count one, two, three. Then we rest.”

Otter grabbed two rhythm sticks. Tap-tap—tap! Tap-tap—tap! Otter tried to copy. But the sticks slipped. Tap—clatter! One stick fell to the floor.

Otter’s whiskers drooped. “I can’t do it!” Otter’s chest felt tight and hot.

Mr. Kim knelt beside Otter. “It is okay to feel upset,” he said softly. “Let’s name the feeling.”

“I feel frustrated,” said Otter, with a small frown.

“Good naming,” said Mr. Kim. “When frustration visits, we can help it go away. Would you like a calm plan?”

Otter nodded.

“Step one,” said Mr. Kim. “Pause paws.” He held up a hand. “Stop and still.”

Otter placed both paws in the air and giggled. “Pause paws.”

“Step two: Breathe like before. Smell the flowers. Blow the bubbles.”

Otter breathed slowly. In. Out. In. Out. The tight feeling softened.

“Step three: Try again, slowly. We follow the directions: listen first, then copy.”

Otter’s ears perked up. “I can do that.”

Mr. Kim tapped again: boom, boom... boom. He counted softly, “One, two, three, rest.”

Otter listened. Then tap, tap... tap. The sticks made a light, happy sound. “I did it!”

“You did,” said Mr. Kim. “You followed directions and used your calm plan.”

Ms. Amina walked in with a bright, patterned scarf and a warm smile. “Hello, musicians,” she said. She held a small thumb piano. It pinged with tiny, bell-like notes. “This is a mbira from Zimbabwe. It sings like rain.”

Ping, ping, ping, went the mbira. It sounded cool and shimmery.

Otter’s eyes grew wide. “It’s beautiful!”

Ms. Amina said, “We will play a welcome song. In my language, we say, ‘Siyabonga,’ which means, ‘We thank you.’ Would you like to try?”

Otter repeated slowly, “See-ya-bon-ga.” The word felt warm and friendly. “Siyabonga!”

“Wonderful,” said Ms. Amina. “We start with soft claps. Listen for the pattern. Clap, clap, pause. Clap, clap, pause.”

She clapped, and Mr. Kim tapped the djembe: boom, boom... boom. The room filled with gentle beats, soft pings, and quick claps that echoed like footsteps in sand.

Otter joined in. Clap, clap—oops! Clap, clap—oops! The claps were too fast. Otter’s tail thumped the floor in a rush. Frustration bubbled again, like fizzy pop.

Otter squeezed eyes shut. “I want it right now!”

Ms. Amina knelt. “It’s okay to want it now,” she said. “Music is patient. Let’s use your calm plan.”

Otter whispered, “Pause paws. Breathe. Try again, slowly.”

Together they breathed. In. Out. Then Ms. Amina said, “Let’s say the words while we clap. ‘Clap, clap, pause.’ Words can help our hands.”

“Clap, clap, pause,” Otter said. Clap, clap, pause. The rhythm felt steady, like walking. Otter smiled and wiggled whiskers.

Mr. Kim nodded. “Now add the djembe.” Boom, boom... boom.

Ms. Amina played the mbira. Ping, ping, ping.

They added the word: “Siyabonga.” The sound blended like a soft rainbow of music—deep booms, shiny pings, and careful claps.

The door peeked open. A little turtle rolled in a small maraca. “I can’t shake it right,” Turtle whispered. Turtle’s eyes were watery.

Otter scooted over. “I can help,” Otter said. “When I get frustrated, I do my calm plan.”

Otter showed Turtle. “Pause paws.” They both held still. “Breathe.” They smelled the flowers. They blew the bubbles. Their bodies softened.

“Now we follow directions,” said Otter. “Listen first.” Otter shook the maraca gently: shhh-shhh, pause. “Your turn.”

Turtle copied. Shhh-shhh, pause. Turtle smiled. “I did it!”

“You did!” Otter cheered. “Siyabonga, Turtle, for trying.”

They all played together. Boom, boom... boom. Ping, ping, ping. Clap, clap, pause. Shhh-shhh, pause. The music felt like a friendly hug. The practice room glowed with happy sounds and kind hearts.

When the song ended, Mr. Kim said, “What did we learn today?”

Otter raised a paw. “When I feel frustrated, I can pause, breathe, and try again. I can follow directions. I can learn songs from other places and say new words, like ‘Siyabonga.’ And I can help others.”

Ms. Amina nodded. “Yes. Music connects us. We listen, we learn, and we share.”

Otter’s tail made a gentle swish. “Siyabonga, friends.”

“Siyabonga,” everyone said together.

The End.
