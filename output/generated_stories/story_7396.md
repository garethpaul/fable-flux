# Benny Beaver’s Sunny Backyard Story

In a sunny backyard, a busy beaver named Benny woke up early. The grass felt cool under his paws. The sun felt warm on his whiskers. Birds chirped sweet little songs. “Today,” said Benny, “I will write my very first story!”

Benny set out paper on a smooth, flat rock. He held a short pencil in his small, careful paws. He took a deep breath. “I can do this,” he whispered. “I will write a happy story.”

Scratch, scratch went the pencil. But after one word, Benny stopped. “Hmm,” he said. “What comes next?” His head felt busy, like buzzing bees. He wanted his ideas to line up, but they swirled like leaves in the wind.

Just then, his friend Pip the sparrow fluttered down. “Good morning, Benny! What are you doing?”

“I’m writing a story,” Benny said. “But my ideas are wiggly. They won’t sit still.”

Pip peeped and tilted her head. “Maybe sing a calm song first. Music helps me think.”

Benny nodded. He closed his eyes and hummed a gentle tune. Hum, hum, hum. The sound was soft like a blanket. He tapped his tail—tap, tap—on the rock, slow and steady. The warm sun, the sweet smell of flowers, and the soft song made his mind feel smooth, like water in a pond.

Benny smiled. “Thank you, Pip. I feel calm now.” He wrote two more words. Scratch, scratch. “Oh! But now I’m stuck again,” he sighed.

Pip chirped, “Try a plan. First, say your story out loud. Beginning, middle, end!”

Benny sat up tall. “Beginning: I wake up in the sunny backyard. Middle: I meet friends. End: I share my story.” He tapped his tail again. Tap, tap. The plan felt good.

Benny wrote the beginning. “I wake up. The sun is warm.” He smelled the fresh earth. He heard the birds sing. He added those sounds and smells to his words. Scratch, scratch. “This is fun,” he said.

Soon, Lulu the ladybug crawled onto the rock. “Hello, Benny! What a nice paper. What are you doing?”

“I’m writing! But I’m slow,” Benny said. “I want to be fast.”

Lulu smiled kindly. “Slow is okay. Slow can be careful. Careful can be beautiful.”

Benny took a calm breath. In, out. He wrote slowly and carefully. He wrote about Pip’s song and Lulu’s tiny red wings. He felt proud. He felt brave.

Then a breeze puffed by. Whoosh! The paper slipped. “Oh no!” cried Benny. The pages fluttered across the grass like white butterflies.

Benny’s heart thumped. “My story!” He wanted to grab and run, but he stopped. “Calm first,” he told himself. He hummed his gentle tune. Hum, hum, hum. Tap, tap. His tail kept a beat. The beat helped him think.

“Let’s solve this,” Benny said. “Problem-solving time!” Pip flew to spot the pages. Lulu pointed with her tiny feet. “There!” “And there!” Benny gathered each page and counted. “One, two, three… All here!”

“How can I stop the wind next time?” Benny wondered.

He looked around the sunny backyard. He saw smooth pebbles by the flower pot. He saw clothespins by the line. He smiled. “I have an idea!”

Benny placed pebbles on the corners of the paper. He clipped the top with two clothespins. “Now my pages won’t fly,” he said.

Pip clapped her wings. Lulu did a tiny hop. “Great problem-solving!” they cheered.

Benny went back to writing. Scratch, scratch. He wrote how the wind tried to dance with his story. He wrote how he stayed calm and used a plan. He wrote how friends helped.

Soon, Benny reached the end. He read his story out loud, with a little music beat—tap, tap, tap. His voice was warm and happy.

“Beginning: I wake up in the sunny backyard.
Middle: I get stuck, sing, plan, and fix the flying pages.
End: I finish my story and share it with friends.”

Pip chirped, “Beautiful!”

Lulu whispered, “Careful and kind.”

Benny’s cheeks felt warm with joy. “Thank you,” he said. “Writing is like building. One word, then another word. A plan helps. Music helps. Calm helps. Friends help, too.”

Pip asked, “Can we hear the title?”

Benny grinned. “The title is: ‘My Sunny Backyard Story.’”

They all hummed together. Hum, hum, hum. The tune was soft and happy. It felt like sunlight on water. Benny placed his story in a safe basket. He patted it gently with his paw.

Benny looked at the bright sky. “Today I learned something big,” he said. “When I write, I can be calm. I can make a plan. I can solve problems. I can use music to help my brain. And I can finish what I start.”

Pip nodded. Lulu sparkled. The backyard glowed golden and sweet. Benny felt proud and peaceful.

“Tomorrow,” Benny said, “I will write another story.”

“And we will sing with you,” said Pip and Lulu together.

Benny smiled. “One word at a time.”

The End.
