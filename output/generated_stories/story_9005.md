# Momo Makes It Right

Momo was a courageous monkey with bright eyes and gentle hands. Momo loved a special place: a cozy closet hideaway. The closet smelled like warm wood and clean soap. Soft scarves hung like jungle vines. A tiny lamp glowed like a firefly.

“I’m brave,” Momo said, patting his chest. “I can build big things!”

One sunny morning, Momo wanted to build a book boat inside the closet. He pulled books, boxes, and blankets into a pile. Thump. Rustle. Plop.

“I’ll finish fast,” Momo whispered, tapping his toes. “No time for cleaning.”

Momo stacked a box. He slid a book. He tugged a scarf. The scarf slipped and swished. A tower of cups wobbled. Clatter! The cups fell and rolled under the shoes. A cold breeze whooshed from the door. A tiny leaf blew in and landed on a fuzzy sock.

Momo frowned. “Oops.”

He kept building. “I’ll clean later,” he said, waving his hand. “I want my boat now.”

Soon the closet was messy. Crinkly paper wrinkled underfoot. Bits of string tangled like spaghetti. A crayon rolled away and bumped the wall. Tap, tap.

Momo climbed into his book boat. “Time to sail!”

But the lamp light was blocked by the mess. The boat wiggled on a slippery scarf. Momo slid. “Whoa!” He bumped a box. Thud. A small glass jar tipped over. It was Momo’s seed jar for planting. Tiny seeds sprinkled everywhere—on the rug, in the shoe, and under the door.

“Oh no,” Momo whispered, feeling a twist in his tummy. “The seeds are for the garden. I made a mess. I didn’t care for our things or our world.”

Just then, Grandma Monkey peeked in. She sniffed the air. “I smell crayon and dust,” she said kindly. “Are you okay, Momo?”

Momo’s ears drooped. “Grandma, I rushed. I didn’t clean. I spilled the seeds. I feel sad.”

Grandma leaned down and touched Momo’s paw. “Feeling sad means you care. That is brave, too,” she said. “What can you do to make it right?”

Momo took a deep breath. He listened to the quiet closet. He could hear a soft tick-tick of the hall clock. He could hear the whisper of the lamp. He felt the rough rug and the cool leaves on his toes.

“I can make amends,” Momo said. “I can fix what I did.”

“How will you start?” asked Grandma.

Momo looked around. “First, I will save the seeds. Seeds are important for Earth,” he said. “They grow into plants and give us clean air and shade.”

He found the little thread broom. Sweep, sweep. He sang a soft clean-up song. “Little seeds, come back to your jar.” He picked seeds gently with sticky tape. Tap, tap. He smiled when each seed went ping into the jar.

Grandma nodded. “That shows care for the environment.”

“Next,” said Momo, “I will sort the cups and put them in the bin. Cups in the cup spot. Books in the book spot.” Clink, clink. Thump. He folded the scarves and hung them like bright rainbow vines. Swish.

Momo felt the warm lamp on his cheek. The closet smelled fresh again. He noticed the time. Tick-tock. “I need a plan,” he said. “I will build and then clean. I will use time in little steps.”

Grandma smiled. “That is time management,” she said. “You can practice.”

Momo held up three fingers. “Step one: Build for a little while. Step two: Stop and tidy. Step three: Play and then put things away.”

He set the clock to a soft ding every few minutes. Ding. Momo stacked two boxes. Ding. He put away the extra box. Ding. He tied string, then rolled up extra string. He practiced and practiced. It felt slow but good. His heart felt light.

Soon the book boat was strong. The edges were smooth. The scarf was tied like a sail. Momo climbed in. The boat did not wiggle. He could see the lamp light like sunshine on water.

Grandma clapped softly. “You built it well.”

Momo nodded. “I made amends. I cleaned the spill. I cared for the seeds. I used time in steps. I practiced. Now my play space is safe and kind to Earth.”

He opened the closet door and looked at the hallway plant. He carried a few saved seeds outside to the garden bed. The soil felt cool and crumbly. Pat, pat. Momo planted them with care and a little water. Drip, drip.

“I’m sorry, seeds,” Momo whispered. “I will help you grow.”

Back in the closet, Momo invited Grandma. “Come sail with me!”

They sat in the boat. “Whoosh!” said Momo. “We sail to Seed Island!”

Grandma laughed. “And we leave no mess on the ocean!”

Momo felt proud and calm. The closet hideaway was neat, bright, and friendly. The soft rug tickled his toes. The lamp hummed.

He whispered, “When I make a mess, I can make amends. I can care for my world. I can use my time in steps. I can practice and get better.”

Grandma squeezed his paw. “That’s the brave way.”

The End.
