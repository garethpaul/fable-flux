# Ollie Otter and the Gentle Hands

Ollie was a playful otter with bright eyes and quick paws. Today, Ollie visited Grandpa Gus’s garage workshop. The air smelled like warm wood and orange soap. Sunlight made soft squares on the floor. Tools hung on the wall—big, small, shiny, and quiet. A tiny fan hummed. Tick, tick, tick went the old clock.

“Welcome, Ollie!” said Grandpa Gus. He wore blue overalls and a kind smile. “We will build a toy boat today.”

“A boat!” Ollie clapped his paws. “Can I use the red screwdriver? It looks so cool!”

Grandpa nodded. “Yes, but we’ll take turns. We will share the tools, and we will use gentle hands.”

Ollie bounced on his toes. His whiskers twitched with excitement. On the workbench sat smooth wood pieces, a little sail, and safe, kid-sized tools. Ollie picked up the red screwdriver. It felt heavy and important.

“Twist, twist,” Ollie said as he turned a tiny screw. The wood squeaked a little. He loved the sound. “Listen! It sings!”

Grandpa chuckled. “Nice and slow. Twist and breathe.”

Just then, Ollie’s friend Mia the mouse peeked in. She wore a tiny yellow apron. “Hi! Can I help?” she asked in a squeaky, cheerful voice.

“Yes,” said Grandpa. “Ollie is using the red screwdriver. Mia, you may sand the wood with this soft paper.”

Mia stroked the wood with the sandpaper. Shh, shh, shh. The wood felt smoother and smoother. Ollie watched. He liked the red screwdriver so much that he did not want to let go.

“May I try the screwdriver?” Mia asked after a while.

Ollie’s paws tightened. “But I’m still using it,” he said. He looked at the shiny red handle. He felt a big bubble in his tummy. The bubble got hot. His face felt warm. His tail flicked.

“I really want a turn,” said Mia softly.

“No!” Ollie shouted. He slammed the screwdriver on the bench. Clack! The sound was loud. The little sail fluttered. The clock went tick, tick, tick, but now it sounded too fast. Ollie’s chest felt tight. His eyes felt prickly.

Grandpa Gus walked over and knelt down. He placed a gentle hand near Ollie’s paw. “Ollie,” he said in a calm voice, “I see a big feeling.”

“It’s my screwdriver,” Ollie said, tears wobbling. “I love it. I don’t want to share.” His words came out fast and bumpy.

Grandpa nodded. “It is hard to share something special. Your feeling is anger. It feels hot and big, like a puffed-up balloon. We can help it get small.”

“How?” Ollie sniffled. He rubbed his nose.

“Use your gentle hands and your calm breath,” said Grandpa. He held up his hands. “Let’s try: Stop. Breathe. Name the feeling. Choose a kind action.”

Ollie tried. He set his paws on his belly. Stop. Breathe in—slow like smelling warm cocoa. Breathe out—slow like blowing a bubble. In… and out. He whispered, “I feel angry.”

The bubble in his tummy started to shrink. His face cooled. His tail rested.

Grandpa smiled. “Good job. Now choose a kind action.”

Ollie looked at Mia. Her ears were droopy. Her apron had a tiny sawdust dot. Ollie felt a soft spot in his heart. “I can share,” he said. “Mia, you can have a turn with the red screwdriver for two songs of the tick-tock. I will sand while I wait.”

Mia’s ears perked up. “Thank you, Ollie! That is kind.”

They switched. Shh, shh, shh went the sandpaper in Ollie’s paws. Tick, tick, tick went the clock. Mia turned the screw. Twist, twist. She hummed a little song. Ollie listened and breathed. The garage smelled like wood and sunshine and a tiny bit of orange soap.

When the clock ticked through two songs, Mia smiled. “Your turn, Ollie!”

Ollie took the screwdriver gently. “Thank you for waiting,” he said, and Mia nodded.

They worked together. Grandpa held the boat while Ollie and Mia added the sail. The fan hummed. The tools were quiet and shiny on the wall. Their boat looked smooth and brave.

“Ready to test?” asked Grandpa. He brought a big blue tub of water. He set it on the floor. Slosh, slosh.

Ollie placed the boat on the water. It bobbed and floated. “We did it!” he cheered.

Mia clapped. “It floats! Teamwork!”

Grandpa Gus winked. “Sharing tools and using gentle hands made a fine boat. And Ollie, you used strong calm.”

Ollie smiled. “When I felt angry, I stopped. I breathed. I named my feeling. Then I chose a kind action. Sharing helped my anger get small.”

Mia nodded. “Your kind choice helped me, too. Thank you.”

Ollie patted the boat. The wood felt smooth and warm. “Next time I feel mad,” he said, “I will use calm breath and gentle hands.”

Grandpa put a sticker on the boat: Team Otter-Mouse. “Compassion means caring about how others feel,” he said. “You showed compassion today.”

Ollie’s heart felt light. He looked at the red screwdriver. It was still shiny and still special, and now it was also a sharing tool.

“Let’s build again tomorrow,” Ollie said.

“Yes!” Mia squeaked.

The boat bobbed on the blue water. The fan hummed. The clock ticked a happy tick, tick, tick. Ollie took a slow breath and smiled.

The End.
