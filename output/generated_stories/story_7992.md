# Gerald's Listening Lab

Gerald the giraffe loved his special basement lab. It had colorful buttons that beeped, soft cushions for thinking, and a big clock that went "tick-tock, tick-tock." Today, Gerald was working on something very important.

"Hmm," Gerald said, tapping his long chin with his hoof. "My friends keep arguing during soccer games. How can I help them?"

Gerald's lab smelled like fresh flowers and had warm, golden lights. He pulled out his special notebook and wrote with a big purple crayon: "LISTENING PROJECT."

Just then, he heard footsteps on the stairs. "Thump, thump, thump!"

"Gerald!" called Benny the bunny. "Can you help us? We were playing soccer, but Tommy Tiger got mad and ran away!"

"And Lucy Lion is crying!" added Sally the squirrel, her voice wobbly.

Gerald's heart felt heavy. He could see that his friends looked sad and confused. Their ears drooped, and their eyes were teary.

"Come sit on my thinking cushions," Gerald said gently. "Tell me what happened."

Benny flopped down on a blue cushion. "Tommy wanted to be goalie, but Lucy wanted to be goalie too. They started yelling, and nobody listened to anybody!"

"That sounds frustrating," Gerald said, nodding his long neck. "What did you feel when they were yelling?"

"Scared," whispered Sally. "And sad. I wanted to play, but the yelling made my tummy feel funny."

Gerald's eyes lit up. "I have an idea! Let's use my Listening Timer!" He pointed to a special hourglass filled with sparkly sand. "When someone talks, we watch the sand fall. That person gets to share their feelings without anyone interrupting."

The friends walked back upstairs together. They found Tommy sitting under a tree, sniffling. Lucy was nearby, wiping her eyes.

"Tommy! Lucy!" Gerald called softly. "We want to try something new."

Gerald explained about the Listening Timer. "Tommy, you go first. Tell us how you felt."

Tommy held the timer and watched the pretty sand fall. "I felt angry because I really wanted to try being goalie. I practice catching at home every day."

When the sand finished falling, Gerald said, "Thank you for sharing, Tommy. Lucy, now it's your turn."

Lucy took the timer. "I felt sad because I wanted to be goalie too. I've been practicing my jumps to catch high balls."

"Wow," said Gerald. "You both want the same thing! You both love goalkeeping!"

Benny's ears perked up. "What if you take turns? Tommy can be goalie for ten minutes, then Lucy for ten minutes!"

"The timer can help us keep track!" added Sally, clapping her paws.

Tommy and Lucy looked at each other. "That sounds fair," Lucy said with a small smile.

"Yeah!" agreed Tommy. "And maybe Lucy can teach me her jumping trick!"

"And Tommy can show me his catching practice!" Lucy giggled.

Gerald felt warm and happy inside. His listening experiment worked! "When we listen with our hearts and ears, we can solve problems together," he said.

The friends played soccer all afternoon, taking turns and cheering for each other. The basement lab's clock kept ticking peacefully as Gerald watched from his window, proud of how well his friends were listening and sharing.

That night, Gerald wrote in his notebook: "Listening helps friends understand each other's feelings. When we listen, we can find solutions that make everyone happy."

The End.
