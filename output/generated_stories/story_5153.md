# Ellie’s Attic School

Ellie was a gentle elephant calf with soft gray ears and bright, curious eyes. She lived in a tall house with a quiet attic that smelled like old paper and cedar wood. One sunny afternoon, Ellie climbed the creaky stairs. Creak, creak, creak. The attic was cool and still. Tiny dust sparkles danced in the sunbeams.

“Oh,” Ellie whispered. “It’s so peaceful up here.”

She flapped her small ears and looked around. There were wooden boxes, a soft old rug, and a stack of cardboard tubes. There were buttons in a tin that went tinka-tinka when she shook it. There were old books with big pictures of birds, trees, and rivers.

Ellie touched a book with her trunk. “Pictures!” she said. She opened it carefully. The pages showed a blue river and a green forest. She saw animals drinking clean water. On another page, there was a bridge that helped animals cross safely.

“I want to build a bridge too,” Ellie said. “I can make a tiny river school right here!”

She set the book open on the floor. “I will learn. I will build. I will help,” she hummed. Her voice was soft and happy.

Ellie began to gather things. She rolled the cardboard tubes with her trunk. Thump, thump. She stacked small boxes like blocks. She found fabric scraps that felt smooth and cool. She listened to the attic. It was quiet, but Ellie could hear the soft whoosh of her own breath and the gentle rustle of the pages.

“I will make a river,” Ellie decided. She laid out a long blue scarf on the rug. It looked like water. “Swish, swish,” she said, sliding her trunk over it.

Then Ellie put two boxes on each side of the scarf. “These are river banks,” she explained. “And this,” she lifted a wide, flat book cover, “will be my bridge.”

She tried to lay the book cover across the boxes, but it slid. Clatter! The cover fell into the “river.”

“Oh no,” said Ellie. Her ears drooped. “This is tricky.”

Ellie took a deep breath. “I can learn,” she told herself. “I can try.”

She looked at the bridge picture again. “The bridge needs strong sides,” she said. “Strong sides help it stay up.” She peeked into a wooden box and found two long cardboard strips. They felt stiff under her trunk. She also found some soft string and a roll of tape that made a sticky sound, rip-rip.

Ellie tried again. She taped the strips to the sides of the book cover. Rip, smooth, press. She put the boxes closer together so the bridge would not stretch too far. “Strong and steady,” she said. “We can do it.”

She lifted the bridge with her trunk. “Easy,” she whispered. She set it carefully on the boxes. This time it stayed. Ellie smiled. “It works!”

Tap, tap—she put buttons on the bridge to test it. The buttons did not fall. “Safe,” she said. “Safe is smart.”

Ellie looked back at the book with the forest. She saw a picture of a little sign. The sign said, “Please protect our water.” Ellie sounded out the letters slowly. “Pro-tect. That means take care,” she said. She felt warm and proud. “I am learning!”

She made tiny trees from paper tubes and green fabric. “Trees help the river,” she said. “They keep soil from falling in. That keeps water clean.” She pressed the pretend trees into the rug. The rug was soft and bumpy like earth.

“Hello, river school!” Ellie called. “Today we learn about water and bridges.”

She picked up a small stuffed mouse from a box. “Squeak!” Ellie said in a tiny voice. “Is the bridge safe?”

“Yes,” Ellie answered gently. “I checked with buttons. I made strong sides. I made it shorter. That solves the problem.”

“Squeak,” the mouse said. “How do we keep the river clean?”

Ellie looked at the blue scarf river. She smoothed it with her trunk. “We do not throw trash in the water,” she said. “We plant trees. We save water. We use just what we need.” She poured the buttons into the tin again. Tinka-tinka. “We put things back where they belong.”

Just then, the attic window made a soft whoo as the wind moved outside. The light felt warm on Ellie’s back. Her heart felt big and calm.

From downstairs, Ellie heard a voice. “Ellie! Snack time!” It was her caregiver’s voice, kind and sweet.

“Coming!” Ellie called. She looked at her little river school. She wanted to keep building, but she also wanted to share what she learned.

She took a deep breath. “I will make a sign,” she said. She found a small card and a crayon. She wrote slowly, saying each sound. “Ri-ver Skool.” She giggled and fixed it. “River School.” Then she drew a tiny leaf and a drop of water.

Ellie put the sign by the bridge. She patted the rug. “Stay safe, bridge. Be strong, river,” she said. She put the buttons back in the tin and closed the books gently, but she left one page open so she could show it later.

Before she went downstairs, Ellie whispered to the mouse, “Today we learned to build, to think, and to care.” The mouse gave a happy “Squeak!”

Ellie smiled and flapped her ears. “I can learn,” she said. “I can solve problems. I can help the Earth.”

She felt proud, and the quiet attic felt proud with her.

The End.
