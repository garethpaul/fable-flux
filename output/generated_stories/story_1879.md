# Fawn’s Balcony Band

The gentle deer fawn peeked over the little railing of the balcony garden. The morning sun felt warm on her nose. She sniffed the air. It smelled like mint leaves and wet soil. Birds chirped on the roof. A soft breeze tickled her ears.

“I love this garden,” the fawn whispered. “Today, I will make music!”

She found a row of tiny flower pots. Tap, tap! Each pot made a different sound. The small pot went ping. The big pot went pong. The metal watering can went ding-dong when she bumped it with her hoof.

“This is fun!” the fawn said. Her tail wiggled. “I will play a big concert!”

She looked around. Neighbors lived close by. There was a baby sleeping next door. A cat curled on a warm brick. A grandpa sat on a chair, reading a book. Bees hummed in the flowers.

The fawn felt excited. She thumped her hoof on the wooden floor. Thump-thump! She shook a string of shells. Clack-clack! She blew across a bottle. Whooo!

“Ta-da!” she sang. “My balcony band!”

But the sounds got very loud. Thump-thump-thump! Clack-clack-clack! Whoooo-oooo! A bird flapped away. The baby in the next home woke and began to cry. “Waaa!”

The cat’s ears flattened. The grandpa frowned and closed his book.

The fawn’s ears drooped. “Oh no,” she said softly. “I did not think about others.” Her heart felt small. The baby cried louder. The fawn felt a twist in her tummy.

Grandpa stood and came to the railing. He spoke gently. “Little fawn, your music is lively. But some neighbors need quiet. Can you make a plan?”

The fawn nodded. “I can make amends,” she said. She took a deep breath. She walked to the neighbor’s door and tapped very softly.

The baby’s mother opened the door a tiny bit. The baby sniffled on her shoulder.

“I am sorry,” said the fawn in a quiet voice. “My music woke the baby. I will play softer now.”

The mother smiled. “Thank you for caring,” she whispered. “The baby will nap soon.”

The fawn turned to the cat. “I am sorry for the loud sounds,” she said.

The cat yawned and blinked. “Mrrr,” he purred, and curled back up.

The fawn looked at Grandpa. “I want to make good music,” she said. “Music should help, not bother.”

Grandpa nodded. “That is civic responsibility,” he said. “We share this place. We respect each other. We choose sounds that fit the time and the people.”

The fawn thought. “I can make a quiet band,” she said. “A gentle band.”

She listened to the garden. Bees hummed. Leaves rustled. A wind chime sang ting-ting-ting. A snail slid across a pot with a tiny rasp. A ladybug tapped the railing with tiny feet: tip-tip.

The fawn smiled. “I hear soft music already.”

She rolled a little towel and set it under the flower pots. “This will make the taps soft,” she said. She put a small leaf on her hoof. Tap… tap… The sound became gentle, like rain on a window.

She filled the bottle with a little more water. She blew. Hooo… It sounded like a quiet moon song.

She tied a ribbon to a stick and swished it in the air. Swish… swish… The ribbon made the breeze dance.

Grandpa sat back down and listened. The baby slept again. The cat purred. The birds returned, hopping on the railing.

The fawn whispered, “This is my soft concert.”

She began to play:
- Tap… tap… tap…
- Ting… ting… ting…
- Hooo… hooo…
- Swish… swish…

She hummed a melody like honey. “Mm-mm-mmm,” she sang. She felt calm and proud.

A small snail peeked out. “Nice song,” he said in a tiny voice.

“Thank you,” the fawn whispered.

When the song ended, Grandpa clapped very quietly. “Bravo, little fawn,” he said. “You made music and you cared for others.”

The fawn’s chest felt warm. “I learned something,” she said. “Music is not only loud or big. Music can be gentle. Music can help people rest. Music can bring smiles. And if I make a mistake, I can make amends.”

She looked at a little sign on the balcony wall. It showed a sun and a moon. “I can make a plan,” she said. “Lively music in the day, soft music at nap time, and very quiet music at night.”

Grandpa nodded. “That shows respect.”

The fawn drew a tiny poster with crayons. It said: “Balcony Band: Soft at Nap Time. Ask Before Loud.” She taped it by the railing with a small piece of washi tape. The tape smelled like strawberries.

The baby’s mother waved from her door. “Thank you,” she whispered.

“You’re welcome,” said the fawn. Her eyes sparkled. She lifted the ribbon and played one more soft swish. The wind chime answered, ting.

The fawn looked at the gardens below. She felt part of something big and kind. She had made music. She had made amends. She had cared for her neighbors.

She smiled. “That is a good song,” she said, “a song of caring.”

The End.
