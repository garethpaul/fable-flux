# The Fawn Who Found Her Words

In a quiet attic, soft dust floated like tiny snow. Sunbeams slipped through a small window and made warm, golden squares on the floor. Boxes sat like little houses. A gentle deer fawn named Fern tiptoed on her small hooves. Tap, tap, tap. The attic smelled like old books and pine wood.

Fern found a big, flat box. Inside were paper, a pencil, and shiny stickers shaped like stars. “Oh!” Fern whispered. “I want to write a story.” Her ears flicked with joy. Her heart felt bubbly.

She pressed the paper flat. She held the pencil in her mouth carefully. “I will write about friends,” she said. “I will write about being kind.” She made one line. Scritch. She made one curl. Scratch. But then she stopped.

Fern’s tail drooped. “I don’t know what to write next,” she sighed. “My words are hiding.” Her chest felt tight, and her eyes felt warm. She took a slow breath. In and out. “I feel stuck,” she said softly. “I feel a little sad.”

Just then, a small gray mouse peeked from behind a book. “Squeak! Hello, Fern,” said the mouse. “I’m Miri. What are you doing?”

“I want to write a story,” said Fern. “But my words are shy.”

Miri scampered closer. “We can find your words together,” she said. “Let’s listen.” The attic was still. Then they heard it: tap, tap, drip. A tiny roof drip made music in a metal pan. “Drip like a drum,” said Miri.

Fern smiled. “I can write that.” She wrote, “Drip, drip, drum.”

A soft flutter came from the window. A gold moth landed on the paper. “Hello,” hummed the moth. “I am Luna. Your letters look like tiny paths.”

Fern’s ears perked up. “Luna, what do you think belongs in a story?”

“Light,” Luna said. “And feelings. I feel calm when I see this sunbeam.” She danced in the golden light.

Fern wrote, “Light and calm.”

Miri pointed to a wooden chest. “What about sounds?” she asked. Miri tapped the chest. Tap-tap. Tap-tap. “I feel excited when I hear tapping.”

Fern wrote, “Tap-tap. Excited.” She paused. “Sometimes I feel shy, too.”

“That is okay,” said Miri. “Feelings can be friends.”

Fern blinked. “Feelings can be friends,” she repeated. “I like that.”

A soft hoot drifted from the rafters. An old owl, Orin, blinked his kind eyes. “Hoo,” said Orin. “I heard writing.” He landed gently near Fern. “When I write, I use small steps,” he said. “First I plan. Then I write. Then I read. Then I fix.”

Fern tilted her head. “Small steps,” she whispered. “Plan, write, read, fix.” The words felt safe.

“Let’s plan,” said Orin. “Who is in your story?”

“Friends,” said Fern. “A fawn, a mouse, a moth, and an owl.”

“Where?” asked Orin.

“In a quiet attic with warm light,” said Fern.

“What happens?” Orin asked.

Fern’s tail gave a happy wiggle. “They feel stuck. They listen. They share. They help. They write.”

“How do they feel?” Orin asked.

“Sad,” said Fern. “Then calm. Then excited. Then proud.”

“Good plan,” said Orin.

Fern began to write. Scritch-scratch. “Once there was a gentle fawn in a quiet attic,” she said as she wrote. “Her words were shy. She told a mouse, a moth, and an owl. They listened to drips and taps and light. They felt sad, then calm, then excited. They wrote together.”

She stopped and read aloud. Her voice was soft but steady. The attic listened, too. Drip, drip, drum. Tap-tap. Flutter.

Miri clapped her tiny paws. “Let’s fix little bits,” she said. “Add how we helped.”

Luna hummed, “Add the warm sun.”

Orin nodded. “Add your heart.”

Fern added, “They took small steps. They shared feelings. They took turns. The sun made a golden hug.”

She put a star sticker at the end. “I wrote my story,” Fern said. Her eyes shone. “We wrote it together.”

Then Fern looked at Miri. The mouse’s whiskers drooped. “What is wrong?” asked Fern.

Miri sighed. “I want to write, too. But my paws are tiny. My letters wobble.”

Fern felt her heart grow warm. “We can help,” she said. “I can hold the paper. Luna can light the page. Orin can read the words. You can say the story.”

Miri’s eyes brightened. “Squeak-yes!”

They worked together. Miri spoke slowly. “Once there was a brave mouse.” Fern wrote the words. Luna glowed. Orin cheered, “Hoo-hoo!” When Miri felt stuck, she took a deep breath. “I feel nervous,” she said. “I need time.”

“We can wait,” said Fern. They waited. The attic felt kind and safe.

At last, Miri smiled. “I am ready.” She finished her story. They put a star sticker on it, too.

Fern looked at the two stories. Her heart felt big and light. “Writing is sharing,” she said. “Writing is feelings. Writing is small steps. Writing is helping each other.”

Orin puffed his feathers. “And writing is practice,” he added gently. “Every day, a little bit.”

Luna twirled in the sunbeam. “Every day, a little light,” she sang.

Miri hugged Fern’s leg. “Thank you for helping,” she squeaked.

Fern nuzzled her friends. “Thank you for helping me,” she said. “We can write more tomorrow.”

The sunbeam moved, and the attic grew soft and sleepy. The papers rustled like leaves. The friends felt calm and proud. Their stories sat side by side, shining with star stickers.

Fern whispered, “I found my words.” She smiled. “We found them together.”

The End.
