# Momo’s Pantry Plan

Momo was a small, courageous monkey with bright eyes and quick hands. One sunny morning, he tiptoed into the pantry. It smelled like sweet bananas and warm bread. The shelves were tall, the jars were shiny, and the floor was cool on Momo’s feet.

“Oh my,” Momo whispered. “My friends are hungry. We need snacks for our picnic!”

But there was a problem. The fruit basket was on the top shelf. The peanut jar was tight. The crackers were way in the back. Momo stretched up high on his toes. He wiggled his fingers. He still could not reach.

Momo took a deep breath. “I can be brave and I can be smart,” he said softly. “I can try a new idea.”

Just then, his friend Pip the mouse peeked in. “Squeak! Momo, do you need help?”

“Yes, please,” Momo said. “We need snacks, but the shelf is too high.”

Pip sniffed the air. “I smell bananas,” he said with a smile. “We can find a way.”

Momo looked around the pantry. He saw a wooden spoon, a soft towel, a little step stool, and some rubber bands in a jar. He also saw a cardboard box and two empty paper rolls.

Momo’s eyes sparkled. “Let’s make a plan,” he said. “Let’s use what we have. We can be inventors!”

Pip clapped his tiny paws. “An inven-what?”

“Inventors,” Momo said. “We make new tools to solve problems. That is called innovation. It means trying new ideas to help.”

Momo put the step stool near the shelf. He climbed up, but he still could not reach the fruit basket. “Hmm,” he hummed. “We need one more idea.”

He tied the wooden spoon to the end of one paper roll with a rubber band. He slipped the second paper roll on top, making a longer stick. “This is my long reacher,” Momo said. “I made it from things we have.”

Pip’s whiskers twitched. “It looks fun!”

Momo stood on the step stool and carefully used the long reacher to nudge the fruit basket forward. “Slide, slide, little basket,” he said gently. The basket moved. Momo felt proud and careful. The shelf felt smooth under his hand. The paper rolls felt light. The spoon made a soft tap on the wicker basket.

“Almost there,” Momo said. With a tiny push, the basket came close enough. Momo lifted it down safely. “We did it!”

Pip cheered. “Innovation! New idea!”

Next, they needed crackers. The box was in the back and kept getting stuck. Momo thought again. He spread the soft towel on the shelf like a tiny road. He used the spoon to pull the cracker box onto the towel. “Now, slide,” he said. He gently pulled the towel. The box slid smoothly, whispering shhh-shhh. Momo smiled. “A towel can be a slide. That is innovation, too.”

Pip squeaked, “Smart and safe!”

Last, they needed peanut butter. The jar lid was tight. Momo tried to twist it. “Oof!” It did not move. He did not give up. He wrapped a rubber band around the lid to make it less slippery. He held the jar with the towel so it would not slide. Twist, twist… pop! The lid opened. It smelled nutty and warm.

“We did it together,” Momo said. “We used new ideas. We helped our friends.”

They packed the snacks in the cardboard box: bananas, crackers, and a small jar of peanut butter. Momo added napkins and a little spoon for sharing. He looked around the pantry and put things back. “Being helpful means cleaning up, too,” he said. Pip nodded and put the rubber bands back in their jar.

When they went outside, their friends—Lulu the lamb and Tiko the turtle—were waiting under a shady tree.

“Did you find snacks?” Lulu asked.

“Yes!” Momo said. He told them the story of the long reacher, the towel slide, and the grippy lid trick.

Tiko blinked slowly. “That is very clever,” he said. “You were brave, and you tried new ideas.”

Lulu smiled. “And you were helpful. Thank you.”

They shared the food. The bananas were sweet and soft. The crackers were crunchy. The peanut butter was smooth and sticky. Birds sang. Leaves rustled. Momo felt proud, warm, and happy.

Pip asked, “Momo, what did we learn today?”

Momo thought for a moment. He looked at his friends. He looked at the pantry door. He felt the gentle wind on his fur. “We learned that innovation means trying new ideas to solve problems,” he said. “We learned to be safe, to be brave, and to be helpful. We used what we had. We worked together.”

Lulu bleated, “Innovation can be small or big.”

Tiko added, “And helping friends makes every idea better.”

Momo nodded. “When we meet a problem, we can pause, look, and think. We can make a plan. We can try, try, try. That is how we grow.”

Everyone cheered. They finished their picnic with giggles and big smiles. Momo hugged his friends. He felt ready for the next new idea.

The End.
