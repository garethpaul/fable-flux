# The Treehouse Concert

The sun peeked over the tops of the tall trees. The leaves shivered softly in the morning breeze. A small treehouse sat high and snug in the branches. Inside the cozy treehouse lived a singing bird named Tika.

Tika’s feathers shimmered like little raindrops. She loved to sing. Her voice was bright and warm, like sunshine after rain. Every morning, Tika opened the little window and called, “Good morning, trees! Good morning, sky!”

Trina the squirrel popped her head in. “Hi, Tika! Want to play leaf hop later?” she chattered, her tail flick-flick-flicking.

“After my song,” Tika chirped. “I am practicing for the Treehouse Concert.”

Soon, other friends climbed up the rope ladder. There was Mo the mole with the muddy paws, Pip the raccoon with shiny pebbles, and Lina the ladybug with tiny, tiny feet. They all liked to listen.

Tika took a deep breath. “La-la-la!” Her song floated out the window. It smelled like pine and felt like warm bread. It made everyone smile.

Then a new sound joined the morning—tap, tap, tap. It came from the next branch. A woodpecker named Poko hopped closer. Tap-tap-tap went his beak on the bark.

Tika stopped. “Oh!” she said.

Poko bobbed his head. “Hello! I am Poko. I peck rhythms. Tap-tap-ta-TAP!”

Pip clapped. “Cool beat!”

Tika tried to sing again. “La-la—” But Poko tapped, tap-tap-tap, at the same time. The sounds bumped into each other like shoes in a small basket.

Mo rubbed his nose. “It’s noisy,” he mumbled.

Tika’s chest felt tight. “This is my concert,” she said softly. “I want it to sound smooth.”

Poko puffed his feathers. “This is my music,” he said. “My family pecks. Tap-tap-tap is our song.”

Tika looked at her friends. She felt a little mad and a little sad. The air felt prickly. The treehouse was not comfy anymore.

Trina whispered, “Maybe we can all share?”

Tika shook her head. “But I can’t sing with all that tapping.”

Poko tapped his toe on the branch. “I can’t stop being me.”

Everyone grew quiet. Even the wind held its breath.

Tika looked out at the green leaves. The leaves looked different—some big, some tiny. Some were round, some pointy. They were all part of one tree. Tika listened with her heart. Tap-tap-tap. La-la-la. Two sounds. Two ways to feel the morning.

She took a small breath. “Poko,” she said gently, “I hear you. Your tapping is special to you. My singing is special to me. Right now, they crash. That hurts my ears.”

Poko nodded. “My ears feel funny too when your la-la-la covers my tap.”

Tika fluffed her wings. “What if we take turns?” she asked. “I can sing a little. Then you can tap a little. We can listen to each other. We can learn.”

Poko tilted his head. “Hmm. Take turns. Like one ladder at a time.”

Mo’s whiskers twitched. “We can count!”

Pip jingled his pebbles. “We can clap!”

Lina buzzed softly. “We can cheer!”

Tika smiled. “Let’s try. I will sing for five claps. Then Poko taps for five claps.”

Trina stood tall. “I will clap the beats. Ready?”

“Ready,” said Tika.

“Ready,” said Poko.

Clap, clap, clap, clap, clap.

Tika sang, soft at first. “La, la, la, la, laaa.” Her song was sweet like ripe berries. The air felt smooth again. Everyone’s shoulders relaxed.

Clap, clap, clap, clap, clap.

Poko tapped, bright and quick. Tap-tap-ta-TAP, tap. It sounded like raindrops on the roof. The floor of the treehouse hummed under their paws.

They took turns again and again. Tika listened when Poko tapped. Poko listened when Tika sang. Their faces softened. The air felt happy. The treehouse felt big again.

After a while, Tika had an idea. “Poko,” she asked, “in your home, why do you tap like that?”

Poko’s eyes sparkled. “It is from my grandpa! He taps the ‘forest hello.’ Tap-tap-ta-TAP. It means, ‘I am here. Are you?’”

Tika’s heart warmed. “In my home, we sing a ‘sunrise song.’ It means, ‘Thank you, new day.’”

Trina clapped quietly. “I like learning your ways,” she said.

Pip smiled. “Me too. I feel lucky.”

Tika and Poko looked at each other. “Would you like,” Tika asked, “to try one together? I sing my sunrise line, and you tap your forest hello after?”

Poko grinned. “Yes. I will listen to your sound and make space for mine.”

They tried. La-la-laaa… tap-tap-ta-TAP. The two sounds did not crash now. They danced. They took turns. They hugged the air. The treehouse seemed to glow.

Neighbors gathered on the branches. Blue jays, beetles, a shy hedgehog, even a butterfly family. They listened and swayed. The breeze smelled like sweet sap and flowers.

When the last note faded, the tree was quiet and calm. Then—cheers! Claps! Tiny taps! Everyone smiled.

Tika bowed. “Thank you for listening.”

Poko bowed. “Thank you for sharing.”

Tika looked at her friends. “Today we learned something,” she said. “We learned that we can be different and still be friends. We can take turns. We can listen. That is called tolerance. It means we make room for each other.”

Poko nodded. “And when we listen, we find a way to fit together.”

The sun slipped higher. The leaves whispered. Tika and Poko planned another song. Trina and Pip set up a leaf-hop game below. Everyone went outside to play and climb and laugh in the bright green day.

They had a concert and an outdoor adventure, all in one happy morning. And in the treehouse and the trees, there was room for every voice.

The End.
