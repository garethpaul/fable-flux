# Ellie’s Balcony Garden Game

Ellie was a gentle elephant calf with soft gray ears and a warm, round belly. She lived in a tall building with a sunny balcony garden. The balcony smelled like mint and wet soil. Tiny cherry tomatoes glowed red. Basil leaves tickled her trunk when she sniffed them. Birds chirped from the roof, and a cool breeze brushed her cheeks.

“Good morning, plants,” Ellie said. She gave the basil a gentle puff of water from her little trunk. “Today is game day!”

Every Saturday, Ellie’s neighbors came to the balcony for a friendly sports game. It was a tiny space, so they played gentle games with a soft, squishy ball. Her friends were from many places. Amir wore a bright green cap and liked soccer. Mei had shiny hair clips and liked ribbon dancing. Nana wore a colorful scarf and loved cricket. Luca had fast feet and loved basketball. They all shared snacks and songs from home. The balcony buzzed with happy voices.

“Let’s play Garden Goal!” Amir said. He put two flower pots down to make a small goal. “We pass the ball and try to score.”

Ellie’s ears wiggled with excitement. “I’ll try my best,” she said.

They started slowly. Tap, pass, giggle. The squishy ball rolled over the wooden boards. The boards felt warm under their feet. Birds cheeped, leaves rustled, and someone’s soup from the kitchen smelled spicy and yummy.

Amir passed to Mei. Mei passed to Nana. Nana tapped to Ellie. Ellie lined up her shot with careful little steps. But the ball bumped a tomato pot and rolled away.

“Oh no,” Ellie said, her cheeks warm. “I missed.”

“It’s okay,” said Mei. “Try again.”

They played more. Luca dribbled the ball and passed. Ellie tried again, but the ball bumped her foot and bounced into the mint. The mint leaves shook and the smell got stronger.

Ellie felt a tight knot in her tummy. Her trunk curled in. Heat rose in her face like a little fire. Her heart thumped. Her feet stomped once—thump.

“I can’t do it!” she shouted. Her voice sounded too loud in the small garden. The birds flew up, startled.

Everyone got quiet. Amir’s eyes were kind. “It’s hard sometimes,” he said softly.

Ellie’s ears drooped. She felt angry and sad. Her body felt big and tight like a balloon. She wanted to stomp again.

Then Nana spoke in a gentle voice. “When I feel mad, I take a ‘pause and puff,’” she said. “Back home, we do this in cricket when we miss. Want to try?”

“What is a pause and puff?” Ellie asked. Her voice was small.

Nana smiled. “We stop. We breathe in slow. We breathe out slow. Like blowing a dandelion.”

Mei nodded. “In my dance class, we count. One, two, three. Soft arms. Soft breath.”

Luca added, “My coach says, ‘Shake it out!’ Wiggle your fingers and toes.”

Amir said, “In soccer, we say, ‘Try again, teammate.’ We cheer each other.”

Ellie listened. Her friends had different ways from different places. That felt warm in her heart.

“Okay,” Ellie said. “I will try the pause and puff.”

She closed her eyes. She felt the breeze on her cheeks. She breathed in slow through her trunk—sssssnuff. She breathed out slow—whoooosh. She wiggled her toes. She counted, “One… two… three.”

The tight knot in her tummy softened. The little fire cooled. Her shoulders dropped. The mint smelled fresh. The basil smelled sweet. The boards felt safe under her feet.

Ellie opened her eyes. “Thank you,” she said. “I feel better.”

“Team hug?” Mei asked.

They all did a gentle group hug. “We are a team,” Luca said.

“Let’s try a new way,” Amir said. He moved the goal a little so the ball wouldn’t hit the tomatoes. “In my country, we pass in a triangle. It helps us share.”

They practiced. Tap, pass to the left. Tap, pass to the right. Ellie took her time. When her tummy squeezed again, she whispered, “Pause and puff.” In, out. Wiggle, count. The feeling passed, like a small cloud in a big blue sky.

Soon, it was Ellie’s turn to shoot again. Her friends cheered softly. “You can do it,” said Nana. “We are with you.”

Ellie lined up her shot. She looked at the tiny goal. She breathed in. She breathed out. Then she tapped the ball with her foot. Tap!

The ball rolled smooth between the flower pots. Goal!

“We did it!” Ellie squeaked. She clapped her ears and laughed. Everyone cheered. The birds chirped again, happy this time. The mint leaves danced in the breeze.

They shared snacks from their homes: sweet dates from Amir, crunchy rice crackers from Mei, spicy plantain chips from Nana, and juicy orange slices from Luca. Ellie passed around little cherry tomatoes from the garden. They tasted like sunshine.

“I learned something today,” Ellie said, her eyes bright. “When I feel mad, I can pause and puff. I can shake it out, count, and try again. I can listen to my friends. We can share our ways and play as a team.”

Amir nodded. “Different ways help us all.”

Mei twirled her ribbon. “Soft breath, soft heart.”

Luca dribbled the ball gently. “Teamwork makes it fun.”

Ellie looked at the balcony. The garden looked extra green. The air felt cool and kind. She placed the soft ball back on the shelf and patted the basil.

“Thank you, garden,” she said. “Thank you, friends.”

Everyone smiled. They made a plan to play again next week, to learn new games and new calm breaths from each other.

The End.
