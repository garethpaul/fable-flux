# Squeaky’s Turn at the Table

Squeaky the squirrel lived in a cozy home with a big oak table in the dining room. The table smelled like warm toast and sweet apples. The chairs made tiny squeaks when they moved. Sunlight danced on the shiny spoons.

Today, friends were coming over for a fruit snack party. There was a big bowl of berries, banana slices, crunchy nuts, and tiny cups for water. Squeaky’s fluffy tail flicked with excitement.

“I can’t wait to share!” Squeaky said, patting the table. “I even made little place cards.” He put a leaf at each seat with a name written in crayon.

Soon, friends arrived. Pip the chipmunk, Dot the duckling, and Momo the mouse hopped in. Their noses wiggled at the yummy smells.

“Hi!” said Squeaky. “Welcome to the dining room! We will practice taking turns. We will listen with kind ears. And we will use our voices to say what we need.”

Dot flapped her soft wings. “Quack! I’m hungry!”

Momo sniffed the berries. “I like the blue ones best.”

Pip drummed tiny paws on the chair. “Me first! Me first!”

Squeaky smiled. His heart beat fast, like tiny drum taps. He felt excited and a little nervous. He wanted everyone to feel happy and safe.

Squeaky held up a wooden spoon like a talking stick. “Let’s take turns. When you hold the spoon, you can choose one thing. Then pass it to the next friend.”

Pip grabbed the spoon. “Blueberries!” he cried. His paws scooped a big pile. Blue juice dotted the table like little moons.

Dot took the spoon next. “Quack, banana please.” She took a few slices and slid the spoon to Momo.

Momo held it close. “I want to think.” He sniffed. “Crunchy nuts, please.” He took a tiny handful.

Now it was Squeaky’s turn. He smiled and reached out. But Pip reached too. Pip’s paw bumped Squeaky’s paw. The bowl wobbled. A blueberry rolled and bonked Squeaky’s nose.

Squeaky blinked. He felt a little sting in his chest, like a small pinch. He took a slow breath. He remembered his own rules.

“I can use my voice,” he whispered to himself. He spoke kindly but clearly. “Pip, it’s my turn now. Please wait.”

Pip pulled his paw back. “Oh,” Pip said. “I got too excited. Sorry, Squeaky.”

“Thank you for listening,” Squeaky said. He put one strawberry on his plate. The berry smelled sweet and sunny. The spoon felt smooth in his paw. He passed it to Pip with a smile.

They began to eat. “Mmm,” said Dot. “The berries are juicy!”

“Crunch, crunch,” went Momo.

The dining room filled with happy sounds: little chews, tiny sips, gentle chair squeaks. The window let in a warm breeze that smelled like grass.

Then Dot saw the nuts. “Quack! I don’t like nuts,” she said, scrunching her bill. “They are too hard. Do I have to eat them?”

Squeaky shook his head. “No, Dot. We can be different. That is okay.” He smiled. “Tolerance means we respect what others like and don’t like.”

Momo nodded. “I like nuts, but I will not put them on your plate.”

Dot’s feathers smoothed. “Thank you. I like soft food.”

Pip looked at the bowl again. His paws twitched. “I’m still hungry,” he said. “But I remember taking turns.”

Squeaky pointed to a tiny paper clock he drew. “Let’s practice. We will count to five. Then the next friend gets a turn.”

They all counted together. “One, two, three, four, five.” Pip took two berries. He passed the spoon to Dot without being asked. Dot smiled and took two banana slices, then passed it to Momo, who took one nut and one blueberry.

Squeaky felt warm inside, like a small sun. “We are practicing so well,” he said. “I’m proud of us.”

Suddenly, Momo’s whiskers drooped. “The cup is too far,” he squeaked. “I can’t reach my water.”

Squeaky looked around. The cups were near the center. The table was wide. He had an idea. He grabbed a clean wooden ruler and a cloth. He wrapped the cloth around the ruler tip to make a soft helper. Carefully, he nudged Momo’s cup closer.

“Here you go,” Squeaky said. “I used a tool to help.”

Momo’s eyes shone. “Thank you, Squeaky.”

“Resourceful!” Dot quacked. “That means you find smart ways to help.”

They ate a little more. Then Pip began to bounce again. “I want more strawberries!” he said, reaching across Momo’s plate.

Squeaky held up his paw gently. “Please wait, Pip. Reaching over a friend can be scary for them.” He turned to Momo. “Momo, would you like to say how you feel?”

Momo took a breath. “I feel squished when paws go over my plate. Please go around.”

Pip paused. His ears drooped. “I didn’t know. Thank you for telling me.” He folded his paws. “I can wait and go around.”

Squeaky nodded. “That is tolerance. We listen. We learn. We make space for each other.”

They finished their snack with soft chatter and happy chewing. When the bowls were empty, Squeaky brought a damp cloth. Everyone helped wipe the table. The wood smelled clean and fresh. The spoons clinked in a cup with a bright little song.

Squeaky looked at his friends. “Today we practiced taking turns,” he said. “We used our voices to say what we need. We respected each other’s likes and feelings.”

Dot fluffed her feathers. “I learned that being different is okay.”

Momo squeaked, “I learned to speak up kindly.”

Pip smiled. “I learned to wait and to listen.”

Squeaky’s tail swished. “And I learned that we can practice every day. That is how we grow.”

They all hugged near the big oak table. The dining room felt warm, like a friendly blanket.

The End.
