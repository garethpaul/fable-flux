# Pebble Paths in the Living Room

Pip the cheerful penguin waddled into the playful living room with a happy hop-hop. The room was bright and cozy. Colorful pillows made soft mountains. A little rug felt fuzzy under Pip’s feet. The window let in warm sunshine that smelled like clean soap and orange peel. In the corner, a globe sat on a shelf, round and blue-green like a big marble of the whole world.

“Good morning, room!” Pip chirped. “I’m ready to play!”

Pip had a plan. He wanted to build a pebble path for his toy bus. He poured out a small bowl of smooth pebbles—click, clack, clink! The pebbles were cool and shiny. Some were white like snow. Some were gray like clouds. Pip lined them up on the rug.

“Bus stop here, turn there,” Pip hummed. He pushed the yellow bus. “Beep-beep!”

But oh no. The bus bumped a pillow mountain and toppled over. “Eep!” Pip said. His tummy felt twisty. He frowned a little. “I want my path to work.”

Pip took a deep penguin breath. In and out. He remembered something Mama Penguin always said: “Use your thinking steps.”

“What are my thinking steps?” Pip asked aloud, tapping his beak softly. “Step one: Stop and look. Step two: Name the problem. Step three: Try a plan.”

Pip stopped and looked. He saw the pillow mountain was too close. He saw the pebbles were scattered. He saw his bus wheels got stuck in the fuzzy rug.

He named the problem. “The bus tips and gets stuck.”

He tried a plan. “I will make a flat road.” He pressed the pebbles closer together, side by side. He moved a pillow to make a gentle hill instead of a steep one. He tried again. Beep-beep. The bus went a little farther, then still flipped. “Oh,” Pip sighed, feeling a tiny pinch of sad. He noticed his shoulders felt tight.

He put his flippers on his chest. “Hello, feelings,” he whispered. “I feel frustrated.” Pip took another slow breath. “I can keep trying.”

Just then, Pip’s friend Nia, a soft teddy with bright button eyes, peeked from the couch. “Hi, Pip,” Nia said. “Can I play?”

Pip wanted to say yes. He also wanted to finish his road. He practiced self-advocacy. “Yes, Nia, you can play,” he said kindly. “But I need a minute to fix the road. Then we can drive together.” He smiled to show he cared. Nia nodded. “Okay! I can help when you’re ready.”

Pip looked at the globe on the shelf. He waddled over and gave it a gentle spin. It made a soft whirr sound. He watched the round Earth turn. “People everywhere make roads,” Pip said. “Some roads are smooth. Some cross rivers. Some go up mountains.” Pip’s eyes sparkled. “Maybe I can make a bridge!”

He returned to the rug with a new plan. “Step one: Stop and look again,” he said. He placed two books like flat ground. “Step two: Name the new idea. Bridge!” He stacked two blocks to hold a ruler like a little bridge. Tap-tap. It felt sturdy.

“Step three: Try it.” Pip lined up the pebbles leading to the bridge. He moved the pillow mountain farther away so the turn was gentle. He pressed the pebbles firm so they didn’t wiggle. Nia watched with quiet excitement, hands pressed together.

“Ready?” Pip asked. “Ready!” Nia said.

Beep-beep. The bus rolled along the smooth pebbles. It climbed the ruler bridge—clip-clip—then zoomed down with a soft whoosh. It didn’t tip. It didn’t get stuck. “We did it!” Pip cheered. Nia clapped. They both giggled. Their giggles sounded like tiny bells.

Pip noticed his body felt calm now. His shoulders were loose. His tummy felt warm and proud. “Thinking steps helped,” he said. “I stopped and looked, I named the problem, and I tried a plan. When it didn’t work, I tried another plan.”

“Can I have a turn to drive?” Nia asked.

“Yes,” Pip said. “Thanks for waiting. That was kind.” Pip handed Nia the bus. He spoke up for himself, too. “After your turn, I want to add a tunnel.”

Nia nodded. “That’s fair.” She drove the bus carefully. “Beep-beep! Over the bridge!”

Pip found an empty tissue box and slid it under the bridge to make a cozy tunnel. “People in different places use different roads,” Pip said, pointing to the globe. “We made a bridge and a tunnel. We are like builders around the world.”

Nia smiled. “We made a plan. We listened to feelings. We took turns.”

Pip beamed. “And we helped each other.”

They drove the bus again—beep-beep, whoosh, clip-clip—over the bridge, through the tunnel, and around the soft pillow hill. The living room felt extra playful now, like it was cheering too.

Pip flapped his little flippers. “Today I learned that when I have a problem, I can use my thinking steps. I can say how I feel. I can ask for what I need. I can try new ideas, like builders all over our big, round world.”

Nia bumped her button nose to Pip’s beak. “That’s a great lesson.”

Pip nodded. “Yes. Be calm. Think carefully. Try again. Ask kindly. Help each other.”

They tucked the bus in its tiny garage between two books. The pebbles lay smooth and proud, like a shiny gray river. Pip felt peaceful and happy.

“Beep-beep, good job,” he whispered.

The End.
