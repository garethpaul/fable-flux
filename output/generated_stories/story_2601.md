# Benny Beaver’s Big Car-Trip Goal

Benny was a busy beaver with bright teeth and a fluffy, flat tail. Today, Benny climbed into the family car with Mom Beaver, Dad Beaver, and Baby Bella. The car smelled like oranges from a snack, and the seat was soft and warm. The sun peeked through the windows, and the engine hummed like a sleepy bee.

“We have a long ride,” said Mom Beaver, clicking the seat belts. Click. “What is your goal for today, Benny?”

Benny’s eyes went wide. He loved goals. Goals were like little bridges you build to get where you want to go. “My goal,” he said, tapping his tail, “is to learn to buckle my seat belt all by myself!”

Dad Beaver smiled in the mirror. “That is a great goal,” he said. “Let’s break it into small steps.”

Benny nodded. “Step one: hold the buckle. Step two: find the clicker. Step three: push until it clicks.”

Baby Bella clapped her tiny paws. “Click! Click!”

Benny took a deep breath. He could smell the new-car smell and hear birds chirping outside. He held the shiny buckle. It felt cool and smooth in his paw. He slid it toward the red button part. The car moved a little as Dad pulled out of the driveway. The engine purred.

“Almost there,” said Mom. “Use both paws.”

Benny pushed. The buckle wiggled. It did not click. His face felt hot, and his tail thumped the seat, thump-thump.

“I can’t do it!” he said, feeling his eyebrows squeeze together. His tummy made a tight knot.

Dad spoke in a calm voice. “When we feel frustrated, we can take a slow breath. In through the nose, out through the mouth.”

Benny tried. In…out. He felt the air cool in his nose and warm as it came out. The knot got softer.

“Let’s make a decision,” said Mom. “Do you want to try again now, or take turns and watch me do it once?”

Benny thought. He was the goal setter. He could choose. “I will watch first,” he decided.

Mom showed him slowly. “Hold here. Line it up. Keep it steady. Push until…click!”

Click! The sound was sharp and happy, like a tiny bell. Benny watched her paws. He watched the angle. He watched how she kept it steady.

“Now my turn,” Benny said. They unbuckled. He tried again. He held the buckle. He lined it up. He pushed. It almost clicked. Almost.

“It’s okay to practice,” said Dad. “Practice is how we grow.”

They drove past tall trees. Leaves whispered shhh-shhh. The car seat’s fabric felt rough under Benny’s tail, and the seat belt smelled faintly like soap. Benny’s paws were a little sweaty. He wiped them on his shorts.

“This time, I will use strong paws,” he said. “Goal: push straight.”

He pressed. The buckle slipped to the side. No click.

Benny’s eyes filled with tears. “I’m still not doing it!”

Mom handed him a tiny sip of water. “Let’s take turns. I will hold the buckle steady. You push.”

Benny nodded. “Teamwork.”

Mom held. Benny pushed. The buckle scooted in. Click!

“I did it with help!” Benny cheered. His tail swished-swish happily.

Baby Bella squeaked, “Click! Benny click!”

Benny grinned, but then he looked at his paws. “My big goal is to do it all by myself. I will keep practicing.”

They came to a red light. The car hummed and waited. Dad smiled. “Good goal, Benny. Sometimes we use helper steps to reach a big goal. We can make a plan.”

Benny made his plan out loud:
- “Step one: calm breath.”
- “Step two: line up straight.”
- “Step three: push strong.”
- “Step four: listen for the click.”

He practiced unbuckling and buckling at the next stop, with Mom watching. The buckle made tiny scraping sounds each time. Scritch. Scritch. The sun warmed Benny’s fur, and a soft breeze came through the window.

“Remember,” said Mom, “if you feel frustrated, pause, breathe, and try again. That is frustration-coping.”

Benny nodded. He took a breath. He tried once more. He lined up the buckle. He kept his paw steady. He pushed straight, strong, and slow.

Click!

Benny froze, then squealed, “I did it by myself! I reached my goal!”

The car filled with happy sounds. Baby Bella giggled. Dad tooted the horn once, beep! Mom clapped. “Great decision-making, Benny. You chose to watch, to take turns, to practice, and to keep calm.”

Benny felt proud. His chest felt warm and floaty. “I like goals,” he said. “Goals help me know what to do next.”

They drove on. The road went whoosh-whoosh under the tires. The car smelled like oranges again as Mom peeled a snack at a rest stop. They took turns with the crayons on a little travel board. “My new tiny goal,” said Benny, “is to draw a round sun.”

“Good idea,” said Dad. “Start with a circle. Then little lines.”

Benny drew slowly. Round and bright. “I did it!”

He looked out the window at the real sun peeking through clouds. “Goals can be big or small,” he said. “We can make a plan, make a choice, practice, and try again.”

Mom nodded. “That’s the lesson. Set a goal, take smart steps, handle big feelings, and keep going.”

Benny leaned back, cozy and safe, the seat belt snug across his chest. He rubbed the smooth buckle with his paw and listened to the gentle hum of the car. He felt ready for new goals and new tries.

“I’m a busy beaver with a busy brain,” he said softly. “I can set goals and reach them.”

“Yes, you can,” said Dad.

“Click!” said Baby Bella.

Benny giggled. “Click, click.”

The End.
