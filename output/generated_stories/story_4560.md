# Pip Sings in the Quiet Attic

Pip was a small, bright singing bird. He had a soft yellow tummy and tiny red feet. One sunny morning, he fluttered up the ladder and peeped into the quiet attic.

“It is so still,” Pip whispered. He could hear his own heart. Thump-thump. He could smell dusty wood and old books. A sunbeam fell across the floor like a warm blanket.

Pip hopped inside. “Today, I will do something by myself,” he said. “I will make my very own song.” He puffed his chest. He set a goal in his mind: a morning song, a lunchtime song, and a bedtime song, all made by Pip.

From a shadowed corner, a sleepy old clock ticked. Tick…tock. Tick…tock.

“Hello, clock,” Pip chirped. “I am making songs on my own today.”

Tick…tock. The clock seemed to say, “You can do it.”

Pip flapped onto a box and looked around. He saw a red ribbon, a silver spoon, a paper fan, and a tiny bell. The attic felt like a treasure cave. Dust sparkled in the light. The air felt cool on Pip’s feathers.

Pip began his morning song. “Chee-chee-chee!” he sang. But the sound felt plain.

“I can fix that,” he told himself. “I can try, and try again.”

He tapped the spoon with his beak. Ting! The sound was bright like a little star. He shook the bell. Ding-a-ling! The attic giggled with echoes. He waved the paper fan with a wing. Whoosh…whoosh. It sounded like gentle wind.

Pip smiled. “I can mix these sounds,” he said. He sang, “Chee-chee-chee!” while tapping the spoon. Ting! Chee! Ting! He shook the bell. Ding-a-ling! The morning song began to grow.

Then the ribbon slipped off the box and covered his eyes. “Oh!” Pip chirped, bumping into a soft hat. Thump!

Pip felt a little worried. His tummy fluttered. He wanted to call for help. But he took a deep breath. In…out. In…out. “I can solve this,” he said. “I can be brave and try again.”

He nudged the ribbon with his beak. Slide. He used his tiny foot. Push. The ribbon fell to the floor like a red snake. Pip stood tall. “I did it by myself!”

He tried his morning song again. Chee-ting, ding-a-ling, whoosh. The notes felt warm and bright. The sleepy clock ticked happily. Tick…tock.

Pip flitted to the window for his lunchtime song. Outside, he saw a tiny sparkle of snow on the roof. The air near the window was cold. “Brrr,” said Pip. He puffed his feathers. He listened. A drip of water fell from a pipe. Drip…drip…drip.

“I can use that sound,” Pip said. He tapped the window gently. Tap-tap. He scraped the spoon on the sill. Ssssk. He sang, “Choo-choo-choo,” like a little train.

But the spoon slipped and rolled under a trunk. Clink…clonk…gone.

Pip blinked. “Oh no. My spoon.” He felt a pinch of sadness. His eyes got wet. He wanted someone to get the spoon for him. Then he remembered his goal. “I am learning to do things on my own. I can think. I can try.”

He looked around. He saw a long feather duster. “Maybe I can reach the spoon with this.” He held the handle with his feet and slid the fluffy end under the trunk. Swish…swish. The spoon peeked out. Pip pulled. “Almost!” he chirped. One more try. Tug…tug…ting! The spoon was free.

Pip hopped with joy. “I solved the problem all by myself!” He made his lunchtime song: drip-drip, tap-tap, ssssk, choo-choo-choo. It sounded like a little rain train. Pip felt proud and strong.

The sunbeam moved across the floor. The attic grew softer and dim. It was time for a bedtime song. Pip felt sleepy, but he still had one more goal.

He wanted a calm, gentle song. He listened to the room. The clock still went tick…tock. The fan still went whoosh when he waved it. Somewhere, a mouse nest rustled like paper. Rustle-rustle. The air smelled like old vanilla from a wooden box.

Pip closed his eyes and took another deep breath. In…out. He sang very softly. “Loo-loo-loo.” He shook the bell once. Ding… The sound floated like a tiny moon. He added the clock. Tick…tock. He added the fan. Whoosh. He hummed, “Mmm-mmm-mmm,” like a cozy blanket.

Pip stopped and listened. The attic seemed to smile. The sounds made a calm night song. Pip’s heart felt peaceful. He had set a goal. He had tried. He had fixed problems. He had done it on his own.

The ladder creaked as the day ended. Creak…creak. Pip tucked his head under his wing. Before he slept, he whispered, “Today, I learned I can do things by myself. I can set a goal, try new ways, and keep going. When I do, I grow strong and proud.”

The clock ticked a soft reply. Tick…tock. Pip smiled. In the quiet attic, the little singing bird drifted to sleep, ready to discover more tomorrow.

The End.
