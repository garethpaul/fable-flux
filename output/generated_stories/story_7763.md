# Pebble the Parrot and the Bathroom Band

Pebble was a creative parrot with bright green feathers and a red, clever beak. Pebble loved making music. He tapped with his toes, clicked with his beak, and hummed happy tunes. One morning, Pebble flapped into the bathroom. The room smelled like bubbly soap. The tiles felt cool on his feet. The mirror was shiny and the faucet went drip, drip, drip.

“Oh! A bathroom band!” Pebble chirped. “Listen to the drips! Listen to the swish of the shower curtain!” He ruffled his feathers and beat a gentle rhythm with his wings. Tap-tap, tap-tap.

Pebble’s friend, Mia, came to brush her teeth. “Hi, Pebble,” she said. She squeezed minty toothpaste onto her brush. “We need quiet so I can listen and brush well.”

Pebble nodded but the music inside him felt big and bouncy. He saw the plastic cup. He saw the soapy sponge. “These can be instruments!” he said. He clacked the cup on the sink, clack-clack, and swished the sponge in the water, swish-swish. The sounds echoed in the bathroom. They grew louder and louder.

Mia covered her ears. “Pebble, that’s too loud,” she said, gently. “I can’t hear my brushing song. I need to listen to my counting.”

“I’m sorry,” said Pebble, but his wings still wanted to flap. His toes still wanted to tap. The bathroom made such fun sounds! The shower rod made a pong when he pecked it. The mirror made a soft thump when he drummed nearby. The toilet paper roll went whirr when he spun it.

Mia put down her brush. She looked at Pebble with kind eyes. “Pebble, this is a problem,” she said. “I need calm to brush and wash. You want music. What can we do?”

Pebble felt a little twist in his tummy. He didn’t like making a problem. He took a big, slow breath. In and out. He remembered what his grandma parrot taught him: “When the music in you is loud, use your self-regulation. Pause, breathe, and choose.”

Pebble perched on the towel rack. “Pause,” he whispered. He closed his eyes and breathed in. The bathroom smelled like orange soap. He breathed out. The air felt warm on his feathers. “Breathe,” he whispered again. Pebble put a wing on his chest. He felt his heart slow down. Thump… thump… thump.

Mia smiled. “Good job, Pebble. What will you choose?”

Pebble opened his eyes. He listened carefully. He heard the drip-drip of the faucet. He heard the tiny hiss of the toothbrush. He heard Mia’s quiet counting: “One-two, three-four.” The sounds were soft and steady.

“I can make soft music,” Pebble said. “I can listen and match your quiet.”

Mia nodded. “Yes! Soft is good in the bathroom.”

Pebble tapped his toes very gently. Tip… tip… tip. He clicked his beak like a tiny clock. Tick… tick… tick. He hummed a slow, quiet tune, mmm-mmm-mmm, like a bedtime song. He made his music fit the bathroom, not fight it.

Mia brushed and counted. “One-two, clean the front. Three-four, clean the back.” Pebble hummed along, mmm-mmm-mmm. The mirror did not rattle. The shower rod did not pong. The cup did not clack. Everything felt calm and easy.

When Mia finished, she rinsed her mouth. “All done!” she said. “I listened to my counting. You listened to your body. You chose soft music.”

Pebble puffed with pride. “I used my self-regulation,” he said. “I paused. I breathed. I chose.”

Mia turned to wash her hands. “Let’s make a hand-wash song,” she said. “It should be twenty seconds long.”

Pebble smiled. “A soft hand-wash song!” He hummed a gentle tune for the time it took to wash. The water went whoosh. The soap smelled sweet. Pebble kept the beat with a tiny tap on the sink: tip… tip… tip. Mia scrubbed palms, backs, and fingers while Pebble counted softly, “One, two, three…” up to twenty.

Mia dried her hands with a fluffy towel. It made a soft whoof sound. “Thank you for listening, Pebble,” she said. “The bathroom is for calm and care. Loud music belongs outside.”

Pebble nodded. “I can make loud music in the yard,” he said. “I can make soft music in the bathroom.” He flapped once. “I can choose the right sound for the right place.”

Mia grinned. “That’s being smart. That’s being kind.”

Pebble felt warm and happy. He had found a way to keep his music and to help his friend. He had learned to listen to the room, listen to the people, and listen to himself.

Later, when they went outside, Pebble sang a bright, big song in the open air. But in the bathroom, he always kept it soft and steady. If the music in him felt too big, he remembered: pause, breathe, choose.

And every time, Pebble’s heart felt calm, and his songs felt just right.

The End.
