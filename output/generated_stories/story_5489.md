# Roo's Big Brave Bounce

Roo was a joyful kangaroo joey with soft brown fur and bright, curious eyes. Roo loved to bounce, hum songs, and help. One sunny morning, Roo’s mama said, “The community center needs helpers. There’s a basement lab where we sort toy parts and make music for kids. Do you want to volunteer, Roo?”

Roo’s ears perked. “Yes! I want to help and sing!”

They hopped together to the community center. Down the stairs they went, step-thump, step-thump, into the basement lab. It smelled like fresh crayons and warm cookies from the kitchen upstairs. The room was cool, with a gentle hum from machines. Shelves held bins of buttons, gears, and bright string. A long table had little drums, shakers, and a small silver bell.

“Welcome!” said Dr. Star, a kind scientist with a twinkly smile. “We help fix toys and make music to test how sound works. We also make care packs for kids. Would you like to volunteer today?”

Roo nodded. “Yes, please!”

Dr. Star handed Roo a shiny name badge. It said “Roo—Helper.” That made Roo’s chest feel warm and proud.

“First, we make music to see which sounds are soft and which are loud,” Dr. Star said. “Then we pack the care bags.”

Roo picked up a small drum and tapped it softly. Tap-tap. The sound felt like raindrops on a leaf. Roo grinned. Then Roo tried the bell. Ding! The bell danced in the air with a bright ring that tickled Roo’s ears.

“Great job,” said Dr. Star. “Now we can sort toys by the sounds they make.”

Roo started to hum and bounce, a gentle bounce that made the drum go tap-tap with each hop. “I like soft sounds,” Roo said.

Just then, Mr. Loud Leo, a big lion volunteer with kind eyes, roared with excitement. “Let’s make music BIG!” He banged the big drum. BOOM! The sound shook the table and made Roo’s tail tingle.

Roo’s smile wobbled. The boom was too big. The room felt too noisy. Roo’s heart beat a little fast. Roo wanted to say something but felt small. Roo took a deep breath, like Mama had taught: in through the nose, out through the mouth. The air smelled like clean soap and lemon.

Roo whispered, “I don’t like it so loud.” But no one heard. The music kept booming. The shakers rattled, the bell dinged, the drum boomed, and Roo’s ears folded back.

Dr. Star noticed Roo’s face. “Roo, are you okay?”

Roo’s paws held the drum close. Roo wanted to be brave and kind. Roo remembered a special phrase Mama had taught: “I can say what I need.”

Roo stood up a little taller. “Excuse me,” Roo said, voice a bit wobbly but strong. “I need the music to be softer. The loud boom hurts my ears. Can we make a quiet song, please?”

The room got very still. Mr. Loud Leo looked surprised, then gentle. “Thank you for telling us, Roo,” he said. “That’s helpful.”

Dr. Star nodded. “Roo is using self-advocacy. That means Roo is saying what Roo needs in a calm way. That helps everyone.”

Roo’s heart slowed down. The air felt comfy again.

Mr. Loud Leo put the big drum aside. “How about we try a quiet song that still feels fun?” he asked.

Roo’s smile came back. “Yes! I can help.”

Roo tapped the small drum, tap-tap, like raindrops. Dr. Star shook a soft shaker, shhh-shhh, like the wind in leaves. Mr. Loud Leo rang the bell just a little, ding…ding, like tiny stars blinking. Roo hummed a sweet tune, mmm-mmm-mmm, like warm cocoa.

The quiet song floated around the lab, gentle and happy. It made everyone sway and sigh with joy. “This is lovely,” Dr. Star said.

“Now that we’ve learned about soft and loud,” Dr. Star added, “let’s fill care bags for the kids.”

Roo’s tail swished with excitement. “I can help with that too!”

They lined up colorful bags. Roo chose items carefully: a soft shaker for calm music, bright stickers of stars, and a small book about feelings. Roo held up a card and read slowly, “You can say what you need.”

“That’s perfect,” Dr. Star said. “You are volunteering with your whole heart.”

As they worked, a little mouse helper named Mimi scurried in. “The bell is still a bit strong for me,” she squeaked.

Roo remembered the brave moment. Roo smiled at Mimi and said, “You can tell us what you need.”

Mimi gulped, then said, “Please ring it very softly.”

Mr. Loud Leo winked. “Soft it is.” He tapped the bell with one gentle claw. Ting… A tiny sparkle of sound danced in the air.

They finished the care bags and tied them with rainbow string. Roo hugged one bag and felt proud. Roo had helped fix the music, helped pack gifts, and helped a friend speak up too.

Dr. Star gave Roo a sticker that said, “I used my voice.” Roo placed it on the name badge. “I did it,” Roo said. “I said what I needed.”

“You did,” Dr. Star agreed. “That’s self-advocacy. When you speak up kindly, people can help. You can help yourself, and you can help others.”

Roo bounced, but just a little, so the drum went tap-tap again. Roo laughed. “Soft and happy,” Roo said.

On the way home, the basement lab now smelled like lemon and a little like rainbow string. Roo hummed the quiet song. Mama asked, “How was volunteering?”

Roo beamed. “I helped. I made soft music. I told people what I needed. It felt brave and good.”

Mama squeezed Roo’s paw. “That is a big lesson. Your voice matters.”

Roo nodded. “I can say what I need. I can listen to others too.”

And Roo knew that next time, in any room, Roo could use brave words, make kind music, and help friends feel safe and happy.

The End.
