# Koala Kori Waits and Wins

Kori the koala sat on a peaceful front porch. The porch was made of smooth, warm wood. A soft breeze moved the green leaves. The sun felt like a cozy blanket. Birds chirped. Bees buzzed. Kori took a slow breath in and out.

“Today, I will plant my tea leaves,” Kori said. “I will make calm tea when the leaves grow.” Kori loved calm tea. It was warm and gentle, like a hug in a cup.

Kori had a little basket. Inside were tiny seeds, a small watering can, a wooden spoon, and a jar of compost. The compost smelled earthy, like the forest floor after rain. Kori smiled. “Good soil helps plants grow. That is caring for the Earth.”

Kori placed three little pots on the porch step. “Hello, pots,” Kori whispered. “We will be patient together.” Kori scooped soil with the spoon. Scoop, pat, scoop, pat. The soil felt soft and cool.

Kori dropped seeds into the pots. Plip, plip, plip. “Grow slow and strong,” Kori said. “We have time.” Kori covered the seeds and gave each pot a tiny drink. Drip, drip, drip. The water sparkled in the sun.

A busy kookaburra landed on the porch rail. “Ha-ha-ha! Are you waiting, Kori?” the bird laughed.

“Yes,” Kori said, calm and kind. “I am waiting for my tea leaves to grow.”

“But waiting is hard!” the bird said, hopping. “You could pick leaves from the big bush right now!”

Kori looked at the big bush. It was far away, and it belonged to the neighbor. Kori shook their head. “No, thank you. These are my plants. I will be patient. I will care for them every day. That is better for me. That is better for the Earth.”

“Ha-ha! Okay!” the bird cried, and flew away.

Kori sat on the porch and watched the pots. Nothing showed. Just brown soil. The porch was quiet again. The breeze tickled Kori’s ears. Kori’s tummy made a small rumble.

“I feel a little grumpy,” Kori said. “I want the leaves now.” Kori put a paw on their chest. Pat, pat. “I can be patient.”

Kori stood up and stretched. The wood creaked softly under Kori’s feet. “I will take care of me while I wait,” Kori said. Kori sipped cool water. Sip. Kori ate a crunchy apple. Crunch. Kori did three tiny shoulder rolls. One, two, three. Kori hummed a gentle song. “Hmm-mmm-mmm.”

A breeze brought the smell of eucalyptus and warm dust. A neighbor snail slowly slid by the step. “Hello, Snail,” Kori said.

“Hello, Kori,” said Snail in a small voice. “Are you moving fast today?”

“No,” Kori said. “I am moving slow. I am waiting.”

Snail smiled. “Slow is okay.” Snail waved with its feelers and slid on.

Kori looked at the pots again. Still soil. The sun moved across the sky. Kori felt a wiggle in their legs. “I want to check the seeds,” Kori said. “But I will not poke them. Poking can hurt them.”

Kori took a deep breath. In and out. Then Kori did a little job. Kori swept the porch with a straw broom. Swish, swish. Leaves and tiny twigs made a soft pile. Kori put the leaves into the compost jar. “We can use this again. That is called reusing. That is good for our Earth.”

A soft voice came from inside the house. “Kori, do you want a rest?” It was Grandma Koala.

“Yes, please,” Kori said. “I am waiting, and my body needs a break.”

Kori sat on a cushion. Grandma put a cool cloth on Kori’s forehead. “Waiting is work,” Grandma said. “When we wait, we care for our minds and bodies.”

Kori nodded. “I am learning patience. It feels like breathing slow.”

After the rest, Kori stepped back onto the porch. The air felt cooler. The shadows were long like tall fingers across the floor. Kori brought the watering can. “Just a little,” Kori said. Drip, drip. The soil drank the water.

Kori leaned in close. Something tiny peeked up. It was a tiny green dot, as small as a pea. Kori’s eyes grew wide. “Oh!” Kori whispered. “Hello, little sprout.”

Kori did not shout or clap hard. Kori held still. The little sprout stood like a tiny flag. Kori’s heart felt warm. “Patience helped you,” Kori said. “And patience helped me, too.”

The kookaburra flew back. “Ha-ha! I see green!” the bird cried.

“Yes,” Kori said with a soft smile. “Look what time, care, and patience can do.”

The bird tilted its head. “Waiting did work. Ha-ha!” Then it flew to a tree.

Kori looked at the other pots. Another small sprout was pushing up like a teeny green finger. The last pot was still just soil. Kori kept calm. “You will come when you are ready,” Kori said. “I can wait.”

Kori felt proud. Kori had taken deep breaths. Kori had stretched. Kori had eaten a snack and had a rest. Kori had cared for the porch and the Earth. Kori had not poked the seeds. Kori had stayed gentle and steady.

Grandma came out with two cups. “Warm water with mint,” she said. Steam curled up like tiny clouds. Kori held the cup and felt the warmth. “One day we will make tea from your leaves,” Grandma said.

“Yes,” Kori said. “We will wait. We will care. We will keep going.”

Kori sat on the peaceful front porch as the sky turned pink and orange. Birds sang evening songs. The little sprouts drank the last light. Kori took one more slow breath. In and out.

“Patience is quiet strength,” Kori said softly. “When I wait with care, good things grow.”

The End.
