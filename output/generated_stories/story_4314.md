# Roo's Table Adventure

Roo was a joyful kangaroo joey with springy feet and big, bright eyes. One sunny afternoon, Roo hopped into the dining room. The wooden table smelled like warm toast. The chairs were smooth and cool. A tiny breeze tickled the curtains and made them swish-swish. It was snack time!

On the table sat a bowl of apples, a cup of water, and a napkin made from soft cloth. Roo clapped. “Snack time makes my tummy hum,” Roo giggled, patting a round belly. Hum, hum, hum.

But when Roo reached for an apple, there was a problem. The apples were all different sizes. One was small and bumpy. One was shiny and big. One had a little bruise.

Roo frowned. “Oh no. Which apple should I choose? I don’t want the bumpy one. I don’t want the bruised one. I only want the perfect one.” Roo’s ears drooped.

Mama hopped in, her tail tapping the floor like a gentle drum. “What’s the matter, Roo?”

“I can’t pick,” Roo sighed. “I want the prettiest apple.”

Mama knelt. She smiled and touched the apples. “Every apple has a story. The small one grew in a shady spot. The big one grew in bright sun. The bruised one took a tumble.”

Roo scrunched up a nose. “I don’t like bruises.”

Mama nodded. “I understand. But the apple still has sweet parts inside. Sometimes we care for things that are a little different. That is called empathy. We listen and we help.”

Roo tilted a head. “Can I help an apple?”

Mama chuckled. “Let’s use our imagination.”

Roo’s eyes sparkled. “Imagination time!”

The dining room began to glow in Roo’s mind. The table became a wooden ship. The napkin turned into a soft sail. The cup of water became a tiny ocean, swish-swish. The apples were passengers on Roo’s ship.

Roo stood tall like a captain. “All aboard! Little Apple, Shiny Apple, and Bruisy Apple!” Roo waved a spoon like a flag. “We will sail to Snack Island.”

“Captain Roo,” said Mama, playing along, “what is our plan for Bruisy Apple?”

Roo bent close to the apples and spoke softly. “Bruisy Apple, are you okay?”

In Roo’s imagination, the apple whispered, “I fell, but I still have sweet parts. I hope someone will see me.”

Roo’s heart felt warm, like a cozy blanket. “I see you,” Roo said. “We can help.”

The ship rocked gently on the table-ocean. The curtains blew—whoosh. Roo felt brave. “Crew, we do not waste food,” Roo said. “We use what we have. That is good for our Earth.” The cup-ocean made tiny waves—plip-plip.

Roo picked up Bruisy Apple and held it gently. “We will care for you.” Mama brought a small wooden board and a safe, round-tipped knife for grown-ups. “I’ll help,” Mama said. “We can cut away the bruise.”

Slice, slice. The apple smelled sweet and fresh. Roo closed eyes and inhaled—mmm. “It smells like sunshine,” Roo said.

Mama cut off the bruised part and made small slices. “See?” she said. “Still yummy.” She put the peel and bruise into a small bowl. “These can go to our compost. Compost feeds the soil. Then new apples can grow.” Roo imagined the peels turning into rich, dark dirt. “Goodnight, peels,” Roo whispered. “Thank you for helping the Earth.”

Roo shared the slices with the other apples on the ship. “Everyone gets a taste,” Roo said, passing pieces onto a little plate. Roo took a bite—crunch! Sweet juice ran on Roo’s tongue. “Delicious!”

Roo smiled at the small bumpy apple. “You can be for tomorrow’s oatmeal.” Roo patted the big shiny apple. “You can be for sharing with a friend.”

Mama poured a little more water—glug-glug. “Captain Roo, how do you feel now?”

Roo’s ears perked up. “I used my imagination to make a ship. I used empathy to help Bruisy Apple. And I helped the Earth by not wasting food. I feel proud.”

Mama hugged Roo. The hug felt warm like fresh bread. “I’m proud too,” she said. “You solved your problem with a kind heart and a creative mind.”

Roo looked around the dining room ship. The table was no longer a ship, but it still felt special. The cloth napkin felt soft. The wooden chair felt smooth under Roo’s paws. “Thank you, dining room,” Roo whispered.

Then Roo tapped the spoon like a bell—ting-ting. “Lesson time!” Roo announced. “When something looks a little different, we can still find the good. We can care. We can share. We can use our imagination to solve problems. And we can help our planet by not wasting food.”

Mama and Roo carried the compost bowl to the bin by the back door. Roo watched the peels slip in—plop. “Grow strong, little garden,” Roo said. “See you in the apples!”

Back at the table, Roo folded the cloth napkin to use again. “We can wash and reuse,” Roo said. “That helps too.”

Mama winked. “Captain Roo, Snack Island was a success.”

Roo giggled, tummy humming again. “Hum, hum, hooray!”

The End.
