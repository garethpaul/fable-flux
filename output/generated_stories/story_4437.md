# Polly Paints the Quiet Closet

Polly was a creative parrot with bright green feathers and a sunny yellow tail. She loved colors, songs, and making new things. Her favorite place was a tiny closet hideaway in the hallway. It smelled like cedar wood and Grandma’s soap. Soft scarves hung like rainbows. Shoes lined up like sleepy boats. A small lamp glowed warm and golden, and a round pillow felt soft under Polly’s feet.

Polly visited the closet hideaway every afternoon. She called it her “quiet nest.” She whispered, “Squawk… but soft,” and giggled. She brought little jars of safe finger paint, paper, and a tiny drum. She drew swirling suns and dotted clouds. She tapped gentle rhythms: tum-tum, tap-tap.

One day, a new sound came from outside the closet. Thump-thump. Rattle-rattle. Someone was trying to open the door.

“Who is it?” Polly chirped, her head tilting. Her feathers fluffed with worry.

“It’s me, Benny,” said a small voice. Benny was a busy beaver from next door. He loved building block towers. He loved making big, big sounds. “I want to play in the closet too!”

Polly felt funny inside. The closet was her quiet nest. But she also liked sharing. She took a deep breath and opened the door just a tiny bit. The hallway smelled like pancakes from the kitchen. Benny’s whiskers twitched. His paws were full of wooden blocks that clacked together.

“Hi, Benny,” Polly said in a gentle voice. “This is my quiet nest. I use soft sounds here. Would you like to come in and try soft play?”

Benny bounced. “I like BIG play!” he said. He pushed the door, and the blocks clattered onto the floor. Clack! Clack! Clack!

Polly’s heart thumped. The closet felt crowded. The lamp wobbled. The scarves swished. “I feel nervous,” Polly said, touching her chest. “The sounds are too loud for my quiet nest.”

Benny didn’t mean to scare anyone. He looked at the cozy pillow, the glowing lamp, and the painted suns. His ears went low. “Oh. I didn’t know,” he said, softer now.

Polly took another calm breath. She remembered what Grandma taught her: Use peaceful words. Say what you need. Listen to others. She said, “Let’s talk. I want to share. But I need this space to be calm. Can we make a plan together?”

Benny nodded. “A plan?” He loved plans. Plans were like building, but with ideas.

Polly smiled. “Yes. We can take turns. We can use quiet voices and gentle hands. We can ask before we change things. And we can keep some traditions.”

“Tradish…what?” Benny blinked.

“Traditions,” Polly explained. “They are special ways we do things. In my quiet nest, I have a painting tradition. Before we paint, we sing the Soft Song. It goes: ‘Breathe in… two, three. Breathe out… two, three. Be kind, be calm, and paint with glee.’”

Benny listened. He liked the little song. It made his shoulders relax. “I can try,” he said.

They sat on the soft pillow. The closet smelled like paint and cedar and a little like pancakes. Polly tapped the tiny drum so softly it sounded like raindrops on leaves. She hummed the Soft Song. Benny hummed too. His big tail twitched quietly.

Polly handed Benny a paper. “Would you like blue or green?” she asked.

“Blue, please,” said Benny, using his gentle voice.

They dipped their fingers into the cool, smooth paint. Swish. Dot. Swirl. Benny made blue rivers and tiny boats. Polly painted yellow suns and little birds. They smiled. Their shoulders relaxed. The lamp glowed warm.

After a while, Benny’s paws wiggled. He wanted to build. “Polly,” he said, “may I bring in three blocks? I will put them down softly.”

Polly thought. She took a breath. “Yes,” she said. “Three soft blocks are okay.” She watched as Benny placed the blocks with quiet hands. Tap. Tap. Tap. He made a tiny bridge for his painted river.

But then, oops! One block slipped. Clack!

Polly’s feathers fluffed again. She put a hand on her chest. “I feel jumpy,” she said calmly. “That sound was too loud for me.”

Benny looked sorry. “I will try again,” he said. He picked up the block and set it down like a feather. Tap. He smiled. “I am learning soft hands.”

“You are doing great,” Polly said. “Thank you for listening.”

They kept painting and building, slowly and softly. When Benny forgot and moved too fast, Polly used peaceful words: “Please slow down.” When Polly took up too much space with her paints, Benny used peaceful words: “Please make room for my bridge.” They both listened. They both tried again. They both kept going. That was persistence.

Soon, they had a beautiful picture: a blue river under a yellow sun, a tiny bridge, and little birds flying above. Benny clapped his paws—softly. “We did it!”

Polly beamed. “We made art and kept our traditions. We were calm. We talked. We took turns. We practiced and tried again.” She opened the closet door wide. The hallway light mixed with the lamp, golden and bright. The world felt big and friendly.

Benny packed his blocks. “Thank you for sharing your quiet nest,” he said. “I will remember the Soft Song.”

Polly nodded. “And I will remember to use peaceful words. We can play again tomorrow.”

They touched elbows, like a gentle high-five. Then they sang the Soft Song one more time, together.

“Breathe in… two, three. Breathe out… two, three. Be kind, be calm, and paint with glee.”

The End.
