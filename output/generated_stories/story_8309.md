# Fawn's Quiet Corner

In a sunny house, there was a small study corner. The corner had a soft blue rug, a low bookshelf, and a wooden desk that smelled like warm pine. Light from the window made little dust sparkles dance. It was a cozy, quiet place.

One morning, a gentle deer fawn named Fawn tiptoed into the study corner. Her hooves made tiny tap-tap sounds on the floor. Her fur was soft and brown, and her ears were tall and twitchy.

“This is my reading place,” Fawn whispered. “I feel calm here.”

On the desk sat a round jar full of leaves, a little watering can, and a basket of crayons. Fawn liked to read, draw, and care for her plant, a small fern named Frond. She patted the fern. “Good morning, Frond,” she said. “I will listen to you today. Do you need a little drink?”

Fawn bent close and listened. She heard the rustle of fern fronds and a tiny drip from the watering can. “Sip, sip,” she said, pouring just a little water. The fern perked up.

Soon, there was a scurry-scritch at the doorway. A squirrel named Pip peeked in with a shiny acorn. “Hi, Fawn! Can I draw here too?” Pip asked, tail swishing.

Fawn smiled. “Yes, Pip. This corner is for quiet work and gentle voices.”

“Gentle voices,” Pip repeated, nodding.

Then, a little robin named Dot hopped to the window ledge. “Tweet-tweet! Can I read a picture book with you?” Dot chirped.

“Yes,” said Fawn. “Let’s make space.” She slid the basket of crayons and moved the books. “We can share.”

They sat together. Fawn opened a big book with bright, leafy pages. Dot read the pictures out loud. Pip drew green trees and a round blue pond. The room smelled like crayons and leaves, and the light felt warm like a hug.

Just then, a young raccoon named Riff shuffled in, his paws dusty. He clutched a crumpled poster that said, “Be Kind to Earth.” His masky eyes looked worried.

“Hi,” Riff said softly. “May I hang my poster here?” He pointed to the wall. “I want friends to see it. It is about caring for the park and picking up litter.”

Fawn looked at the neat wall. “This corner is for quiet work,” she said. “And the wall is very clean.”

Riff’s ears drooped. “I don’t fit anywhere,” he mumbled. He held the poster tighter. “No one wants my messy hands. But the park is messy too. I want to help.”

Fawn’s ears twitched. She remembered her rule: gentle voices. She also remembered her heart: be kind. She took a slow breath. The dust sparkles twirled in the sun.

“Let’s listen,” Fawn said. “Riff, tell us more.”

Riff’s eyes brightened. “I saw cans by the creek,” he said. “And wrappers under the big oak. It made the ducks sad. I want to fix it. I made this poster but the classroom wall is full, and the hallway is loud. I came here because it is quiet.”

Pip tilted his head. “I like clean parks. I like trees to climb.”

Dot fluttered. “Tweet! Ducks like clean water.”

Fawn felt her chest feel warm and soft. “It is fair,” she said slowly. “Everyone who cares gets a turn. This is justice. Your voice matters.”

She touched the edge of the crumpled paper. It felt bumpy and cool. “We can make a plan,” she said. “A plan that is kind to the wall and kind to your message.”

“How?” Riff whispered.

Fawn smiled. “We can use the cork board.” She pointed to a small board by the desk. “It is made for posters. We will use a soft pin. And we will wash paws before we touch books.”

Riff nodded fast. “I can wash!” He hurried to the little sink by the door. The water splashed, and the soap smelled like oranges. He rubbed and rinsed until his paws were clean and shiny.

Pip fetched a pin from a tiny cup. “Here you go,” he said.

Dot held one corner of the poster. “Steady, steady,” she chirped.

Fawn held the other corner and softly pressed the pin. “There,” she said. The poster hung straight and proud. The words “Be Kind to Earth” gleamed in the sun.

Riff beamed. “Thank you for listening,” he said. “I feel welcome.”

Fawn nodded. “You are welcome here,” she said. “We accept friends as they are. We make room.”

Pip pointed to the jar of leaves. “Let’s do a park plan,” he said. “We can make a list.”

Fawn got a big sheet of paper and a fat blue crayon. She wrote slowly, sounding out the words. “Plan: pick up litter. Use a bag. Be safe. Share water. Say thank you to trees.”

Dot added a little heart and a duck drawing. Riff drew a raccoon paw in the corner. They all giggled.

They sat back and looked. The study corner felt extra warm now. It smelled like soap and pine and crayons. The fern rustled happily, as if saying, “Yes, yes.”

Fawn spoke in a gentle voice. “Today we learned to listen, to be fair, and to accept. Riff had a big idea. We made space for it.”

Riff nodded. “And we will help the Earth.”

Pip waved his tail. “Justice means everyone gets a turn.”

Dot chirped. “And listening helps hearts feel safe.”

Fawn hugged her friends with her eyes. “Our quiet corner is for all of us,” she said. “We can be different and still belong.”

They cleaned up together. Crayons clicked into the basket. Books slid onto the shelf with soft shush sounds. Fawn gave the fern one tiny sip. “Sip, sip.”

As the sun moved and the dust sparkles danced, the friends sat in peaceful silence, happy and proud.

The End.
