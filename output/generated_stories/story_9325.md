# Rollo’s Gentle Song

In a quiet attic, where sunbeams made dusty sparkles in the air, a musical raccoon named Rollo wiggled his striped tail. The attic smelled like old wood and warm blankets. It felt soft and still, like a whisper. Rollo’s favorite thing in the whole wide world was music.

“I will play a song,” Rollo said. His voice was bright and bouncy. He picked up a small toy xylophone with rainbow bars. Tap, tap, tap! went the tiny mallet. The sound was tinkly and sweet, like raindrops on a window.

Rollo wanted his song to be fast and fancy. He wanted it to be perfect right away. He took a deep breath. “Ready, set—music!” he said.

Clink. Clank. Clonk.

Oops. The notes bumped and bonked into each other. The melody wiggled in the wrong places. Rollo’s ears drooped. His whiskers twitched.

“I want it now,” Rollo grumbled. “I want my big, perfect song right now.”

From a shadowy corner came a soft hoot. “Hoo-hoo,” said Old Owl, who liked to nap in the rafters. He blinked his round, golden eyes. “Songs grow slowly, little friend.”

Rollo’s tail swished. “But I tried,” he said. “I tried once.”

Old Owl nodded. “Try again. Try slowly.” He pointed his feathery wing to the smallest bar on the xylophone. “Start with one note. One note is fair for today.”

Rollo tapped the tiny red bar. Ting! The sound was bright and clear. He tapped again. Ting! He felt the sound in his paws and in his chest. It tickled his ears. Ting… ting… ting.

“Good,” said Old Owl. “Now two notes.”

Rollo tapped red, then orange. Ting, tong. He missed. Clank! His face got hot. His paws felt jumpy. He wanted to toss the mallet and stomp.

From a stack of old scarves came a gentle voice. “We can wait,” said Moth, fluttering her powdery wings. “We can wait with you.” She settled on the window and made tiny dust circles with her feet.

Rollo huffed, then sat crisscross on a soft rug. He looked at the window. He watched the dust dance in the sunshine. He listened to his own breath. In and out. Slow and quiet.

“I can be patient,” Rollo whispered. “I can give myself time.”

He tried again. Ting, tong. He smiled a little. “I did it!” he said.

Old Owl nodded. “Fair and steady,” he said. “When we are fair to ourselves, we do not ask for big things all at once. We give our bodies and brains time to learn.”

Rollo kept going. He practiced three notes: red, orange, yellow. Ting, tong, tang. He practiced softly. He practiced slowly. His paws felt calmer. His whiskers stopped twitching.

From under a hatbox, a tiny mouse peeked out. “Can I listen?” asked Mouse in a squeaky voice.

“Yes,” said Rollo. “But only if I take turns with myself.”

Mouse blinked. “What does that mean?”

Rollo tapped the mallet on his knee. “It means I play, then I pause. I rest. I breathe. That is fair.” He looked at Mouse kindly. “And after I practice, you can try too. That is fair for both of us.”

Rollo played. He paused. He breathed. He tried again. Some notes slipped. Some notes stayed.

Clink. Clank. Ting. Tang. Tong.

The attic filled with little sounds—wood creaks, soft wings, tiny squeaks, and Rollo’s growing song. It was not fast. It was not fancy. But it was gentle and true.

Soon, the sunbeam moved across the floor. Rollo stretched his arms. “Time for a break,” he said. He set the mallet down.

Mouse clapped with tiny paws. “My turn?” she asked.

Rollo nodded. “Yes. That’s fair.” He showed her the red and orange bars. “Start with one note,” he said. “One note is fair for today.”

Mouse tapped. Ting. She missed the next one. Clank. Her whiskers drooped.

Rollo smiled softly. “It’s okay to wait,” he said. “We can be patient together.” He tapped the rug in a steady beat. “One and breathe. Two and breathe.”

Old Owl hooted happily. “Practice is a kind of patience,” he said. “It is waiting with your hands and your heart.”

Rollo listened to his own heart. Thump, thump. He matched his taps to the beat. Ting… breathe… tong… breathe. The melody started to line up like neat buttons on a coat.

At last Rollo tried his whole little tune. Red, orange, yellow, green, blue. Ting, tong, tang, ting, toong! The notes chimed like wind in a bottle. They made the attic feel warm and bright.

Rollo’s eyes sparkled. “I did it!” he laughed. The sound echoed softly in the rafters.

“You did,” said Old Owl. “You were patient. You practiced. You were fair to yourself and your friend.”

Mouse twirled. “We all waited, and we all learned,” she squeaked. “Your song is kind.”

Rollo hugged his xylophone. “Today I learned: big things grow from small steps. I can wait. I can practice. I can share and be fair.”

He put the mallet down gently. “Tomorrow,” he said, “I will learn one new note.”

The attic felt proud and peaceful. Dust sparkles swirled like tiny stars. The quiet was full of a soft, happy hum.

The End.
