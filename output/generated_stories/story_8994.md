# Leo’s Tidy Treehouse Adventure

Leo was a brave little lion cub with soft golden fur and a curious nose. He had a special treehouse high in a big leafy tree. The treehouse smelled like warm wood and sunshine. Birds sang, leaves rustled, and the ladder creaked a tiny “ee-ee” when Leo climbed up.

“Today is adventure day!” Leo cheered, swishing his tail. He pushed open the door. Toys rolled to his paws. Blocks clattered. Crayons tumbled. A puzzle piece slid under a pillow.

“Oh,” Leo whispered, eyes wide. “It’s messy.”

Leo wanted to build a tall block tower. He also wanted to draw a sun. He also wanted to play his drum. But he could not find his red block. He could not find his yellow crayon. He could not even find his drumsticks.

Leo’s whiskers drooped. “I feel grumpy,” he said.

Knock, knock.

Two friends climbed up the ladder—Mina the monkey and Tiko the tiny turtle. Mina wore a bright scarf. Tiko wore a shy smile.

“Hi, Leo!” Mina chattered. “Let’s play!”

Leo sighed. “I can’t find anything. My treehouse is too messy.”

Mina looked around. “I see blocks under the blanket. I see crayons in a shoe!” She giggled. “This is a treasure hunt!”

Tiko blinked slowly. “Maybe we can organize,” he said in a soft, steady voice. “Organize means put things where they belong.”

Leo’s ears perked up. “Organize? Is that a game?”

“It can be,” said Mina. “We can make spots for everything.”

“How?” asked Leo. He wanted to learn. He was brave, and he was curious too.

Tiko pointed with his small foot. “We can make three baskets. One for blocks. One for art. One for music.” He tapped each basket. “Blocks. Art. Music.”

Leo nodded. “That is a good plan. Let’s try!”

The friends made a rule: “We take turns,” said Mina. “I’ll put one thing away, then you, then Tiko.”

Leo smiled. “Taking turns is fair.”

They began. Mina picked up a blue block. “Blue block goes in the block basket,” she sang.

Leo found a crayon. “Yellow crayon goes in the art basket,” he said.

Tiko found a drumstick. Tap, tap. “Drumstick goes in the music basket,” he said, placing it gently.

They kept going. The treehouse made friendly sounds—clink, rustle, tap. Dust sparkled in the sunbeam. It smelled less like “lost things” and more like clean wood again.

Leo found a funny sock. He held it up. “Where does this go?”

Mina tilted her head. “That’s not a toy.”

Tiko thought. “Hmm. We can make a new place. A small basket for ‘other.’ That is logical.” He smiled. “Logical means the choice makes sense.”

Leo clapped. “A basket for other! That is smart.”

They kept taking turns. Mina put away a puzzle piece. Leo stacked the last block. Tiko lined up the music shaker and the tiny bell. Sometimes they had to look under the rug. Sometimes they had to move slowly and try again. That was persistence. They did not give up.

“I still can’t find my red block,” Leo said, feeling a little worried.

“Let’s be open-minded,” Tiko said kindly. “Maybe the red block is not on the floor. Maybe it is somewhere new.”

“Open-minded means we try new ideas,” Mina added. “Let’s check high places!”

Leo stood on tiptoes. He peeked on the shelf. “I see my red block!” He laughed, happy and surprised. “It was in the snack bowl!”

Mina giggled. “Silly red block.”

Soon the floor looked clear. The baskets looked neat. Each had a picture label: a square for blocks, a crayon for art, and a drum for music. Leo drew the pictures himself. He liked the way the crayons felt smooth in his paw.

“Now let’s play,” Mina said. “What first?”

Leo took a deep breath. “Let’s build a tower. Then we can draw. Then we can make music.” He pointed. “We will take turns choosing.”

Tiko nodded. “I choose drawing next.”

Mina bounced. “I choose music last!”

Leo set the blocks by the mat. “I am ready.” He found the red block quickly. He knew right where it lived.

Stack, stack, stack. The blocks clicked softly together. The tower grew tall. Leo’s paws felt steady. His heart felt calm. “Organization helps me feel brave,” he said.

After blocks, they drew a big bright sun. The crayons were easy to find. Yellow, orange, and a happy pink. The paper smelled like fresh trees. Their lines made soft scratchy sounds. Then they played music—tap, shake, ding! The treehouse filled with joyful notes.

At cleanup time, Leo said, “Let’s use our baskets again.”

Mina nodded. “We take turns!”

Tiko smiled. “We follow our plan.”

They put each thing in its place. It was quick. It was simple. It felt good.

Leo looked around. His treehouse was tidy and cozy. His whiskers lifted. His tail swished. “I learned something today,” he said. “When we organize, we can find our things. When we take turns, we are fair. When we keep trying and use our minds, we solve problems.”

Mina hugged him. “And when we are open-minded, we find new ways.”

Tiko patted the music basket. “Everything has a home.”

Leo beamed. “I am a brave cub with a tidy treehouse. Tomorrow, we can have another adventure!”

The sun warmed the floor. The leaves whispered. The friends shared a crunchy apple slice, one turn each bite. The treehouse felt peaceful and proud.

The End.
