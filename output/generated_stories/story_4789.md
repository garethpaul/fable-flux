# Elli Shares the Sunshine

Elli was a gentle elephant calf with soft gray ears and a warm, wiggly trunk. One bright morning, she woke up in the guest room at Grandma Gita’s house. The room smelled like clean soap and sweet oranges. Sunlight danced on the curtains. A little breeze tickled Elli’s cheeks.

“Good morning, sunshine,” Grandma Gita called. “Would you like to help me today?”

“Yes, please!” Elli said, her trunk swinging happily.

On the bed sat a basket full of colorful kites. They were red, blue, yellow, and green. Elli loved how the kites felt—smooth, crinkly, and light. She pressed one to her ear. It made a tiny rustle, like a whisper.

“These kites are for the park,” Grandma said. “We will share them with the children.”

Elli’s eyes grew wide. “Share?” she asked.

“Yes,” Grandma smiled. “Being generous means we give and we care. We make sure everyone gets a turn. That is fair. That is justice.”

Elli liked that word—fair. It felt good in her heart.

They carried the basket through the hallway. Elli’s steps were soft on the rug. “Thump, thump,” went her little feet, and “swish, swish,” went the kites.

At the park, the sky was big and blue. The grass smelled fresh and a little wet. Birds sang, “Tweet, tweet!” and the wind hummed, “Whoooo.”

Children ran to the basket. “Kites!” they cheered.

Elli smiled. “We can all fly one,” she said. “Line up, please. We will take turns.”

A boy named Max pushed to the front. He grabbed the biggest red kite. “Mine!” he shouted.

Elli blinked. Her ears drooped. She felt a bump in her tummy. It didn’t feel fair.

Grandma Gita stepped beside her. “Elli, what can we do to make this fair?” she asked softly.

Elli took a deep breath. The wind touched her cheeks. She remembered Grandma’s words. Generous. Fair.

Elli tapped her trunk gently and said, “Max, we can all fly kites if we share. Let’s make a plan.”

Max frowned. “But I want the big red one.”

Elli nodded. “It is a beautiful kite,” she said. “You can fly it first for three windy songs.” She pointed to the trees. “We will listen to the wind sing. Then you pass it to the next friend. Everyone gets a turn. That is fair.”

Max looked at the red kite. He looked at the other children. He looked at Elli’s kind eyes. The wind rustled the leaves—one song, two, three. Max’s frown softened. “Okay,” he said.

Elli smiled and clapped her little feet. “Thank you.”

Soon, the kites lifted up. Whoosh! The red one danced like a bright bird. The blue one spun like the sky. The yellow one giggled in the sun. The green one swayed like a leaf. The children laughed. “Look! Look!” they called.

Elli helped tie strings to little wrists. Her trunk was gentle and warm. “There you go,” she whispered. “Hold tight.”

When the wind sang three windy songs for Max, Elli touched his shoulder. “Time to share,” she said.

Max sighed, but he nodded. He handed the red kite to a small girl named Ana. “Your turn,” he said. “It goes high!”

Ana beamed. “Thank you, Max.”

Elli felt her heart glow. Being generous made the world feel big and bright.

Then a small problem popped up. A tiny turtle toy lay under a bench. Two boys found it at the same time.

“I found it first!” said Leo.

“No, I did!” said Sam.

Their faces crinkled. Their voices got loud. Elli’s tummy felt tight again. She wanted to help.

Elli knelt. “Friends,” she said softly, “let’s be fair. Let’s ask a question.”

She picked up the toy gently. “Whose turtle is this?”

A little girl raised her hand. “It’s mine,” she whispered. “I dropped it.”

Elli smiled. “Thank you for telling the truth.” She handed the turtle back. “Justice means we return things to the right owner.”

Leo and Sam looked at each other. “Sorry,” they said together.

Elli nodded. “Thank you for being honest. Would you like to share a kite together?” She held out a long green string.

Leo and Sam grinned. “Yes!”

They held the string together, side by side, and the green kite rose up, up, up. The boys laughed and bounced on their toes.

Grandma Gita clapped. “Elli, you led with kindness,” she said. “You were generous with time and turns. You were fair, and you helped make things right.”

Elli felt proud and peaceful. The wind kissed her ears. The sun warmed her back.

When it was time to go, Elli gathered the kites. She offered one last turn to a quiet child who had waited and watched. “Your turn,” she said. “I will help you.”

The child smiled and the kite lifted, floating like a happy cloud.

Back in the guest room, Elli and Grandma folded the kites. Crinkle, crinkle. The room smelled like oranges again. Elli pressed her cheek to Grandma’s hand.

“Today I learned to be generous,” Elli said. “I can share. I can be fair. I can help make things right.”

Grandma hugged her. “Yes, little Elli. When we share, the world feels sunny for everyone.”

Elli closed her eyes and listened to the soft wind at the window.

The End.
