# Tully’s Table Adventure

Tully was a shy turtle with a smooth, green shell. He liked quiet places and soft sounds. Today, the dining room felt big and busy. Sunlight made warm squares on the floor. The table smelled like toast and sweet jam. The chairs made little squeaks. Tully pulled his head in a little. His tummy fluttered.

“I feel small,” Tully whispered.

Mama Turtle smiled. “It’s okay to feel small,” she said. “We can prepare together.”

On the table sat a big bowl of fruit, a pitcher of water with lemon slices, and a stack of napkins. The napkins were bright blue. The lemons smelled fresh and tangy. Tully wiggled his toes. He wanted to help, but the room felt loud and busy. His heart thumped, thump, thump.

“I want to help,” Tully said softly, “but I feel shy.”

Mama Turtle nodded. “Feelings are welcome. Let’s make a plan. A plan can help shy feelings.”

“A plan?” Tully peeked out more.

“Yes,” Mama said. “Resourcefulness means using what we have, and thinking of smart ways to do things.”

Tully looked around. What did they have? He saw napkins, a small stool, a spoon, a basket, and his own shell.

First, Tully took a deep breath. In and out. In smelled like lemons and toast. Out felt warm and slow. He felt a tad braver.

“Step one,” Tully said, “I will pick jobs I can do.”

“Good thinking,” Mama said.

Tully looked at the napkins. “I can fold napkins.”

He climbed onto the small stool. It wobbled a little. “Oops,” he said. He placed the stool on the rug so it wouldn’t slide. “There. Safer.” That was preparation.

Tully folded a napkin into a triangle. Then another. The fabric felt soft on his feet. “One for each chair,” he said. He counted slowly. “One… two… three… four.” He placed them neatly. He smiled. His shy feelings grew smaller.

“Step two,” Tully said, “I will carry things in a safe way.”

He looked at the fruit bowl. It was big and heavy. Tully’s shell was strong, but he was small. He thought. He did not want to spill. He used his resourcefulness. He put the fruit in the basket, one by one. Plop went the apple. Plop went the pear. Plop went the banana. The basket had a handle. It was easy to carry.

“Smart plan,” Mama said. “You used what we have.”

Tully carried the basket to the table. He set it down with a gentle thump. He listened to the little thump and felt proud.

Then he saw the pitcher of lemon water. The pitcher was tall. The glasses were short. “How can I pour without spilling?” Tully asked.

“Let’s practice,” Mama said. She gave him a wooden spoon. “Use the spoon to scoop water. Then pour slowly.”

Tully practiced over the sink. Scoop. Pour. Drip, drip. The lemon smell tickled his nose. He giggled. “I can do it!”

Back at the table, Tully put the glasses close to the edge, but not too close. He set a napkin under each glass. “Just in case,” he said. “That is preparation.”

He scooped water with the spoon. Splash—just a tiny splash. “Oops,” Tully said. His cheeks felt warm. He took a breath. In and out. He dabbed the drop with a napkin. “It’s okay,” he told himself. “I am learning.” He tried again. Scoop. Pour. No spill this time. He felt brave.

Mama Turtle clapped softly. “You listened to your feelings. You made a plan. You used tools. That is resourcefulness.”

Tully’s shy tummy flutter changed to a happy flutter. He looked around. The dining room did not feel so big now. It felt ready. It felt like a place he helped make.

“Step three,” Tully said, “I will check my work.”

He walked around the table. Napkins? Yes. Glasses? Yes. Fruit? Yes. He even put a tiny salt shaker in the middle so it would not tip. He pushed the chairs in neatly. Squeak, squeak. He liked the friendly sound.

The doorbell rang. Ding-dong! Tully pulled his head in a little again. Guests! His heart went thump, thump. He took another breath. “I am prepared,” he told himself. “I know my plan.”

Grandma Turtle and Baby Tia came in. They smelled like sunshine and grass. “What a lovely table!” Grandma said.

Baby Tia clapped. “Lemon water!” she squeaked.

Tully smiled. “I helped,” he said. His voice was still small, but steady.

They sat together. The glasses tinkled. The fruit was sweet and juicy. Crunch went the apple. Mmm went the pear. The room felt warm and happy. Tully’s shell felt cozy.

“Thank you, Tully,” Grandma said. “You were very helpful.”

Tully felt proud. “When I feel shy, I can breathe, make a plan, and use what I have,” he said.

Mama Turtle nodded. “That is resourcefulness. That is preparation. And you took care of your feelings.”

Tully rubbed his smooth shell. “I can do small steps,” he said. “Small steps make big brave.”

Everyone smiled. The dining room glowed with soft afternoon light. Tully took another sip of lemon water. It tasted cool and bright. He felt ready for more small steps tomorrow.

The lesson was clear in Tully’s heart: Use what you have, make a simple plan, and take gentle breaths. With resourcefulness, preparation, and care for your feelings, you can do big things, one small step at a time.

The End.
