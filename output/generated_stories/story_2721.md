# Ruby Rabbit and the Laundry Room Song

Ruby was a small, determined rabbit with big ears and bright eyes. She loved to make music with anything she found. One rainy morning, she hopped into the laundry room. It smelled like clean soap and warm cotton. The floor felt cool on her paws. The washing machine hummed, “Whirr, whirr,” and the dryer clicked, “Tick, tick.”

“Ooh!” said Ruby. “Music! I can make a laundry song.”

Ruby tapped a plastic basket. Tap-tap. She patted a soft towel. Thump-thump. She shook a box of clothespins. Click-clack! She swayed her ears and sang, “Tap, thump, click-clack, whirr, whirr!”

Mama was folding shirts at the counter. She smiled. “That sounds fun, Ruby.” Then she held up a finger. “But remember, this is the laundry room. We must be careful and respectful. Please ask before you use things.”

Ruby’s whiskers wiggled. “I will ask,” she said. “I can be careful. I can be respectful.”

Ruby wanted to make the very best song. She stretched on her tiptoes to reach the shiny bottles. The lids were bright and colorful. “Maybe they can make a sound,” she whispered.

She touched a bottle and it wobbled. “Oops!” The bottle slid. Ruby’s heart jumped. Mama steadied it fast.

Ruby’s cheeks got hot. “I’m sorry, Mama. I forgot to ask.”

Mama crouched down. “Thank you for saying sorry. That was respectful. Let’s talk. We use kind words and careful hands here. If you want to make music, we can choose safe things together.”

Ruby took a deep breath. She nodded. “Yes, please. I want to make music. I will ask and wait.”

Mama pointed to a wooden spoon. “Try this,” she said. “And you may use the empty basket and those clean towels. No tall shelves. No heavy bottles.”

“Okay,” said Ruby. “Thank you for helping me.”

Ruby tapped the wooden spoon on the basket. Tap-tap. She drummed on a folded towel. Bop-bop. She listened to the washer. Whirr. She listened to the dryer. Tick. She smiled and sang softly, “Tap-tap, bop-bop, whirr, tick.”

Just then, Max the cat came in. His gray fur was soft like a cloud. His tail swished. He liked quiet naps. He yawned and blinked. “Ruby,” he said in a sleepy voice, “your music is loud. My ears are tired.”

Ruby stopped. She felt a little sad. She also felt a little proud of her song. She looked at Max’s droopy eyes. She remembered Mama’s words: kind words and careful hands.

Ruby placed the spoon on her lap. She said, “Max, I hear you. You want quiet. I want to play music. How can we both be okay?”

Max sat. His whiskers twitched. “Maybe you can play softly,” he said. “And maybe I can nap on the rug, far from the tapping.”

Ruby thought. She nodded. “Yes! I can play soft-soft.” She whispered to her paws, “Like a gentle raindrop.”

She tried again. Tap… tap. Bop… bop. Whirr… tick. Max purred. “That’s better,” he murmured, curling up on the far rug. His purr sounded like a tiny drum: purrrrr.

Ruby felt calm. The laundry room felt cozy and warm. The air smelled like lemons and sunshine. She could hear the gentle sounds. She smiled. “Thank you, Max, for telling me,” she said. “I respect your ears.”

Max opened one eye. “Thank you for listening,” he said. “I respect your music.”

Ruby kept playing softly. But when the dryer beeped, “Beep!” it startled her. She jumped and dropped the spoon. Clatter! Her heart thumped fast. She wanted to stop. She wanted to run.

She put her paw on her chest. “I can try again,” she whispered. “I am determined. I will keep going, but I will be careful.”

Mama showed Ruby how to count a slow beat. “One, two, rest. One, two, rest,” Mama said. “You can keep your song steady.”

Ruby practiced. She tapped and counted. She listened and paused. She asked before moving anything. “May I use this towel?” she asked. “Yes,” said Mama. “Thank you for asking.”

Soon, Ruby made a beautiful laundry room song. It was soft and sweet. It sounded like a hug. Tap… bop… whirr… tick… purrrrr.

Max lifted his head and smiled. “Your song helped me nap,” he said.

Mama clapped quietly. “You worked hard. You were safe. You spoke kindly. You listened to others. That is respect.”

Ruby’s ears stood tall. “Respect means I use kind words, I listen, and I care about how others feel,” she said. “I can make music and be gentle.”

Mama gave Ruby a warm hug. “Yes,” she said. “And when we have a problem, we can talk, listen, and find a way that works for everyone.”

Ruby picked up the spoon one last time. She whispered a thank-you to the washer, the dryer, the towels, and Max. Then she played a tiny good-bye tune.

Tap… bop… whirr… tick… purrrrr.

The laundry room felt peaceful and happy. Ruby felt proud and calm. She had made music. She had kept trying. And she had shown respect to her family and the things around her.

The End.
