# Pippa Parrot’s Plan

In a sunny home office, Pippa the creative parrot fluffed her rainbow feathers. The room smelled like warm paper and lemony cleaner. The keyboard made tiny click-clack sounds, and the printer hummed like a friendly bee. Pippa loved this place.

“I want to make my own poster,” Pippa chirped. “I can do it by myself!”

On the desk, there were crayons, sticky notes, and a roll of washi tape with stars. A big white paper sat in the middle like a blank cloud. Pippa tapped her beak. Tap-tap. “First, a plan,” she said softly.

She looked at the steps chart on the wall. It had simple pictures:
1. Think.
2. Gather.
3. Try.
4. Check.
5. Fix.
6. Share.

Pippa nodded. “I will be independent. I will follow the steps.”

She looked at the clock—tick, tock. “I have time before lunch,” she said. “Great!”

Pippa thought. “What should my poster say?” She listened to the breeze whoosh against the window. She heard the soft purr of the laptop fan. She smelled the sweet banana from her snack bowl. “I got it!” she chirped. “My poster will say: I Can Try!”

She gathered crayons—red, blue, green, and sunny yellow. She found the tape and the safe scissors with round tips. She pulled the paper close. “Time to try,” she said.

Pippa drew big letters. I CAN TRY. The crayons made a soft scritch-scratch sound. The blue crayon smelled a little like new books. She drew a small parrot with a brave smile. She added stars and a tiny sun.

But the paper kept sliding. Swoop! It almost fell. Pippa took a calm breath. “Check,” she said, looking at step four. “What is the problem?”

She looked closely. The desk was smooth and slippery. “I need the paper to stay,” she said. “What can I do?”

She tapped her beak again. Tap-tap. “I could use tape,” she decided. She tore a piece, but—rip!—it stuck to her feather. “Oh no,” she giggled. “Hello, sticky tape.”

Pippa did not shout. She spoke gently to herself. “It’s okay, Pippa. Try again.” She used her talon to hold one edge. She placed the tape carefully on the corner. Press, press. Then the other corners. Press, press. The paper stayed. “Yes!” she chirped. “I fixed it.”

She colored more stars. She cut a strip of star tape to make a border. Snip, snip. The scissors made a quiet click. She felt proud. The poster looked bright and happy.

Just then, a soft meow came from the doorway. It was Milo the cat. His fur was gray and cozy, like a blanket. He stepped in, tail swishing. “Mrrr?” he said.

“Hi, Milo,” Pippa chirped. “I’m making a poster by myself.”

Milo sniffed the crayons. He sneezed—achoo! “Mrrr.” He reached a paw toward the washi tape.

Pippa held up a wing. “Milo, please stop,” she said in a calm voice. “I am working. You may watch. You may not grab.”

Milo blinked his golden eyes. He pulled his paw back. He curled up on the rug and purred, rumble-rumble, like a tiny engine.

Pippa smiled. “Thank you for listening,” she said. “You can help by being my quiet friend.”

She checked her poster again. One letter was a little wiggly. “Fix,” she said. She traced the letter slowly. The crayon felt warm in her toes. She added a small heart for kindness.

Pippa wanted to hang the poster by the door. She looked up. It felt high. She felt a tiny flutter in her tummy. “I want to do it by myself,” she whispered. “But I also want to be safe.”

She looked around. There was a step stool with rubber feet. She checked it with a gentle push. It did not wobble. “Good,” she said. “I can use tools to be independent and safe.”

Pippa placed the stool. She tested it. Step. Step. She kept one wing on the wall and the poster under her other wing. She stuck the tape to the back. Press, press. She reached up. Press. The poster held. Pippa climbed down slowly. Her heart felt big and proud.

Milo stretched and meowed, “Mrrr-wow.”

Pippa read the words out loud: “I Can Try.” The letters sounded strong. She stood tall. “I did it,” she said. “I made a plan. I spoke kindly. I solved problems. I asked no one to do it for me. I did it myself, and I stayed safe.”

Just then, the door opened, and a gentle voice said, “Pippa, it looks wonderful.” It was their grown-up, smiling. “I saw you working. You were calm and careful.”

Pippa fluffed her feathers. “Thank you. I used steps. I used peaceful words with Milo. I fixed problems with my own ideas.”

The grown-up nodded. “That is independence. You tried first, you thought, and you used kind, clear words. I am proud of you.”

Pippa chirped, “And I am proud of me, too.”

She patted the poster. “I Can Try,” she read again. Milo rubbed his head on her wing, soft and warm. The office still smelled like lemon, and the little printer hummed. Everything felt bright.

Pippa smiled. “Next, I will pour my own water,” she said. “I will go slow. I can do it.”

She and Milo padded to the kitchen. Pippa held the small cup with two careful claws. She poured. Drip, drip. No spill! She took a sip. “Cool and tasty,” she said.

Back in the office, the poster shone by the door, a sunny sign for every day.

Today Pippa learned: Try first, think kindly, speak calmly, and use safe tools. Independence grows when we practice.

The End.
