# Tully the Turtle Counts to Calm

In a quiet house, down creaky stairs, there was a basement lab. The lab smelled like warm earth and clean water. Lights hummed softly. Glass tanks bubbled with tiny streams. Plants reached up under bright lamps. Beep, beep, went a little machine that counted water drops.

In this lab lived Tully, a shy turtle. Tully had a smooth green shell and gentle eyes. He liked soft moss, cool pebbles, and the tickle of bubbles on his toes. He also liked to watch Dr. Dot, the kind scientist with curly hair who cared for all the small creatures.

“Good morning, Tully,” said Dr. Dot. “Today we will clean the tank and sort the bottle caps we found at the pond. We keep the water clean for fish and frogs. That’s called conservation.”

Tully nodded. He liked helping, but he was shy. When things got noisy, Tully pulled his head into his shell.

Dr. Dot set out a small blue bucket, a brush, and a pile of colorful caps. Red, yellow, green, and blue. “We clean together,” she said. “We sort and save. We use and reuse.”

Tully reached for a bright red cap. It slipped. Plop! It fell into the water. Splash! The water tickled his face. Tully jumped. He did not like the surprise.

He tried again. The brush bumped the cap. Plink! It rolled away under a plant. Tully’s cheeks felt hot. His tummy felt tight. His heart thump-thumped.

“I can’t do it!” Tully cried. His voice came out sharp. He stomped his little foot. The bucket wobbled. Water sloshed. Drip, drop, drip.

Dr. Dot crouched low so her eyes were kind and level. “I see you feel mad, Tully,” she said softly. “Mad feels hot and fast. That’s okay. Big feelings happen. We can make a plan.”

Tully sniffled. He pulled his head in a little. It was dark and safe in his shell. But he could still hear the gentle hum of the lights and the bubble-pop-pop.

Dr. Dot tapped the floor lightly. Tap, tap. “Would you like help?” she asked. “We can use turtle calm.”

“What is turtle calm?” Tully whispered.

“It is a way to rest your mad,” said Dr. Dot. “We stop. We breathe. We count. We use kind words.”

Tully peeked out. “How?”

Dr. Dot showed him. “Hands on shell. Breathe in like you smell a flower.” She sniffed. “Breathe out like you blow a bubble.” She puffed. “We count with the bubbles. One… two… three.”

Tully put his hands on his shell. He smelled the wet earth smell. He blew like a bubble-maker. Puff. The air felt cool on his tongue.

“Let’s try together,” said Dr. Dot.

“In…” sniffed Tully. “Out…” puffed Tully. “One.”

“In…” sniff. “Out…” puff. “Two.”

“In…” sniff. “Out…” puff. “Three.”

Tully’s cheeks cooled. His tummy softened. His heart slowed to a gentle thump. The lab sounded soft again. Bubble-pop-pop. Beep, beep. Hummmm.

“I feel better,” Tully said.

Dr. Dot smiled. “Good noticing. Now we use kind words for ourselves. Try this: ‘I am learning. I can try again.’”

Tully said, “I am learning. I can try again.”

They looked at the caps. Red, yellow, green, blue.

“Let’s make it a game,” said Dr. Dot. “Sort by color to help the Earth. Red caps in the red bin. Yellow caps in the yellow bin. Each cap we sort keeps our water friends safe.”

Tully took a deep breath. He reached slowly. “Slow is steady,” he whispered. He picked up a red cap. Drop! It landed in the red bin. Clink!

He smiled. He picked a blue cap. Clink! A green cap. Clink! A yellow cap tried to roll away. Tully felt a tiny spark of mad. “Stop,” he told himself. “Turtle calm.” He breathed in like a flower. He breathed out like a bubble. “One… two… three.”

Then he used a gentle finger and guided the cap to the yellow bin. Clink!

“You did it!” said Dr. Dot. “You used your calm. You were kind to yourself. And you helped the pond.”

Tully’s shell felt warm and proud. He liked this feeling. He liked helping the water stay clean. He liked being gentle.

They cleaned the tank. Swish, swish went the brush. Bubbles danced. Tully put soft moss in place. He placed smooth pebbles just right.

Dr. Dot held up the last cap. “Want to try?” she asked.

Tully nodded. He took the cap in both hands. He felt steady. He dropped it into the bin. Clink!

“You are a calm helper,” said Dr. Dot. “When we feel mad, we can stop, breathe, count, and use kind words. That keeps our hearts safe. It keeps our hands gentle. It helps us care for the Earth.”

Tully looked at the clean tank. The water was clear like glass. Little fish swam swoosh, swoosh. Plants waved hello. The basement lab hummed happy.

Tully whispered, “When I feel mad, I will count to calm. I will be kind. I will help the water.”

Dr. Dot gave a soft clap. “That’s the lesson,” she said. “Count to calm. Be kind. Care for our home.”

Tully tucked into his cozy moss. He felt shy and brave at the same time. He listened to the bubble-pop-pop and the gentle hum.

“Good work today,” said Dr. Dot.

“Good work, me,” said Tully, smiling.

The End.
